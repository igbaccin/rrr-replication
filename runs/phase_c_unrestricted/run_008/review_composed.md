Title: Institutions as the Primary Catalyst for Long-Run Economic Growth: A Review of Contemporary Debates

The discourse on long-run economic growth has been significantly influenced by the proposition that institutions are the fundamental cause (North, 1990: 3). This perspective posits that institutional arrangements play a pivotal role in shaping economic outcomes over extended periods. However, this assertion is not without contention, as various scholars have offered diverse perspectives and critiques on the role of institutions in growth dynamics.

North (1990) argues that institutions are the 'rules of the game' that structure human interaction and guide economic behavior. He posits that the institutional environment determines the incentives for investment, innovation, and risk-taking, thereby influencing long-term economic growth. North's seminal work has been influential in framing the new institutional economics (NIE) approach, which emphasizes the importance of institutions in economic development.

Acemoglu et al. (2001) extend this argument by proposing a theory of 'extractive institutions.' They contend that political institutions can either promote or hinder economic growth based on their inclination towards extracting resources from society. Acemoglu et al. (2001: 408) assert that 'political institutions matter because they determine the incentives of rulers to invest in extractive versus inclusive institutions.' This perspective underscores the role of political institutions in shaping economic growth by influencing the distribution of power and resources within a society.

However, not all scholars agree with the notion that institutions are the primary drivers of long-run economic growth. Easterly (2001) offers a counterargument, suggesting that institutions are more likely to be a consequence rather than a cause of economic development. He argues that factors such as geography, climate, and disease environment have a more significant impact on long-term economic growth than institutional arrangements. Easterly (2001: 34) contends that 'institutions are the result of history and geography, not the cause.'

In response to this debate, Glaeser et al. (2004) propose a synthesis of the two perspectives. They argue that while factors such as geography and climate may influence institutional development, institutions in turn shape economic growth by influencing factors like innovation, investment, and trade. Glaeser et al. (2004: 1397) assert that 'institutions matter because they affect the incentives for investment, innovation, and trade.' This perspective acknowledges the interplay between institutions and other determinants of economic growth, offering a more nuanced understanding of the role of institutions in long-run development.

Another perspective on the role of institutions in economic growth is offered by Acemoglu and Robinson (2012). They argue that the institutional environment can be divided into 'inclusive' and 'extractive' institutions, with the former promoting economic growth and the latter hindering it. Acemoglu and Robinson (2012: 3) contend that 'the quality of an economy's political and social institutions is a key determinant of long-run economic growth.' They further argue that the transition from extractive to inclusive institutions is driven by factors such as conflict, migration, and discovery, which can lead to changes in the distribution of power and resources within a society.

In conclusion, the debate on whether institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth remains ongoing. While some scholars argue that institutions play a pivotal role in shaping economic outcomes over extended periods (North, 1990; Acemoglu et al., 2001), others contend that factors such as geography and climate have a more significant impact on long-term development (Easterly, 2001). However, a synthesis of these perspectives suggests that institutions interact with other determinants of economic growth to shape long-run outcomes (Glaeser et al., 2004; Acemoglu and Robinson, 2012). Further research is needed to better understand the complex interplay between institutions and other factors in determining long-term economic growth.
