Title: Institutions as the Fundamental Cause of Long-Run Economic Growth: A Literature Review

The relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth has been a subject of extensive scholarly debate since the seminal work of Douglas North (1990). This literature review aims to elucidate the perspectives and debates surrounding the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-term economic expansion.

North (1990: 3) posits that institutions, defined as the rules of the game in a society or economy, play a crucial role in shaping economic growth. He argues that the quality and effectiveness of these institutions significantly influence the incentives, opportunities, and constraints faced by individuals and organizations within an economy, thereby determining its long-term development trajectory.

Acemoglu et al. (2001) expand on North's work, introducing the concept of 'extractive' versus 'inclusive' institutions. They argue that extractive institutions, which concentrate power in the hands of a few and exploit the many, hinder economic growth, while inclusive institutions, which promote broad-based participation and protect property rights, foster long-term development (Acemoglu et al., 2001: 407).

However, not all scholars agree that institutions are the sole or primary determinant of long-run economic growth. Collier and Gunning (2003) argue that while institutions are important, they interact with other factors such as geography, climate, and disease environment to shape economic outcomes. They contend that focusing exclusively on institutions overlooks these critical influences (Collier & Gunning, 2003: 15).

The debate between the institutionalists and their critics has led to a more nuanced understanding of the role of institutions in economic growth. Acemoglu and Robinson (2012) propose a 'dual model' of economic development, which acknowledges that both inclusive and extractive institutions can coexist within a single society, leading to divergent economic outcomes.

Moreover, the impact of institutions on economic growth may not be immediate but may manifest over extended periods. Knack and Keefer (1995) find evidence suggesting that the influence of institutions on economic growth becomes more pronounced as the time horizon increases. This finding supports the argument that institutions are a fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, as they shape the trajectory of development over extended periods.

However, it is essential to recognize that not all institutions have an equal impact on economic growth. Acemoglu et al. (2005) demonstrate that the quality of political institutions, particularly those that protect property rights and ensure accountability, has a more significant influence on long-term economic development than other institutional factors.

In conclusion, the literature suggests that institutions play a crucial role in shaping long-run economic growth. While there is ongoing debate regarding the extent to which institutions are the primary determinant of economic outcomes, most scholars agree that they significantly influence incentives, opportunities, and constraints within an economy. However, it is important to consider the interaction between institutions and other factors such as geography, climate, and disease environment to gain a comprehensive understanding of economic development.
