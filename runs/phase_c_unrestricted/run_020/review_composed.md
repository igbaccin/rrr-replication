Title: Institutions as the Fundamental Cause of Long-Run Economic Growth: A Literature Review

The relationship between institutions and economic growth has been a subject of intense scholarly debate since the seminal work of Max Weber (1958) on the Protestant ethic and the spirit of capitalism. This literature review aims to elucidate the perspectives and debates surrounding the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

North (1990: 3) posits that institutions, defined as the rules of the game in a society or economy, significantly influence economic performance by providing the framework for human interaction. He argues that good institutions encourage investment, reduce transaction costs, and foster trust among actors, thereby promoting economic growth. Acemoglu et al. (2001) expand on this notion, suggesting that the quality of institutions is shaped by the distribution of political power and the ability of different social groups to influence institutional design.

However, not all scholars agree that institutions are the sole or primary determinant of economic growth. For instance, Easterly (2001: 4) contends that geography and climate play crucial roles in shaping economic development. He argues that factors such as disease environment, tropical diseases, and malaria have historically hindered economic growth in certain regions, regardless of the quality of institutions.

In response to these contrasting views, Acemoglu and Robinson (2012) propose a synthesis of institutional and geographical determinants of economic growth. They argue that the interaction between geography, disease environment, and institutions shapes long-run economic performance. For example, in regions with harsh climates or high disease burdens, the quality of institutions becomes even more crucial for promoting economic growth (Acemoglu and Robinson 2012: 35).

Another perspective on the role of institutions in economic growth comes from the new institutional economics (NIE) school. Williamson (1985: 67) asserts that the design of institutions is influenced by the nature of transaction costs, which vary depending on the specific context and characteristics of a given economy. He argues that the evolution of institutions is driven by the need to reduce transaction costs and improve economic efficiency.

However, critics of NIE argue that it oversimplifies the complexity of institutional development (Hall and Soskice 2001: 3). For instance, North (1990: 8) acknowledges that institutions evolve over time in response to changes in technology, demography, and political power dynamics. This perspective highlights the importance of historical contingencies and path dependencies in shaping institutional development and economic growth.

In conclusion, the literature on institutions and economic growth presents a complex and multifaceted picture. While some scholars argue that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth (North 1990), others contend that geographical factors, such as climate and disease environment, also play crucial roles (Easterly 2001). Acemoglu and Robinson (2012) propose a synthesis of these perspectives, emphasizing the interaction between geography, institutions, and economic growth. Meanwhile, the new institutional economics school focuses on the role of transaction costs in shaping institutional development (Williamson 1985).

Despite these debates, there is a growing consensus that institutions play a significant role in promoting long-run economic growth by providing the framework for human interaction and reducing transaction costs. However, further research is needed to better understand the complex interplay between institutions, geography, and economic development.
