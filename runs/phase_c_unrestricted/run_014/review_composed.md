Title: Institutions as the Primary Catalyst for Long-Run Economic Growth: A Literature Review

The debate on the causality between institutions and long-run economic growth has been a subject of extensive scholarly discourse. This literature review aims to synthesize key perspectives, arguments, and debates surrounding the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-term economic development.

North (1990: 3) posits that institutions are the 'rules of the game' in society or, more formally, the humanly devised constraints that shape human interaction. He argues that these rules determine the structure of incentives in any political economy and have a significant impact on economic performance. Acemoglu et al. (2001: 4) concur, stating that institutions are crucial for economic growth as they provide the framework for property rights, contract enforcement, and control of violence.

One perspective emphasizes the role of inclusive institutions in promoting long-run economic growth. Acemoglu and Robinson (2012: 37) suggest that the quality of a country's political and economic institutions determines whether it follows a path of 'extractive institutions' or 'inclusive institutions.' Extractive institutions, characterized by limited property rights, weak rule of law, and high levels of political instability, hinder economic growth. In contrast, inclusive institutions, which offer strong property rights, effective contract enforcement, and political stability, foster long-term economic development.

Another perspective focuses on the role of cultural norms in shaping institutions and economic performance. Greif (2006: 15) argues that cultural norms influence the development of formal institutions by determining the level of trust among individuals within a society. In societies with high levels of trust, formal institutions can evolve more efficiently, leading to better economic outcomes.

However, not all scholars agree that institutions are the primary driver of long-run economic growth. Easterly (2001: 3) contends that geography and climate play a significant role in shaping economic development, arguing that these factors can limit the ability of even well-designed institutions to promote growth. Collier (2007: 5) further argues that institutional quality is endogenous to income levels, suggesting that poor countries may struggle to develop inclusive institutions due to their low levels of income and economic development.

The debate on the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth has also led to discussions on the role of path dependency in shaping institutional development. North (1990: 97) argues that historical events and decisions can lock societies into particular institutional configurations, making it difficult for them to transition to more efficient institutions even when there is a strong incentive to do so. This perspective emphasizes the importance of understanding the historical context in which institutions develop to fully appreciate their impact on economic growth.

In conclusion, the literature review reveals a robust scholarly discourse on the role of institutions in long-run economic growth. While many scholars argue that inclusive institutions are crucial for promoting economic development, others contend that geography and climate play significant roles in shaping economic outcomes. The debate also highlights the importance of path dependency in understanding the evolution of institutional configurations and their impact on economic performance.
