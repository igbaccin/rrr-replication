Title: Institutions as the Fundamental Cause of Long-Run Economic Growth: A Literature Review

The relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth has been a subject of intense scholarly debate since the seminal works of economists like Adam Smith (Smith, 1776) and Max Weber (Weber, 1958). This literature review aims to elucidate the perspectives and debates surrounding the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

North (1990: 3) posits that institutions are the rules of the game in society or, more formally, those constraints that shape human interaction. He argues that the nature of these institutions significantly influences a country's economic performance over the long run. Acemoglu et al. (2001: 4) concur, suggesting that the institutional environment determines the incentives for investment and innovation, thereby shaping economic growth.

However, not all scholars agree on the primacy of institutions in determining long-run economic growth. Collier and Gunning (2009: 63) argue that while institutions are crucial, they are not the sole determinant. They contend that geography, climate, and disease environment also play significant roles in shaping economic performance.

One of the key debates revolves around the nature of institutional change. Acemoglu and Robinson (2012: 3) propose a theory of 'inclusive institutions' that foster broad-based growth by providing incentives for investment, innovation, and participation. On the other hand, North (1990: 97) argues that institutional change is often path-dependent, meaning that historical events and decisions can lock societies into particular institutional configurations that may hinder economic growth.

The role of institutions in promoting economic growth is further complicated by the presence of 'extractive institutions' (Acemoglu et al., 2001: 9). These are institutions that extract resources from society rather than facilitating productive activities. Acemoglu and Robinson (2012) argue that the rise and fall of empires can be explained by their transition from inclusive to extractive institutions, which ultimately led to their decline.

Another perspective on the institutional-growth nexus is provided by Knack and Keefer (1995: 643). They empirically demonstrate a strong correlation between 'good' institutions—defined as those that provide property rights, rule of law, and control of corruption—and economic growth. However, they also acknowledge the potential for reverse causality, suggesting that economic growth may lead to better institutions rather than the other way around.

The debate on the primacy of institutions in long-run economic growth is further complicated by the 'institutional complementarities' (Rodrik, 2003: 148). This concept suggests that certain institutional arrangements work well together but not with others, leading to different development outcomes. For instance, a market-oriented economy may thrive under democratic institutions but falter under authoritarian ones.

In conclusion, the literature on institutions and long-run economic growth presents a complex picture. While many scholars argue for the primacy of institutions, others caution against overstating their role. The debate encompasses various perspectives, including the nature of institutional change, the role of extractive institutions, the correlation between institutions and growth, and the concept of institutional complementarities. Future research should aim to disentangle these complexities to gain a deeper understanding of the institutional-growth nexus.
