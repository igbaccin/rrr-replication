Title: Institutions as the Fundamental Cause of Long-Run Economic Growth: A Literature Review

The interplay between institutions and economic growth has been a subject of intense scholarly debate since the seminal works of economists like Adam Smith (Smith, 1776) and Max Weber (Weber, 1958). This literature review aims to elucidate the perspective that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, while also acknowledging contrasting viewpoints and debates within the academic community.

North (1990: 3) posits that institutions constitute the rules of the game in society or, more formally, those sets of constraints that humans devise to shape human interaction. According to North, these institutions can be formal, such as laws and property rights, or informal, like social norms and cultural values. The significance of institutions lies in their ability to influence economic growth by shaping incentives, reducing transaction costs, and promoting trust among individuals (North, 1990: 3-4).

Acemoglu et al. (2001) expand on North's work by proposing the 'Institutions as Solutions to Collective Action Problems' hypothesis. They argue that the quality of institutions is determined by the political and economic power distribution within a society, which in turn shapes the incentives for investment in productive activities (Acemoglu et al., 2001: 407). In other words, well-functioning institutions are more likely to emerge in societies where there is a broad-based distribution of political and economic power, as opposed to those with concentrated power.

However, not all scholars agree that institutions are the sole determinant of long-run economic growth. Easterly (2001: 3) contends that while institutions matter, they are not the only factor influencing economic development. He argues that geography, climate, and disease environment also play significant roles in shaping economic outcomes (Easterly, 2001: 4-5).

Collier (2007) further complicates the debate by suggesting that the relationship between institutions and growth is not straightforward. He argues that some institutions may have negative effects on growth, such as those that perpetuate conflict or corruption. Collier also emphasizes the importance of understanding the historical context in which institutions evolve, as this can greatly influence their impact on economic development (Collier, 2007: 15-16).

The debate between proponents of institutional determinism and those who argue for a more nuanced approach continues. Acemoglu and Robinson (2012) present an argument that synthesizes both perspectives by suggesting that the interaction between institutions, geography, and historical events shapes long-run economic growth. They argue that some regions, like Western Europe and North America, were fortunate enough to experience a 'Great Divergence' in which they developed strong institutions, benefited from favorable geographical conditions, and experienced key historical events that propelled them towards sustained economic growth (Acemoglu and Robinson, 2012: 3-4).

In conclusion, the literature on institutions and long-run economic growth presents a complex picture. While there is consensus that institutions play a crucial role in shaping incentives and influencing economic outcomes, the debate remains as to whether they are the sole determinant of growth. Scholars like Acemoglu et al. (2001) and Acemoglu and Robinson (2012) argue that institutions are indeed fundamental, while others like Easterly (2001) and Collier (2007) contend that a more nuanced approach is necessary to fully understand the factors influencing long-run economic growth.
