Title: Institutions as the Fundamental Cause of Long-Run Economic Growth: A Literature Review

The relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth has been a subject of extensive scholarly debate, with numerous studies positing that institutions play a pivotal role in shaping economic development (North & Thomas, 1973; Acemoglu et al., 2001). This literature review aims to elucidate the various perspectives and debates surrounding the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

The seminal work by Douglass North and Robert Thomas (1973) introduced the concept of 'institutional economics,' which posits that the rules of the game, or institutional arrangements, significantly influence economic performance. They argued that the development of formal institutions, such as laws and property rights, is crucial for promoting long-run economic growth by providing a stable environment conducive to investment and innovation (North & Thomas, 1973: 2).

Acemoglu et al. (2001) further expanded on this idea in their influential study, 'The Colonial Origins of Comparative Development.' They contend that the quality of institutions is shaped by historical events and power relations, with the establishment of extractive institutions leading to economic stagnation, while inclusive institutions fostering long-run growth (Acemoglu et al., 2001: 408).

However, not all scholars agree that institutions are the sole determinant of long-run economic growth. Knack and Keefer (1995) argue that while institutions play a role in promoting economic development, other factors such as geography, culture, and political stability also contribute significantly to growth outcomes (Knack & Keefer, 1995: 75).

The debate over the primacy of institutions versus other factors is further complicated by the issue of endogeneity. Engerman and Sokoloff (2002) argue that it is difficult to establish causality between institutions and economic growth due to the interplay between institutions and other factors, such as geography and technology (Engerman & Sokoloff, 2002: 13).

Moreover, the nature of the relationship between institutions and economic growth can vary across different contexts. Glaeser et al. (2004) found that while formal institutions are crucial for promoting long-run economic growth in developing countries, informal institutions, such as social networks and norms, play a more significant role in advanced economies (Glaeser et al., 2004: 165).

In conclusion, the literature on institutions and long-run economic growth presents a complex picture. While many scholars argue that institutions are fundamental to economic development, others contend that other factors also play significant roles. The debate over endogeneity further complicates matters, as does the recognition that the relationship between institutions and growth can vary across different contexts.
