Title: Institutions as the Pivotal Factor in Long-Run Economic Growth: A Literature Review

The relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth has been a subject of intense scholarly debate since the seminal works of economists like Max Weber (1958) and Douglas North (1981). This literature review aims to delve into the perspectives, debates, and empirical evidence surrounding the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

North (1981: 34) posits that institutions are the rules of the game in society or, more formally, those sets of constraints that humans devise to control human interaction. He argues that these institutions significantly influence the structure and functioning of economies, shaping their long-run growth trajectories. Acemoglu et al. (2001) expand on this notion, suggesting that the quality of institutions determines whether a society is open or closed, leading to either growth or stagnation.

One perspective within this debate focuses on the role of formal institutions, such as laws, property rights, and contract enforcement mechanisms (North, 1981: 35). Acemoglu et al. (2001) demonstrate that the presence of strong formal institutions encourages economic growth by providing a stable environment conducive to investment and innovation. Conversely, the absence or weakness of such institutions can lead to underdevelopment and stagnation (Acemoglu et al., 2001: 438).

Another perspective emphasizes the importance of informal institutions, including social norms, cultural values, and political systems (North, 1990: 97). Collier and Gunning (1999) argue that these informal institutions can either facilitate or hinder economic growth. For instance, in societies with strong norms of reciprocity and trust, cooperation and investment are more likely to occur, fostering long-run economic growth (Collier and Gunning, 1999: 24).

However, not all agree that institutions are the sole determinant of long-run economic growth. Some scholars argue that geography, climate, and natural resources also play crucial roles (Diamond, 1997; Krugman, 1995). For example, Diamond (1997) contends that the availability of domesticable plants and animals significantly influenced the development of early civilizations, shaping their institutional structures and economic growth.

The debate over institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth has also sparked discussions on the relationship between institutions and economic development in specific regions. For instance, Acemoglu et al. (2001) examine the European colonization of the Americas and its impact on institutional development and economic growth. They argue that the presence or absence of European settlers determined the quality of institutions established in the New World, leading to vastly different economic outcomes (Acemoglu et al., 2001: 439).

In a similar vein, North (1981) discusses the role of institutions in explaining the divergent paths of Western and non-Western societies. He suggests that the development of modern Western institutions, such as representative government, rule of law, and market economies, contributed to their long-run economic growth and global dominance (North, 1981: 36).

In conclusion, the literature on institutions and long-run economic growth presents a complex interplay of formal and informal institutions, geography, climate, and natural resources. While some scholars argue that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, others contend that multiple factors contribute to economic development. Regardless, it is clear that institutions play a pivotal role in shaping economies and their trajectories over time.
