Title: Institutions as the Pivotal Factor in Long-Run Economic Growth: A Synthesis of Scholarly Perspectives

The interplay between institutions and economic growth has been a subject of intense scholarly debate since the seminal works of economists like Adam Smith (Smith_1776) and Max Weber (Weber_1958). The consensus, as articulated by North (1990), posits that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. This literature review aims to elucidate the various perspectives and debates surrounding this assertion.

North (1990) argues that institutions, encompassing formal rules (such as laws and property rights) and informal constraints (like social norms and cultural values), significantly influence economic growth by shaping incentives, reducing transaction costs, and fostering trust among individuals. He posits that the evolution of these institutions over time is a key determinant of long-term economic performance.

Acemoglu et al. (2001) expand upon North's work by proposing the 'Institutional Diversity' hypothesis, which suggests that the type and quality of institutions can have profound effects on economic growth. They contend that inclusive institutions, which protect property rights, encourage competition, and provide equal opportunities for all citizens, foster economic growth more effectively than extractive institutions, which concentrate power in the hands of a few elites at the expense of the majority (Acemoglu et al._2001: 468).

However, not all scholars agree that institutions are the sole determinant of long-run economic growth. Collier and Gunning (2005) argue that while institutions play a crucial role, they are not the only factor influencing economic performance. They posit that geography, climate, and natural resources also significantly impact economic growth, particularly in developing countries (Collier & Gunning_2005: 18).

In response to this debate, Easterly (2006) proposes the 'Pluralist' view, which acknowledges that multiple factors contribute to long-run economic growth. He contends that while institutions are important, they interact with other factors like geography and technology in complex ways that are difficult to disentangle empirically (Easterly_2006: 14).

The debate over the primacy of institutions in long-run economic growth is further complicated by the issue of endogeneity. Knack and Keefer (1995) argue that it is challenging to establish causality between institutions and economic growth due to reverse causation, where economic development may lead to the creation or improvement of institutions rather than vice versa (Knack & Keefer_1995: 74).

In conclusion, while there is a strong scholarly consensus that institutions play a crucial role in long-run economic growth, the debate remains ongoing regarding their primacy among other factors. The 'Institutional Diversity' hypothesis and the 'Pluralist' view offer valuable insights into this complex relationship, acknowledging both the importance of institutions and the need to consider other factors influencing economic performance. However, the issue of endogeneity persists as a significant challenge in empirical research on this topic.
