The central question under examination in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, a thesis that has garnered significant attention among scholars. The stakes are high, as understanding the determinants of economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting development and reducing poverty in various regions.

Supporting the thesis, Austin (2008) argues that institutions play a crucial role in long-term economic growth, particularly in Africa, where certain trades reinforced rather than originated the continent's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe. Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) focus on the political factors underpinning economic growth and the development of markets, emphasizing the role of institutions in enforcing and changing economic rules. Acemoglu et al. (2001) also support this view, demonstrating a large effect of institutions on economic performance.

However, not all scholars agree. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate the argument by pointing out that Sub-Saharan Africa has been absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth due to the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance, arguing that understanding the processes by which women's economic contributions have been subsumed under household production is essential for policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles.

As the debate continues, it becomes clear that while institutions play a significant role in long-run economic growth, other factors such as technological innovation and societal changes also play crucial roles. The challenge for future research lies in understanding the complex interplay between these various determinants of economic growth and development.

Institutions, as fundamental causes of long-run economic growth, have been extensively studied and supported by various scholars. Kuznets (1973) posits that not only are high aggregate growth rates associated with rapid changes in economic structure, but these structural changes also influence other aspects of society (p.4). Furthermore, he argues that the succession of technological innovations characteristic of modern economic growth and the social innovations providing necessary adjustments play significant roles in economic and social structure (p.6).

North & Weingast (1989) focus on political factors underlying economic growth and market development, emphasizing the role of institutions governing how rules are enforced and changed (p.2). They demonstrate that representative government with multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs are crucial to economic growth and political freedom (p.28).

North (1989) discusses how centralized enforcement mechanisms, such as those observed in Spain, contribute to economic growth by reducing transaction costs and increasing security rights (pp.6, 9). Acemoglu et al. (2002) highlight the importance of institutions in understanding economic and political development over the past 500 years, emphasizing that a reversal in the distribution of economic prosperity is related to institutional changes (p.49).

The significance of institutions in driving long-term economic growth is further underscored by Austin (2008) who posits that they are "the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth" (p.26). This assertion is based on the premise that institutions, such as property rights and rule of law, play a crucial role in shaping economic incentives and fostering economic development over extended periods.

Austin (2008) also suggests that institutional changes have been instrumental in explaining the reversal in economic prosperity between Africa and Western Europe. The author argues that these changes reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe (p.24). This perspective aligns with Acemoglu et al.'s (2002) findings, which highlight the role of institutions in understanding economic and political development over the past 500 years.

Abramovitz (1986) also supports this view, stating that the elaboration of mixed economies is a function of economic growth itself (p.20). The author identifies three key factors contributing to productivity growth: technological change, structural change, and macroeconomic conditions encouraging and sustaining capital investment and effective demand. These findings underscore the importance of institutions in facilitating these factors and promoting long-term economic growth.

Structural change, as evidenced by shifts in economic activities from agriculture to industry and services, is closely associated with long-term economic growth (BroadberryEtal_2015: p.4). This transition, often facilitated by improvements in institutions, enables the efficient allocation of resources, encourages innovation, and stimulates productivity growth.

The work of Broadberry et al. (2015) provides a comprehensive analysis of India's long-run economic performance. They suggest that population estimates play a crucial role in understanding the country's historical development. For instance, their research indicates that a third method based on the size of armies suggests a population of 140 to 150 million (BroadberryEtal_2015: p.4). This population growth likely contributed to the agricultural demand that fostered structural change and eventually led to economic expansion.

Furthermore, Bryant's (2006) study highlights the importance of institutions in promoting long-term economic growth. He argues that certain European regions provided more than adequate environments for the growth of commercial exchange due to their fusion of mercantilist imperialism with a technologically driven capitalistic transformation (Bryant_2006: p.5). This fusion, rooted in strong institutions, facilitated structural change and stimulated economic growth.

While many argue that strong institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth (Bryant_2006), some scholars challenge this notion by pointing towards instances where economic growth occurred independently of such institutions. For instance, Deirdre McCloskey critiques the idea that ideologies and interests, rather than institutions, played a crucial role in driving economic growth in France during the 18th century (McCloskey_2015: p.9). She suggests that the lack of economic growth in France could be attributed to conservative reasons, despite the examples of economic success in other regions such as Holland and England.

Similarly, van Zanden (1999) questions the consensus view that early modern economies were unable to generate economic growth before the industrial revolution. He argues that on theoretical and empirical grounds, pre-industrial economic growth was possible, despite a negative link between output measures of standard living and the level of economic development in the mid-18th century (vanZanden_1999: p.1).

Moreover, Humphries (2013) contends that eighteenth-century economic growth rested on a growing division of labor, the rise of workshop production, and the eventual emergence of the factory system (Humphries_2013: p.18). This suggests that economic growth may not always require strong institutions but rather could be driven by technological advancements and changes in production methods.

These counterarguments challenge the thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, as they point towards instances where economic growth occurred independently of strong institutions or were influenced by factors such as ideologies, technological advancements, and changes in production methods. Further examination of these cases could provide a more nuanced understanding of the relationship between institutions and economic growth.

The relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth is complex, as evidenced by various studies. For instance, Temin (2002) posits that the exhaustion of cheap labor was a significant factor in slowing economic growth, rather than institutions being the primary cause. Similarly, Hopkins (2019) argues that postmodernism may have limited the diversity of independent African viewpoints, potentially influencing the study of long-run economic and political change.

Moreover, Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) suggest that initial conditions or factor endowments could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run growth. They highlight the variation in growth across societies due to differences in institutions relevant to economic growth. Frankema (2012) also supports this notion, arguing that colonial education and the degree of British "liberalism" were key factors influencing long-run growth disparities.

On the other hand, Goldin (2006) argues that the study of labor was once focused on institutions within the labor market. Lindert & Williamson (1985) further complicate the relationship by suggesting a potential causal link between economic growth and inequality, which could influence policymakers' decisions regarding trade-offs between the two.

These studies collectively suggest that while institutions are undoubtedly influential in long-run economic growth, other factors such as technological advancements, labor conditions, educational systems, and political contexts also play significant roles. A comprehensive understanding of this relationship requires a nuanced approach that considers these various factors simultaneously. Further examination of these cases could provide a more nuanced understanding of the relationship between institutions and economic growth.

The relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth is further complicated by the factors of industrialization and income disparities, as evidenced in Komlos (1998) and Allen et al. (2011). In the Industrial Revolution era, physical stature was significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economies (Komlos_1998: p.16), indicating that economic processes played a crucial role in shaping biological growth. Moreover, Komlos (1998) highlights that this period was marked by rapid population growth, urbanization, and income inequality (Komlos_1998: p.17).

In parallel, Allen et al. (2011) demonstrate that during the early 20th century, the income disparities between China and Europe were largely due to stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe (AllenEtAl_2011: p.1). This suggests that industrialization can lead to significant income disparities, potentially complicating the relationship between institutions and economic growth. Furthermore, Allen et al. (2011) reveal that while the German economy was growing rapidly, real wages for workers in Leipzig were increasing as well (AllenEtAl_2011: p.20). This indicates that structural change may correlate with growth, further nuancing our understanding of the relationship between institutions and economic growth.

These findings underscore the complex nature of the link between institutions and long-run economic growth. The process of industrialization and the resulting income disparities can both support and challenge the thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. A more nuanced examination of these factors could provide a deeper understanding of this relationship.

The relationship between institutions and economic growth is further complicated by factors such as climate and environmental conditions, as suggested by Koepke & Baten (2005). They argue that while economic growth may have occurred during certain periods, such as the Roman Empire, it did not necessarily lead to improvements in living standards or nutrition, as indicated by stagnant heights among the population (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.16). Furthermore, their study on the Habsburg monarchy reveals that stature and nutrition can serve as indicators of economic development (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.33).

Moreover, Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) emphasize the interconnectedness of strategies among players in the market, suggesting that the "strategy" of one actor can significantly impact the strategies of others (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.21). This perspective complicates the argument that institutions are the sole determinants of long-run economic growth, as it underscores the role of other factors, such as market dynamics, in shaping economic development.

The examination of institutions as the primary cause of long-term economic growth is a complex issue, with scholars both converging and diverging in their perspectives. Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), albeit discussing different contexts, emphasize the role of labor and market dynamics in shaping economic development.

Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) contends that when labor is tied to land or an owner, competition among employers ceases, which can hinder growth. This notion aligns with the argument that institutions are crucial, as they determine the degree of competition and efficiency in labor markets. However, Domar also acknowledges that other factors, such as the availability of resources (Domar_1970: p.4), play a role in economic growth.

Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.10) similarly highlights the impact of Western institutions on African societies, but stresses that these influences extend beyond economic ones. The arrival of the British, for instance, affected social structures and trading practices (Bohannan_1959: p.7), which in turn could have influenced long-term growth patterns.

Ravallion's (2001) work, though difficult to interpret due to its mathematical and symbolic nature, suggests a correlation between economic indicators and growth (Ravallion_2001: p.4, p.10, p.12). However, the specific role of institutions within this context remains unclear.
