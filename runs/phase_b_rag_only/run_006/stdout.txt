The debate on the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth, particularly in the context of Africa, has been a subject of intense scholarly discussion. One key question that arises is whether institutions play a pivotal role in driving economic growth and development over the long run.

Supporting this view, Simon Kuznets (1973) posits that the succession of technological innovations characterizing modern economic growth and the social innovations required for their effective implementation are significant factors influencing both economic and social structure. He further argues that high aggregate growth rates are associated with rapid changes in economic structure, which in turn impact other aspects of society (Kuznets_1973: p.4, p.6).

On the other hand, Douglas North and Barry Weingast (1989) argue that representative government institutions, characterized by an explicit set of multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are intimately related to economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28). They suggest that these institutions played a necessary role in making possible economic growth and political freedom.

However, not all scholars agree with the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth. For instance, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has kept Sub-Saharan Africa absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). They imply that understanding the historical patterns of growth and development in Africa is crucial to determining whether institutions are indeed the primary drivers of economic growth.

Similarly, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) complicate the argument by emphasizing the need to understand the processes by which women's economic contributions have been subsumed under household production and their labor formally unaccounted for, undermining policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3).

The stakes of this debate are high, as understanding the fundamental causes of long-term economic growth can inform policy decisions and development strategies in various regions, particularly Africa. The argument continues with a deeper exploration of the roles of institutions in shaping economic growth and development, considering various perspectives and nuances.

Institutions play a crucial role in shaping economic growth and development, particularly in the realm of education. The works of various scholars provide compelling evidence supporting this thesis. For instance, Mokyr (2005) suggests that developments in intellectual reasoning concerning "useful knowledge" were instrumental in driving economic growth in the West, making it significantly richer today (Mokyr_2005: p.4). North (1989) highlights the importance of centralized enforcement mechanisms and institutions that reduce transaction costs in fostering economic growth (North_1989: p.1).

Furthermore, Nunn (2008) demonstrates that ethnic fractionalization, which can be influenced by institutions, has a significant impact on long-term economic development (Nunn_2008: p.4). Similarly, Nunn & Wantchekon (2011) show that both internal and external institutional factors contribute to economic growth, with the internal factor being at least twice as influential (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.4).

Peters (2004) emphasizes the role of culture, which is shaped by institutions, in promoting economic growth, sustainable land management, and poverty reduction (Peters_2004: p.7). These findings underscore the importance of institutions in shaping educational systems and fostering economic development.

However, it's essential to recognize that the relationship between institutions and economic growth is complex and multifaceted, requiring a nuanced understanding of the specific historical, cultural, and political contexts in which these institutions operate. Future research should continue to explore these nuances to gain a more comprehensive understanding of the role of institutions in long-term economic growth.

Institutional development plays a crucial role in long-term economic growth, as evidenced by various scholarly works. Acemoglu et al. (2001) and (2002) emphasize the significant impact of institutions on economic performance, with more fundamental institutions affecting property rights and investment positively or negatively (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). The authors also highlight that differences in mortality rates are not the primary cause of institutional variation (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3). Acemoglu et al. (2986) further support this notion, suggesting that the elaboration of mixed economies is a function of economic growth itself (Abramovitz_1986: p.20).

Moreover, Frankema & van Waijenburg (2012) demonstrate how West African commercial centers attracted economic migrants, leading to high land-labor ratios and real wage rises, further illustrating the link between institutional development and economic growth (Frankema&vanWaijenburg_2012: p.15, p.17). These findings support the argument that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

However, it is essential to acknowledge that these institutions operate within specific historical, cultural, and political contexts. As such, future research should continue to explore the nuances of institutional development in various regions to gain a more comprehensive understanding of their role in long-term economic growth.

Institutions play a crucial role in shaping long-term economic growth, as they provide the framework for efficient resource allocation and promote innovation (Ogilvie_2007: p.2). The interdependency of individual institutions within this broader framework is essential to their effectiveness, as highlighted by studies of European economic institutions (Ogilvie_2007: p.27).

The skill premium, or the difference in wages between skilled and unskilled labor, can significantly impact economic growth due to its effect on the relative prices of capital goods (vanZanden_2009: p.27). This relationship underscores the importance of institutions that foster human capital formation and encourage technological advancement.

Moreover, the study of econometric models reveals that certain factors such as gunpowder imports can influence slave exports, providing insights into the complex interplay between economic institutions and their impact on growth (Whatley_2018: p.13). This demonstrates the need for comprehensive understanding of institutional development in various regions to gain a more holistic perspective on their role in long-term economic growth.

The efficiency theory, which posits that most people in an economy want the overall size of the economic pie to be as large as possible, is an important consideration in understanding institutions' impact on growth (Ogilvie_2007: p.14). However, it is essential to recognize that institutions may resist encroachment and fail if they are not compatible with a specific historical, cultural, or political context (Ogilvie_2007: p.27).

While institutions are indeed crucial for long-run economic growth, these sources challenge this notion by emphasizing the significance of ideologies and ideas in shaping economic trajectories. Simson (2019) highlights the case of Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda, where state expenditure and deficits expanded during a period of high economic growth in the 1960s and 1970s (Simson_2019: p.25). This suggests that while institutions may play a role, other factors such as ideologies and ideas could also significantly influence economic development.

McCloskey (2015) further supports this argument by pointing out instances where conservative ideologies throttled economic growth in France and Spain, despite the examples of economic success in countries like Holland, England, Scotland, and America (McCloskey_2015: p.9). This indicates that while a material base can influence economic growth, it does not necessarily require reducing mind to matter or ignoring the impact of ideologies and ideas.

In these contexts, institutions may have been resistant to change or failed due to their incompatibility with specific historical, cultural, or political contexts (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). Therefore, while institutions are undeniably important, it is equally crucial to consider the role of ideologies and ideas in shaping economic growth trajectories.

The relationship between institutions and labor markets is a complex one, as evidenced by various studies. Komlos (1998) emphasizes that economic growth during the Industrial Revolution was influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic processes and associated structural changes (Komlos_1998: p.17). Moreover, Temin (2002) suggests that the exhaustion of cheap labor could have caused economic growth to slow (Temin_2002: p.4).

Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that policymakers often struggled with balancing economic growth and equality, indicating a potential interplay between institutional structures and labor market dynamics (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1). Furthermore, Koepke & Baten (2005) reveal that climate might have played a role in shaping living standards and economic development, suggesting that institutions may not be the sole determinant of long-run economic growth (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.20).

These sources complicate the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by highlighting factors such as labor market conditions, climate, and policymaking considerations that may influence growth trajectories. The interplay between these factors and institutional structures warrants further investigation to fully understand the complexities shaping economic growth over time.

The role of institutions in shaping long-run economic growth is further nuanced by the impact they have on education systems, a factor that significantly influences the trajectory of economic development. Sokoloff and Engerman (2000) argue that initial conditions, including factor endowments and unique characteristics of individual countries, have profound and enduring impacts on long-run growth (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4). They suggest that the United States and Canada achieved sustained economic growth during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, while other regions did not until late in the nineteenth century (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.12).

Frankema (2012) supports this notion by highlighting the importance of colonial education in explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa. He argues that the former British colonies grew faster due to their educational lead (Frankema_2012: p.2). However, he also emphasizes that the degree of British "liberalism" was endogenous to the local political and economic context (Frankema_2012: p.19), suggesting that the impact of colonial education may have varied across regions.

Hopkins (2019) complicates this understanding by pointing out that the study of long-run economic and political change in Africa was inhibited by 'totalism' and 'essentialism', which were regarded as Western impositions on the rest of the world (Hopkins_2019: p.10). He also suggests that food exports made very little contribution to economic growth in West Africa during the period of the slave trade (Hopkins_2019: p.13), implying that the role of institutions may extend beyond education systems and economic policies.

The role of institutions in fostering long-run economic growth is further substantiated by the works of Bolt & van Zanden (2014), Braudel & Wallerstein (2009), and Allen Et Al (2011). These scholars offer nuanced perspectives on the relationship between institutions, technological advancements, and economic development.

Bolt & van Zanden (2014) argue that institutional changes in various countries such as Germany, Sweden, Italy, Greece, Russia, and Bulgaria played a significant role in economic growth during the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries (p.5). They also suggest that income disparities between Europe and China were due to long-run stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe (p.1).

Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) contend that the disagreement regarding the role of institutions in economic growth is more fundamental than it may seem (p.30). They emphasize that institutions are interconnected, affecting each other's "strategy" (p.21), and suggest that this perspective can be found in Marx's writings (p.21).

Allen Et Al (2011) support the argument by demonstrating that structural change correlates with growth (p.1). They also highlight that during the early twentieth century, rapid growth of the German economy led to rising real wages for workers in Leipzig (p.20), and Chinese workers had lower wages compared to their European counterparts due to long-run stagnation in China (p.1).

These sources complicate the argument by suggesting that institutions play a crucial role not only in education systems and economic policies but also in technological advancements, trade patterns, and wage disparities between regions. This implies that a comprehensive understanding of the role of institutions in long-run economic growth must consider these factors as well.

The literature review on the role of institutions in long-run economic growth highlights a convergence among scholars that institutions play a significant role in shaping economic outcomes, particularly growth. Both Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) acknowledge that institutions can affect labor market structures, which in turn impact economic development. For instance, Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) asserts that when labor is tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, potentially hindering growth. Similarly, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) observes that the Western impact on African societies centered on economic institutions such as slave dealing and status acquisition.

However, there are differences in emphasis between the two scholars. Domar (1970) primarily focuses on labor market structures, while Bohannan (1959) examines the impact of Western influence on African societies more broadly. This suggests a divergence in scope, with Domar focusing more narrowly on domestic institutions and their effects, whereas Bohannan considers both domestic and external factors shaping economic development.

Ravallion (2001), on the other hand, presents mathematical models and equations that are less directly related to the role of institutions in long-run economic growth. The tangential nature of Ravallion's work makes it difficult to draw clear conclusions about his stance on institutions, but it does provide a valuable tool for understanding the quantitative aspects of economic growth.

Despite these differences, several issues remain unresolved. For example, while scholars agree that institutions are crucial for long-run economic growth, there is no consensus on which specific institutional arrangements foster the most effective growth or how to measure and compare the effectiveness of different institutional configurations across time and space. Furthermore, the interplay between institutions, technological advancements, trade patterns, and wage disparities remains a complex and understudied area. Future research should aim to address these gaps in our understanding of the role of institutions in long-run economic growth.
