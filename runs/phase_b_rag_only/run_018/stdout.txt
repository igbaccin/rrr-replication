LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question in the literature on long-run economic growth is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of such growth. This question holds significant implications for understanding historical and contemporary economic disparities between regions, particularly Africa and Western Europe.

Austin (2008) supports the thesis that institutions are the primary driver of long-term economic growth. He argues that trades, such as the Atlantic one, reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe. Austin further endorses the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history, revealing relationships that recur due to path dependencies (Austin_2008: p.1).

Kuznets (1973) also supports this view, stating that high aggregate growth rates are associated with rapid changes in economic structure and society. He emphasizes the role of technological innovations and social adaptations in driving economic growth (Kuznets_1973: p.4).

However, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate this argument by pointing out that Sub-Saharan Africa has been absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth due to the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950. They argue that understanding the extent to which there are still risks of economic shrinkage is crucial for determining whether recent growth provides a path to convergence (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.18).

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance to the debate by highlighting the role of women's economic contribution in Africa, which has often been subsumed under household production and unaccounted for in policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles. They note that women's contributions were largely dependent on their husbands' economic provision (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.9).

North & Weingast (1989) and Acemoglu et al. (2001) further support the thesis that institutions are crucial for economic growth. North & Weingast argue that representative government, with multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, played a necessary role in making possible economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28). Acemoglu et al. demonstrate a large effect of institutions on economic performance, showing that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19).

The stakes in this debate are high, as understanding the role of institutions in long-run economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting economic development in regions such as Africa. The argument continues with a deeper exploration of the critiques and nuances offered by scholars like Broadberry & Gardner and Akyeampong & Fofack.

The role of institutions in fostering long-run economic growth is further substantiated by various scholars, notably North & Weingast (1989) and Acemoglu et al. (2001). North & Weingast (1989: p.2) assert that the fundamental institutions of representative government, such as multiple veto points and common law courts over economic affairs, are crucial in facilitating economic growth and political freedom. Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3) suggest that differences in institutional structures significantly impact economic performance.

Mokyr (Mokyr_2005: p.4) posits that the change in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West can be attributed to developments in intellectual spheres, specifically the growth of "useful knowledge." This concept encompasses elements such as individualism, formal law, corporatism, self-governance, and rules established through an institutionalized process.

North (1989: p.1) emphasizes the importance of institutions in reducing transaction costs, thereby contributing to economic growth. Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49) reiterate this point, stating that understanding the relationship between institutions and economic development is essential for comprehending historical and contemporary development patterns.

The relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth is further substantiated by various scholarly works. Ogilvie (2007) highlights that geographical characteristics, such as climate and resource endowments, play a crucial role in shaping economic development through influencing the formation of institutions (Ogilvie_2007: p.2). Similarly, Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) emphasize that initial conditions, or factor endowments, can have profound and enduring impacts on long-run growth paths (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4).

Allen (2001) supports this perspective by pointing out the significance of wages and prices in understanding economic development, economic leadership, and standard of living (Allen_2001: p.1). Additionally, Bryant (2006) underscores the role of institutions in facilitating commercial exchange, suggesting that European regions with more conducive institutional environments experienced greater growth (Bryant_2006: p.13).

However, it is essential to acknowledge that the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework can influence their effectiveness (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). This interdependence highlights the complexity of institutional development and emphasizes the need for a comprehensive understanding of the various factors shaping economic growth over the long run.

Institutions significantly impacted economic growth in agriculture during the early modern period, as evidenced by the interplay of markets and agricultural practices (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.9). The service sector played a crucial role in this growth, providing essential support to rural areas where peasants supplemented their agricultural activities with manufacturing (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.9). This synergy between agriculture and industry was instrumental in driving economic expansion during the early modern era.

Moreover, the commercialization of agriculture accelerated significantly following the Black Death, which led to a decline in population and an increase in real wages (Pamuk_2007: p.4, p.5). This demographic shift created opportunities for agricultural productivity growth, as labor became more expensive and farmers adapted by adopting new technologies and farming methods (Pamuk_2007: p.24). Consequently, the growing commercialization of agriculture contributed to long-term economic growth.

Furthermore, institutions influenced the effectiveness of these agricultural practices through their ability to facilitate market integration and enable the exchange of goods between rural areas and urban centers (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). This interdependence highlights the importance of understanding the complex relationship between institutions, agriculture, and economic growth over the long run.

Lastly, it is important to note that models such as Whatley's (2018) demonstrate how institutions can shape trade patterns and influence the dynamics of economic growth (Whatley_2018: p.3, p.11, p.13). For instance, his model reveals that gunpowder imports were linked to slave exports, suggesting a long-run equilibrium relationship between these two variables (Whatley_2018: p.13). This underscores the intricate nature of institutional development and its role in shaping economic growth over the long run.

Other sources challenge the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by highlighting factors such as structural change, industrial development, and population dynamics. For instance, Simson (2019) argues that in the East African region, state expenditure and deficits expanded during a period of high economic growth in the 1960s and 1970s, suggesting that institutional factors may not have been the primary driver of growth in this context (Simson_2019: p.25).

Similarly, Humphries (2013) contends that economic growth in the eighteenth century was largely driven by a growing division of labor, the rise of workshop production, and the emergence of the factory system, rather than institutions (Humphries_2013: p.18). Furthermore, Humphries points out that relative needs of women and children may have played a significant role in shaping economic growth, as French fertility remained much lower than British fertility during this period (Humphries_2013: p.11, p.19).

These findings suggest that while institutions undoubtedly play a role in long-run economic growth, they may not be the sole or primary determinant. Other factors such as structural change, industrial development, and population dynamics may also contribute significantly to economic growth over the long term. Further investigation into these factors is warranted to gain a more comprehensive understanding of the complex interplay between institutions and economic growth.

The complexities surrounding the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth are further elucidated in various scholarly works. For instance, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) point out that Sub-Saharan Africa has been overlooked in recent debates due to a lack of data on pre-1950 aggregate economic performance. This omission might have skewed our understanding of long-run growth dynamics, particularly in regions such as Africa (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.1).

Similarly, Frankema (2012) emphasizes the role of colonial education in explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa. However, Frankema also acknowledges that the degree of British "liberalism" was endogenous to the local political and economic context, suggesting a more nuanced relationship between colonial policies and economic growth (Frankema_2012: p.19).

Hopkins (2019) delves into the historical evolution of African Economic History, noting that postmodernism may have limited the diversity in independent African viewpoints, potentially influencing our understanding of long-run economic and political change (Hopkins_2019: p.4).

Goldin (2006) discusses the study of labor markets, demonstrating how institutional changes can impact women's choices and decisions over time (Goldin_2006: p.3). Skocpol & Sommers (1980) compare social revolutions in Japan and China, revealing the particularities of each case that might contribute to different long-term growth patterns (Skocpol&Sommers_1980: p.9). Lastly, Stoler (1989) discusses how colonizers lived in "imagined communities" that were consciously created to overcome economic and social disparities, which could have significant implications for understanding the long-term impact of colonialism on economic growth (Stoler_1989: p.5).

These sources complicate the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by highlighting the role of factors such as historical data availability, colonial education policies, local political and economic contexts, and the impact of postmodernism on research diversity. Further examination of these factors could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the complex interplay between institutions and economic growth over the long term.

The interplay between institutions and technological advancements significantly contributes to the long-run economic growth, as evidenced by various scholarly works. Komlos (1998) highlights that the Industrial Revolution was marked by significant physical stature changes, suggesting a correlation between institutional structures and biological growth processes (Komlos_1998: p.16). Similarly, Austin (2007) notes that the railway, an essential technological advancement, had a cost advantage over lorries, indicating its role in promoting economic growth (Austin_2007: p.8).

Moreover, Bolt and van Zanden (2014) emphasize the importance of institutional changes in shaping long-term economic growth. They mention revisions to nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth rates for several countries, which did not fundamentally change their viewpoint but highlighted the impact of institutions on economic growth (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5).

However, it is essential to note that this relationship between institutions and technological advancements is complex and multifaceted. Braudel and Wallerstein (2009) argue that the strategies of one player in the market can affect the strategies of others, implying that institutional structures influence the adoption and diffusion of technology (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.21).

Further examination of this relationship could provide a more nuanced understanding of how institutions shape technological advancements and, consequently, long-term economic growth. For instance, investigating the role of institutional structures in promoting technological innovation or hindering its dissemination could offer valuable insights into this complex interplay. Additionally, studying the impact of postmodernism on research diversity might shed light on how contemporary institutional contexts influence our understanding of the relationship between institutions and economic growth.

The relationship between institutions and economic growth is further nuanced when considering their impact on labor markets. Lindert & Williamson (1985) suggest that policymakers often face trade-offs between economic growth and equality, as the British classical economists believed (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1). Furthermore, understanding long-run distribution trends is intertwined with the search for explanations of growth itself (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.9).

Williamson (1996) highlights that mass migrations could reduce the pace of real wage growth in receiving countries by glutting labor markets abroad (Williamson_1996: p.17). Understanding the sources of convergence is crucial for understanding who gains and loses from convergence, as well as policy responses (Williamson_1996: p.25).

Feinstein (1998) notes that changes in farming, combined with migration and emigration, were significant factors in dispersing labor gluts in rural areas (Feinstein_1998: p.28). Additionally, the long plateau of economic growth in Britain was consistent with other economic, political, and demographic indicators (Feinstein_1998: p.2).

These findings suggest that institutional structures play a significant role in shaping labor markets and influencing economic growth. Policymakers must carefully consider the potential trade-offs between economic growth and equality while crafting policies to address labor market issues. Understanding the historical context of these relationships is essential for contemporary policy responses aimed at promoting long-term economic growth.

The scholarly discourse on the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, as depicted in the provided evidence, highlights a convergence on the significance of institutions in shaping economic trajectories, while also displaying divergences in the specific factors considered essential.

Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) both emphasize the role of institutions in determining labor market dynamics, albeit from different perspectives. Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) posits that with labor tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, which impedes economic growth. In contrast, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) focuses on the impact of Western institutions, particularly those related to slave dealing and status acquisition, on African societies.

Ravallion's (2001) work appears more tangential to the central argument, presenting complex mathematical equations and symbols that are challenging to interpret in the context of this literature review (Ravallion_2001: p.4, p.10, p.12). However, it is possible that his work contributes to the understanding of economic growth through the analysis of development indicators such as GDP and poverty levels.

Despite the convergence on institutions' importance, several issues remain unresolved. For instance, there is a need for further investigation into how specific institutional arrangements drive economic growth, as well as the interplay between institutions and other factors like technology, geography, and cultural norms. Additionally, it would be beneficial to explore the mechanisms through which institutions evolve over time and their impact on economic growth in different historical contexts.
