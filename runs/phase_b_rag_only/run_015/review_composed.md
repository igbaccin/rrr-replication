The central question under investigation in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. This question holds significant implications for understanding historical and contemporary economic development, as well as informing policy decisions aimed at promoting growth and reducing poverty in various regions.

Austin (2008) complicates the argument by suggesting that while institutions play a crucial role, they may not be the sole determinant of long-run economic growth. He argues that historical analyses should examine growth theories against long-term history to reveal relationships that recur due to path dependency rather than attributing causality solely to institutions (Austin_2008: p.1).

Kuznets (1973) supports the thesis by emphasizing the role of institutional innovations in facilitating modern economic growth and the necessary adjustments within society. He also acknowledges that this growth can result in unexpected negative outcomes, even within developed countries (Kuznets_1973: p.11).

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate the argument by pointing out that a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has led to Sub-Saharan Africa's absence in recent debates about comparative long-run growth. However, they acknowledge that understanding the extent to which historical risks of shrinking continue to undermine economic progress is essential for determining whether current growth provides a path to convergence (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.18).

Acemoglu et al. (2001) provide support for the thesis by demonstrating that institutions can have detrimental effects on investment and economic progress, particularly in historical contexts (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27).

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) complicate the argument by highlighting the importance of understanding how women's economic contributions have been subsumed under household production and their labor formally unaccounted for, which undermines policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3).

Komlos (1998) complicates the argument by using the concept of modern economic growth in a Kuznetsian sense, yet acknowledges that the timing and conceptualization of the Industrial Revolution remain topics for discussion (Komlos_1998: p.2).

The stakes of this debate are high, as understanding the role of institutions in long-run economic growth is crucial for informing policy decisions aimed at promoting economic development and reducing poverty. The literature suggests that while institutions play a significant role, they may not be the sole determinant of economic growth, and other factors such as historical path dependencies, structural changes, and gender roles should also be considered. Further research is needed to better understand these complex relationships and inform effective policy interventions.

The assertion that institutions play a pivotal role in long-term economic growth is substantiated by various scholars. Kuznets (1973) posits that economic and social structure undergo rapid changes concurrent with modern economic growth, suggesting the importance of institutions in facilitating such transformations (Kuznets_1973: p.4). Furthermore, Mokyr (2005) argues that the development of progressive societies, characterized by individualism, formal law, corporatism, self-governance, and rules determined through an institutionalized process, significantly contributed to economic growth (Mokyr_2005: p.56).

Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) emphasize the role of political institutions in enabling economic growth and market development (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). They contend that absolutist states with weak institutions retarded long-run economic growth (North&Weingast_1989: p.7), while those with well-established institutions fostered both economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.30).

Moreover, Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) contend that initial conditions, or factor endowments, could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth, implying the importance of institutions in shaping these conditions (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4). Ogilvie (2007) also acknowledges the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework, emphasizing their significance to economic analysis and growth (Ogilvie_2007: p.27).

Taken together, these scholars suggest that institutions are essential in facilitating structural changes, fostering progressive societies, enabling market development, and shaping initial conditions conducive to long-term economic growth. However, further research is needed to better understand the intricate relationships between institutions and other factors such as technological progress, historical path dependencies, and gender roles.

The significance of institutions in fostering long-term economic growth is further emphasized by various scholarly works. Acemoglu, Johnson, and Robinson (2002) assert that the evolution of institutions plays a crucial role in understanding both economic and political development over the past 500 years (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49). They also highlight an interesting change in the distribution of economic prosperity, suggesting that the reversal could be institutionally driven (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.6).

DeVries (1994) reinforces this notion by acknowledging the Industrial Revolution as the most important monument in economic history, indicating a strong correlation between institutional development and industrial growth (deVries_1994: p.2). Furthermore, deVries points out that Europe's urban network, which established a framework for regional economic development, was an achievement of the early modern period (deVries_1994: p.5).

Whatley (2018) provides additional evidence by demonstrating structural change correlates with growth through econometric analysis (Whatley_2018: p.3). For instance, Equation 2 of Model 2 in Whatley's work captures the long-run equilibrium relationship between gunpowder imports and slave exports, suggesting a significant institutional influence on economic dynamics (Whatley_2018: p.13).

These studies underscore the fundamental role institutions play in driving long-term economic growth. By examining historical developments, econometric models, and urban networks, these scholars collectively provide compelling evidence for the thesis that institutions are indeed a key factor in fostering sustained economic prosperity.

The relationship between institutions and agriculture is crucial in understanding long-term economic growth, as demonstrated by Broadberry et al. (2015) and Bryant (2006). According to Broadberry et al., India's economic performance since the late sixteenth century has been a subject of debate, but their research provides population estimates that suggest a significant agricultural output (BroadberryEtal_2015: p.4). This agricultural productivity is considered a key factor in fostering sustained economic prosperity.

Bryant (2006) argues that the European fusion of mercantilist imperialism with a technologically driven capitalistic transformation played a significant role in promoting commercial exchange, which in turn led to growth (Bryant_2006: p.5). The author also highlights that the radical revisionist dissent regarding the civilizational depth of the Indian subcontinent has been challenged by evidence pointing towards its ability to provide more than adequate environments for commercial exchange (Bryant_2006: p.13).

These studies support the thesis that institutions are a fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, as they demonstrate the role of agricultural productivity and commercial exchange in fostering sustained prosperity. The evidence suggests that institutions play a critical role in shaping agricultural practices and encouraging commerce, which ultimately contribute to economic development over extended periods. Further examination of historical developments and urban networks may provide additional insights into this relationship.

The assertion that institutions are the primary cause of long-term economic growth faces challenges when considering the role of labor and industrial development, as evidenced by the critiques presented in various scholarly works. For instance, Stern (2004) argues that economic growth may lead to environmental degradation rather than being a safeguard against it, suggesting that institutions may not always be the driving force behind sustainable economic development (Stern_2004: p.1, p.3).

Similarly, Simson (2019) challenges the notion by highlighting the emphasis placed on expanding public employment and military autonomy during periods of economic growth in certain regions, rather than protecting those already in government jobs or prioritizing institutional reforms (Simson_2019: p.27). This focus on employment and military spending may not necessarily contribute to long-term economic growth through the establishment of effective institutions.

McCloskey (2015) also provides a counterargument by demonstrating that ideologies and ideas, rather than institutions, played a significant role in shaping economic growth in certain countries, such as France and Spain, during critical periods (McCloskey_2015: p.9). This suggests that the impact of institutions on economic growth may be overstated, and other factors like ideologies and ideas should not be disregarded.

Lastly, Humphries (2013) challenges the idea by pointing out the ahistorical assumption that all families had a male head, which is crucial for understanding the division of labor and the emergence of the factory system during economic growth (Humphries_2013: p.14). This underscores the importance of considering social structures and family dynamics in addition to institutions when examining long-term economic growth.

These critiques suggest that while institutions play a role in economic growth, they may not be the fundamental cause as proposed. The interplay between labor, industrial development, ideologies, ideas, and social structures should also be taken into account for a comprehensive understanding of long-term economic growth. Future research should further explore these factors to gain a more nuanced perspective on this complex relationship.

The relationship between institutions and labor is a complex one, as evidenced by various studies. For instance, Komlos (1998) highlights that economic growth during the Industrial Revolution was influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic processes and associated structural changes (p.16, p.17). This suggests that institutional factors can have an impact on labor productivity over the short term, contributing to long-run economic growth.

Similarly, Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that the explanation of long-run distribution trends is closely linked to the search for explanations of growth itself (p.9). They further propose a causal direction between inequality and growth, implying that institutions play a role in shaping both income distribution and economic growth over time.

Hopkins (2019) complicates this argument by suggesting that the study of long-run economic and political change was previously hindered by certain perspectives and methodologies (p.4, p.10). However, he also emphasizes the importance of understanding the impact of colonial education on post-colonial economic growth in Africa (p.13).

Frankema (2012) supports this notion, arguing that British colonies experienced faster long-run growth due to their educational lead (p.2). Yet, he also acknowledges that the degree of British "liberalism" was endogenous to the local political and economic context (p.19), suggesting that institutions can vary significantly across regions, potentially leading to different outcomes in terms of labor productivity and growth.

Goldin (2006) focuses on the study of labor and its relationship with institutions. He notes that the distinction between evolution and revolution in labor markets is significant (p.3). This implies that institutional changes can lead to transformative shifts in labor behavior, which in turn may contribute to long-term economic growth.

Feinstein (1998) discusses the impact of wars on British economic growth over the Industrial Revolution period (p.5). He also emphasizes the importance of structural change and its correlation with growth (implicit in the discussion of rural changes and labor market conditions before 1850, p.28).

The assertion that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth is not without complications, as evidenced by various scholarly works. Austin (2008) acknowledges the importance of institutions in driving economic growth but argues that trade, even transatlantic trade, may have reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe and parts of Asia (Austin_2008: p.24). This perspective introduces a nuanced view on the relationship between institutions, trade, and growth in different regions.

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) also contribute to this discussion by pointing out the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 for Sub-Saharan Africa, which has been absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). They emphasize that understanding the extent to which there are still risks of shrinking in Africa is crucial for determining whether its growth provides a path to convergence (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.18). This highlights the importance of historical data and context in evaluating the role of institutions in long-term economic growth.

Austin's (2007) work also complicates the argument by demonstrating that until the mid-1920s, railways had a fundamental cost advantage over lorries (Austin_2007: p.8). This finding underscores the importance of infrastructure development and its impact on economic growth, suggesting that institutions alone may not fully explain long-run economic growth.

Bolt & van Zanden (2014) add to this conversation by discussing revisions of nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth rates for various countries, including Germany, Sweden, Italy, Russia, and Bulgaria (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5). These revisions may have significant implications for understanding the role of institutions in long-run economic growth across different regions.

The role of institutions in long-run economic growth is further nuanced when considering the contribution of women to economic development, particularly in Africa. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) argue that understanding the processes by which women's labor is subsumed under household production and often unaccounted for is crucial for strengthening women's economic roles (p.3). This underscores the importance of institutions in recognizing and valuing women's contributions to economic growth, which may have been overlooked in some analyses.

Moreover, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) highlight that the major historical developments in West Africa regarding gender and economic growth find parallels elsewhere in Africa (p.4). This suggests that the role of institutions in promoting women's economic participation may be a common theme across various African regions, complicating the understanding of institutional effects on long-run economic growth.

On a different note, Temin (2002) asserts that the exhaustion of cheap labor contributed to the slowing of economic growth (p.4). This implies that institutions play a role in managing labor markets and ensuring access to affordable labor, which can impact long-term economic growth. However, Temin (2002) also points out that recent theories of economic growth should consider the component of economic transition, as national economies around 1800 were predominantly agricultural (p.7). This suggests that institutions may need to adapt to facilitate structural change in order to promote long-run economic growth.

Lastly, Williamson (1996) emphasizes the importance of understanding the sources of convergence and agreeing on the meaning of convergence to answer questions about institutional effects on long-run economic growth (p.4, p.25). This underscores the need for a clear definition and understanding of convergence in order to accurately assess the role of institutions in promoting long-run economic growth across different regions.

The literature on the role of institutions in promoting long-run economic growth presents a nuanced perspective, with some scholars emphasizing the fundamental causal link between institutions and growth (e.g., North & Weingast_1989), while others view this relationship as tangential (Domar_1970; Bohannan_1959).

Scholars such as Domar (1970) argue that the organization of production and labor relations play a crucial role in determining economic growth. For instance, when labor is tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, hindering economic development (Domar_1970: p.4). On the other hand, Bohannan (1959) suggests that Western impact on African societies was not limited to economic institutions but also included cultural and social aspects.

Ravallion's (2001) work is less directly related to the topic at hand but offers insights into economic growth measurement and its correlation with structural change, which could potentially be linked to institutional quality (Ravallion_2001: p.4, p.10, p.12).

Despite these differing perspectives, there is a general consensus that institutions significantly influence long-run economic growth. What remains unresolved is the exact nature of this relationship and the mechanisms through which institutions drive growth. Further research is needed to clarify the specific institutional factors that are most conducive to promoting sustainable economic development across different regions.
