LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question in the literature review on institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is whether institutional factors are the primary drivers of economic development over time. This question carries significant implications for understanding historical and contemporary economic disparities across regions, particularly between Western Europe and Africa.

Several scholars support the thesis that institutions play a crucial role in long-term economic growth. For instance, Austin (2008) argues that institutions are indeed 'the' fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, emphasizing their importance in shaping economic structures and historical development patterns (Austin_2008: p.26). Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) focus on the political factors underpinning economic growth and the development of markets, suggesting that certain institutions are essential for economic progress and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.2).

However, not all scholars agree with this stance. Kuznets (1973) acknowledges the importance of institutions in promoting economic growth but also highlights potential negative consequences of modern economic growth, such as unexpected social disruptions even within developed countries (Kuznets_1973: p.11). Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate the debate by pointing out that a lack of data on African economic performance before 1950 has made it difficult to include Sub-Saharan Africa in recent debates about comparative long-run growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1).

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance to the discussion by focusing on the role of women's economic contributions and how they have been historically undervalued, which can undermine policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). Acemoglu et al. (2001) support the thesis by demonstrating that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27).

The stakes of this debate are high, as understanding the role of institutions in long-term economic growth can provide valuable insights into historical and contemporary economic disparities. Moreover, identifying the factors that promote or hinder economic development could inform policy decisions aimed at fostering economic growth and reducing poverty in various regions. The argument continues as scholars delve deeper into the specific aspects of institutional structures that contribute to long-run economic growth and the challenges faced in implementing policies based on these findings.

The interplay between institutions and agricultural development significantly contributes to long-run economic growth, as evidenced by several scholarly works. Kuznets (1973) posits that technological innovations, a hallmark of modern economic growth, are closely linked with social innovations that facilitate necessary adjustments in economic and social structure (Kuznets_1973: p.6). Furthermore, the succession of such tech-innovations is crucial for rapid changes in economic structure, which in turn drive economic growth (Kuznets_1973: p.4).

North & Weingast (1989) argue that representative government institutions, characterized by multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, play a fundamental role in enabling economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28). They highlight how these institutions provide the necessary conditions for market development and ensure that rules governing economic exchange are enforced effectively (North&Weingast_1989: p.2).

Mokyr (2005) suggests that the change in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West should be attributed to developments in intellectual realms concerning "useful knowledge" (Mokyr_2005: p.4). He emphasizes that many elements of a progressive society, such as individualism, man-made formal law, corporatism, self-governance, and rules determined through an institutionalized process, have significantly contributed to economic growth (Mokyr_2005: p.56).

These findings collectively support the notion that institutions are fundamental causes of long-run economic growth. The specific aspects of institutional structures that contribute to agricultural development, such as laws governing property rights and market regulations, play a crucial role in fostering technological innovations and facilitating economic growth (Kuznets_1973: p.6; Mokyr_2005: p.56). Moreover, the presence of representative government institutions that provide checks and balances on power and ensure the rule of law contribute to a conducive environment for market development and economic growth (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

The assertion that institutions play a pivotal role in long-term economic growth is further substantiated by the works of various scholars. Austin (2008) asserts that institutions are indeed 'the fundamental cause' of long-run economic growth (Austin_2008: p.26). He argues that certain trade patterns, such as the Atlantic one, reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe (Austin_2008: p.24). This suggests a historical link between institutions and economic development.

Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2001) demonstrate that more fundamental institutions have a significant impact on property rights and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). They find that detrimental institutions were harmful to investment and economic growth. Furthermore, their findings indicate a large effect of institutions on economic performance (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19), underscoring the importance of institutions in long-run development.

Acemoglu et al. (2002) also support this view, stating that understanding economic and political development over the past 500 years necessitates an examination of institutions (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49). They question whether a change in the distribution of economic prosperity is related to institutions (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5), implying that institutions play a crucial role in this shift.

Abramovitz (1986) supports this perspective by suggesting that the elaboration of the mixed economy is a function of economic growth itself (Abramovitz_1986: p.20). He identifies three factors contributing to economic growth, one of which is the evolution of institutions (implicitly structural change in institutions) (Abramovitz_1986: p.7). These findings collectively reinforce the argument that institutions are indeed a primary cause of long-run economic growth.

The evidence presented in deVries (1994) supports the assertion that institutions play a significant role in long-run economic growth, particularly in the context of the Industrial Revolution. DeVries argues that Europe's urban network, which established a framework for regional economic development, was an achievement of the early modern period (deVries_1994: p.5). Moreover, he emphasizes the importance of the Industrial Revolution as the most significant monument in economic history (deVries_1994: p.2). These findings suggest that institutions, such as urban networks and industrial revolutions, are indeed key drivers of long-term economic growth.

Similarly, vanZanden (2009) provides evidence supporting the thesis by focusing on the skill premium, which is influenced by the skill premium of carpenters and masons, the designers and makers of capital goods (vanZanden_2009: p.27). This relationship between institutions (skilled labor) and economic growth is further supported when considering that new growth theory posits human capital formation as a determinant of long-term economic growth (vanZanden_2009: p.24).

Allen (2001) reinforces the argument by highlighting the centrality of wages and prices in economic development, economic leadership, and the standard of living (Allen_2001: p.1). While the issue of representativeness must be considered, the British industrial revolution provides some reassurance that the wages of building craftsmen are indicative of trends in average earnings (Allen_2001: p.4). This implies that institutional changes, such as the Industrial Revolution, can have far-reaching effects on wage levels and, by extension, economic growth.

Collectively, these sources provide compelling evidence supporting the thesis that institutions are indeed a primary cause of long-run economic growth. The structural changes in institutions, as evidenced by urban networks, industrial revolutions, and skilled labor markets, have significant impacts on regional economic development and wage levels, ultimately leading to long-term economic growth.

The argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is challenged by evidence suggesting that other factors, such as agricultural development, may play a significant role in economic growth. For instance, Simson (2019) argues that state expenditure and deficits expanded in Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda during a period of high economic growth in the 1960s and 1970s (Simson_2019: p.25). This expansion occurred despite inefficiencies, leakages, and costly delays to reform, suggesting that institutional changes may not be the sole determinant of economic growth.

McCloskey (2015) also questions the importance of institutions, stating that ideologies and ideologies, not interests or institutions, were responsible for economic growth in France during two centuries after the Unfinished Revolution of 1789 (McCloskey_2015: p.9). The author suggests that conservative reasons, such as the desire to impose a rigid form of Catholicism, throttled economic growth in Spain despite examples of bourgeois economic and political successes in other countries (McCloskey_2015: p.9).

These findings challenge the thesis by suggesting that factors beyond institutional changes, such as ideologies and state expenditure, can significantly impact economic growth. However, further research is needed to determine the relative importance of these factors compared to institutions in driving long-term economic growth.

The relationship between institutions and agricultural development significantly contributes to understanding the long-term economic growth. According to Broadberry & Gardner (2022), Sub-Saharan Africa's absence from debates about comparative long-run growth is due to a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950, which emphasizes the importance of historical national accounting in understanding economic growth patterns.

Moreover, Komlos (1998) highlights that physical stature during the Industrial Revolution was significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic processes and associated structural changes. This finding suggests that institutions play a crucial role in shaping human living standards and economic development.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that understanding long-run distribution trends is closely linked to explaining growth itself, as policymakers often need to balance economic growth with equality. Furthermore, Koepke & Baten (2005) demonstrate that climate and human living standards have a complex relationship, implying that institutions play a significant role in mediating this relationship.

Frankema (2012) points out that colonial education was a key factor in explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa, with former British colonies growing faster due to their educational lead. Hopkins (2019) also emphasizes the importance of institutions by suggesting that postmodernism removed diversity from African viewpoints on economic and political change, which may have inhibited the study of long-term economic growth.

These sources complicate the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by showing how they shape agricultural development, human living standards, and educational systems, all of which contribute to economic growth. However, further research is needed to determine the relative importance of these factors compared to others in driving long-term economic growth.

The role of industrialization in economic growth is further elucidated by the works of Temin (2002), Williamson (1996), and Allen et al. (2011). These scholars provide nuanced perspectives on the relationship between institutions, industrialization, and long-term economic growth.

Temin (2002) suggests that the exhaustion of cheap labor may have contributed to a slowdown in economic growth. He argues that European economies experienced a decline in growth rates after the Golden Age due to their return to equilibrium growth paths rather than adverse shocks in the 1970s (Temin_2002: p.14). Additionally, he posits that recent theories of economic growth should consider the context of economic transition, as most national economies at the turn of the 20th century were primarily agricultural (Temin_2002: p.7).

Williamson (1996) focuses on the relationship between schooling and Swedish economic growth before World War I. He emphasizes the importance of understanding the sources of convergence to effectively address policy responses (Williamson_1996: p.25). Furthermore, he discusses how mass migrations reduced the pace of real wage growth in receiving countries by glutting labor markets abroad (Williamson_1996: p.17).

Allen et al. (2011) highlight the income disparities between Europe and China during the early 20th century, attributing them to long-run stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe (AllenEtAl_2011: p.1). They also discuss the role of structural change in economic growth, as rapid growth in the German economy led to rising real wages for workers in Leipzig (AllenEtAl_2011: p.20).

These findings complicate the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by emphasizing the role of industrialization and structural change, as well as factors such as labor markets and educational systems. Future research may seek to explore these aspects further to determine their relative importance in driving long-term economic growth.

The role of institutions in driving long-term economic growth is further nuanced when examining the African context, as highlighted by Akyeampong and Fofack (2014). Their research reveals that women's contributions to economic growth in Africa have often been overlooked due to their labor being categorized as household production, thereby going unaccounted for in formal economic analyses.

Akyeampong and Fofack (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3) argue that this oversight undermines policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles. The authors further contend that the contribution of African women to economic growth and development has been largely dependent on the economic provision of their husbands (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.9). This suggests that institutions in Africa may not only fail to recognize but also hinder women's economic participation, which could potentially slow down long-term growth.

Moreover, Akyeampong and Fofack (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.4) point out that the historical developments in West Africa, with their focus on gender roles and economic growth, have parallels elsewhere in Africa. This implies that the institution-growth relationship in Africa may be influenced by similar factors across various regions, thus warranting a comprehensive examination of these institutions to fully understand their impact on long-term economic performance.

The relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth is a subject of significant debate among scholars, as evidenced by the works of Domar (1970), Bohannan (1959), and Ravallion (2001). While these authors approach the topic from different perspectives, they collectively highlight the crucial role that institutions play in economic development.

Domar (1970) argues that the institution of land tenure and labor systems can significantly impact economic growth. He contends that when labor is tied to land or an owner, competition among employers ceases, hindering economic progress. Conversely, free labor markets foster competition and drive economic development.

Bohannan (1959) focuses on the impact of Western institutions on African societies, particularly in regards to slavery and trade. He suggests that these external influences altered traditional African institutions, leading to changes in economic behavior and social structure. However, he also notes that the number of women was limited under these new institutions, which could have hindered long-term economic growth.

Ravallion (2001) presents complex mathematical models to analyze economic growth, but his work is less directly related to the institution-growth relationship. His equations suggest a correlation between structural change and growth, though the specific role of institutions within these structures remains unclear.

Despite these insights, several questions remain unresolved. For instance, it is not yet clear how the impact of institutions varies across different regions or historical periods. Additionally, the interplay between institutions, culture, and economic development requires further exploration to fully understand the factors driving long-term growth. Future research should aim to address these gaps in our understanding of the institution-growth relationship, particularly in the context of Africa.
