LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The debate surrounding the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, particularly in the context of Africa, has been a subject of intense discussion among economists and historians. A central question that arises is whether institutions are indeed the primary driver of economic growth, as posited by several scholars, or if other factors play a more significant role.

Institutional theory, which suggests that the structure of an economy's political and social systems significantly influences its long-term growth, has garnered strong support from researchers such as Austin (2008) and North & Weingast (1989). Austin (Austin_2008: p.26) notes that institutions are "the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth," while North & Weingast (North&Weingast_1989: p.2) argue that the development of markets and economic growth are closely tied to political institutions. Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27) also support this view, asserting that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress.

However, not all scholars agree with this position. Broadberry & Gardner (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1) complicate the argument by pointing out that a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has kept Sub-Saharan Africa out of recent debates about comparative long-run growth. Akyeampong & Fofack (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3) similarly add nuance to the discussion by emphasizing the role of women's economic contribution and the challenges in understanding their labor due to its being subsumed under household production.

This debate carries significant implications for policy-making, as understanding the primary drivers of long-run economic growth can inform strategies aimed at fostering development and reducing poverty. The ongoing discussion among scholars underscores the need for further research to clarify the role of institutions in economic growth, particularly in regions like Africa where data is limited. As the debate continues, it becomes increasingly clear that a comprehensive understanding of this complex issue requires an interdisciplinary approach that considers multiple factors and perspectives.

The importance of institutions in fostering long-run economic growth is underscored by various studies, providing compelling evidence that supports this thesis. Douglas North's work (North_1989) emphasizes the role of institutions in reducing transaction costs and promoting market efficiency, which are crucial elements for economic development. He argues that the centralization of enforcement mechanisms, such as those observed in Spain, can lead to economic growth by providing security rights and facilitating the functioning of markets (North_1989: p.1).

Furthermore, North contends that when institutions are more rigid or resistant to change, they may hinder economic growth if they fail to adapt to new circumstances. This idea is supported by Ogilvie's assertion that the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework plays a significant role in economic development (Ogilvie_2007: p.27).

Pamuk's research (Pamuk_2007) supports this argument by demonstrating how changes in institutions, such as the growing commercialization of agriculture following the Black Death, led to significant improvements in economic growth during the last millennium (Pamuk_2007: p.4).

Moreover, Nunn's work (Nunn_2008) provides evidence that ethnic fractionalization and its impact on long-term economic growth are interconnected, as individuals in uncertain environments require institutions to mitigate risk and facilitate economic activity (Nunn_2008: p.4).

Institutions play a crucial role in fostering long-term economic growth, as they provide a framework for individuals to engage in productive activities and mitigate risks (Nunn_2008). Acemoglu et al. (2001) demonstrate that institutions significantly impact economic performance, with detrimental institutions being detrimental to investment and progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). This is further supported by their findings that differences in mortality rates are not the primary cause of institutional variation, but rather the other way around (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3).

In a similar vein, Acemoglu et al. (2002) assert that institutions are a fundamental factor in understanding economic and political development over the past 500 years (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49). They argue that the reversal of economic prosperity seen in some regions can be linked to changes in institutions (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5).

Support for this argument can also be found in Abramovitz's (1986) work, where he suggests that the elaboration of mixed economies is a function of economic growth itself (Abramovitz_1986: p.20). Additionally, Allen (2001) highlights that wages and prices have long been central concerns for understanding issues such as the pace of economic development (Allen_2001: p.1).

Institutions play a crucial role in shaping long-term economic growth, as highlighted by various scholars. Kuznets (1973) emphasizes the association between rapid changes in economic structure and societal aspects such as family roles and urbanization (Kuznets_1973: p.4). The succession of technological innovations during modern economic growth is linked to social innovations that facilitate necessary adjustments, further underlining the importance of institutions (Kuznets_1973: p.6).

North & Weingast (1989) underscore the significance of representative government and its institutions in fostering economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28). They argue that the fundamental institutions, including multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are essential for economic development (North&Weingast_1989: p.2).

Mokyr (2005) supports this notion by suggesting that changes in intellectual developments, such as the growth of economic reasonableness and formal law, contributed significantly to the West's economic superiority (Mokyr_2005: p.4, p.56). The author also points out that many elements of a progressive society, including self-governance and rules determined through an institutionalized process, are linked to economic growth (Mokyr_2005: p.56).

The works of Clark and Jacks (2007) and Feinstein (1998) challenge the thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, particularly in the context of agriculture.

Clark and Jacks (2007) critique the cliometric account of the coal industry's role in the Industrial Revolution, suggesting that technological advancements enabled the coal industry to respond effectively to increased demand rather than institutional factors (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.4). They also argue that prices were deﬂated by an economy-wide price index, implying a broader economic context influencing growth (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.3).

Feinstein (1998) questions the impact of institutions on agricultural productivity and economic growth in the pre-industrial period. He points out that significant changes in farming practices did not occur until the 1850s and 1860s, challenging the notion that institutional improvements were essential for long-term economic growth (Feinstein_1998: p.28). Furthermore, Feinstein highlights the difficulty of measuring unemployment in this period due to a lack of trustworthy direct information, suggesting that other factors may have played a more significant role in economic development (Feinstein_1998: p.23).

Bryceson's work (2002) also challenges the thesis by focusing on the impact of agricultural practices and land access on economic growth. Bryceson demonstrates that traditional hierarchical orders within communities can influence women's economic and social spheres, limiting their participation in non-agrarian activities (Bryceson_2002: p.9). Moreover, Bryceson shows that young households with restricted land access have become more dependent on non-agricultural economic activities or migrant labor, suggesting that agricultural institutions may not be the primary drivers of long-term growth (Bryceson_2002: p.11).

These sources challenge the idea that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, particularly in agriculture, by highlighting technological advancements, broader economic contexts, and traditional social structures as influential factors.

The relationship between institutions and technology in long-run economic growth is a complex interplay, as evidenced by various studies. Koepke & Baten (2005) suggest that climate plays a significant role in human living standards, potentially influencing economic growth indirectly through its impact on health and nutrition, as seen in the Roman Empire and Guatemala (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.16, p.20, p.31, p.32, p.33). However, their study does not necessarily challenge the institutional perspective but rather adds a layer of complexity by highlighting the environmental factors that could influence growth.

Similarly, Frankema (2012) argues that colonial education and British "liberalism" were crucial in explaining long-run growth disparities, particularly in Africa (Frankema_2012: p.2). This suggests that institutional changes brought about by colonization may have played a role in shaping economic growth trajectories. Yet, it is important to note that the British controlled many fertile areas, which could have contributed to their economic success (Frankema_2012: p.2).

Hopkins (2019) discusses the impact of economic base and superstructure on long-run economic growth in Africa. While food exports made little contribution to economic growth during the period of the slave trade, the superstructure assumed importance as the economic base crumbled (Hopkins_2019: p.9, p.13). This implies that institutional structures may have played a role in shaping economic growth even when the traditional economic base was weakened.

Goldin (2006) examines the role of labor market institutions and their evolution in long-run economic growth. The study suggests that legal changes can significantly impact women's choices and decisions, potentially reshaping the economy over time (Goldin_2006: p.1, p.3, p.14). This underscores the importance of institutional changes in shaping labor markets and, by extension, economic growth.

The works of Broadberry & Gardner (2022), Bolt & van Zanden (2014), and Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) provide nuanced perspectives on the role of institutions in long-run economic growth, challenging the assertion that they are the fundamental cause.

Broadberry & Gardner (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1) argue that Sub-Saharan Africa's absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth is due to a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950. This suggests that the lack of historical data may have limited our understanding of institutional impact on economic growth, particularly in certain regions.

Bolt & van Zanden (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5) add that revisions of nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth have been made for various countries but do not fundamentally change our view. This implies that while institutional changes may have contributed to economic growth, other factors might also play a significant role.

Braudel & Wallerstein (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.30) point out a disagreement among scholars about the role of institutions in economic growth. They suggest that institutions are interconnected and affect each other's strategies, implying that the impact of institutional changes on economic growth may not be as straightforward as initially thought.

These sources complicate the argument by suggesting that the influence of institutions on long-run economic growth might be more complex than previously assumed. The lack of historical data in certain regions (Broadberry & Gardner, 2022), the presence of other contributing factors (Bolt & van Zanden, 2014), and the interconnectedness of institutions (Braudel & Wallerstein, 2009) all reshape the understanding of the relationship between institutions and economic growth. Further examination of these factors could provide a more comprehensive view of this relationship.

The examination of institutions and their role in long-run economic growth is further nuanced by considering the economic contributions of women, as discussed in Akyeampong & Fofack (2014). The authors highlight that the historical undervaluation of women's labor has significant implications for policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles. In West Africa, women's contribution to economic growth and development was largely dependent on their husbands' economic provision, which underscores the importance of understanding these connections (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.9). Furthermore, the authors note that the processes by which women's economic contributions are subsumed under household production and unaccounted for have a detrimental impact on policies aimed at strengthening their economic roles (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.3).

Moreover, Austin (2007) provides insights into the role of transportation infrastructure, such as railways and lorries, in shaping economic growth. The author demonstrates that until the mid-1920s, railways had a significant cost advantage over lorries, which could have influenced the pace and direction of economic development (Austin_2007: p.8). Additionally, the author discusses population growth and its impact on economic growth in Ghana, suggesting that factors such as population growth rates need to be taken into account when examining long-run economic growth (Austin_2007: p.12).

Allen et al. (2011) also contribute to the understanding of institutions and economic growth by focusing on income disparities in the early 20th century. The authors show that the income disparities between China, Japan, and Europe were due to long-run stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe (AllenEtAl_2011: p.1). Furthermore, they note that workers in Leipzig experienced rapid growth in real wages during the same period, which highlights the importance of considering both regional and sectoral differences when studying long-run economic growth (AllenEtAl_2011: p.20).

These sources complicate the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by drawing attention to factors such as women's labor, transportation infrastructure, population growth rates, and regional and sectoral differences. A comprehensive understanding of this relationship requires examining these additional factors in conjunction with institutions.

The relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth is a topic of significant interest among scholars, as evidenced by the works of Domar (1970), Bohannan (1959), and Ravallion (2001). While these authors offer valuable insights into the role of institutions in economic development, their perspectives diverge in certain aspects.

Domar (1970) emphasizes the impact of institutional arrangements on labor mobility and competition among employers, arguing that when labor is tied to land or owners, competition ceases, thereby hindering growth. Conversely, Bohannan (1959) focuses on the influence of Western economic institutions, particularly those related to slave dealing and status acquisition, in shaping African societies.

Ravallion's (2001) work is more mathematical in nature, providing equations and formulas to analyze poverty and economic growth, but its direct relevance to institutions is less apparent compared to the other sources. However, all three authors agree that institutions play a crucial role in economic development, albeit through different mechanisms.

Despite these insights, several questions remain unresolved. For instance, there is a need for further research on how specific institutional factors, such as property rights, rule of law, and political stability, contribute to long-term economic growth. Additionally, understanding the interplay between institutions and other factors like women's labor, transportation infrastructure, population growth rates, and regional and sectoral differences requires more comprehensive analysis.

In sum, scholars generally agree that institutions are essential for long-run economic growth. However, the specific ways in which they influence development and the interactions among various institutional and non-institutional factors remain topics for further investigation.
