The debate on the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, particularly in the context of sub-Saharan Africa (SSA), has been a subject of intense scholarly discussion. One prominent perspective posits that institutions are the primary driver of economic growth over the long term (Austin_2008, AcemogluEtAl_2001). However, other scholars argue that this view oversimplifies the complexities involved in understanding economic growth and development, particularly in Africa (Broadberry&Gardner_2022, Akyeampong&Fofack_2014).

Central to this debate is the question of whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Proponents of this view argue that institutions play a crucial role in shaping economic outcomes by influencing property rights, incentives, and investment (AcemogluEtAl_2001). For instance, Austin (2008) asserts that institutions are 'the' fundamental cause of long-run economic growth due to their ability to reinforce or originate economic positions.

On the other hand, critics argue that this perspective overlooks other significant factors contributing to economic growth and development, particularly in SSA. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) point out that the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has led to SSA's absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) argue that understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution is subsumed under household production is crucial for policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles.

The stakes of this debate are high, as understanding the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting economic development in SSA and other regions. The argument continues with a more detailed exploration of the evidence supporting and challenging the thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

Structural change, a shift in an economy's composition from agriculture to industry and services, is closely linked with economic growth (Kuznets_1973: p.4). This transformation, characteristic of modern economic growth, is accompanied by significant changes in societal structures such as family roles and urbanization (Kuznets_1973: p.4). Furthermore, the succession of technological innovations during this period plays a crucial role in driving economic growth and the necessary social adjustments (Kuznets_1973: p.6).

However, it's important to note that while modern economic growth offers numerous benefits, it also presents non-conventional costs within developed countries, and less developed countries struggle to harness the potential of modern technology effectively (Kuznets_1973: p.11).

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) support this notion by highlighting that country-specific conditions and factor endowments have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth. They argue that the divergence in paths can be traced back to the achievement of sustained economic growth by the United States and Canada during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, while other countries did not manage to attain this goal until late in the nineteenth century (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.12).

The establishment and development of urban networks played a crucial role in fostering long-run economic growth, as evidenced by North (1989) and deVries (1994). According to North (1989), the centralized enforcement mechanisms of institutions, such as those implemented by Spain, significantly contributed to economic growth by reducing transaction costs and increasing security rights in the capital market (North_1989: p.12). Similarly, deVries (1994) highlights that Europe's urban network, connecting cities through commercial interaction, was a significant achievement of the early modern period, providing a framework for regional economic development that facilitated industrial growth (deVries_1994: p.5).

Moreover, North (1989) asserts that the nested tracts of a more renegotiation-resistant institutional structure could be fundamental to economic growth by promoting stability and reducing the likelihood of rule alteration (North_1989: p.6). In addition, deVries (1994) argues that the Industrial Revolution, particularly in Britain, marked a monumental turning point in economic history, with industrial growth occurring within the established urban network (deVries_1994: p.2).

The resolution of institutional contradictions plays a crucial role in fostering long-run economic growth, as evidenced by Ogilvie (2007) and Pamuk (2007). According to Ogilvie (2007: p.3), economists have endeavored to incorporate institutions into economic analysis, recognizing their significant impact on economic development. The author highlights that the efficiency theory, which attributes growth solely to factor endowments, encounters challenges when considering institutional frameworks (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). This acknowledgement underscores the importance of institutions in shaping economic outcomes.

Pamuk (2007) supports this notion by examining the impact of the Black Death on European wages and incomes, asserting that it led to significant changes during the last millennium (Pamuk_2007: p.4). The decline in population following the plague facilitated structural change in agriculture, leading to its commercialization (Pamuk_2007: p.24). This shift likely contributed to long-term economic growth by promoting efficiency and innovation within agricultural production.

Structural change, a key factor in economic growth following the plague (Pamuk_2007: p.24), is often associated with environmental degradation rather than improvement, according to Stern (2004). He argues that economic growth can lead to increased environmental damage during its early stages, contradicting the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth (Stern_2004: p.1, p.3).

For instance, Stern suggests that the Environmental Kuznets Curve (EKC) hypothesis, which postulates an inverted U-shaped relationship between income and environmental degradation, may not hold true (Stern_2004: p.1). He contends that economic growth can lead to environmental degradation initially but eventually result in improved environmental conditions as economies mature (Stern_2004: p.3).

However, Stern does acknowledge the role of changes in economic factors and structures in driving variations in environmental impacts during economic development (Stern_2004: p.3). He emphasizes that these changes can either lead to rising or declining environmental degradation over time. Nevertheless, he assumes that environmental damage does not significantly hinder economic activity to halt the growth process, and any irreversibility does not significantly reduce future income levels (Stern_2004: p.8).

These arguments challenge the thesis by suggesting that economic growth may have a detrimental impact on the environment in its early stages, rather than institutions being the primary drivers of long-term economic growth and environmental improvement.

The assertion that institutions are the primary drivers of long-term economic growth and environmental improvement is not universally accepted. Some scholars challenge this view by highlighting the impact of economic growth on the environment, particularly in its early stages. For instance, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that Sub-Saharan Africa has been absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth due to a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950. They suggest that understanding the extent to which there are still risks of economic shrinkage is crucial in determining whether current growth provides a path to convergence (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.18).

Similarly, Temin (2002) posits that the exhaustion of cheap labor caused economic growth to slow, implying that growth might not always be beneficial for long-term development. He also asserts that recent theories of economic growth should consider the context of economic transition, which was prevalent in many countries during the past century or two (Temin_2002: p.7).

Moreover, Koepke & Baten (2005) complicate the argument by focusing on the relationship between climate and human living standards. They find that over the period of the Roman Empire, heights did not increase, contrasting with the common view that living standards and purchasing power increased during this time due to economic growth (Koepke & Baten_2005: p.16).

In a similar vein, Hopkins (2019) suggests that the study of long-run economic and political change in Africa was inhibited by 'totalism' and 'essentialism,' which were regarded as Western impositions on the rest of the world. He argues that successive generations of researchers may have limited the diversity that independent African viewpoints would otherwise have supplied (Hopkins_2019: p.4).

Lastly, Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) argue that there has been a movement toward economic and social history at the expense of political history, which could potentially reshape our understanding of long-term economic growth and its relationship with institutions (Braudel & Wallerstein_2009: p.7).

These sources add nuance to the thesis by suggesting that factors such as climate, labor exhaustion, and the focus on certain historical perspectives can complicate the relationship between institutions and long-term economic growth. Future investigations should consider these factors in order to gain a more comprehensive understanding of this complex relationship.

The economic contributions of African women, long overlooked and undervalued, significantly impact the region's growth and development (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.19). However, understanding the historical processes that subsume women's economic roles under household production is crucial for formulating effective policies aimed at strengthening their economic positions (Akyeampong&Fofock_2014: p.3).

The authors emphasize the importance of recognizing these contributions, as they find parallels in West Africa that extend across other African regions where gender and economic growth are concerned (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.4). This realization underscores the fundamental role institutions play in shaping women's economic roles, and highlights the need for institutions that support equitable participation by women in long-term economic growth.

Moreover, the authors note that the structural changes associated with economic growth often fail to account for women's contributions (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). This omission complicates our understanding of the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth in Africa, as it suggests that traditional measures may not accurately capture the full extent of women's economic impact.

During high economic growth, the role of public employment becomes a significant factor in understanding the relationship between institutions and long-term economic development. Komlos (1998) delves into this aspect, offering nuanced insights that complicate our understanding.

Komlos (Komlos_1998: p.2) defines modern economic growth in a Kuznetsian sense, suggesting that it is not merely an increase in GDP or productivity but also involves structural changes and population growth. This perspective aligns with the thesis by implying that institutions shaping public employment during high economic growth could be instrumental in driving such structural changes.

Moreover, Komlos (Komlos_1998: p.16) discusses the discovery of 8-year height cycles similar to classical business cycles. This finding implies that biological growth processes were significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economics, suggesting a strong interplay between economic institutions and long-term growth.

Lastly, Komlos (Komlos_1998: p.17) highlights that the increase in inequality, variability of income, and urbanization during the Industrial Revolution were associated with economic processes and structural changes. This observation underscores the importance of understanding the role of institutions in shaping public employment during high economic growth, as these factors could influence long-term economic development.

The role of institutions in fostering long-run economic growth is a topic that garners significant attention among scholars, as evidenced by the works of Domar (1970), Bohannan (1959), and Ravallion (2001). These authors allude to the critical function of institutions in shaping economic processes and structural changes.

Domar (1970) posits that when labor is tied to land or an owner, competition among employers ceases, which may hinder economic growth. This notion aligns with Bohannan's (1959) assertion that the Western impact on economic institutions was not limited solely to economic factors but also extended to social structures.

However, there is a divergence in the focus of their discussions. Domar delves into the economic cost of slaveholding and the effect of labor immobility on growth, while Bohannan examines the impact of Western influence on African societies, particularly in terms of status acquisition and trade practices. Ravallion's (2001) work appears more mathematical and abstract, offering equations and formulas rather than explicit discussions on institutions and economic growth.

Despite these differences, a common theme emerges: the importance of institutions in facilitating structural changes that promote long-term economic development. What remains unresolved is the need for a comprehensive understanding of how specific institutional arrangements influence economic processes and the subsequent effects on growth. Further research should aim to elucidate this relationship, providing insights into effective institution-building strategies for promoting sustainable economic growth.
