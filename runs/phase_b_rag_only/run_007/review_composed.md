The central question in the literature review on institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is whether institutions play a crucial role in shaping economic development over time. This question holds significant implications for understanding the historical trajectories of various regions and economies, particularly those that have experienced slow or stagnant growth.

Austin (2008) supports the thesis, arguing that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. He contends that these trades, even the Atlantic one, reinforced rather than originated Africa’s relatively poor economic position compared to western Europe. Austin also endorses the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history, revealing relationships that recur because of path dependence.

Kuznets (1973) also supports this perspective, stating that high aggregate growth rates are associated with rapid changes in economic structure and social innovation. He highlights that modern technological innovations and social adjustments are major factors affecting economic and social structure.

North & Weingast (1989) focus on the political factors underlying economic growth and the development of markets, emphasizing the role of institutions in enforcing and changing rules governing economic exchange. They demonstrate how institutions played a necessary role in making possible economic growth and political freedom.

However, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate the argument by pointing out that Sub-Saharan Africa has been absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth due to the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950. They suggest that understanding the extent to which there are still risks of shrinking undermining economic progress is crucial for determining whether current growth provides a path to convergence.

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) further complicate the issue by highlighting how understanding the processes by which women’s economic contribution becomes subsumed under the category of household production and their labor formally unaccounted for is essential for strengthening women’s economic roles. They argue that the major historical developments in West Africa find parallels elsewhere in Africa where gender and economic growth are concerned.

The debate continues as scholars explore the nuances and complexities of institutions' role in long-run economic growth, with some emphasizing their importance while others caution against oversimplifying their impact. The stakes are high, as understanding the factors shaping economic development over time can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting growth and reducing inequality across regions and economies.

The role of institutions in driving long-term economic growth is a central theme in contemporary economic literature. North and Weingast (1989) argue that representative government, characterized by multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, plays a crucial role in fostering economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28). This perspective emphasizes the importance of political institutions in shaping economic development.

Mokyr (2005) also supports this view by suggesting that changes in intellectual developments, particularly the growth of "useful knowledge," were instrumental in driving economic growth in the West (Mokyr_2005: p.4). He highlights how elements such as individualism, formal law, corporatism, and self-governance contributed to the development of a progressive society that fostered economic growth.

North (1989) further supports this argument by focusing on transaction costs and security rights within different economic systems. He argues that institutions that reduce transaction costs and provide secure property rights contribute to the development of markets and, in turn, economic growth (North_1989: p.9).

In a similar vein, Nunn and Wantchekon (2011) demonstrate that both internal (within-group) and external (between-group) factors play significant roles in shaping economic development (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.4). They find that the internal channel, which refers to the impact of institutions on group cohesion and cooperation, is at least twice as large as the external channel in determining economic growth.

The influence of institutions on long-run economic growth is a significant factor, as supported by various studies. Acemoglu and his colleagues (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27) argue that more fundamental institutions play a crucial role in shaping property rights, which in turn impact economic development. They also assert that differences in mortality rates are not the primary cause of variation in institutions (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3). Acemoglu and his collaborators (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49) further emphasize that understanding economic development requires considering the impact of institutions, stating it as a "first-order fact."

Similarly, Abramovitz (Abramovitz_1986: p.20) suggests that the elaboration of mixed economies is a function of economic growth itself, implying that institutions play a role in fostering such growth. Allen (2001: p.4) also highlights that wages and prices have long been central concerns of economic historians due to their influence on economic development. Furthermore, Abramovitz (Abramovitz_1986: p.7) identifies macroeconomic and monetary conditions encouraging capital investment and effective demand as essential factors for economic growth.

These findings collectively support the assertion that institutions are indeed a fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. The internal channel, which refers to the impact of institutions on group cohesion and cooperation, as well as the direct effect on property rights and macroeconomic conditions, significantly contribute to determining economic growth (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.4). Future investigations may delve deeper into specific institutional factors and their respective impacts on economic development across various regions and time periods.

The interplay between institutions and industrialization is a crucial factor in understanding long-run economic growth, as supported by various scholars (Ogilvie_2007, Sokoloff&Engerman_2000, Peters_2004).

In the context of economic development, institutions can be viewed as the framework that shapes and influences individual behavior and collective action (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). The interdependency of these institutions within a broader framework has been recognized as significant in shaping the trajectory of economic growth (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). For instance, the efficiency theory, which posits that institutions evolve to maximize overall utility, may face challenges when individual institutions resist encroachment (Ogilvie_2007: p.27).

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) argue that initial conditions, or factor endowments broadly conceived, could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth. They trace the divergence in paths of economic development back to the achievement of sustained economic growth by the United States and Canada during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.12).

Peters (2004) also emphasizes the role of institutions in shaping economic growth, particularly through land policy reform efforts aimed at promoting economic growth, sustainable management of land, and poverty reduction (Peters_2004: p.7). The dynamics of social transformation are closely tied to culture, which has its own autonomy and dynamic of development (Peters_2004: p.16).

The assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth faces challenges when considering the role of agriculture, as suggested by some scholars. For instance, Clark & Jacks (2007) critique the cliometric account of the coal industry in the Industrial Revolution, arguing that technological advancements enabled the coal industry to respond to increases in demand, rather than institutional changes (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.4). Similarly, Feinstein (1998) notes that economic growth in rural areas did not significantly change until the 1850s and 1860s, questioning the impact of institutions on agricultural development during earlier periods (Feinstein_1998: p.28).

Moreover, Bryceson (2002) challenges the notion that institutions are solely responsible for long-run economic growth by highlighting the persistence of traditional hierarchical orders within communities, which can influence women's economic and social spheres (Bryceson_2002: p.9). In addition, Bryceson suggests that in some areas, such as Njombe, Tanzania, young households with restricted land access have become more dependent on non-agricultural economic activities or migrant labor due to changes in the agricultural sector (Bryceson_2002: p.11). These findings suggest that while institutions may play a role in promoting economic growth, other factors such as technological advancements and socio-cultural structures also significantly impact agricultural development and long-run economic growth.

The interplay between institutions and technological advancements further nuances the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Koepke & Baten (2005) challenge this notion by suggesting that climate, rather than institutions, may have played a significant role in shaping human living standards over time. For instance, they found no increase in heights during the Roman Empire, despite economic growth and protection (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.16). Similarly, Frankema (2012) asserts that colonial education might have been a key factor in explaining long-run growth disparities, rather than institutions alone (Frankema_2012: p.2).

Moreover, Hopkins (2019) highlights the potential limitations of focusing solely on institutions when examining long-run economic growth. He argues that the study of African economic history was hindered by 'totalism' and 'essentialism,' which may have overshadowed other crucial factors such as food exports and superstructures (Hopkins_2019: p.9, p.13). Goldin (2006) also complicates the argument by emphasizing that the study of labor was once centered around institutions but should also consider evolution and revolution (Goldin_2006: p.3).

These findings suggest that while institutions may contribute to economic growth, they are not the sole determinant. Climate, education, food exports, superstructures, and labor dynamics must also be considered when evaluating long-run economic growth. Future research should continue to explore these factors in various contexts to gain a more comprehensive understanding of their respective roles and interactions.

The complexity of long-run economic growth is further highlighted by the historical performance of Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA), a region that has been largely overlooked in recent debates due to the scarcity of data before 1950 (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). Collier and Gunning (1999) note that African economic performance has been markedly worse than other regions, while Sachs and Warner (1997) observe that slow growth in SSA may have contributed to its underdevelopment (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). However, understanding the extent to which this growth can lead to convergence requires an examination of the risks of regression that have undermined economic progress in the past (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.18).

The lack of data for SSA is not unique; revisions of growth in various regions, such as Switzerland, Germany, Sweden, Italy, Greece, Russia, and Bulgaria, have been made, but these do not significantly alter the overall picture (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5). The sources of long-term economic growth continue to be a subject of debate, with Marx's writings suggesting a model where institutions play a significant role in shaping economic strategies (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.21). This disagreement is more fundamental than it may seem, as the interplay between institutions, labor dynamics, and structural change can significantly impact long-run economic growth.

In this context, the role of institutions in fostering or hindering long-run economic growth becomes even more crucial. Future research should explore these factors in various contexts to gain a more comprehensive understanding of their respective roles and interactions, particularly focusing on regions like SSA where data is scarce but the need for insights is great. The complexity of institutional change and development, particularly in regions like Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA), is further illuminated by various studies. For instance, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) highlight the underestimation of women's economic contribution, which is often subsumed under household production and formally unaccounted for. This undermines policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3).

Moreover, the historical developments in West Africa that impact gender and economic growth have parallels elsewhere in Africa (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.4). This suggests that institutional changes affecting women's economic roles may be more widespread across the continent than previously thought.

In a different context, Austin (2007) discusses the impact of transportation infrastructure on economic growth. Until the mid-1920s, railways had a significant advantage over lorries due to their lower cost (Austin_2007: p.8). However, as population growth accelerated after 1918, the need for more efficient and flexible transportation systems became increasingly apparent (Austin_2007: p.12).

Allen et al. (2011) provide insights into income disparities in the early twentieth century. They note that the income disparities were due to long-run stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe (AllenEtAl_2011: p.1). This underscores the importance of institutional changes fostering industrialization for economic growth.

These studies complicate the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by emphasizing the role of factors such as gender, transportation infrastructure, and industrialization. They suggest that understanding these interconnected factors is crucial for a comprehensive analysis of institutional change and its impact on economic growth, particularly in regions like SSA where data is scarce but the need for insights is great.

The scholarship on the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth presents a complex interplay of convergence and divergence. Both Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) acknowledge the significance of institutions in shaping economic growth, albeit in tangential contexts.

Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) posits that factors of production, including institutions, play a crucial role in economic development. He suggests that when labor is tied to land or an owner, competition among employers ceases, which can hinder economic growth. This notion aligns with the argument that effective institutions foster competition and promote efficiency.

Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) discusses how Western economic institutions impacted African societies, particularly through slave dealing and status acquisition. He notes that these changes could limit the number of women, potentially affecting population growth and long-term economic development. This perspective underscores the importance of understanding how external factors can influence institutional change and its impact on economic growth.

However, both scholars' works are tangential to the central argument, focusing more on specific instances rather than providing a comprehensive analysis of institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Ravallion (2001) seems less relevant to this discussion, as his work contains mathematical equations and symbols that do not directly address the role of institutions in economic growth.

What remains unresolved is a clear consensus on the specific institutional factors that drive long-term economic growth and their relative importance compared to other factors such as geography, culture, or technology. Future research should aim to identify these key institutional factors and explore their interplay with other determinants of economic development, particularly in regions like Sub-Saharan Africa where data is scarce but the need for insights is great.
