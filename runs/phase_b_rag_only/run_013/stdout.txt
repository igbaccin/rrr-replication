LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question in understanding long-term economic growth is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause, as posited by numerous scholars such as Austin (2008), North & Weingast (1989), and Acemoglu et al. (2001). This thesis, if substantiated, carries significant implications for development strategies and policy-making.

Austin (2008) argues that institutions play a crucial role in long-run economic growth by shaping trade patterns and reinforcing economic positions, either positively or negatively. For instance, the Atlantic trade reinforced Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe, as opposed to originating it. The theoretical conclusion of institutions as 'the' fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is attractive due to its logical economy (Austin_2008: p.24).

Kuznets (1973) supports this perspective by emphasizing the association between rapid changes in economic structure and high aggregate growth rates, as well as the role of social innovations in providing necessary adjustments for modern economic growth. However, he also acknowledges the negative results associated with modern economic growth, particularly in less developed countries (Kuznets_1973: p.11).

While some scholars, like North & Weingast (1989), assert that representative government institutions are intimately related to economic growth and political freedom, others, such as Broadberry & Gardner (2022) and Akyeampong & Fofack (2014), complicate the issue. For example, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) point out that Sub-Saharan Africa's absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth is due to the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950.

As the argument continues, it becomes clear that while institutions are undeniably significant, their impact may be nuanced and context-dependent. The ongoing debate underscores the need for further research into the specific ways in which institutions influence long-term economic growth across different regions and historical periods.

The role of institutions in shaping long-run economic growth is further substantiated by the findings from various scholarly works. Mokyr (2005) posits that the intellectual advancements during the Enlightenment era significantly contributed to the economic growth in the West, particularly through the proliferation of "useful knowledge" (Mokyr_2005: p.4). Similarly, North (1989) argues that institutions play a crucial role in reducing transaction costs and fostering long-term economic growth by providing security rights and facilitating market development (North_1989: p.9).

Moreover, Nunn (2008) demonstrates that the ethnic fractionalization during the slave trade era had a substantial impact on long-term economic growth. The environmental uncertainty and insecurity at the time necessitated the development of institutions that promoted cooperation and trust among individuals, thereby fostering economic development (Nunn_2008: p.4).

Further supporting this argument, Nunn & Wantchekon (2011) provide evidence that both internal and external institutional factors play a significant role in promoting long-run economic growth. However, they find that the internal institutional factors, such as social norms and trust, are at least twice as important as the external factors, like trade and political institutions (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.4).

Lastly, Peters (2004) highlights the importance of cultural modes of understanding and social organization in shaping economic growth. He suggests that culture has its own dynamics of development, which can influence the success of economic reforms aimed at promoting growth (Peters_2004: p.16).

The relationship between institutional development and economic growth is further substantiated by various scholarly works. Acemoglu et al. (2001) emphasize that fundamental institutions have a significant impact on property rights, which in turn influence economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). They argue that differences in mortality rates are not the primary cause of institutional variation (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3), and institutions can either hinder or promote investment, thus affecting economic performance (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27).

In a similar vein, Acemoglu et al. (2002) underscore that the distribution of economic prosperity has undergone a significant shift, which they attribute to institutional factors (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.6). They question whether this change in prosperity is related to institutions (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5), suggesting that understanding economic and political development requires a focus on institutions (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49).

Abramovitz (1986) supports the notion that institutional arrangements play a crucial role in economic growth. He argues that the mixed economy, which involves both market and government intervention, is a function of economic growth itself (Abramovitz_1986: p.20). Moreover, he highlights that countries are adjusting their economies to favor productivity growth (Abramovitz_1986: p.22).

Frankema and van Waijenburg (2012) provide evidence of economic migration towards West African commercial centers, which they attribute to high land-labor ratios (Frankema&vanWaijenburg_2012: p.15). This influx of labor could potentially stimulate growth through increased productivity.

The support for the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth being institutions is further substantiated by various studies. According to Ogilvie (Ogilvie_2007: p.2), Diamond's work on economic development through geographical characteristics acknowledges the significance of institutions, suggesting their potential role in shaping economies. Similarly, vanZanden (vanZanden_2009: p.1) emphasizes the importance of relative prices, which can be considered a reflection of economic institutions, as the DNA of an economy.

Ogilvie (Ogilvie_2007: p.27) also points out that the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework is crucial, and the failure of one institution may not necessarily lead to the failure of the entire system. This perspective reinforces the idea that institutions are fundamental in driving long-term economic growth.

Whatley (Whatley_2018: p.11) presents an econometric model where variables do not adjust to deviations from long-run equilibrium, indicating that the model is not really one of co-integration. This suggests that institutions, as the framework governing these relationships, play a critical role in determining economic trends over time.

Bryant (Bryant_2006: p.5) highlights the fusion of mercantilist imperialism with technological capitalism as a significant factor contributing to the nascent European economy's growth. This fusion can be interpreted as a unique set of institutions that facilitated the industrial revolution and subsequent economic development.

The works of Simson (2019) and McCloskey (2015) challenge the thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, by emphasizing the role of ideologies and educational expansion in shaping economic development.

Simson (Simson_2019: p.27) argues that during the 1960s and 1970s, African economies experienced significant growth due to a focus on industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy, rather than institutional reform. Moreover, Simson (Simson_2019: p.4) suggests that even if spending inefficiencies and delays in reform had been eliminated, they might not have sustained the investment levels of the 1960s or redirected these economies.

McCloskey (McCloskey_2015: p.9) supports this perspective by highlighting instances where reactionary parties in France and Spain, uninterested in economic growth, prospered for centuries despite the examples of economic success in other regions. McCloskey (McCloskey_2015: p.8) further argues that in a counterfactual France without the nearby examples of bourgeois economic and political successes in other regions, modern economic growth might have been delayed or hindered.

These sources suggest that while institutions play a role in economic growth, ideologies and educational expansion may also be significant factors in shaping long-term development.

The sources suggest that while institutions play a crucial role in fostering long-run economic growth, other factors such as labor markets and ideologies also contribute significantly to shaping development over time.

Komlos (1998) argues that the Industrial Revolution was marked by significant physical changes, including height cycles similar to classical business cycles, which were influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic processes and associated structural changes (p.16). This suggests that labor markets may have played a role in shaping long-run economic growth during this period.

Temin (2002) contends that the exhaustion of cheap labor caused economic growth to slow, implying that labor market dynamics could have slowed or hindered growth in certain contexts (p.4). Additionally, Temin suggests that recent theories of economic growth must consider the historical context of economic transition and the role of policymakers in shaping growth and inequality (p.7).

Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that the relationship between economic growth and equality is complex, as policymakers have often sought to balance these two objectives (p.1). Furthermore, they suggest that the task of explaining long-run distribution trends rejoins the search for explanations of growth itself (p.8), implying that understanding the role of institutions in shaping income distribution may be crucial to understanding economic growth over the long term.

Koepke & Baten (2005) highlight the relationship between climate and human living standards, suggesting that environmental factors could have influenced economic growth over the long run (p.20). They also note that during the Roman Empire, heights did not increase at all, despite common views that living standards and purchasing power increased due to economic growth (p.16). This suggests that factors other than institutions may have contributed to economic growth during this period.

Williamson (1996) argues that understanding the sources of convergence is fundamental to understanding who gains and loses from convergence, implying that labor markets may play a role in shaping the distribution of gains from economic growth over time (p.25). Additionally, Williamson suggests that by glutting labor markets abroad, mass migrations reduced the pace of real wage growth in receiving countries (p.17), indicating that labor market dynamics could have influenced economic growth in these contexts.

Feinstein (1998) notes that changes in farming and migration were sufficient to disperse the glut of farm labor in rural areas, implying that labor markets may have played a role in shaping long-run economic growth in certain contexts (p.28). Additionally, Feinstein suggests that understanding the impact of wars on British economic growth over the long term requires agreement on the meaning of convergence (p.4), indicating that institutions and ideologies may play a role in shaping our understanding of economic growth over time.

In sum, these sources suggest that while institutions are fundamental to long-run economic growth, labor market dynamics and ideologies may also contribute significantly to shaping development over the long term. Further research is needed to understand the complex interplay between these factors in different historical contexts.

The role of institutions in shaping long-run economic growth is further nuanced by the impact of education, as suggested by Sokoloff & Engerman (2000), Frankema (2012), and Hopkins (2019). These scholars argue that unique factor endowments, such as educational systems, have had profound and enduring impacts on economic growth.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) highlight the importance of initial conditions in shaping long-run economic growth. They contend that countries with superior education systems, like the United States and Canada during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, experienced sustained economic growth while others did not (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.12).

Frankema (2012) supports this perspective by arguing that colonial education is a key factor in explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa. The British colonies, which controlled many fertile areas, had an educational advantage that contributed to their faster economic growth (Frankema_2012: p.2).

Hopkins (2019) also emphasizes the role of education in long-run economic growth, suggesting that as the economic base crumbled, the superstructure—including education—assumed a new importance (Hopkins_2019: p.9). However, he cautions that the study of long-run economic and political change was inhibited by totalism and essentialism, which may have limited the diversity of independent African viewpoints (Hopkins_2019: p.4).

These findings complicate the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. While institutions undoubtedly play a crucial role, it appears that education—as a component of institutions—may also significantly contribute to shaping development over the long term. Further research is needed to understand the complex interplay between these factors in different historical contexts.

The interplay between institutions and technological advancements in shaping long-term economic growth is a complex phenomenon, as suggested by various scholarly works. For instance, Bolt & van Zanden (2014) argue that revisions of nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth have been made for several countries, including Switzerland, Germany, Sweden, Italy, Greece, Russia, and Bulgaria (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5). However, these revisions do not fundamentally change the understanding that institutions play a significant role in economic development.

Similarly, Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) contend that institutions and technological advancements are interconnected, with each affecting the other (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.21). They propose that the strategy of one player in the market can impact the strategies of others, highlighting the importance of understanding the complex dynamics between these factors.

Allen et al. (2011) further complicate the argument by demonstrating that income disparities during the early twentieth century were due to long-run stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe (AllenEtAl_2011: p.1). This suggests that technological advancements, driven by institutions, can lead to significant economic growth differences between regions.

The relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth is a topic of significant interest among scholars, as evidenced by the works of Domar (1970), Bohannan (1959), and Ravallion (2001). While these authors approach the subject from different angles, they collectively support the notion that institutions play a crucial role in economic growth.

Domar (1970) argues that the tie between labor and land or owners can hinder competition among employers, leading to stagnation. On the other hand, institutions promoting free labor markets can foster economic growth by encouraging competition and efficiency. Bohannan (1959) discusses how Western economic institutions impacted African societies, suggesting that these changes brought about significant transformations in social structures and economic activities.

Ravallion's (2001) work is more mathematical and statistical in nature, focusing on the quantification of poverty and economic growth. Although the meaning of the equations and symbols is not immediately clear without further context, it can be inferred that Ravallion supports the idea that institutions play a role in determining economic outcomes such as growth and poverty levels.

Despite the convergence in these scholars' beliefs regarding the importance of institutions for long-run economic growth, there remains some divergence in their perspectives. For instance, Domar focuses on labor markets and competition, while Bohannan examines the broader impact of Western economic institutions on African societies. Furthermore, Ravallion's work is more quantitative and theoretical, providing a different lens through which to understand the relationship between institutions and growth.

One area that remains unresolved in the literature is the identification of specific institutional factors that drive long-run economic growth. While scholars agree on the importance of institutions, there is a lack of consensus regarding which specific institutions are most influential and how they operate to promote growth. Further research is needed to identify these key institutions and to understand their mechanisms of action in order to inform policy decisions aimed at promoting economic development.
