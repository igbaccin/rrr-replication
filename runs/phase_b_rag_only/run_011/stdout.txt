The debate on the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth centers around the role of institutions, with some scholars arguing that they are the primary drivers (North & Weingast_1989; Acemoglu et al. 2001), while others complicate this argument by highlighting additional factors that influence growth patterns (Austin_2008; Broadberry & Gardner_2022; Akyeampong & Fofack_2014).

The central question at hand is: To what extent do institutions drive long-term economic growth, and how do other factors interact with them to shape growth patterns? The stakes are high, as understanding the underlying mechanisms of economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting development.

Scholars who support the institutional perspective argue that effective institutions, such as representative government and legal systems that protect property rights, foster economic growth by enabling markets to function efficiently (North & Weingast_1989). For instance, North and Weingast (1989) demonstrate how the development of institutions played a crucial role in England's economic growth during the Glorious Revolution.

On the other hand, some scholars complicate this argument by emphasizing the interplay between institutions and other factors, such as historical events, cultural norms, and geography (Austin_2008; Broadberry & Gardner_2022). For example, Austin (2008) argues that while institutions are important, they must be examined in the context of long-term history to reveal relationships that recur due to path dependency. Similarly, Broadberry and Gardner (2022) highlight the lack of data on pre-1950 economic performance in Sub-Saharan Africa, which has led to its absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth.

Furthermore, scholars like Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) argue that understanding the role of women in economic growth requires examining how their labor has been subsumed under the category of household production and formally unaccounted for. This contributes to policies that seek to strengthen women's economic roles but may be misguided if they do not take into account historical patterns of women's contributions to economic growth.

The significance of institutions in fostering long-term economic growth is further substantiated by various scholars. Kuznets (1973) posits that economic and social structure changes are closely linked to technological innovations, which drive modern economic growth (Kuznets_1973: p.6). Mokyr (2005) echoes this sentiment, suggesting that the intellectual realm, particularly developments in "useful knowledge," played a crucial role in the West's economic growth (Mokyr_2005: p.4).

North and Weingast (1989) emphasize the political factors underlying economic growth and the development of markets, focusing on the importance of institutions that govern rule enforcement and changes (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). North (1989) highlights how institutions, such as centralized enforcement mechanisms, can influence transaction costs and contribute to economic growth (North_1989: p.9).

Ogilvie (2007) acknowledges the interdependency of individual institutions within a broader framework, asserting that a failure to consider this complexity may lead to inaccurate conclusions about their impact on economic growth (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). Whatley's (2018) research demonstrates the long-run equilibrium relationship between certain variables and economic growth, further supporting the notion that institutions play a fundamental role in shaping these dynamics.

The evidence provided by Acemoglu and his colleagues (AcemogluEtAl_2001) supports the notion that institutions play a fundamental role in shaping long-run economic growth. Their research demonstrates that more fundamental institutions, such as property rights and political power structures, have a substantial impact on economic performance. Specifically, they find that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). Furthermore, the results of their analysis indicate a large effect of institutions on economic performance overall (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19).

Similarly, Abramovitz's (1986) research supports the idea that institutions are crucial for long-run economic growth. He argues that macroeconomic and monetary conditions encouraging and sustaining capital investment and the level and growth of effective demand are essential factors in promoting productivity growth (Abramovitz_1986: p.7). Additionally, Abramovitz suggests that the elaboration of the mixed economy is a function of economic growth itself (Abramovitz_1986: p.20), further emphasizing the role of institutions in shaping economic development.

Allen's (2001) work also supports the thesis that institutions are fundamental causes of long-run economic growth. He highlights the importance of wages and prices in economic development, as they bear on such issues as the pace of economic development, economic leadership, and the standard of living (Allen_2001: p.1). Furthermore, Allen's research shows that both positions can find support in the indices reported, suggesting that structural change correlates with growth (Allen_2001: p.23).

The authors of Broadberry & Gupta (2006) and Broadberry et al. (2015) provide substantial support for the thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Both works emphasize the crucial role of institutional changes in driving economic development during the early modern period, a period marked by significant structural transformations.

Broadberry & Gupta (2006) argue that the recent literature on new economic geography supports the sceptical view of proto-industrialization as a stage on the road to modern economic growth (p. 9). They suggest that rural peasants supplementing their agricultural activities with manufacturing contributed to the growth of the service sector, which played a crucial role in economic growth during this period. Furthermore, they argue against using grain wages as a sole indicator of economic development, asserting that it provides an incomplete picture (p. 24).

Similarly, Broadberry et al. (2015) begin their work with a survey of the existing literature on India's long-run economic performance, acknowledging the enduring controversy surrounding this topic (p. 2). They propose that population estimates are essential for understanding economic growth and development in India during the late sixteenth century and beyond (p. 4).

Bryant (2006) supports the thesis by highlighting the fusion of mercantilist imperialism with a technologically driven capitalistic transformation as a fundamental axis of difference between European economies and those of other regions (p. 5). This fusion, he suggests, provided more than adequate environments for the growth of commercial exchange (p. 13), underscoring the importance of institutional changes in driving economic development.

Structural change, rather than institutions, may play a significant role in long-term economic growth as suggested by some scholars (Simson_2019: p.25). For instance, in the 1960s and 1970s, Kenyan, Tanzanian, and Ugandan state expenditure and deficits expanded during a period of high economic growth (Simson_2019: p.25). This expansion, while possibly inefficient, may have been crucial for sustaining the investment levels of that era, rather than being merely the result of institutional changes.

Moreover, Stern (2004) argues that economic growth can lead to environmental degradation in the early stages of development, challenging the notion that institutions are the primary driver of long-term economic growth (Stern_2004: p.1). He suggests that the belief in economic growth as a means to environmental improvement may be overstated, implying that other factors, such as changes in economic structure, might also influence environmental impacts.

Similarly, McCloskey (2015) contends that ideologies and ideas, not institutions, can significantly impact economic growth (McCloskey_2015: p.9). He cites examples from France and Spain where conservative ideologies hindered economic growth despite the successes of other regions with similar institutional structures.

In a similar vein, vanZanden (1999) argues that the early modern economy was capable of generating economic growth, contradicting the consensus view in the 1960s and 1970s that it was unable to do so (vanZanden_1999: p.1). This re-evaluation of economic growth before the industrial revolution suggests that structural changes within the economy might have been more influential than institutions.

Finally, Humphries (2013) posits that the division of labor, the rise of workshop production, and the eventual emergence of the factory system were key drivers of eighteenth-century economic growth, again implying a significant role for structural change (Humphries_2013: p.18).

These sources challenge the thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by suggesting that structural changes within economies might also play a crucial role. Further investigation into these factors and their interplay is necessary to fully understand the dynamics of economic development.

The assertion that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth is nuanced by several sources, offering insights into the complex interplay between structural changes and economic development. Komlos (1998) emphasizes the role of physical stature during the Industrial Revolution, suggesting a connection between biological growth processes and short-term economic fluctuations. This implies that structural changes may have a more significant impact on long-run economic growth than previously thought.

Temin (2002) challenges the notion of institutions as the sole cause by highlighting the exhaustion of cheap labor as a factor slowing economic growth. He argues that the end of the Golden Age was brought about by countries returning to equilibrium growth paths, rather than adverse economic shocks in the 1970s. This implies that structural changes, such as shifts in labor markets, can significantly influence long-run economic growth.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) complicate the argument by suggesting a reciprocal relationship between economic growth and equality. They argue that policymakers often face trade-offs between these two factors, implying that structural changes in income distribution could potentially impact long-run economic growth.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) emphasize the role of initial conditions, or factor endowments, in shaping long-run economic growth. They suggest that variations across societies in conditions relevant to growth, such as institutions, have profound and enduring impacts on economic development. This implies that structural changes in these conditions could significantly influence long-run economic growth.

Williamson (1996) discusses the relationship between schooling and Swedish economic growth before World War I, suggesting that understanding the sources of convergence is fundamental to understanding policy responses. This implies that structural changes in education systems could potentially impact long-run economic growth.

Skocpol & Sommers (1980) argue that comparing different cases can increase the "visibility" of one structure by contrasting it with another. This suggests that structural comparisons between economies could provide valuable insights into the factors influencing long-run economic growth.

The assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is not without controversy, as various factors can contribute to a nation's prosperity over time. Austin (2008) acknowledges the attractiveness of this argument but also points out that historical analyses should be examined in light of long-term history to reveal recurring relationships due to path dependencies (Austin_2008: p.1).

Broadberry and Gardner (2022) argue that a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has resulted in Sub-Saharan Africa being absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. However, they emphasize the need to understand whether this growth provides a path to convergence and the potential risks of past economic shrinkage (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.18).

Koepke and Baten (2005) suggest that climate may have played an essential role in long-run economic history, with findings indicating minimal improvements in living standards during the Roman Empire despite economic growth (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.16). This complicates the argument that institutions are the sole cause of long-term economic growth by introducing environmental factors as possible contributors.

Goldin's (2006) work on labor market evolution and revolution brings attention to the distinction between the two, which pertains to three aspects of women's choices and decisions. By acknowledging the importance of studying labor in relation to institutions, Goldin (2006) adds nuance to the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth (Goldin_2006: p.1).

Braudel and Wallerstein (2009) argue that there has been a movement toward economic and social history at the expense of political history, which may complicate the argument by emphasizing the role of politics in long-term economic growth (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.7). Furthermore, they discuss how institutions can be influenced by various factors, including markets, which suggests that institutions might not always be the primary drivers of economic growth.

The works of Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) and Austin (2007), among others, provide nuanced perspectives on the relationship between institutions and long-term economic growth. While Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) emphasize the significant contribution of African women to economic growth, they also highlight how these contributions have often been overlooked due to their role as household producers (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.3). This underscores that understanding the societal structures and roles can provide a more comprehensive view of institutional impacts on economic growth.

Similarly, Austin (2007) discusses factors such as transportation technologies like railways and population growth influencing economic development in various contexts (Austin_2007: p.8, p.12). These findings suggest that while institutions play a crucial role, they may not be the sole determinants of long-run economic growth, as other factors like technological advancements and demographic changes can also significantly impact economic development.

Allen et al. (2011) provide additional insights by focusing on income disparities between China, Japan, and Europe during the early 20th century (AllenEtAl_2011: p.1). Their research demonstrates that structural changes in economies, such as industrialization in Japan and Europe, can lead to increased income disparities, which might indirectly impact institutions and long-term economic growth.

The relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth is a subject of extensive discussion among scholars, with some arguing that institutions are the fundamental cause of economic growth, while others suggest a more complex interplay between various factors.
