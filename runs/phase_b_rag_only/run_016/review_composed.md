The debate surrounding the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, particularly in the context of Africa, has been a subject of intense scholarly discussion. Institutions, as argued by Austin (2008), North & Weingast (1989), and Acemoglu et al. (2001), are posited as the primary drivers of economic growth over the long term. However, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) and Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) complicate this argument by suggesting that other factors, such as data availability and the role of women in economic growth, may also play significant roles.

Austin (2008) supports the institutional view, arguing that these trades, even the Atlantic one, reinforced rather than originated Africa’s relatively poor economic position compared to western Europe. North & Weingast (1989) focus on the political factors underpinning economic growth and the development of markets, showing how institutions played a necessary role in making possible economic growth and political freedom. Acemoglu et al. (2001) also support this stance, demonstrating that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress.

On the other hand, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate the argument by pointing out the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 in Sub-Saharan Africa, which has led to its absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) further nuance the discussion by highlighting the underrepresentation of women's economic contribution and the challenges in understanding the processes by which their labor is formally unaccounted for.

The stakes of this debate are significant, as understanding the fundamental causes of long-run economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting economic development. If institutions are indeed the primary driver, then policies that strengthen and reform these institutions could potentially lead to substantial economic growth. However, if other factors, such as data availability or the role of women in economic growth, play significant roles, then a more comprehensive approach may be necessary.

The question at the heart of this debate is: What are the fundamental causes of long-run economic growth, and how can we best address these factors to promote economic development? Institutions, as fundamental drivers of long-run economic growth, play a crucial role in shaping societies and economies (North&Weingast_1989). North and Weingast (1989) argue that the development of representative government, characterized by multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, is intimately related to economic growth and political freedom. This perspective highlights the importance of political institutions in fostering an environment conducive to market development and growth.

Furthermore, Mokyr (2005) supports this view by emphasizing the role of intellectual developments in shaping economic growth. He suggests that changes in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West can be explained through advancements in "useful knowledge" that promoted individualism, formal law, corporatism, self-governance, and rules determined through institutionalized processes (Mokyr_2005). These intellectual developments not only fostered economic growth but also enabled societies to become more progressive.

Similarly, North (1989) focuses on the role of institutions in reducing transaction costs and increasing security rights, which are essential for market development and economic growth. He argues that institutions can facilitate structural change, which is a critical factor correlated with long-term economic growth (North_1989).

Finally, Nunn and Wantchekon (2011) offer evidence supporting the link between institutions and economic growth. Their research shows that both internal and external institutional factors contribute to economic growth, but the internal channel is at least twice as significant as the external channel (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011). This finding underscores the importance of domestic institutions in driving long-term economic growth.

Together, these sources provide compelling evidence that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. They showcase how various aspects of institutional development, such as political representation, intellectual advancements, and internal governance structures, contribute to market development and economic expansion over time. Future research may focus on understanding the specific mechanisms through which these institutions drive growth and identifying strategies for fostering strong institutions in developing economies.

Institutions play a pivotal role in driving long-run economic growth, as evidenced by several studies. Acemoglu et al. (2001) demonstrate that more fundamental institutions, such as property rights and political power structures, significantly impact economic performance (p.19). They argue that these institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress in certain societies (p.27), suggesting a strong correlation between institutional quality and growth.

Furthermore, Acemoglu et al. (2002) assert that the distribution of economic prosperity has undergone a significant change over time, which they attribute to differences in institutions (p.6). They posit that understanding these institutions is crucial for both comprehending historical and contemporary development and evaluating theories of long-term growth (p.49).

Abramovitz (1986) supports this perspective, arguing that the elaboration of mixed economies is a function of economic growth itself (p.20). He emphasizes the importance of macroeconomic and monetary conditions that encourage capital investment and effective demand levels for fostering growth (p.7). Additionally, Abramovitz (1986) suggests that countries are currently adjusting their balance towards productivity growth (p.22), further highlighting the role of institutions in shaping economic trajectories over time.

The role of institutions in fostering long-run economic growth is further supported by evidence suggesting their impact on environmental conditions. Ogilvie (2007) notes that geographical characteristics and ecological factors have played a significant role in shaping economic development over the last 9,000 years (p.2). He also emphasizes the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework, arguing that the failure of one can potentially affect others (p.27). Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) support this notion by tracing the divergence in economic paths back to unique factor endowments and initial conditions, which have led to varying institutional structures (p.4, p.12).

Peters (2004) highlights the dynamic relationship between culture, social modes of organization, and economic growth. He argues that culture has its own autonomy and dynamics of development, which can influence the effectiveness of institutions in promoting long-term economic growth (p.16). These findings collectively suggest that institutions not only shape economic trajectories but also interact with environmental factors and cultural conditions to drive long-run growth.

Furthermore, the interdependency of individual institutions within a broader framework implies that the failure or success of one institution can have cascading effects on others. This underscores the importance of understanding the complex web of interactions between various institutions in shaping economic growth over time.

The argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth faces challenges when considering the role of agriculture, as highlighted by Clark & Jacks (2007) and Bryceson (2002).

Clark & Jacks (2007) critique the cliometric account of the coal industry during the Industrial Revolution, suggesting that technological advancements played a significant role in the industry's ability to respond to increases in demand. This challenge implies that institutions may not be solely responsible for long-term economic growth, as technological progress can also contribute substantially.

Bryceson (2002) further contests the thesis by examining livelihood diversification among rural communities. He demonstrates that women's economic and social spheres are influenced by traditional hierarchical orders within the community, with women primarily engaged in agrarian family work efforts. This observation suggests that institutions may not be the primary driver of economic growth, as the agricultural sector, and specifically gender roles within it, can significantly impact overall economic development.

The relationship between institutions and economic growth is a complex one, as suggested by various scholars. Komlos (1998) argues that while institutions play a role in economic growth, other factors such as structural changes and population growth may also contribute significantly. For instance, he highlights the discovery of 8-year height cycles during the Industrial Revolution, which implies that biological growth was influenced by short-term economic fluctuations (Komlos_1998: p.16). Similarly, Koepke & Baten (2005) complicate the argument by suggesting that climate and environmental factors could potentially impact human living standards and economic development, contradicting the common view that economic growth led to improved living conditions (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.16).

Hopkins (2019) further adds nuance by discussing how the study of African economic history has been influenced by Western perspectives, potentially limiting the diversity of independent African viewpoints and thus our understanding of long-run economic and political change (Hopkins_2019: p.4). Goldin (2006) also points out that the study of labor was once focused on institutions within the labor market, but she emphasizes the importance of considering women's choices and decisions in this context (Goldin_2006: p.3).

These findings suggest that while institutions may play a role in long-run economic growth, they are not the sole factor. Other factors such as structural changes, environmental conditions, climate, and cultural norms must also be taken into account to fully understand the complex interplay of forces driving economic development over time.

However, he also points out that not all capital-deepening and TFP-improving forces of modern economic growth were present in the context of Ghana during this period (Austin_2007: p.16).

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) bring attention to the role of women in economic growth, particularly in Africa. They argue that understanding the historical processes by which women's contributions have been subsumed under household production is crucial for policies aiming to strengthen women's economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). Furthermore, they suggest that while the major developments in West Africa have unique characteristics, parallels can be found elsewhere in Africa regarding gender and economic growth (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.4).

Allen et al. (2011) discuss income disparities during the early 20th century between China, Japan, and Europe. They highlight that the difference in labor costs between China and Europe was significant, but they also point out that this gap narrowed over time due to industrialization in Japan and Europe (AllenEtAl_2011: p.1).

These sources add nuance to the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. They suggest that other factors, such as wages, transportation infrastructure, gender roles, and income disparities, also play significant roles in shaping economic development over time. Therefore, a comprehensive understanding of economic growth requires considering these additional factors alongside institutional structures.

They also highlight that understanding the extent to which there are still risks of economic shrinkage is crucial for determining whether growth in certain regions provides a path to convergence (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.1, p.18). This suggests that while institutions play a significant role in shaping economic development, other factors such as data availability and risks of economic shrinkage may also influence long-run economic growth.

Bolt & van Zanden (2014) provide insights into patterns of growth and development over centuries by discussing numerous revisions of nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth in various countries. They mention that these revisions do not fundamentally change our view, but they still contribute to a better understanding of economic growth (Bolt & van Zanden_2014: p.5). This implies that while institutional structures are important, ongoing research and data revisions may lead to more accurate assessments of long-run economic growth.

Lastly, Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) emphasize the disagreement among scholars regarding the primary driver of economic growth, with some focusing on institutions while others prioritize other factors such as political history and market dynamics (Braudel & Wallerstein_2009: p.30). This disagreement underscores the complexity of understanding long-run economic growth and highlights that various factors may play significant roles in shaping economic development over time.

The examination of institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth finds support in various scholarly works, albeit with some nuances and tangential discussions. Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) both acknowledge the significance of institutions but do not explicitly focus on their role as a primary driver of economic growth.

Domar (1970) delves into the factors of production, identifying labor tied to land or owners as hindering competition among employers, thereby stunting economic development (p.4). This suggests that institutional arrangements can impact economic growth by influencing market dynamics. On the other hand, Bohannan (1959) discusses the Western impact on economic institutions in Africa but does not specifically link these changes to long-term economic growth (p.10). However, he notes the potential limitations of women's numbers and the importance of kinship networks before European contact, which could indirectly influence institutional structures and thus economic growth over time.

Ravallion's (2001) work is less directly related to the primary topic but does provide insights into development economics, using mathematical models and equations that could potentially be applied to understanding long-run economic growth when interpreted within a broader context (pp.4, 10, 12).

Despite these contributions, the question of whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth remains unresolved. While scholars such as Domar and Bohannan suggest that institutions can play a crucial role in shaping economic development, further research is needed to establish a definitive causal relationship between institutions and long-term economic growth. Future studies should aim to develop more robust theoretical frameworks and empirical evidence to support or challenge the notion that institutions are the primary drivers of long-run economic growth.
