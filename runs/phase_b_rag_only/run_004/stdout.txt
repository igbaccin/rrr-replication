LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

Long-run economic growth is a topic of significant interest and debate among economists, with institutions often cited as a fundamental cause. This discussion revolves around the role of institutions in shaping long-term economic growth, drawing from various scholarly works such as Austin_2008, Kuznets_1973, Broadberry&Gardner_2022, Akyeampong&Fofack_2014, North&Weingast_1989, and AcemogluEtAl_2001.

The central question at hand is whether institutions are indeed the primary driver of long-run economic growth, or if other factors play an equally significant role. The stakes are high, as understanding this relationship can provide valuable insights into economic development strategies for various regions, particularly those experiencing slow growth.

Austin_2008 and North&Weingast_1989 support the notion that institutions significantly influence long-run economic growth. Austin argues that institutions "as ‘the’ fundamental cause of long-run economic growth" (Austin_2008: p.26), while North and Weingast demonstrate how "representative government...are intimately related to the struggle for constitutional economic change" (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

However, not all scholars are in agreement. Kuznets_1973 highlights that while structural changes correlate with growth, there are also unexpected negative results associated with modern economic growth (Kuznets_1973: p.11). Similarly, Broadberry&Gardner_2022 complicates the argument by pointing out the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 in Sub-Saharan Africa, which has been absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1).

Akyeampong&Fofack_2014 also adds nuance to the discussion by focusing on the contribution of African women to economic growth and development, arguing that understanding the processes by which women's labor is subsumed under household production is crucial for policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3).

The debate continues as scholars grapple with the complex interplay between institutions, structural changes, and other factors in shaping long-run economic growth. The implications of this research extend beyond academia, influencing policy decisions that aim to foster economic development and improve living standards worldwide.

The impact of institutions on long-run economic growth is further substantiated by the findings of North & Weingast (1989) and Mokyr (2005). North & Weingast (1989: p.2) emphasize that political factors, such as representative government with multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are crucial in fostering economic growth and market development. These institutions not only enable economic growth but also contribute to political freedom (North & Weingast_1989: p.30).

Mokyr (Mokyr_2005: p.4) suggests that developments in the intellectual realm, particularly in the realm of "useful knowledge," were key drivers of economic growth in the West. He highlights elements such as individualism, formal law, and self-governance as significant factors contributing to a progressive society and economic prosperity (Mokyr_2005: p.56).

These sources underscore the fundamental role institutions play in shaping long-run economic growth, providing evidence that supports the notion that they are indeed a primary cause of such growth. The influence of these findings extends beyond academic circles, as they inform policy decisions aimed at fostering economic development and improving living standards worldwide.

The works of Acemoglu and colleagues substantiate the crucial role of institutions in shaping long-run economic growth. In their study published in 2001, they found that specific institutions have a significant impact on property rights and investment, thereby fostering or hindering economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27, p.3). Moreover, the results from Table 4 of their research reveal an extensive effect of institutions on overall economic performance (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19).

Acemoglu and colleagues' 2002 publication further emphasizes this point, stating that understanding long-run development necessitates considering the influence of institutions (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49). They also note a shift in economic prosperity distribution, suggesting a potential link to institutional changes (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.6). The researchers question if this reversal could be related to institutions (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5), indicating the significant role they play in economic development.

Additionally, Abramovitz's 1986 work supports the thesis by highlighting that macroeconomic and monetary conditions encouraging capital investment and effective demand are essential for economic growth (Abramovitz_1986: p.7). He also suggests that the evolution of mixed economies is a function of economic growth itself, further underscoring the importance of institutions in fostering long-term prosperity (Abramovitz_1986: p.20).

The evolution of economic institutions plays a crucial role in fostering long-term prosperity, as suggested by Abramovitz (1986). This concept is further supported by Ogilvie (2007), who notes that the efficiency theory's Achilles heel lies in the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework (Ogilvie_2007: p.27).

In his work, Ogilvie discusses the theories of Diamond and Pomeranz, both of whom attribute economic development to geographical characteristics or factors such as coal deposits and disease ecology (Ogilvie_2007: p.2). However, he emphasizes that these theories do not fully account for the resilience of certain institutions that may resist change, potentially leading to failure. This underlines the importance of considering the broader institutional framework when examining economic growth.

Similarly, Pamuk (2007) highlights the impact of the Black Death on modern economic growth, suggesting that it led to significant changes in wages and incomes during the last millennium (Pamuk_2007: p.4). These changes paved the way for the commercialization of agriculture, further underscoring the role of institutions in shaping economic structures and growth.

Peters (2004) also supports this argument, stating that the aims of land policy reform have long been to promote economic growth, sustainable management of land, and poverty reduction (Peters_2004: p.7). He insists on the autonomy and dynamic development of culture, suggesting that understanding different social modes of organization and cultural modes of understanding is essential for grasping the dynamics of social transformation and economic growth.

Structural change, rather than institutions, may be a significant factor contributing to long-run economic growth. McCloskey (2015) argues that in France and Spain, economic growth was hindered due to conservative ideologies and religious preferences, despite the examples of economically successful nations like Holland and Britain (McCloskey_2015: p.9). Humphries (2013) supports this notion by emphasizing the role of a growing division of labor, workshop production, and the emergence of the factory system in eighteenth-century economic growth (Humphries_2013: p.18). Additionally, lower fertility rates in France compared to Britain likely reduced the burden of dependency and facilitated economic growth (Humphries_2013: p.19). These findings challenge the assertion that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth.

The assertion that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth becomes more nuanced when considering the role of women in economic activities, as highlighted by Akyeampong & Fofack (2014). Their research underscores the importance of understanding how women's economic contributions have often been subsumed under household production and their labor formally unaccounted for. This oversight can undermine policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles.

Moreover, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) emphasize that the contribution of African women to economic growth and development was largely dependent on the economic provision of their husbands. However, they also note that these findings have parallels elsewhere in Africa where gender and economic growth are concerned, suggesting a broader applicability of this observation.

Allen (2001) contributes to the discussion by focusing on wages and prices as central concerns in economic development. While the author acknowledges that illness, injury, bad weather, and economic fluctuations prevented work on every permissible day, they also suggest that there was no long-run trend in their incidence. This finding implies that economic growth may not always be directly linked to reduced incidences of these factors, thereby complicating the relationship between institutions and economic growth.

Austin (2007) provides insights into the advantages of railways over lorries until the mid-1920s and the population growth trends in Ghana. While the railway had the fundamental advantage of being much cheaper, few of the capital-deepening and TFP-improving forces of modern economic growth were at work (Austin_2007: p.16). This suggests that the presence of institutions alone may not be sufficient for economic growth, as other factors such as technological advancements also play a crucial role.

Allen et al. (2011) further complicate the relationship between institutions and economic growth by demonstrating income disparities in the early 20th century due to long-run stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe. They also highlight that rapid growth of the German economy was translating into rising real wages for workers, indicating a correlation between institutional development and wage growth.

The relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth is further nuanced when considering industrialization and technological advancements, as highlighted by various sources.

Koepke & Baten (2005) argue that climate may have played a significant role in human living standards throughout history, suggesting that even during periods of economic growth, such as the Roman Empire, stature and nutrition did not necessarily improve (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.16). This complicates the argument that institutional development is the sole cause of long-run economic growth by introducing an environmental factor that could potentially influence living standards.

Frankema (2012) challenges the notion that colonial education was a primary driver of long-run growth disparities in Africa, instead emphasizing the role of political and economic contexts in shaping British "liberalism" (Frankema_2012: p.19). This suggests that while institutions are important, they may not always be the primary determinant of economic growth, as other factors such as geography and colonial policies could play a significant role.

Hopkins (2019) points out that the study of long-run economic and political change in Africa has been influenced by various ideologies, including postmodernism, which may have limited the diversity of independent African viewpoints (Hopkins_2019: p.4). This raises questions about the validity of generalizations made about the impact of institutions on economic growth in Africa, as these may be shaped by broader intellectual and political trends.

Goldin (2006) discusses the evolution and revolution of women's roles in the labor market, suggesting that the distinction between the two is specific to certain aspects of women’s choices and decisions (Goldin_2006: p.1). This complicates the argument that institutional development leads to long-run economic growth by introducing gender as a factor that could potentially influence economic outcomes.

The works of Austin (2008), Komlos (1998), and Lindert & Williamson (1985) add nuance to the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by introducing various complicating factors.

Austin (Austin_2008: p.26) questions whether institutions were the originators or reinforcers of Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe, suggesting a more complex relationship between institutions and economic development. Similarly, Austin (Austin_2008: p.1) advocates for examining growth theories against long-term history, revealing relationships that recur due to similar situations or path dependencies.

Komlos (Komlos_1998: p.16) finds evidence of 8-year height cycles during the Industrial Revolution, implying that biological growth processes were significantly influenced by short-term economic fluctuations. This suggests that while institutions may play a role in long-run economic growth, other factors such as short-term economic cycles could also have significant impacts on growth outcomes.

Lindert & Williamson (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.9) argue that the task of explaining long-run distribution trends rejoins the search for explanations of growth itself, suggesting that the relationship between inequality and growth is more complex than a simple causal direction from institutions to economic growth. Furthermore, they suggest that policymakers have traditionally focused on balancing economic growth and equality, indicating that trade-offs between these two objectives should be considered when examining the role of institutions in long-run economic growth (Lindert & Williamson 1985: p.1).

These findings complicate the argument that institutional development leads to long-run economic growth by introducing factors such as path dependencies, short-term economic cycles, and trade-offs between inequality and growth. Future research should consider these nuances in order to gain a more complete understanding of the role of institutions in long-run economic growth.

The examination of the role of institutions in long-run economic growth, as presented by various scholars, reveals both convergence and divergence in their perspectives. On one hand, Bohannan (1959) and Ravallion (2001), albeit discussing different contexts, acknowledge the impact of external factors on the formation of institutions. Bohannan highlights the influence of Western impact on economic institutions (Bohannan_1959: p.10), while Ravallion touches upon the significance of economic policies and institutions in development (Ravallion_2001: p.4).

On the other hand, both authors seem to diverge in their focus. Bohannan's work primarily focuses on social structures and cultural norms within a specific community (Bohannan_1959: p.7), whereas Ravallion delves into economic policies, poverty reduction strategies, and income distribution (Ravallion_2001: p.10).

Despite these differences, both scholars agree that institutions play a crucial role in shaping long-run economic growth. However, what remains unresolved is the extent to which specific institutions contribute to economic growth and how they interact with other factors such as technological advancements, political stability, and cultural norms. Future research should aim to address these nuances to gain a more comprehensive understanding of the role of institutions in long-run economic growth.
