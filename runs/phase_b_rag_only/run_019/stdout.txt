The central question in the literature review on institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is whether institutional factors play a decisive role in shaping economic trajectories over extended periods. This question carries significant implications for understanding historical and contemporary development patterns, particularly in regions like Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA), where growth has been sluggish compared to other parts of the world.

Supporting this thesis, Austin (2008) argues that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. He contends that these institutions reinforce economic positions, as seen in the case of Africa's relatively poor economic standing compared to Western Europe and parts of Asia. Furthermore, Austin emphasizes the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history to reveal recurring relationships and path dependencies.

Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2001) posit that institutions have a profound impact on economic progress. They suggest that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic development in various regions.

However, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate this argument by pointing out the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 in SSA, which has led to its absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. They also note that some economists have suggested using alternative data to track African economic growth in recent decades.

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) further add nuance by discussing the underrepresentation of women's economic contributions and the need to understand how their labor is often subsumed under household production. They argue that this understanding is crucial for policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles.

Komlos (1998), in his discussion on modern economic growth, complicates the argument by emphasizing the importance of considering physical stature during the Industrial Revolution and the influence of short-term fluctuations in economic processes on biological growth.

The stakes of this debate are high, as understanding the role of institutions in long-run economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting development and reducing poverty in various regions. The literature suggests that while institutions play a crucial role, other factors such as data availability and women's contributions to the economy also need to be considered. The argument continues as researchers explore these complexities further.

The relationship between institutions and technological advancements in fostering long-run economic growth is further substantiated by the works of various scholars. Kuznets (1973) posits that rapid changes in economic structure, often associated with technological innovations, are major factors affecting economic and social structure (Kuznets_1973: p.4). North & Weingast (1989) emphasize the role of political institutions in enabling economic growth and market development (North&Weingast_1989: p.2).

Mokyr (2005) suggests that the change in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West can be attributed to developments in intellectual realms concerning "useful knowledge" (Mokyr_2005: p.4). North (1989) argues that centralized enforcement mechanisms, such as those found in Spain, hindered long-term economic growth (North_1989: p.1).

Ogilvie (2007) highlights the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework and the potential for endogenous economic analysis to bring institutions into the mainstream of economic thought (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) explore how initial conditions, or factor endowments broadly conceived, could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4).

These findings collectively support the assertion that institutions play a crucial role in promoting long-term economic growth by facilitating technological advancements and fostering an environment conducive to sustainable development. The literature suggests that while institutions are indeed fundamental, understanding their complex interplay with other factors such as data availability, women's contributions to the economy, and geographical characteristics is essential for a comprehensive analysis of long-run economic growth.

The role of institutions in fostering long-run economic growth is further underscored by the works of Austin (2008) and Acemoglu et al. (2001). Austin (2008) emphasizes the fundamental nature of institutions as a cause of long-term economic growth, suggesting that they not only originated but also reinforced Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe. This perspective aligns with the thesis, underscoring the importance of institutions in shaping economic trajectories over extended periods.

Acemoglu et al. (2001) provide additional support for this view, arguing that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress. They propose that differences in mortality rates are not the primary cause of institutional variation but rather that these institutions persist due to various economic mechanisms. This persistence, in turn, impacts long-term economic growth by either promoting or hindering capital investment, a critical factor for sustained development (Acemoglu et al., 2001: p.27).

Understanding the complex interplay between institutions and capital investment is essential for a comprehensive analysis of long-run economic growth. The works of Austin (2008) and Acemoglu et al. (2001) demonstrate that institutions play a fundamental role in shaping this relationship, either by originating or reinforcing economic positions over time. Future research could delve deeper into the specific mechanisms through which institutions influence capital investment and long-term economic growth.

Institutions play a crucial role in shaping long-run economic growth by influencing capital investment, as demonstrated by Abramovitz (1986). The evolution of mixed economies is linked to economic growth itself, suggesting that institutions develop in response to growing economies rather than being the primary cause (Abramovitz_1986: p.20).

Moreover, Abramovitz (1986) posits that the elaboration of these mixed economies has a generalized effect on productivity growth among leading nations. The author argues that profits and the incentives to invest and innovate are reduced under institutional systems that do not align with economic growth (Abramovitz_1986: p.16).

In essence, Abramovitz's work supports the assertion that institutions are fundamental in long-run economic growth by influencing capital investment. The author suggests a reciprocal relationship between institutions and economic growth, where the evolution of institutions is partly driven by the growth of the economy. However, further exploration is required to understand the specific mechanisms through which these interactions occur.

The assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth faces challenges when considering technological advancements, as suggested by various scholars. For instance, McCloskey (2015) critiques this notion by highlighting instances where economic growth was throttled due to conservative ideologies and lack of interest in economic prosperity, despite the examples of economically successful nations (McCloskey_2015: p.9).

Similarly, Bryceson (2002) challenges this perspective by demonstrating that traditional hierarchical orders within communities can influence women's economic and social spheres, often limiting their involvement in non-agrarian activities (Bryceson_2002: p.9). These findings suggest that technological advancements may be driven by societal factors beyond institutions alone.

Furthermore, Humphries (2013) contends that economic growth in the eighteenth century was underpinned by a growing division of labor, the rise of workshop production, and the eventual emergence of the factory system (Humphries_2013: p.18). This evidence implies that technological advancements may have played a significant role in driving economic growth independent of institutions.

However, it is essential to recognize that these observations do not discount the influence of institutions on economic growth. Rather, they emphasize the complexity of their interaction and the need for further exploration of the specific mechanisms through which technological advancements and institutions intertwine to shape long-term economic development.

The sources provided complicate the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by introducing nuances and providing evidence of complex relationships between technological advancements, institutions, and economic development.

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) emphasize the lack of data on aggregate economic performance in Sub-Saharan Africa before 1950, suggesting that the region has been absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. Furthermore, they note that some economists have suggested the use of other data to track African economic growth in recent decades (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.10). This implies that the role of institutions in shaping economic growth may vary across different regions and periods, with Sub-Saharan Africa potentially requiring a unique approach.

Komlos (1998) discusses the relationship between physical stature and economic growth during the Industrial Revolution, finding that living standards and purchasing power increased, but heights did not (Komlos_1998: p.16). This suggests that while institutions might have played a role in driving technological advancements, other factors such as nutrition and health may also have contributed to long-run economic growth.

Temin (2002) argues that the exhaustion of cheap labor caused economic growth to slow, implying that technological advancements may not always be the primary driver of economic growth. Additionally, he suggests that recent theories of economic growth should consider the role of economic transition, which could involve changes in institutions and their interactions with technological advancements (Temin_2002: p.7).

Koepke & Baten (2005) explore the relationship between climate and human living standards, finding that over the period of the Roman Empire, heights did not increase, despite common views suggesting economic growth and increased purchasing power during this time (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.16). This evidence complicates the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by highlighting the potential role of environmental factors in shaping economic development.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) discuss the relationship between inequality and economic growth, finding that Japan's economic history supports the notion of imbalance and inequality (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.14). This implies that institutions might not always promote equal distribution of resources, which could impact long-run economic growth.

Hopkins (2019) discusses the limitations on the diversity of independent African viewpoints in studying long-run economic and political change, suggesting that postmodernism may have removed valuable perspectives from the field (Hopkins_2019: p.4). This could complicate the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by emphasizing the importance of diverse perspectives in understanding complex historical processes.

The work of Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) sheds light on the complexities surrounding institutions, gender roles, and long-run economic growth. Their study underscores the importance of recognizing women's contributions to the economy, a factor that has often been overlooked due to their labor being categorized as household production (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). This oversight could potentially undermine policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles.

Moreover, Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) highlight that the historical developments in West Africa regarding gender and economic growth have parallels elsewhere in Africa (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.4). This suggests that the role of institutions in shaping gender roles and long-term economic growth may be a pan-African phenomenon, adding nuance to the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

The authors also emphasize the significant economic contributions made by African women (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.19). This underscores the importance of institutions that facilitate and recognize these contributions, further complicating the argument that institutions are solely responsible for long-run economic growth.

The argument that institutions are the primary driver of long-run economic growth becomes more nuanced when considering capital investment, as demonstrated by Austin (2007). Austin's research reveals that until the mid-1920s, railways had a significant cost advantage over trucks, which could be attributed to institutional factors supporting infrastructure development (Austin_2007: p.8). However, this advantage did not persist as other modes of transportation emerged and gained popularity, suggesting that institutions may not always maintain their influence over technological advancements in the long run.

Furthermore, Austin's work underscores the importance of population growth in relation to capital investment and economic development. He notes that while population growth was faster after 1918, few of the capital-deepening and TFP-improving forces of modern economic growth were at work (Austin_2007: p.16). This implies that institutions may not solely be responsible for facilitating these crucial factors for long-run economic growth.

In addition, Austin highlights that the population figures used in his research have been revised to account for more accurate population growth rates between 1931 and 1960 (Austin_2007: p.12). This revision underscores the complexity of measuring economic growth accurately and suggests that institutions may not always be able to provide reliable data for long-term analysis.

The literature review on the role of institutions in long-term economic growth reveals a consensus among scholars that institutions play a significant role in shaping economic development. However, there is a divergence in the specific factors and mechanisms through which this influence occurs.

Domar (1970) emphasizes the role of institutions in regulating competition among employers, arguing that when labor is tied to land or owners, competition ceases, leading to stagnant growth (p.4). On the other hand, Bohannan (1959) focuses on the impact of Western economic institutions, particularly those related to slave dealing and status acquisition, on African societies (p.7, p.10).

Ravallion (2001), while not directly addressing institutions, provides mathematical models that suggest a correlation between structural change and economic growth (p.4). However, the complexity of these equations makes it challenging to draw straightforward conclusions about the role of institutions.

Despite these differences, all three scholars imply that institutions can either promote or hinder economic growth, depending on their nature and how they are implemented. What remains unresolved is a clear understanding of the specific institutional factors that drive long-term economic growth and how these factors can be effectively implemented in various contexts.

Further research is needed to identify the key institutional elements that contribute to sustained economic growth, taking into account the unique characteristics of different societies and historical periods. This would provide a more nuanced understanding of the complex relationship between institutions and long-term economic development.
