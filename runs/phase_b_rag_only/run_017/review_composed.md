The question of whether institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth has been a subject of intense debate among scholars, with various perspectives presented in the literature review. The central question at hand is whether the institutional framework significantly impacts a nation's ability to achieve sustainable and consistent economic growth over extended periods.

The proponents of this viewpoint argue that well-designed institutions play a crucial role in fostering economic development by providing an environment conducive to investment, innovation, and trade (North & Weingast_1989: p.2; Acemoglu et al._2001: p.1). For instance, the authors North and Weingast (1989) assert that the development of representative government with multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs is intimately related to economic growth and political freedom. Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2001) demonstrate that institutions determine the distribution of property rights, which in turn affects investment and economic progress.

On the other hand, some scholars complicate this argument by emphasizing that factors such as population growth, geography, and historical events also play significant roles in shaping economic outcomes (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.1; Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.3). For example, Broadberry and Gardner (2022) argue that the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has made it difficult to assess Sub-Saharan Africa's role in debates about comparative long-run growth. Meanwhile, Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) highlight the importance of understanding how women's economic contributions have been subsumed under household production, which undermines policies aimed at strengthening their economic roles.

Trade between Africa and Western Europe, a topic extensively explored by Austin (2008), sheds light on the role of institutions in long-term economic growth. According to Austin, these trades reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe (Austin_2008: p.24). This observation aligns with the thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, as the trade patterns established during this period were shaped by the institutional structures in place.

Furthermore, Austin argues that these trades were not the catalyst for structural change in Africa's economy, but rather a reflection of existing institutional arrangements (Austin_2008: p.24). This suggests that the long-term economic growth in Western Europe was not primarily driven by trade with Africa, but rather by their own institutional structures that facilitated and benefited from this trade.

Moreover, Austin emphasizes the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history to reveal relationships that recur due to similar situations and path dependencies (Austin_2008: p.1). This approach underscores the significance of institutions in shaping economic trajectories over time, providing further support for the thesis.

The rapid changes in economic structure, as observed over time, further underscore the crucial role of institutions in fostering long-term growth. As Kuznets (Kuznets_1973: p.4) notes, these structural shifts are not only linked to high aggregate growth rates but also bring about significant transformations in various aspects of society, such as family structures and urbanization patterns.

Moreover, Kuznets (Kuznets_1973: p.6) highlights that the succession of technological innovations during modern economic growth and the social innovations necessary for adaptation are major factors influencing both economic and social structure. This suggests that institutions play a key role in facilitating these transformative processes by providing the necessary framework for innovation and societal adjustment.

Kuznets (Kuznets_1973: p.11) further emphasizes this point when discussing nonconventional costs associated with economic growth, indicating that even developed countries experience negative consequences alongside positive achievements during periods of growth, while less developed countries struggle to utilize modern technology effectively. This underscores the idea that institutions can either facilitate or hinder the process of structural change and, consequently, long-term economic growth.

The internal channel of institutions, as North and Weingast (1989) argue, plays a pivotal role in fostering long-run economic growth. They emphasize that representative government, characterized by multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, is crucial for economic development and the establishment of markets.

North and Weingast (1989) highlight how these institutions enable a balance between competing interests, thereby reducing rent-seeking activities that can hinder growth. The presence of multiple veto points in representative government ensures that laws are fair and stable, encouraging long-term investment and structural change. The primacy of common law courts over economic affairs further supports this argument by providing an impartial and efficient system for resolving disputes related to property rights and contracts.

Moreover, North and Weingast (1989) demonstrate that these institutions facilitate the development of markets by promoting trust among individuals, which is essential for trade and cooperation. The stability provided by representative government and the rule of law reduces uncertainty and allows for the emergence of complex economic institutions, such as banks and corporations.

Industrial Development, Educational Expansion, and Military Autonomy are factors that challenge the notion of institutions being the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth. According to Simson (2019), these elements were prioritized in several African economies during periods of high growth in the 1960s and 1970s, suggesting that they might contribute significantly to economic development (Simson_2019: p.25).

Moreover, Simson's research indicates an emphasis on expanding public employment rather than protecting those already in government jobs, casting doubt on the importance of representative government and rule of law for fostering long-term economic growth (Simson_2019: p.4). The author also questions whether eliminating spending inefficiencies, leakages, and delays in reform would have been sufficient to sustain investment levels during those periods (Simson_2019: p.4).

Furthermore, Simson's work highlights that military autonomy could play a role in economic growth, challenging the idea that stable governments are essential for long-term prosperity (Simson_2019: p.27). This evidence implies that industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy may provide alternative paths to economic growth, potentially diminishing the importance of institutions in this process.

The lack of substantial data on aggregate economic performance in Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA) before 1950 poses challenges to the comprehensive understanding of the region's long-run growth and its relationship with institutions (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). This data deficiency has resulted in SSA being overlooked in recent debates about comparative economic growth, as Collier and Gunning (1999) and Sachs and Warner (1997) both acknowledge the region's historically poor performance (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1).

However, new contributions to historical national accounting have begun to shed light on patterns of growth and development in other regions over centuries, potentially redefining our understanding of economic divergence and convergence since before the industrial revolution (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). The absence of such data for SSA means that the role of institutions as a fundamental cause of long-run economic growth may be less definitive in this context, as other factors such as structural change could potentially play a more significant role.

The potential correlation between structural change and economic growth, while not explicitly stated in relation to SSA, is suggested by Broadberry & Gardner's (2022) discussion of patterns of growth and development in other regions (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.18). This implies that industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy may provide alternative paths to economic growth in SSA, potentially diminishing the importance of institutions in this process.

The contributions of African women to economic growth and development, as highlighted by Akyeampong & Fofack (2014), offer nuances that enrich our understanding of the role of institutions in long-run economic growth in Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA). While industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy may provide alternative paths to economic growth in SSA (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.18), the historical subsumption of women's economic contributions under household production and the formal unaccounting of their labor (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3) potentially complicates the significance of institutions in this process.

The structural change correlating with growth in SSA may be influenced by the overlooked role of African women in economic activities. Their contributions were largely dependent on the economic provision of their husbands (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.9), which could have implications for the development and institutionalization of economic structures. Understanding these historical patterns may help reshape our perspective on the role of institutions in long-run economic growth in SSA.

Moreover, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) note that major historical developments in West Africa and the themes discussed find parallels elsewhere in Africa where gender and economic growth are concerned (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.4). This suggests that the impact of women's contributions on institutional development and long-run economic growth may be a pan-African phenomenon, necessitating a broader examination of this relationship across various regions in SSA.

Structural changes, a key aspect of modern economic growth, are significantly linked to long-run economic expansion, as evidenced by Komlos (1998). The author delves into the Kuznetsian sense of modern economic growth, which encompasses population growth, urbanization, income inequality, and income variability (Komlos_1998: p.2). These structural shifts are not only associated with the Industrial Revolution but also display cyclical patterns that mirror classical business cycles (Komlos_1998: p.16).

Moreover, Komlos's work suggests that these structural changes may have profound implications for long-term economic growth. For instance, the rapid population growth and urbanization he discusses could potentially foster institutional development over time, contributing to sustainable economic expansion (Komlos_1998: p.17).

However, it is important to note that Komlos does not explicitly focus on the role of institutions in this process. Future research could build upon his findings by exploring how these structural changes influence the development and evolution of institutions, thereby shedding light on their impact on long-run economic growth across various regions in Sub-Saharan Africa.

The scholarship on the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth reveals a convergence in recognizing their fundamental role, yet diverges in understanding the specific mechanisms driving this connection. Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) both emphasize the importance of institutions in facilitating structural change, which in turn influences economic growth.

Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) posits that with labor tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, hindering economic development. In contrast, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.10) argues that the Western impact on African societies not only affected economic institutions but also social and political ones.

While both authors acknowledge the significance of institutional change, they differ in their focus areas. Domar's work leans towards the economic aspects, while Bohannan delves into the cultural and societal dimensions. This divergence highlights the multifaceted nature of institutions and the need for comprehensive research to fully understand their impact on long-run economic growth.

Ravallion (2001) provides mathematical models related to economic development, but his work does not directly address the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth, leaving some aspects unresolved. The literature review suggests that further investigation is necessary to bridge the gap between theories on institutional change and empirical evidence of their impact on economic growth across various regions in Sub-Saharan Africa.
