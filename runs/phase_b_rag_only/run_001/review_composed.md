The central question under investigation in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth. This question holds significant importance as understanding the driving forces behind economic growth can provide valuable insights for policymakers and economists seeking to promote sustainable development.

The thesis that institutions are the primary drivers of long-run economic growth finds strong support from several sources. For instance, North & Weingast (1989) argue that representative government institutions, characterized by multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, play a crucial role in promoting economic growth and political freedom. Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2001) demonstrate that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress, thereby impeding long-term growth.

However, not all sources support this thesis without qualification. Austin (2008), for example, complicates the argument by suggesting that while institutions are important, they may reinforce rather than originate certain economic positions, such as Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) also add nuance to the debate by noting that a lack of data on pre-1950 economic performance in Sub-Saharan Africa has led to its absence from recent growth debates.

Kuznets (1973) supports the thesis by highlighting the role of technological innovations and social adjustments in driving modern economic growth. However, he also acknowledges unexpected negative results associated with modern economic growth, particularly in less developed countries.

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) further complicate the issue by discussing the underrepresentation of women's economic contributions and the impact of gender roles on economic growth and development.

The relationship between economic institutions and long-run growth is further substantiated by the works of various scholars. Kuznets (1973) posits that economic growth, particularly in modern times, is associated with significant changes in societal structures, including family dynamics, urbanization, and the formation of institutions (Kuznets_1973: p.4). He also emphasizes the role of technological innovations in driving economic growth, which necessitate social adjustments to accommodate them effectively (Kuznets_1973: p.6).

North & Weingast (1989) focus on political factors that underpin economic growth and market development, not just the rules governing economic exchange but also the institutions responsible for enforcing and changing these rules (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). They argue that representative government, with multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, plays a crucial role in fostering economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

Mokyr (2005) suggests that the change in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West can be attributed to developments in the intellectual realm concerning "useful knowledge." He highlights the importance of individualism, man-made formal law, corporatism, self-governance, and rules determined through an institutionalized process in promoting economic progress (Mokyr_2005: p.56).

These scholars' findings collectively support the assertion that institutions are indeed fundamental causes of long-run economic growth. They demonstrate that societal structures, political systems, and intellectual developments all contribute to this relationship, emphasizing the multifaceted nature of institutional impact on economic development. Further exploration into how these factors interact and evolve over time could provide valuable insights into the dynamics of economic growth.

The relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth is further substantiated by various scholarly works. Acemoglu, Johnson, and Robinson (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27) highlight the significant impact of fundamental institutions on property rights and economic development, suggesting that these institutions can either foster or hinder investment and progress. Moreover, they argue that differences in mortality rates are not the primary cause of institutional variation (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3).

In a similar vein, Acemoglu, Johnson, Robinson, and Verdier (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49) assert that institutions play a crucial role in understanding economic and political development over the past 500 years. They also notice a change in the distribution of economic prosperity, which they attribute to institutional factors (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.6).

Abramovitz (Abramovitz_1986: p.20) supports this perspective by suggesting that the elaboration of mixed economies is a function of economic growth itself. Additionally, he emphasizes the importance of macroeconomic and monetary conditions in encouraging capital investment and sustaining effective demand (Abramovitz_1986: p.7).

Allen (2001: p.1) also underscores the significance of wages and prices in understanding economic development, leadership, and standard of living. His research indicates that structural change correlates with growth, providing further evidence for the crucial role of institutions in long-run economic prosperity.

The transformation in economic structure, as highlighted by Pamuk (2007), provides compelling evidence supporting the fundamental role of institutions in long-run economic growth. According to his research, the Black Death and the onset of modern economic growth were the two most significant events influencing wages and income during the last millennium (Pamuk 2007: p.4). This structural change paved the way for the growing commercialization of agriculture (Pamuk 2007: p.24), indicating that institutions played a crucial part in shaping economic development.

Moreover, Pamuk's study reveals that Europe experienced a population decline of nearly one-third until the end of the fourteenth century, following the Black Death (Pamuk 2007: p.5). This demographic shift likely contributed to the structural changes observed during this period, further underscoring the importance of institutions in shaping long-term economic prosperity.

Critics argue that institutions may not be the sole driver of long-term economic growth, as other factors such as ideologies and cultural attitudes can also play significant roles. For instance, McCloskey (2015) contends that in France and Spain, economic growth was throttled due to conservative ideologies that prioritized Catholicism over economic expansion (McCloskey_2015: p.9). Similarly, Simson (2019) suggests that state expenditure and deficits expanded during periods of high economic growth in Africa, challenging the notion that strong institutions are a prerequisite for economic prosperity (Simson_2019: p.25).

Moreover, van Zanden (1999) argues that the consensus view in the 1960s and 1970s was that the early modern economy was unable to generate economic growth, implying that factors other than institutions might have been more influential during this period (vanZanden_1999: p.1). Humphries (2013) asserts that the division of labor, workshop production, and the emergence of the factory system were key drivers of eighteenth-century economic growth, potentially undermining the importance of institutions in this context (Humphries_2013: p.18). These perspectives challenge the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

The assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is not without its complications, as several scholars have argued for factors that may influence or interact with institutional development to shape economic trajectories. For instance, Temin (2002) posits that the exhaustion of cheap labor could have contributed to the slowing of economic growth in Europe during the eighteenth century, potentially challenging the primacy of institutions in this context (Temin_2914: p.4). Similarly, Lindert & Williamson (1985) suggest a potential interplay between economic growth and inequality, implying that understanding long-run distribution trends may rejoin the search for explanations of growth itself (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1).

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) complicate the argument by proposing that initial conditions or factor endowments could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth, which may suggest a more nuanced role for institutions in shaping economic outcomes (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4). Koepke & Baten (2005) introduce climate as another factor that may influence long-term economic growth, challenging the sole emphasis on institutional factors (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.20).

Frankema (2012) highlights colonial education as a key factor in explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa, potentially implying that certain institutions imposed by European powers may have played a significant role in shaping economic trajectories (Frankema_2012: p.2). Lastly, Hopkins (2019) discusses the impact of postmodernism on the study of long-run economic and political change in Africa, which may have limited the diversity of independent African viewpoints and potentially influenced the understanding of institutions' role in economic growth (Hopkins_2019: p.4).

These perspectives complicate the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by emphasizing other factors, such as labor exhaustion, climate, colonial education, and postmodernism, that may also play significant roles in shaping economic trajectories over time. Further examination of these factors in conjunction with institutional development could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth.

The relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth is further nuanced when considering their impact on labor markets, as evidenced by Komlos (1998), Williamson (1996), and Stoler (1989).

Komlos (1998) complicates the thesis by highlighting the role of physical stature during the Industrial Revolution. He suggests that economic processes and associated structural changes, including population growth, urbanization, and income inequality, significantly influenced the biological growth process and short-term fluctuations in the economy (Komlos_1998: p.16, p.17).

Williamson (1996) adds complexity by discussing the relationship between schooling and economic growth before World War I, as well as the impact of labor market glutting due to mass migrations on wage growth in receiving countries (Williamson_1996: p.20, p.17).

Stoler (1989) further complicates the argument by emphasizing the creation and fashioning of "imagined communities" among colonizers to overcome economic and social disparities (Stoler_1989: p.5). This alliance between plantation elite and colonial administration potentially had far-reaching effects on institutional development and subsequent economic growth trajectories.

These sources demonstrate that institutions play a crucial role in shaping labor markets, which in turn influence long-run economic growth. The interplay between these factors is multifaceted and intricate, necessitating further examination to gain a comprehensive understanding of the relationship between institutions and economic development over time.

The impact of colonialism on Africa's economic growth is a complex issue that adds nuance to the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Austin (2008) asserts that while trade, such as the Atlantic trade, may have reinforced rather than originated Africa’s relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe (Austin_2008: p.24). This suggests that colonial institutions, which were often established to facilitate trade, may have inadvertently perpetuated economic disparities between Africa and other regions.

On the other hand, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) highlight the significant role of African women in economic growth and development (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.19). However, their contributions were often subsumed under household production and formally unaccounted for (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). This underscores the importance of considering the role of informal institutions, such as gender roles, in shaping long-run economic growth.

These findings complicate the argument that institutions are the sole determinant of long-run economic growth. They suggest that colonial institutions and informal institutions, such as gender roles, also play significant roles in shaping Africa's economic development over time. Further examination of these factors is necessary to gain a comprehensive understanding of the relationship between institutions and economic growth in Africa.

Scholars Domar (1970), Bohannan (1959), and Ravallion (2001) provide valuable insights into the relationship between institutions and economic growth, particularly in the context of Africa. While their perspectives are not directly focused on the primary argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, they offer tangential yet significant contributions to understanding this complex relationship.

Domar (1970) emphasizes the role of labor-tied institutions, suggesting that such arrangements can hinder competition among employers and potentially stifle economic growth. Bohannan (1959), on the other hand, highlights the impact of Western economic institutions on African societies, particularly in relation to slave dealing and status acquisition.

Ravallion's (2001) work is more mathematical and abstract, offering equations and formulas related to economic growth and development. However, without a clear interpretation of these complex mathematical expressions, their direct relevance to the primary argument remains unclear.

Despite these contributions, there are areas where the scholars converge and diverge. They all acknowledge the importance of institutions in shaping economic outcomes, but their focus on different types of institutions (labor-tied, Western, mathematical) suggests a need for further research to understand the broader implications of institutional structures on long-term growth.

What remains unresolved is a comprehensive understanding of how various institutional factors interact and influence each other in driving economic growth over time. Future studies could benefit from integrating these diverse perspectives to gain a more holistic view of the relationship between institutions and economic growth, particularly within the African context.
