LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The debate on the primary cause of long-term economic growth, with a particular focus on institutions, is a contentious issue in contemporary economic literature. The central question at hand is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, as posited by North and Weingast (1989) and Acemoglu et al. (2001).

On one side, proponents argue that institutions play a crucial role in fostering economic growth. North & Weingast (1989) assert that the establishment of representative government with multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs is integral to economic growth and political freedom. Acemoglu et al. (2001) further support this view, suggesting that institutions significantly impact economic performance.

On the other hand, critics complicate the argument by highlighting various factors that may influence economic growth beyond institutions. Austin (2008), for instance, argues that trade patterns, such as the Atlantic one, can reinforce rather than originate a region's economic position compared to others, suggesting that factors outside of institutional structure might be at play. Similarly, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) emphasize the importance of understanding the extent to which historical growth provides a path to convergence, acknowledging potential risks and obstacles beyond institutions.

Moreover, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) point out that understanding the processes by which women's economic contributions are subsumed under household production is essential for formulating policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles. This nuance suggests that the impact of institutions on economic growth may be more complex than previously thought, with gender dynamics playing a significant role.

The stakes of this debate are high, as understanding the primary cause of long-term economic growth is crucial for policymakers and economists aiming to foster development in various regions. As the evidence shows, the thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is supported by some but challenged by others, with many offering nuanced perspectives on this complex issue. The argument continues as scholars seek to further unravel the intricate relationship between institutions and economic growth.

Structural change, a key characteristic of modern economic growth, is closely linked to advancements in technology (Kuznets_1973: p.4). The author emphasizes that the succession of technological innovations plays a significant role in driving economic growth and inducing adjustments within society (Kuznets_1973: p.6). Kuznets also acknowledges the potential negative consequences associated with economic growth, even within developed countries, suggesting that these issues may be exacerbated by the rapid adoption of new technologies (Kuznets_1973: p.11).

Furthermore, Kuznets discusses the non-conventional costs of economic growth, which include environmental degradation, increased inequality, and social unrest (Kuznets_1973: p.5). These challenges underscore the importance of institutions in managing technological innovations to ensure sustainable long-term economic growth. The author's work supports the notion that institutions are essential for navigating the complexities of technological advancements and their impact on society, thereby reinforcing the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

The authors North and Weingast (1989) provide a compelling case for the significance of institutions in fostering economic growth, specifically focusing on the role of credible sovereign commitment. They argue that representative government, characterized by multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, plays an integral part in facilitating economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.2).

By emphasizing the importance of political institutions, North and Weingast's work underscores the idea that these structures are not merely passive containers for economic exchange but active participants in shaping the trajectory of growth. Their study highlights how credible sovereign commitment—ensured by multiple veto points—allows for the efficient allocation of resources through a stable political environment conducive to long-term investment (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

Moreover, North and Weingast's work demonstrates that institutions are essential in navigating the complexities of technological advancements and their impact on society. As they explain, "the institutional structure is the key to understanding why some societies have been able to maintain a stable political order and others not" (North&Weingast_1989: p.7). This finding reinforces the notion that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

The persistence of institutions significantly contributes to a society's economic performance, as demonstrated by Acemoglu and colleagues in their study (AcemogluEtAl_2001). They found that the more fundamental institutions regarding property rights and political power have a substantial impact on economic progress. Specifically, they noted that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and overall economic growth (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27).

Furthermore, Acemoglu et al. argue that differences in mortality rates do not primarily account for variations in institutions (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3). Instead, they suggest that the focus should be on understanding the institutional foundations that underpin economic growth.

Their research also reveals a strong correlation between the quality of institutions and economic performance. Table 4 in their study shows a large effect of institutions on economic development (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19). These findings underscore the importance of institutional persistence for long-term economic growth, reinforcing the thesis that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of such growth.

However, it is worth noting that while Acemoglu et al. provide compelling evidence for the role of institutions in long-run economic growth, further research is needed to fully understand the complex interplay between institutional factors and other determinants of economic performance.

Structural change, rather than institutions, may be a more significant factor in long-run economic growth. Stern (2004) challenges the notion that institutions are the primary driver of economic growth, arguing that economic factors such as structural change drive changes in environmental impacts and may contribute to rising or declining environmental degradation over the course of economic development (Stern_2004: p.3). Stern further contends that the assumption that environmental damage does not reduce economic activity sufficiently to halt growth is valid, implying that structural change could potentially outweigh any negative institutional factors (Stern_2004: p.8).

Furthermore, Humphries (2013) suggests that the division of labor and the rise of workshop production, rather than institutions, were key drivers of economic growth in the 18th century (Humphries_2013: p.18). Humphries also points out that the relative needs of women and children may have hindered economic growth in some cases, suggesting that demographic factors could play a role in economic performance (Humphries_2013: p.11, p.19).

These findings challenge the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Future research may benefit from exploring the interplay between structural change and institutional factors to gain a more comprehensive understanding of the determinants of economic performance.

The assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is not without challenges, as other factors such as structural change and initial conditions also play significant roles in shaping economic performance.

Temin (2002) complicates this argument by suggesting that the exhaustion of cheap labor, rather than institutions, may have been the primary cause for the slowing of economic growth. He argues that the end of the Golden Age was brought about by the return to equilibrium growth paths, not adverse economic shocks in the 1970s (Temin_2002: p.14). Furthermore, Temin posits that recent theories of economic growth need to consider the context of economic transition, which was prevalent around 1800 with very few exceptions (Temin_2002: p.7).

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) also challenge the notion that institutions are solely responsible for long-run economic growth. They highlight the importance of initial conditions, or factor endowments broadly conceived, in shaping the economic paths of individual countries (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4). The authors suggest that contrasting records in growth can be traced back to the achievement of sustained economic growth by the United States and Canada during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, while others did not manage to attain this goal until late in the nineteenth century (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.12).

Williamson (1996) adds to this discussion by emphasizing the importance of understanding the sources of convergence for a comprehensive understanding of economic growth. He argues that to answer questions about the meaning of convergence, we must agree on its definition (Williamson_1996: p.4). Furthermore, Williamson suggests that understanding the sources of convergence is fundamental to understanding who gains and who loses from convergence (Williamson_1996: p.25).

The assertion that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth is further nuanced when considering trade between Africa and Western Europe, as highlighted by Austin (2008). He argues that while these trades may have fostered growth in some regions, they did not originate Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe.

Austin (2008) suggests that the existing theories of convergence, particularly those focusing on trade relationships, need a reevaluation in light of long-term history. By examining recurring relationships and path dependencies, we can gain a more comprehensive understanding of how institutions have shaped economic growth over time.

Moreover, Austin (2008) emphasizes the importance of structural change as a correlate of growth. In the context of trade between Africa and Western Europe, it is evident that institutional structures played a significant role in determining the long-term impact of these trades on economic growth. The author argues that these trades reinforced rather than originated Africa's poor economic position compared to Western Europe.

The lack of data before 1950 poses a significant challenge in fully understanding Africa's economic growth trajectory, as highlighted by Broadberry & Gardner (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). Prior studies have noted the stark contrast between Sub-Saharan Africa's (SSA) performance and other regions (Collier & Gunning_1999; Sachs & Warner_1997). However, the absence of historical data in SSA has hindered a comprehensive comparison.

Broadberry & Gardner (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1) underscore that recent debates about long-term growth have largely overlooked SSA due to this data deficiency. They further emphasize that understanding whether Africa's past growth patterns offer a path to convergence requires an examination of the risks associated with the economic shrinking that has historically hindered progress (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.18).

In contrast, other regions have benefited from new contributions to historical national accounting, which have provided insights into growth and development patterns stretching back centuries (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.1). These advancements have rewritten the history of economic divergence and convergence even before the industrial revolution (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.1).

The examination of the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth reveals a consensus among scholars that institutions play a significant role in shaping economic trajectories (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.1). Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), for instance, both acknowledge the impact of institutional arrangements on economic performance. Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) emphasizes that with labor tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, which can hinder economic growth. Similarly, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) highlights how Western impact centered on slave dealing and the acquisition of status, which had profound economic consequences.

However, there are differences in their perspectives. Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) argues that the Russian situation prior to peasants' enserfment corresponds to a case where labor is tied to land or owners, while Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.10) points out that before the arrival of the British, people did not venture far beyond their kinship groups, limiting economic interactions.
