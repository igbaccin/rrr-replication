The question of whether institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth has been a subject of intense debate among scholars. This discussion is significant as it directly impacts our understanding of historical and contemporary economic development, particularly in regions such as Africa.

Several scholars support the assertion that institutions play a crucial role in fostering long-term economic growth. For instance, Austin (2008) argues that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, stating that they provide a logical explanation for economic disparities between regions over time (Austin_2008: p.26). Similarly, North and Weingast (1989) demonstrate how certain political institutions have been instrumental in enabling economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

On the other hand, some scholars offer critiques and complications to this argument. Broadberry and Gardner (2022) argue that a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has made it difficult to include Sub-Saharan Africa in recent debates about comparative long-run growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) also challenge the argument by highlighting how women's economic contributions have often been overlooked or subsumed under household production (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3).

The stakes of this debate are high, as understanding the role of institutions in long-term economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting development. Moreover, it can help explain historical and contemporary economic disparities between regions. As the argument continues, it becomes clear that while institutions may play a significant role in long-run economic growth, other factors such as geography, technology, and cultural practices also need to be considered.

The role of institutions in long-run economic growth is further supported by various scholars, as evidenced in the works of Kuznets (1973), North & Weingast (1989), Mokyr (2005), North (1989), Ogilvie (2007), and deVries (1994).

Kuznets (1973) emphasizes the interconnectedness between economic growth, societal changes, and technological innovations. He suggests that concurrent with remarkable positive achievements of modern economic growth are unexpected negative results even within developed countries (Kuznets_1973: p.11). This underscores the complex relationship between institutions and economic development, where improvements in one area may lead to unintended consequences elsewhere.

North & Weingast (1989) focus on the political factors underlying economic growth and market development. They argue that fundamental institutions, such as representative government with multiple veto points and a primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are essential for economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28). This highlights the importance of institutional design in fostering long-term prosperity.

Mokyr (2005) contends that developments in intellectual reasoning concerning "useful knowledge" played a crucial role in driving economic growth in the West, particularly during the Enlightenment period (Mokyr_2005: p.4). He suggests that the change in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West must be explained through advancements in intellectual domains such as individualism, formal law, corporatism, self-governance, and institutionalized processes (Mokyr_2005: p.56). This supports the notion that institutions are integral to long-run economic growth.

North (1989) argues that transaction costs, security rights, and capital market development are critical factors in understanding the relationship between institutions and economic growth (North_1989: p.9). He suggests that the tracts of a more renegotiation-resistant institutional framework may fail if they are not nested within a broader institutional system (North_1989: p.6). This underscores the interdependency of various institutions in promoting economic growth.

Ogilvie (2007) acknowledges that geographical characteristics, such as disease and ecology, have contributed to economic divergence between regions (Ogilvie_2007: p.2). However, he also emphasizes the importance of economic institutions in shaping long-run growth, noting that their interdependency within a wider framework is crucial for their effectiveness (Ogilvie_2007: p.27).

deVries (1994) highlights the role of urban networks and commercial interaction systems in establishing a framework for regional economic development during the early modern period, which ultimately facilitated industrial growth (deVries_1994: p.5). This supports the idea that institutions play a fundamental role in shaping long-term economic growth patterns.

The relationship between institutions and technology in shaping long-term economic growth is further supported by various studies. Austin (2008) asserts that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, suggesting that they reinforce existing economic positions rather than originating them (Austin_2008: p.24). This perspective aligns with the idea that institutions play a crucial role in determining economic trajectories over time.

Broadberry & Gupta (2006) also substantiate this claim by emphasizing the service sector's importance during the early modern period in driving economic growth, similar to its role in the modern era (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.9). They argue that increases in living standards of north-western Europeans were primarily due to falling relative prices of manufactured goods rather than technological advancements alone.

Furthermore, Bryant (2006) highlights the fusion of mercantilist imperialism with a technologically driven capitalistic transformation as a fundamental axis of difference between Europe and other regions during the early modern period (Bryant_2006: p.5). This observation underscores the significance of institutions in facilitating technological progress and economic growth.

Institutions and trade significantly intertwine in shaping economic growth, a relationship further substantiated by the works of Acemoglu et al. (2001, 2002) and Abramovitz (1986).

Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3) assert that differences in mortality rates are not the main cause of variation in institutions, suggesting that other factors, such as institutions themselves, play a more significant role. These institutions, according to Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27), were detrimental to investment and economic progress, thereby hindering long-term growth. The authors also report a large effect of institutions on economic performance (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19).

In a similar vein, Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5) question whether the reversal in the distribution of economic prosperity is related to institutions. They argue that this change is a first-order fact for understanding development over the past 500 years (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49).

Abramovitz (Abramovitz_1986: p.7) emphasizes the importance of structural change in correlation with growth, stating that macroeconomic and monetary conditions encouraging capital investment and effective demand are crucial factors. Furthermore, Abramovitz (Abramovitz_1986: p.22) suggests that countries are now readjusting their balance in favor of productivity growth, indicating a shift towards more productive institutions.

These findings underscore the fundamental role of institutions in facilitating economic growth and trade, providing support for the assertion that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth. However, further investigation is needed to fully understand the intricate relationship between institutions, trade, and growth in various historical and contemporary contexts.

The relationship between institutions and economic growth is not as straightforward as some argue, with certain scholars challenging this perspective. For instance, McCloskey (2015) contends that ideologies and ideas, rather than institutional structures, played a significant role in shaping economic growth in France during the 18th and 19th centuries. He suggests that conservative ideologies, such as a strong adherence to Catholicism, hindered economic growth in France compared to more liberal societies like Holland and England (McCloskey_2015: p.9).

Similarly, Bryceson (2002) argues that cultural dynamics can significantly impact economic growth, particularly in rural communities. In these contexts, traditional hierarchical orders may limit women's involvement in non-agricultural activities and undermine their role as family provisioners (Bryceson_2002: p.9). This suggests that the relationship between institutions and economic growth is influenced by cultural factors, which can either facilitate or hinder growth depending on the specific context.

These findings challenge the notion that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth. Instead, they suggest that ideologies, cultural dynamics, and other factors may play equally important roles in shaping economic outcomes over time. Further research is needed to better understand these complex interrelationships and their implications for economic development.

The argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth faces a challenge from those who contend that economic growth may actually lead to environmental degradation, rather than being a solution as claimed by the proponents of the Environmental Kuznets Curve (EKC) hypothesis (Stern_2004: p.1). According to this perspective, economic development initially causes increased environmental damage, but over time, as economies mature and structural changes occur, the level of environmental degradation decreases (Stern_2004: p.3).

This critique suggests that institutions may not be the sole determinant of long-run economic growth, as they are intertwined with the environmental impacts of economic development. The assumption is that environmental damage does not reduce economic activity sufficiently to halt the growth process and that any irreversibility is not so severe that it reduces future income levels (Stern_2004: p.8). This perspective challenges the notion that institutions are the primary drivers of long-run economic growth, as it emphasizes the importance of environmental factors in shaping economic outcomes over time.

The assertion that institutions are the primary drivers of long-run economic growth is not without complications, as other factors such as cultural dynamics play a significant role in shaping economic outcomes over time. For instance, Komlos (1998) emphasizes the interplay between physical stature and economic processes during the Industrial Revolution, suggesting that biological growth was significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic conditions. This perspective underscores the importance of considering the impact of environmental factors on long-run economic growth.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) also contribute nuance to the argument by highlighting the interdependence between economic growth and inequality. They argue that understanding long-run distribution trends is crucial for explaining growth itself, implying that the relationship between economic growth and equality is complex and bidirectional. This perspective challenges the notion that institutions are the sole determinants of long-run economic growth.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) complicate the argument by exploring the possibility that initial conditions or factor endowments could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth. They suggest that the contrasting records in growth can be traced back to the achievement of sustained economic growth by certain countries during specific periods, while others did not manage to attain this goal until later. This perspective underscores the importance of considering historical context when assessing the role of institutions in shaping long-run economic growth.

Koepke & Baten (2005) add another layer of complexity by examining the relationship between climate and human living standards over time. Their findings suggest that climate can have a significant impact on long-run economic growth, challenging the notion that institutions are the sole determinants of economic outcomes.

Frankema (2012) complicates the argument further by arguing that colonial education is a key factor in explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa. He suggests that the former British colonies grew faster as a result of their educational lead, which was endogenous to the local political and economic context. This perspective underscores the importance of considering the role of cultural factors, such as education, in shaping long-run economic growth.

Finally, Hopkins (2019) challenges the notion that institutions are the primary drivers of long-run economic growth by highlighting the impact of postmodernism on the study of African economic history. He argues that successive generations of researchers have been limited in their diversity and independence due to the influence of Western thought, which may have inhibited the understanding of long-run economic and political change in Africa. This perspective underscores the importance of considering the impact of cultural dynamics on the study of long-run economic growth.

The interplay between institutions and technology further complicates the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

Technological advancements, such as the railway system, played a significant role in economic development before the mid-1920s, offering cost advantages over other modes of transportation (Austin_2007: p.8). However, the adoption and implementation of these technologies were influenced by institutional factors, suggesting that institutions may have indirectly impacted technological progress.

Moreover, population growth, a critical factor in long-run economic growth, was found to be faster after 1918 (Austin_2007: p.12). This rapid population expansion could have led to increased demand for goods and services, thereby stimulating technological innovations and economic growth.

On the other hand, institutional arrangements can also hinder technological progress. For instance, in land-abundant West Africa, many of the capital-deepening and TFP-improving forces of modern economic growth were not at work (Austin_2007: p.16). This lack of development could be attributed to institutional factors that discouraged investment in capital-intensive industries.

The relationship between institutions and technology is further illustrated by the Maddison Project's revisions of nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth rates for various countries (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5). These revisions suggest that institutional changes played a role in technological advancements, thereby influencing economic growth.

Institutions, while playing a significant role in economic growth, may not be the sole cause as suggested by some researchers. Temin (2002) complicates this perspective by highlighting the exhaustion of cheap labor as a factor that contributed to slower economic growth in Europe during the Golden Age. He argues that the end of this period was not due to adverse economic shocks, but rather a return to equilibrium growth paths (Temin_2002: p.14). Furthermore, Temin suggests that recent theories of economic growth should consider the context of economic transition, as many national economies in 1800 were largely agricultural (Temin_2002: p.7).

Similarly, Williamson (1996) adds nuance to the argument by discussing the relationship between schooling and Swedish economic growth before World War I. He points out that mass migrations could have reduced the pace of real wage growth in receiving countries by glutting labor markets abroad (Williamson_1996: p.17). Williamson also emphasizes the importance of understanding the sources of convergence to determine who gains and loses from it, thereby shedding light on policy responses (Williamson_1996: p.25).

These findings suggest that while institutions are crucial for long-run economic growth, other factors such as labor availability and schooling may also play significant roles. Future research could explore these interrelationships in more detail to provide a comprehensive understanding of the factors driving economic growth.

The scholars Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), while not directly addressing the primary thesis of institutions being the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, provide tangential insights that contribute to a broader understanding of this complex phenomenon. Both authors highlight the role of institutions in shaping economic structures and outcomes.

Domar (1970) emphasizes the impact of labor-tied institutions on competition among employers, suggesting that such arrangements can hinder economic growth by limiting the mobility of labor. He also discusses the economic cost of slaveholding, which is relevant to understanding the long-term effects of oppressive institutional structures on growth.

Bohannan (1959), on the other hand, focuses on the impact of Western institutions on African societies, particularly in relation to trade and status acquisition. He notes that these institutions often disrupted traditional social structures, leading to changes in economic behavior. However, he also points out that these changes were not solely economic, suggesting a more comprehensive interplay between economic and societal institutions.

Despite their insights, both authors do not provide a direct causal link between institutions and long-run economic growth. This leaves an important gap in the literature, as a comprehensive understanding of this relationship remains elusive. Future research could explore these interrelationships in more detail to provide a comprehensive understanding of the factors driving economic growth.
