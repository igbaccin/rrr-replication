LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The question of whether institutions are the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth has been a subject of extensive debate among economists and historians. This discussion is significant as it sheds light on the factors driving economic development and growth across different regions and periods.

Several scholars support the argument that institutions play a pivotal role in economic growth. For instance, Kuznets (1973) posits that both technological innovations and social innovations are major factors affecting economic and social structure, suggesting a strong link between institutions and growth. Acemoglu et al. (2001) argue that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress, further emphasizing the importance of institutions in shaping economic growth. North & Weingast (1989) focus on the political factors underlying economic growth and market development, indicating a crucial role for institutions in this process.

However, not all scholars agree that institutions are the sole cause of long-term economic growth. Austin (2008) complicates this argument by suggesting that while institutions are important, other historical factors may also contribute to economic performance differences between regions. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has made it difficult for Sub-Saharan Africa to be part of recent debates about comparative long-run growth, potentially questioning the centrality of institutions in this context.

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance to the debate by discussing the role of women's economic contribution and how it may be undervalued or unaccounted for, suggesting that a more comprehensive understanding of institutional dynamics is necessary to fully grasp their impact on growth.

The works of Acemoglu et al. (2001, 2002) provide substantial support for the thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. In their studies, these authors emphasize the role of institutional dynamics in shaping economic progress over extended periods.

Acemoglu and colleagues (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.8) argue that there are various economic mechanisms leading to institutional persistence, which in turn significantly impact economic development. They further assert that institutions play a crucial role in determining the level of investment and economic progress (Acemoglu et al., 2001: p.27).

In their subsequent work, Acemoglu et al. (2002) highlight the importance of institutional changes in understanding long-run development over the past five centuries (Acemoglu et al., 2002: p.49). They also suggest that the reversal in economic prosperity distribution might be related to institutions (Acemoglu et al., 2002: p.5).

These findings underscore the significance of institutional analysis in understanding the long-run economic growth process. The works of Acemoglu and colleagues demonstrate that institutions can either hinder or promote investment, thereby influencing a nation's economic trajectory over time. The role of these fundamental institutions on property rights, as discussed by Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27), further supports the argument that institutional dynamics are crucial for long-term growth.

Moreover, Whatley's (2018) study on the econometric analysis of economic development also provides support for the thesis. While not directly focusing on institutions, Whatley's work highlights the importance of understanding the mechanisms that allow variables to adjust to deviations from long-run equilibrium (Whatley_2018: p.8). This suggests that a comprehensive understanding of institutional dynamics is necessary to fully grasp their impact on growth.

Structural change, as evidenced by the evolution of economic systems, plays a significant role in long-run economic growth (Abramovitz_1986: p.20). Abramovitz suggests that the development of mixed economies is a direct result of economic growth itself, implying a causal relationship between institutional changes and growth. Furthermore, he questions whether this evolution has a generalized effect on productivity growth among leaders, indicating its broad impact (Abramovitz_1986: p.16).

Similarly, van Zanden's research highlights the importance of human capital formation in determining long-term economic growth (vanZanden_2009: p.24). By examining the skill premium, or the wage gap between skilled and unskilled labor, van Zanden demonstrates that changes in labor markets, such as integration and reduced segmentation, can significantly impact economic growth (vanZanden_2009: p.23). This suggests that institutional dynamics related to human capital development are crucial for understanding long-term economic growth.

Nunn's work supports this argument by showing a correlation between ethnic fractionalization and long-term economic growth (Nunn_2008: p.4). In times of uncertainty and insecurity, individuals required institutions that provided security and reduced risk, which were essential for economic development (Nunn_2008: p.4). These findings reinforce the idea that institutional arrangements play a fundamental role in shaping long-run economic growth.

Lastly, Nunn and Wantchekon's research supports the thesis by demonstrating that both internal and external factors contribute to the development of effective institutions (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.4). Their findings suggest that the internal factor, which refers to the capacity of individuals within a society to establish and maintain trust among themselves, is at least twice as important as the external factor in shaping institutional development (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.4). This emphasizes the importance of institutions in facilitating long-run economic growth.

The relationship between institutions and technology is crucial in understanding long-run economic growth. Technological innovations, as key drivers of economic progress, require supporting structures to facilitate their implementation and adoption (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). Kuznets (1973) highlights that the succession of technological innovations during modern economic growth is accompanied by social innovations necessary for adjustments in economic and social structure (Kuznets_1973: p.6).

Mokyr (2005) emphasizes the importance of the Industrial Revolution, noting that it took its time to impact GDP per capita significantly in the West (Mokyr_2005: p.3). This underscores the need for robust institutions that can facilitate the integration and diffusion of new technologies, thereby fostering long-term economic growth.

North & Weingast (1989) argue that political factors underpinning economic growth and the development of markets are as essential as the rules governing economic exchange (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). They contend that institutions play a necessary role in making possible economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.30). This suggests that strong institutions are vital for the successful implementation of technological innovations, which is crucial for long-run economic success.

While North and Weingast (1989) assert that strong institutions are essential for technological innovations to contribute to long-run economic growth, Stern (2004) challenges this perspective by suggesting that the relationship between economic growth and environmental degradation may not be as detrimental as previously thought.

Stern (Stern_2004: p.1) argues that economic growth, rather than being a threat to the environment as claimed by some, could potentially lead to environmental improvement in the long run, a concept known as the Environmental Kuznets Curve (EKC) hypothesis. According to Stern, this hypothesis assumes that environmental damage does not significantly impede economic activity and that any irreversibility does not reduce future income levels (Stern_2004: p.8).

Moreover, Stern discusses the economic factors driving changes in environmental impacts, suggesting that these factors may be responsible for either rising or declining environmental degradation over the course of economic development (Stern_2004: p.3). The author further notes that if the EKC hypothesis were true, it would imply that technological innovations and structural change correlate with growth, potentially challenging the notion that strong institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

However, it is important to note that Stern's critique does not negate the importance of institutions entirely but rather questions the immediate negative impact of economic growth on the environment, suggesting a more complex interplay between institutions, technology, and environmental degradation in the long run. Further exploration of this dynamic could provide valuable insights into the relationship between these factors and economic growth.

The interplay between institutions and technology in relation to economic growth is a complex dynamic that requires further exploration. Komlos (1998) argues that the socio-economic changes associated with the onset of modern economic growth were sufficient by themselves to bring about decline in physical stature, suggesting that institutional changes could have significant impact on various aspects of society beyond economic growth. Similarly, Temin (2002) contends that it was the exhaustion of cheap labor that caused economic growth to slow, implying a potential role for institutions in shaping labor markets and influencing growth rates.

Williamson (1996) highlights the importance of understanding the sources of convergence as fundamental to understanding policy responses, suggesting that institutions play a crucial role in determining who gains and loses from economic convergence. Clark & Jacks (2007) demonstrate how technological advancements in the coal industry enabled it to respond to increases in demand during the Industrial Revolution era, thereby underscoring the importance of institutions in facilitating technological progress and economic growth.

The significance of institutions in facilitating economic growth is further highlighted by examining their impact on education, a critical factor in long-term development. Austin (2008) contends that institutions played a pivotal role in shaping Africa's economic position compared to Western Europe and parts of Asia, not only originating but reinforcing the continent's relatively poor economic standing (Austin_2008: p.24). This perspective complicates the argument that technological advancements were solely responsible for the Industrial Revolution era's economic growth, as institutions also influenced the educational systems and opportunities available during this time.

Similarly, Hopkins (2019) argues that food exports made little contribution to economic growth in West Africa during the period of the slave trade (Hopkins_2019: p.13). This finding suggests that other factors, such as institutions shaping educational opportunities and access, might have played a more significant role in driving long-term economic growth.

Skocpol & Sommers (1980) emphasize the importance of comparative analysis in understanding historical trajectories and social change (Skocpol&Sommers_1980: p.2). By comparing Japan and China, they reveal the particularities of these cases and demonstrate that Western intrusions influenced the development of their educational institutions, further underscoring the role of institutions in shaping long-term economic growth.

Stoler (1989) argues that colonizers lived in "imagined communities" to overcome economic and social disparities (Stoler_1989: p.5). This concept can be extended to understand how educational institutions were shaped during the colonial era, as they attempted to bridge the gap between colonizer and colonized by providing a common educational system that served the interests of both parties.

The role of institutions in shaping long-term economic growth is further elucidated through an examination of their impact on agriculture, particularly in Africa during the colonial era.

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has often excluded Sub-Saharan Africa from debates about comparative long-run growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). This highlights a gap in our understanding of how agricultural institutions during the colonial era may have contributed to the region's economic growth or lack thereof.

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) emphasize the significance of women's contributions to economic growth and development, particularly in agriculture (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). Their work complicates the argument by suggesting that understanding the role of institutions in shaping these contributions is crucial for effective policy-making aimed at strengthening women's economic roles. For instance, the subsumption of women's labor under household production may have undermined policies seeking to empower them economically (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3).

The literature on the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth presents a mixed picture, with some scholars emphasizing the critical role of institutions while others highlight their tangential nature in this context. Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), both cited as tangentially related, offer insights that both converge and diverge on this issue.

Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) posits that the subjugation of labor to land or owners eliminates competition among employers, suggesting that institutions play a significant role in shaping economic growth by dictating the dynamics of labor markets. Similarly, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) notes that Western impact on African societies was centered on economic institutions such as slave dealing and curing, indicating an institutional foundation for economic change.

However, both authors also offer perspectives that diverge from the primary thesis. Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) argues that the Russian peasants' enserfment corresponds to a case where labor is tied to land or owner, implying that such institutions may not always promote economic growth. Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.13), on the other hand, points out that the number of women in African societies was limited, which could have implications for growth potential due to the underutilization of a significant portion of the labor force.

Despite these contrasting viewpoints, both authors acknowledge the importance of institutions in understanding economic change, albeit with different emphases. What remains unresolved is the extent to which specific institutional configurations lead to sustainable long-run economic growth and the factors that determine whether these institutions will promote or hinder such growth. Further research is needed to clarify these complexities and provide a more nuanced understanding of the role of institutions in driving long-term economic development.
