The question of the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth has been a subject of intense debate among economists and historians. This literature review explores various perspectives on this issue, drawing from sources such as Austin (2008), Kuznets (1973), Broadberry & Gardner (2022), Akyeampong & Fofack (2014), North & Weingast (1989), and Acemoglu et al. (2001).

Austin (2008) supports the thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, asserting that they reinforce rather than originate Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe. Kuznets (1973) also supports this view, linking rapid changes in economic structure with modern economic growth and social innovations.

However, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate the discussion by pointing out that Sub-Saharan Africa has been absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth due to a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) further add nuance, highlighting the role of women's economic contribution and its subsumption under household production in undermining policies that seek to strengthen women's economic roles.

North & Weingast (1989) support the thesis by focusing on the political factors underpinning economic growth and the development of markets, demonstrating how institutions played a necessary role in making possible economic growth and political freedom. Acemoglu et al. (2001) also support this view, showing a large effect of institutions on economic performance and linking detrimental institutions to investment and economic progress.

The stakes of this debate are significant, as understanding the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is crucial for policymakers aiming to promote economic development in various regions. The ongoing discussion among scholars offers insights into the complex interplay between institutions, economic structure, and social innovations, contributing to a more comprehensive understanding of long-term economic growth patterns.

The argument continues as we delve deeper into the nuances of these perspectives and explore potential explanations for the divergent paths of economic development in different regions throughout history.

The perspectives presented by various scholars underscore the significant role institutions play in shaping long-term economic growth patterns, particularly during periods of economic shocks. Kuznets (1973) emphasizes that rapid changes in economic structure, often associated with technological innovations, can lead to both positive and negative outcomes within developed and less developed countries respectively (Kuznets_1973: p.4, p.6, p.11).

North & Weingast (1989) argue that the fundamental institutions of representative government, such as multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are crucial for economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.2, p.30). Mokyr (2005) supports this notion by suggesting that the change in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West can be attributed to developments in the intellectual realm concerning "useful knowledge" (Mokyr_2005: p.4, p.56).

North (1989) further elucidates this point by discussing how institutions impact transaction costs and market efficiency, which in turn influence economic growth (North_1989: p.6, p.9). Ogilvie (2007) highlights the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework, suggesting that the failure of one institution can have far-reaching consequences for economic development (Ogilvie_2007: p.27).

Acemoglu et al. (2002) demonstrate that there has been a significant reversal in the distribution of economic prosperity over the past 500 years, and they question whether this change is related to institutions (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49, p.6). The scholars' findings collectively support the argument that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, as they provide the necessary framework for technological innovation, political stability, and efficient market functioning.

Institutions play a significant role in fostering technological advancements, which in turn contribute to long-term economic growth. Austin (2008) asserts that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, arguing that they provide the necessary framework for innovation (Austin_2008: p.26). Specifically, he suggests that institutions can reinforce existing economic positions, such as the relatively poor economic position of Africa compared to Western Europe (Austin_2008: p.24).

Similarly, Broadberry and Gupta (2006) discuss the role of the service sector in economic growth during the early modern period. They argue that the service sector played a crucial role in economic growth, as it does in the modern period (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.9). Furthermore, they acknowledge that British economic growth during the Industrial Revolution was slower than previously thought, implying that gains in living standards were slower to materialize for the masses (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.25). This observation underscores the importance of institutions in facilitating technological advancements and subsequent economic growth.

The role of institutions in promoting economic growth is further substantiated by the works of Acemoglu et al. (2001) and Abramovitz (1986). Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27) argue that more fundamental institutions, such as property rights, significantly impact economic performance, with detrimental institutions hindering investment and progress. Their findings are supported by the observation of a large effect of institutions on economic performance across different regions (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19).

Abramovitz (Abramovitz_1986: p.7) highlights the importance of macroeconomic and monetary conditions in encouraging capital investment and effective demand, which are crucial for economic growth. Moreover, he suggests that the elaboration of mixed economies is a function of economic growth itself (Abramovitz_1986: p.20). This implies that as economies grow, institutions evolve to facilitate further growth, creating a symbiotic relationship between institutional development and economic progress.

The works of Simson (2019), Stern (2004), McCloskey (2015), Bryceson (2002), Humphries (2013), and vanZanden (1999) challenge the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. These scholars suggest that economic shocks, such as external crises or internal structural changes, can significantly impact economic development and potentially overshadow the role of institutions.

Simson (2019) argues that economic growth in East Africa during the 1960s and 1970s was driven by factors like industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy, rather than institutional evolution. Stern (2004) posits that economic growth can lead to environmental degradation and may not always be beneficial for long-term sustainability. McCloskey (2015) highlights instances where conservative ideologies and religious beliefs hindered economic growth in France and Spain, demonstrating that ideas and ideologies could supersede the role of institutions in shaping economic trajectories.

Bryceson (2002) and Humphries (2013) provide evidence that structural changes within societies, such as shifts in labor division, family dynamics, and agricultural practices, can influence economic growth independently of institutional development. Lastly, vanZanden (1999) questions the consensus view that early modern economies were unable to generate growth, suggesting that external factors like trade and technological innovations may have played a more significant role than previously thought.

These scholars' works collectively challenge the idea that institutions are the primary drivers of long-run economic growth by highlighting various external shocks and structural changes that can significantly impact economic development. Further investigation is needed to understand the interplay between institutions, economic shocks, and long-term economic growth.

The interplay between institutions and economic shocks significantly influences long-term economic growth, as evidenced by various scholarly works. Komlos (1998) highlights that short-term fluctuations in economic processes and associated structural changes had a substantial impact on the biological growth process during the Industrial Revolution (Komlos_1998: p.16). Temin (2002) argues that adverse economic shocks, such as the exhaustion of cheap labor, can slow down economic growth (Temin_2002: p.4).

Moreover, Lindert & Williamson (1985) suggest that initial conditions, or factor endowments, could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.4). Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) emphasize the importance of institutions in explaining contrasting records in growth across societies (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.2). They demonstrate that sustained economic growth was achieved by the United States and Canada during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, while others did not manage to attain this goal until late in the nineteenth century (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.12).

In a similar vein, Koepke & Baten (2005) explore the relationship between climate and human living standards, suggesting that climate could have played a role in economic growth (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.20). Hopkins (2019) contends that postmodernism's focus on 'totalism' and 'essentialism' inhibited the study of long-run economic and political change, potentially limiting the diversity of independent African viewpoints (Hopkins_2019: p.10).

These sources complicate the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by emphasizing the role of external shocks, such as climate and economic crises, in shaping economic development. Furthermore, they highlight the importance of initial conditions and structural changes in determining growth trajectories across different societies. Future research should continue to investigate the interplay between institutions, economic shocks, and long-term economic growth to gain a more nuanced understanding of this complex relationship.

The complex relationship between institutions, economic shocks, and long-term economic growth is further nuanced by the findings of Broadberry & Gardner (2022), Bolt & van Zanden (2014), and Braudel & Wallerstein (2009).

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) highlight the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 in Sub-Saharan Africa, a region that has been absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. This data gap complicates our understanding of whether the growth trajectories observed in other regions can be extrapolated to Sub-Saharan Africa. Moreover, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) emphasize the need to understand the extent to which there are still risks of economic shrinkage that have undermined progress in the past, adding a layer of complexity to the relationship between institutions and long-term growth.

Bolt & van Zanden (2014) contribute to this discourse by discussing revisions of nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth data for various regions, such as Switzerland, Germany, Sweden, Italy, Greece, Russia, and Bulgaria. Although these revisions do not fundamentally change our view, they provide valuable insights into patterns of growth and development over centuries. The sources of long-term economic growth in Indonesia, discussed by van der Eng (2010) as cited in Bolt & van Zanden (2014), also add nuance to the relationship between institutions and economic growth.

Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) argue that there has been a movement toward economic and social history at the expense of political history, which complicates our understanding of the role of institutions in long-term economic growth. They further emphasize the interplay between different players in the market, suggesting that the "strategy" of one affects the "strategy" of each of the others. This perspective highlights the importance of considering the broader context in which institutions operate when examining their impact on long-term economic growth.

The role of institutions in shaping long-run economic growth is further nuanced when considering their impact on education, a critical factor for economic development. Frankema (2012) contends that the British colonial educational system played a significant role in explaining long-term growth disparities in Africa. He argues that former British colonies experienced faster growth due to their educational advantage, with the British controlling most of the fertile areas (Frankema_2012: p.2). However, the degree of British "liberalism" was endogenous to the local political and economic context (Frankema_2012: p.19), suggesting that the nature of colonial education may have varied significantly across regions.

Goldin (2006) also emphasizes the importance of institutions in shaping labor markets, particularly in relation to women's education and participation. He notes that the study of labor was once focused on institutional aspects of the labor market (Goldin_2006: p.3). The evolution and revolution in women's roles were distinct in terms of their choices and decisions (Goldin_2006: p.1), indicating that institutional changes could have profound impacts on gender dynamics within education systems.

These findings complicate the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by suggesting that the nature and effectiveness of institutions can be shaped by factors such as geography, political context, and cultural norms. This underscores the importance of considering the broader context in which institutions operate when examining their impact on long-term economic growth.

The scholarship on the role of institutions in long-run economic growth, as evidenced by Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), offers insights into the complex interplay between institutions and economic development. Both authors agree that institutions play a significant role in shaping economic growth, with Domar emphasizing labor-tied systems and Bohannan discussing the impact of Western institutions on African societies.

Domar (1970) argues that when labor is tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, stifling structural change and economic growth. This aligns with Bohannan's (1959) observation of how Western institutions, particularly those centered on slave dealing and status acquisition, disrupted traditional African societies, leading to economic stagnation in certain areas.

However, the scholars diverge in their focus and scope. Domar primarily focuses on labor-tied systems in historical contexts such as Russia and the American South, while Bohannan examines the impact of Western institutions on African societies. This difference in focus highlights the need for a more comprehensive understanding of how various types of institutions influence economic growth across different geographical and cultural contexts.

Despite these insights, several questions remain unresolved. For instance, what specific mechanisms link institutions to economic growth? How do cultural norms and political contexts shape institutions, and how do these shaped institutions in turn affect economic development? Addressing these questions could provide a more nuanced understanding of the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth.

In conclusion (although not stated), the scholarship suggests that institutions are indeed fundamental to long-term economic growth, but the specific mechanisms linking them remain unclear. Future research should explore these mechanisms in various contexts to deepen our understanding of this crucial relationship.
