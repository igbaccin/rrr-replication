This question carries immense importance as understanding the drivers of economic growth can inform policies aimed at promoting development and reducing poverty in various regions.

The relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth is a central theme in contemporary economic discussions. North & Weingast (1989) argue that representative government, characterized by multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, plays a crucial role in economic growth and political freedom. They further suggest that these institutions were instrumental in making possible the economic growth and political development witnessed in Europe during the Middle Ages (North & Weingast_1989: p.28).

Mokyr (2005) supports this view, attributing the economic prosperity of the West to developments in intellectual reasoning, particularly in the realm of "useful knowledge." He argues that changes in intellectual thought were fundamental to the growth of economic reasonableness and the subsequent economic development (Mokyr_2005: p.56).

North (1989) also underscores the significance of institutions, suggesting that they can influence transaction costs, security rights, and market development, all of which contribute to long-term economic growth (North_1989: p.9). Acemoglu et al. (2002) concur, noting a correlation between institutional changes and the distribution of economic prosperity over the past 500 years (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.6).

Structural changes in an economy, as Kuznets (1973) posits, are not only associated with rapid economic growth but also with significant transformations in various aspects of society (Kuznets_1973: p.4). For instance, changes in the economic structure can lead to shifts in family structures and urbanization patterns. Moreover, Kuznets (1973) argues that technological innovations play a crucial role in both economic growth and the necessary social adjustments.

Acemoglu et al. (2001) support this notion by demonstrating a substantial effect of institutions on economic performance (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19). They assert that certain institutional structures, such as those detrimental to investment and economic progress, can hinder economic growth. Conversely, more favorable institutional changes can foster economic prosperity.

Ogilvie (2007) also acknowledges the significance of institutions in economic development, suggesting that they provide the necessary framework for economic efficiency (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). However, he cautions that the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework can impact their effectiveness.

Broadberry & Gupta (2006) support this idea by highlighting the role of the service sector in economic growth during both the early modern period and the modern era (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.9). They argue that the service sector played a crucial role in driving economic growth, as it did during the Industrial Revolution.

Structural change in the economy, as evidenced by the shift from agriculture to industry and services, is a significant factor contributing to long-run economic growth (BroadberryEtal_2015: p.2). This transformation, often associated with industrial revolutions, is supported by the findings of Whatley (2018) who demonstrates that an increase in gunpowder imports correlates with a rise in slave exports, indicating a shift from agricultural to industrial economies (Whatley_2018: p.13).

Moreover, Bryant (2006) highlights the role of mercantilist imperialism and technological advancements in fostering commercial exchange, which is a critical aspect of structural change (Bryant_2006: p.5). The author emphasizes that this fusion led to the growth of economies, providing an environment conducive to long-term economic development.

However, it is essential to note that not all variables in these models adjust to deviations from long-run equilibrium, indicating that the relationship between structural change and economic growth may not necessarily be one of co-integration (Whatley_2018: p.11). This suggests that while structural change plays a crucial role in driving economic growth, other factors might also be at play.

Furthermore, the service sector, as argued by Broadberry and Gupta (2006), played a significant role during both the early modern period and the modern era (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.9). The service sector, particularly in its role as an intermediary between agriculture and industry, could potentially influence the relationship between structural change and economic growth.

Structural change, particularly in the service sector, may not be the primary driver of long-run economic growth as some argue. Instead, institutional changes and ideologies might play a more significant role in shaping economic outcomes.

For instance, Simson (Simson_2019: p.27) notes that during the post-World War II period, there was an emphasis on industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy across various economies. This suggests that institutional changes and ideological shifts could have a profound impact on economic growth, potentially overshadowing the role of structural change.

Similarly, McCloskey (McCloskey_2015: p.8) argues that in France, the lack of bourgeois economic and political successes due to conservative ideologies might have hindered economic growth. The author also points out that Spain's economic growth was throttled for centuries despite being a hegemon during the sixteenth century (McCloskey_2015: p.9). These examples challenge the notion that structural change is the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, as they illustrate how ideologies and institutions can significantly influence economic outcomes.

However, it's important to note that these sources do not discount the potential role of structural change entirely. Instead, they emphasize the need to consider institutional changes and ideological shifts alongside structural changes when examining long-run economic growth. Further exploration is needed to understand the interplay between these factors in driving economic growth over extended periods.

The assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is complicated by several factors, as highlighted in various scholarly works. Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) emphasize the role of initial conditions and factor endowments in shaping long-term economic paths, suggesting that these unique circumstances could have profound and enduring impacts on growth (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4). Similarly, Koepke & Baten (2005) argue that climate might also play a significant role in determining living standards and economic development over extended periods (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.20).

Frankema (2012) further complicates the argument by focusing on colonial education as a key factor explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa, with former British colonies demonstrating faster growth due to their educational lead (Frankema_2012: p.2). Hopkins (2019) discusses how the study of long-run economic and political change was inhibited by totalism and essentialism, which were viewed as Western impositions on the rest of the world (Hopkins_2019: p.4).

Bolt & van Zanden (2014) provide a comprehensive overview of various revisions to nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth data, highlighting that these revisions do not fundamentally change our understanding of the great divergence (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5). Goldin (2006) discusses the evolution and revolution in women's choices and decisions, suggesting that these shifts could impact economic growth over time (Goldin_2006: p.3).

These sources add nuance to the thesis by demonstrating that institutional changes are intertwined with other factors such as initial conditions, climate, education, and colonial history in driving long-run economic growth. Further exploration is needed to understand the intricate interplay between these various elements in shaping economic development over extended periods.

He also suggests that recent theories of economic growth should consider the historical context of economic transition, which includes the transformation from largely agricultural economies to industrial ones (Temin_2002: p.7).

This underscores the importance of technological advancements that enable the formal recognition and accounting for women's work, which can contribute significantly to long-term economic growth.

Williamson (1996) discusses the relationship between schooling and Swedish economic growth before World War I, suggesting that understanding the sources of convergence is fundamental to comprehending policy responses (Williamson_1996: p.25). This underscores the importance of technological advancements in education as a driver of economic growth, particularly in the context of institutional development.

Scholars Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), though writing in different contexts, share insights on the relationship between institutions and economic growth. Both acknowledge that institutions play a crucial role in shaping economic development, with Domar emphasizing the impact of labor-tied institutions on competition among employers, and Bohannan highlighting the influence of Western economic institutions on African societies (Domar_1970: p.4, Bohannan_1959: p.7).

However, they diverge in their focus. Domar primarily discusses labor-tied institutions as a hindrance to economic growth, while Bohannan explores the introduction and impact of Western economic institutions on African societies (Domar_1970: p.4, Bohannan_1959: p.7).

Despite these insights, what remains unresolved is a comprehensive understanding of how specific institutional changes lead to long-term economic growth. For instance, while Domar discusses the negative effects of labor-tied institutions, he does not provide a clear path for transitioning from such systems to ones that promote growth (Domar_1970: p.4). Similarly, Bohannan's work leaves open questions about the long-term implications of Western economic institution introduction on African societies (Bohannan_1959: p.7).
