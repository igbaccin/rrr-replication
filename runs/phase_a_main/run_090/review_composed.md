The debate on the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, particularly in the context of Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA), has been a subject of intense scholarly discourse. A central question that arises is whether institutions are indeed the primary driver of economic growth over the long term, and if so, what implications does this have for understanding SSA's economic development?

Supporting this thesis, Austin (2008) posits that institutions play a crucial role in economic growth, particularly in explaining Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe. He argues that the Atlantic trade reinforced rather than originated Africa's economic disparity, highlighting the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history (Austin_2008: p.1). Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2001) suggest that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress.

On the other hand, Kuznets (1973) suggests that while high aggregate growth rates are associated with rapid changes in economic structure, these changes also bring about unexpected negative results, even within developed countries. This complicates the argument that institutions are the sole cause of long-run economic growth, as it implies that other factors may also play a significant role.

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has led to SSA's absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. They suggest that understanding the extent to which there are still risks of shrinking, which has undermined economic progress in the past, is crucial for determining whether current growth provides a path to convergence (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.18).

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) complicate the argument by noting that understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution becomes subsumed under household production is essential for strengthening women's economic roles. They also highlight that major historical developments in West Africa, where gender and economic growth are concerned, find parallels elsewhere in Africa (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.4).

Komlos (1998) further complicates the argument by suggesting that while institutions play a role in economic growth, physical stature during the Industrial Revolution was significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economics and associated structural changes (Komlos_1998: p.16).

The role of institutions in facilitating long-run economic growth is further substantiated by the works of several scholars, notably North & Weingast (1989), Mokyr (2005), North (1989), Ogilvie (2007), deVries (1994), and Pamuk (2007). These researchers focus on the political and economic factors that underpin growth, emphasizing the importance of institutions in shaping market dynamics and fostering economic freedom.

North & Weingast (1989) argue that the development of markets and economic growth relies not only on the rules governing economic exchange but also on the institutions that enforce these rules and facilitate their change. They demonstrate how absolutist states, such as early modern Spain, created economic conditions that hindered long-term growth due to a lack of constraint in rule enforcement.

Mokyr (2005) suggests that changes in intellectual developments concerning "useful knowledge" are essential for understanding the shift in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West. He highlights the importance of individualism, man-made formal law, corporatism, self governance, and rules determined through an institutionalized process in promoting economic progress.

North (1989) examines the role of institutions in reducing transaction costs by increasing security rights, which, in turn, contributes to market development and economic growth. He also emphasizes the interdependence of individual institutions within a broader framework, suggesting that the failure of one institution can impact others.

Ogilvie (2007) discusses the endogenous nature of institutions within economic analysis, arguing that they play a crucial role in shaping market dynamics and influencing economic development. He also highlights the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework, noting that changes to one institution can have far-reaching effects on others.

deVries (1994) underscores the importance of Europe's urban network in establishing a framework for regional economic development during the early modern period, which enabled industrial growth to occur. Pamuk (2007) supports this argument by demonstrating how changes in population and agriculture paved the way for commercialization and set the stage for future economic growth.

Institutional development played a crucial role in enabling long-run economic growth, particularly during the early modern period. According to Hopkins (2009), diminishing interest in African economic history was partly a product of the decay of economic history in general, indicating that economic history and its related institutions significantly impacted the study of long-term growth (Hopkins_2009: p.4). Furthermore, Hopkins (2009) highlights the need for demonstrating that other causes of solidarity and motives for action can be discounted to support the argument that ethnic fragmentation is responsible for unproductive institutions (Hopkins_2009: p.22).

Abramovitz (1986) supports this claim by suggesting that profits and the inducements to invest and innovate are reduced when economic systems are not conducive to growth, implying that the development of productive institutions is essential for economic progress (Abramovitz_1986: p.16). Additionally, Abramovitz (1986) notes that the elaboration of the mixed economy, which combines market and state mechanisms, is a function of economic growth itself, further emphasizing the interplay between institutions and long-term economic development (Abramovitz_1986: p.20).

Broadberry & Gupta (2006) also support this argument by stating that the recent literature of the new economic geography is consistent with the sceptical view of proto-industrialization as a stage on the road to modern economic growth (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.9). This suggests that the development of productive institutions, such as those enabling industrialization, was essential for long-run economic growth. Moreover, Broadberry & Gupta (2006) point out that the service sector played a crucial role in economic growth during the early modern period, which parallels its importance in contemporary times (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.9).

Institutions and trade are inextricably linked in their influence on long-run economic growth, as supported by both Austin (2008) and Acemoglu et al. (2002). According to Austin (Austin_2008: p.26), "institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth" is an attractive conclusion due to its logical economy. This perspective suggests that trades, such as the Atlantic one, reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe (Austin_2008: p.24).

Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49) argue that understanding economic and political development over the past 500 years necessitates focusing on institutions as a first-order fact. They further emphasize that there has been a change in the distribution of economic prosperity, which could be related to institutions (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5). These findings underscore the critical role of institutions in shaping trade patterns and driving long-term economic growth.

While the prevailing view suggests that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5), some scholars challenge this notion, arguing that other factors such as structural change and ideologies also play significant roles in shaping economic prosperity.

Nicholas Stern (2004) posits that economic growth may not always lead to environmental degradation, contradicting the idea that institutions are solely responsible for long-term growth (Stern_2004: p.1). He further argues that the structure and technology of an economy can impact environmental impacts over the course of development, suggesting a more complex relationship between institutions and economic growth.

Similarly, Simson's work (2019) highlights the importance of industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy in driving economic growth, rather than the protection of existing government jobs or institutional reforms (Simson_2019: p.27). This perspective challenges the notion that institutions are the primary drivers of long-term economic growth.

McCloskey (2015) argues that ideologies and ideas, not interests or institutions, significantly influenced economic growth in various regions, including France and Spain (McCloskey_2015: p.9). This perspective challenges the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

Lastly, van Zanden's work (1999) questions the consensus view that the early modern economy was unable to generate economic growth before the industrial revolution (vanZanden_1999: p.1). Instead, he suggests that wage levels were largely maintained until the end of the 18th century, implying a more complex relationship between institutions and economic growth.

These findings challenge the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by highlighting the roles of structural change, ideologies, and other factors in shaping economic prosperity. Future research should explore these factors further to gain a comprehensive understanding of the drivers of long-term economic growth.

The role of institutions in shaping long-run economic growth is further elucidated by examining their relationship with urbanization. Urban centers often serve as crucibles for institutional development due to the concentration of people and resources, which can lead to the creation and evolution of formal and informal rules that govern society (Sokoloff & Engerman_2000: p.2).

For instance, Sokoloff and Engerman (2000) argue that initial conditions, such as factor endowments and institutional structures, can have profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth. In the context of urbanization, these factors can influence the development of institutions that facilitate trade, property rights, and innovation, all of which are crucial for sustained economic prosperity (Sokoloff & Engerman_2000: p.4).

Temin (2002) also highlights the importance of urbanization in the context of economic growth. He suggests that the exhaustion of cheap labor in rural areas led to a shift towards industrialization, which was facilitated by the development of institutions that enabled the mobilization and organization of labor (Temin_2002: p.4). However, it is worth noting that Temin also emphasizes that recent theories of economic growth consider urbanization as part of economic transition rather than a cause of long-term growth (Temin_2002: p.7).

Urbanization can also play a role in shaping ideologies, which in turn influence institutions and economic growth. For example, Hopkins (2019) contends that urban centers were critical to the emergence of modernist ideologies that emphasized scientific progress, rationality, and efficiency, all of which contributed to the development of institutions conducive to long-term economic growth (Hopkins_2019: p.4).

Frankema (2012) also discusses the role of colonial education in African urban centers, arguing that it played a significant role in shaping institutional structures and promoting economic growth (Frankema_2012: p.2). Williamson (1996) further explores the relationship between education and economic growth, suggesting that the quality of schooling can have a profound impact on long-run growth rates (Williamson_1996: p.20).

The relationship between institutions and economic growth is further nuanced by considering their impact on environmental factors, as highlighted in various sources. Komlos (1998) emphasizes that physical stature during the Industrial Revolution was significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic processes and associated structural changes (Komlos_1998: p.16). This suggests that institutional structures may have played a role in shaping not only economic growth but also human health and development, factors which could contribute to long-run growth rates.

Moreover, Koepke & Baten (2005) discuss the intriguing relationship between climate and human living standards, indicating that environmental quality might be intertwined with institutional structures (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.20). The authors' findings on the stagnation of heights over the period of the Roman Empire contrast common views of economic growth during this time, implying a complex relationship between institutions, environmental quality, and long-run economic development (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.16).

Lastly, Lindert & Williamson (1985) propose that the task of explaining long-run distribution trends reconnects with the search for explanations of growth itself (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.9). This statement implies that institutions and their role in income distribution may be crucial in understanding the causes of economic growth, thus complicating the thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

These sources add nuance to the argument by suggesting a more complex relationship between institutions, environmental factors, and economic growth. Further exploration is needed to better understand how these relationships shape long-term development.

The relationship between institutions and labor is further complicated by several factors, as suggested by Goldin (2006), Feinstein (1998), and Allen et al. (2011). These sources highlight the significance of evolution and revolution in shaping labor markets, the impact of wars on economic growth, and income disparities between regions due to industrialization.

Goldin (2006) emphasizes the importance of understanding the evolution and revolution in labor markets. The author suggests that the turning points in various indicator series are strikingly similar by cohort, indicating a shift from evolution to revolution in labor market dynamics. This shift may have profound implications for long-term economic growth, as it could signal a transition towards more efficient institutions that better facilitate labor productivity and technological advancement.

Feinstein (1998) provides insights into the role of wars and other factors on British economic growth during specific periods. The author discusses the impact of the wars and indicates that in rural areas, changes in farming were not sufficient to disperse the glut of labor until the 1850s and 1860s. This suggests a complex interplay between institutions, environmental factors, and economic growth, with war and agricultural conditions playing significant roles in shaping labor markets and long-term development.

Allen et al. (2011) demonstrate that income disparities between China, Japan, and Europe during the early 20th century were due to long-run stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe. The authors also highlight the rapid growth of the German economy, which translated into rising real wages for workers in Leipzig. These findings indicate that institutions play a crucial role in facilitating economic growth by promoting technological advancement, labor productivity, and income equality.

The scholarship on the role of institutions in long-run economic growth demonstrates a shared understanding that these factors play a significant part in facilitating growth, as evidenced by Domar's (1970) assertion that institutions are among the key determinants of economic progress. However, both Domar and Bohannan (1959) offer tangential insights that highlight differences in their perspectives.

Domar (1970) emphasizes the importance of labor mobility and competition in driving growth, suggesting that when labor is tied to land or an owner, as in the case of serfdom or slavery, economic progress is hindered due to the absence of competition among employers (Domar_1970: p.4). In contrast, Bohannan (1959) focuses on the impact of Western institutions on African societies, particularly in relation to trade and status acquisition (Bohannan_1959: p.7, p.10).

Despite these differences, both scholars agree that institutions can either promote or hinder economic growth. Domar's (1970) analysis of the Russian peasants' enserfment and its negative impact on economic development underscores this point, as does Bohannan's (1959) discussion of how Western institutions affected African societies.

However, a key area that remains unresolved is understanding the specific mechanisms through which institutions influence long-run economic growth. While scholars like Domar and Bohannan provide valuable insights, further research is needed to clarify the intricate relationships between institutions, technological advancement, labor productivity, income equality, and economic growth.
