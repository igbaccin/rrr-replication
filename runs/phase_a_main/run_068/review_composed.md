The central question under investigation in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth, a thesis that has garnered significant attention among scholars. The stakes are high, as understanding the root causes of economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at fostering prosperity and reducing poverty in various regions.

Support for this assertion comes from several sources. Austin (2008) argues that institutions play a crucial role in long-run economic growth, stating that they reinforce rather than originate Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe (and parts of Asia). Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) focus on the political factors underpinning economic growth and the development of markets, demonstrating how institutions played a necessary role in making possible economic growth and political freedom.

On the other hand, some scholars offer critiques or complications to this thesis. Broadberry & Gardner (2022), for instance, highlight that Sub-Saharan Africa has been absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth due to a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) argue that understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution becomes subsumed under the category of household production is essential for policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles.

As the argument continues, it becomes clear that while institutions are undeniably important factors in long-term economic growth, their impact may be qualified or nuanced in various contexts. The debate surrounding this issue remains vibrant and ongoing, with scholars continuing to delve into the complex interplay between institutions, economic growth, and development.

The assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is substantiated by a multitude of scholarly works. North & Weingast (1989) argue that representative government, specifically an explicit set of multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are intimately related to economic growth and political freedom (p.2). They further emphasize that institutions played a necessary role in making possible economic growth and political freedom (p.30).

Acemoglu et al. (2001) support this perspective by demonstrating a large effect of institutions on economic performance (p.19), with detrimental institutions being harmful to investment and economic progress (p.27). They also suggest that differences in mortality rates are not the main cause of variation in institutions (p.3).

Mokyr (2005) supports this thesis by highlighting how elements of a progressive society, such as individualism, man-made formal law, corporatism, self governance, and rules determined through an institutionalized process, contributed to economic growth (p.56). He also suggests that the change in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West must be explained through developments in the intellectual realm concerning "useful knowledge" (p.4).

North (1989) supports this argument by focusing on how institutions can reduce transaction costs, leading to increased security rights and contributing to economic growth (p.9). He also examines centralized enforcement mechanisms of Spain to understand economic growth (p.1).

Ogilvie (2007) supports the thesis by acknowledging the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework, suggesting that the failure of one institution can impact the entire system (p.27). He also discusses how geographical characteristics and other factors have influenced economic development (p.2).

Whatley (2018) contributes to this debate by presenting econometric results that suggest a correlation between structural change and growth, although the model is not one of co-integration (p.3, p.11).

These sources collectively provide robust evidence supporting the thesis that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. The complexity and interplay between institutions, economic growth, and development continue to be explored by scholars, ensuring that this debate remains vibrant and ongoing.

Institutions and colonialism have played a significant role in shaping long-term economic growth, as evidenced by several scholarly works. Austin (2008) argues that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, asserting that trades, such as the Atlantic one, reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe. This perspective underscores the idea that colonialism, through its influence on institutions, has had a lasting impact on economic growth.

Further supporting this notion is Frankema & van Waijenburg (2012), who highlight the role of West African commercial centers in attracting economic migrants year after year. The high population growth rates observed in these centers suggest that they experienced structural change, which is often associated with economic growth. The authors also demonstrate that real wage rises in these areas could be attributed to high land-labor ratios and overpopulation, indicating a self-sustaining economic dynamic.

These findings underscore the importance of institutions in understanding long-term economic growth, particularly in the context of colonialism. By examining the historical development of institutions under colonial rule, we can gain insights into how these structures have influenced economic growth patterns that persist to this day. Future research should continue to explore the complex interplay between institutions, economic growth, and development, especially in regions heavily impacted by colonialism.

The interplay between institutions and labor markets significantly influences long-term economic growth, as evidenced by various studies. According to Kuznets (1973), structural change is not only associated with rapid economic growth but also with substantial shifts in societal aspects such as family structures and urbanization (Kuznets_1973: p.4). Furthermore, the succession of technological innovations during modern economic growth necessitates social adjustments that can impact economic structure (Kuznets_1973: p.6).

Acemoglu et al. (2002) support this notion by arguing that institutions play a crucial role in understanding and evaluating long-term development over the past 500 years (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49). They also noticed an alteration in the distribution of economic prosperity, which could be linked to institutional changes (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.6). The question of whether this reversal is related to institutions remains unanswered, indicating a need for further exploration (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5).

The argument that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth faces challenges, as highlighted by Simson (2019) and Bryceson (2002). These authors suggest that other factors such as structural change, livelihood diversification, and traditional hierarchical orders may play significant roles in economic growth.

For instance, Simson (Simson_2019: p.25) points out an expansion of state expenditure and deficits in the 1960s and 1970s during a period of high economic growth in Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda, which questions the emphasis on institutional changes as the sole driver of economic prosperity. Similarly, Bryceson (Bryceson_2002: p.9) notes that men's labor time and economic returns from cash cropping have diminished, undermining their role as family provisioners, suggesting that factors beyond institutions could impact economic growth.

Moreover, Simson (Simson_2019: p.4) raises doubts about the elimination of spending inefficiencies, leakages, and costly delays in reform being enough to sustain investment levels and reorient economies, implying that other factors might be instrumental in driving economic growth. Additionally, Bryceson (Bryceson_2002: p.2) critiques expensive large-scale surveys for failing to record or generate an understanding of livelihood diversification, indicating that this phenomenon may contribute significantly to economic growth beyond institutional changes.

These findings challenge the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by suggesting that other factors such as structural change, livelihood diversification, and traditional hierarchical orders might play crucial roles in economic development. Further exploration of these factors could provide valuable insights into understanding economic growth dynamics.

The role of institutions in long-run economic growth is a complex and multifaceted issue, as highlighted by several scholars. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) shed light on the underestimation of women's contributions to economic growth due to their labor being subsumed under household production. This oversight undermines policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles, a crucial factor in development dynamics.

Temin (2002) offers insights into the relationship between economic growth and labor supply, arguing that the exhaustion of cheap labor can lead to slower growth. He also suggests that recent theories of economic growth should consider historical transitions, further complicating the understanding of long-run economic growth.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) discuss the interplay between economic growth and inequality, suggesting that the relationship is complex and may not have a straightforward causal direction. They also highlight the need for extensive empirical data to address trade-offs between economic growth and equality.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) emphasize the role of initial conditions, or factor endowments, in shaping long-run economic growth paths. They argue that unique country-specific conditions can have profound and enduring impacts on growth, complicating the search for universal explanations.

Koepke & Baten (2005) explore the relationship between climate and human living standards, suggesting that this factor may influence economic development. However, their findings show that during the Roman Empire, living standards did not necessarily increase with economic growth, adding nuance to our understanding of long-run economic growth dynamics.

Bolt & van Zanden (2014) provide revisions to historical growth data for various countries, emphasizing the importance of considering a broad range of factors in explaining long-term economic growth. They also highlight the complexity of the great divergence between Europe and other regions, complicating our understanding of this historical phenomenon.

These sources add nuance to the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by emphasizing the complex roles played by gender, labor supply, initial conditions, climate, and historical data revisions. Further exploration of these factors could provide valuable insights into understanding economic growth dynamics more fully.

The role of education in long-run economic growth is further illuminated by examining colonial educational systems, as discussed by Frankema (2012). Bolt and Bezemer (2009) contend that the educational lead established by former British colonies significantly contributed to their subsequent growth. However, Frankema (Frankema_2012: p.19) suggests that the extent of British "liberalism" was influenced by local political and economic contexts, implying a complex relationship between colonial education and long-term economic development.

Additionally, Goldin (2006) highlights the importance of labor market institutions in understanding growth dynamics. The author emphasizes that the distinction between evolution and revolution in women's choices and decisions plays a crucial role in this context (Goldin_2006: p.1). This perspective underscores the need to consider gender-related factors when examining long-run economic growth, suggesting that the influence of institutions extends beyond traditional economic structures.

Hopkins (2019) sheds light on the impact of superstructures during periods of economic decline. As economic bases weakened, the importance of superstructural elements, such as education and political systems, increased (Hopkins_2019: p.9). This observation underscores the resilience of institutions during times of economic adversity and emphasizes their enduring role in shaping long-term growth trajectories.

The literature on industrialization and economic growth further substantiates the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Komlos (1998) delves into the concept of modern economic growth, emphasizing its Kuznetsian sense, which encompasses structural changes such as population growth, urbanization, and income inequality (Komlos_1998: p.2). These structural changes are indeed crucial components of institutional frameworks that foster long-term economic growth.

Moreover, Komlos' study reveals an interesting correlation between the Industrial Revolution and biological growth processes, suggesting that short-term fluctuations in economics might have influenced biological development during this period (Komlos_1998: p.16). This observation underscores the interplay between economic institutions and other aspects of society, further highlighting their significant role in shaping long-term growth trajectories.

Austin's work (2007) also sheds light on this topic by focusing on the role of transportation infrastructure, specifically railways, in promoting economic growth (Austin_2007: p.8). The cheaper cost of railways compared to lorries provided a fundamental advantage during the early stages of industrialization, contributing to economic development. Furthermore, Austin discusses how population growth was faster after 1918, which might be attributed to institutional changes that encouraged population expansion (Austin_2007: p.12).

The works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), albeit tangentially related to the central thesis, provide valuable insights into the relationship between institutions and economic growth. Both scholars agree that institutional changes play a significant role in fostering economic development. Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) emphasizes that institutions can influence factors of production, such as labor and capital, which are crucial for growth. Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.10), on the other hand, discusses how Western impact on African societies extended beyond economic institutions to include cultural changes, further underscoring the importance of institutions in shaping economic trajectories.

However, they diverge in their focus and the specific aspects of institutions they explore. Domar primarily concentrates on labor-tied institutions and their impact on competition among employers (Domar_1970: p.4), while Bohannan delves into the social organization of African societies before and after Western influence (Bohannan_1959: p.7, p.13).

Despite these contributions, several questions remain unresolved. For instance, Domar's work does not explicitly link labor-tied institutions to economic growth, leaving room for further investigation into the mechanisms through which such institutions contribute to long-term economic development. Similarly, Bohannan's focus on cultural changes raises questions about how these changes interact with economic institutions and impact economic growth over time.
