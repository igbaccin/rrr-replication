LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question in the literature review on institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is whether institutional factors are indeed the primary driver of economic development over extended periods. This question carries significant implications for understanding historical and contemporary economic disparities between regions, informing policies aimed at promoting growth, and shaping perspectives on the role of institutions in fostering societal progress.

Several scholars support the thesis that institutions play a crucial role in long-term economic growth. For instance, Kuznets (1973) posits that technological innovations and social adjustments characteristic of modern economic growth are major factors affecting economic and social structure. North & Weingast (1989) argue that the fundamental institutions of representative government, such as multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are intimately related to economic growth and political freedom. Acemoglu et al. (2001) also support this stance, demonstrating a large effect of institutions on economic performance.

On the other hand, some scholars complicate the argument by emphasizing that historical circumstances, such as the Atlantic trade, may reinforce rather than originate economic disparities between regions, casting doubt on the notion that institutions are solely responsible for long-run growth (Austin_2008). Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that a lack of data before 1950 has made Sub-Saharan Africa absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth, suggesting that the role of institutions in this context may be more complex than previously thought. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) further nuance the discussion by highlighting how understanding the processes by which women's economic contributions have been subsumed under household production is essential for formulating policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles.

As the debate continues, it becomes evident that while institutions play a significant role in long-run economic growth, other factors such as historical circumstances and societal structures must also be considered to fully understand economic development over extended periods. The stakes of this discussion are high, as understanding the drivers of economic growth is crucial for policymakers seeking to promote sustainable development and reduce global disparities.

The significance of institutions in fostering long-term economic growth is evident from various perspectives, as supported by several scholarly works. North & Weingast (1989) emphasize the role of political institutions in enabling economic growth and market development. They argue that representative government, with multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, plays a crucial role in this process (North&Weingast_1989: p.2).

Mokyr (2005) also supports the notion by highlighting how intellectual developments contributed to changes in economic growth patterns in the West. He suggests that the change in the rate and nature of economic growth can be explained through advancements in useful knowledge (Mokyr_2005: p.4).

North (1989) contributes to this discussion by focusing on how institutions impact transaction costs, which in turn influence market development and economic growth (North_1989: p.9). Ogilvie (2007) acknowledges the interdependency of individual institutions within a broader framework, emphasizing that understanding the efficiency theory requires considering this interconnectedness (Ogilvie_2007: p.27).

Acemoglu et al. (2002) provide evidence for the reversal in economic prosperity distribution over time and suggest a possible link to institutions (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5). Abramovitz (1986) discusses the role of macroeconomic conditions in encouraging capital investment and effective demand, which are essential drivers of economic growth (Abramovitz_1986: p.7).

The evidence provided in Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) supports the notion that institutions are fundamental to long-run economic growth. These authors argue that unique initial conditions, or factor endowments, have had profound and enduring impacts on economic growth across societies (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4). For instance, the United States and Canada achieved sustained economic growth during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, while other countries did not until late in the nineteenth century (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.12). This divergence can be attributed to differences in institutions and conditions relevant to growth such as property rights, political power, and economic organization (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.2).

Whatley (2018) also provides evidence supporting the link between institutions and long-run economic growth. The author's econometric model demonstrates that gunpowder imports, a proxy for institutional quality, are positively correlated with slave exports, which are negatively associated with economic growth (Whatley_2018: p.13). This suggests that better institutions, as represented by higher levels of gunpowder imports, lead to lower levels of slave exports and higher economic growth.

Broadberry & Gupta (2006) support the thesis by discussing the role of the service sector in economic growth during the early modern period. They argue that the service sector played a crucial role in economic growth as it provided essential infrastructure, financial services, and labor for manufacturing industries (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.9). The authors also highlight the importance of institutions in determining economic development by noting that observations based on grain wages alone may not accurately reflect the level of economic development in different regions (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.24).

The relationship between institutions and economic growth is further substantiated by the findings of Kuznets (1973) and Acemoglu et al. (2001). Kuznets (Kuznets_1973: p.4) notes that rapid changes in economic structure, often associated with modern economic growth, are accompanied by significant social innovations that provide the necessary adjustments. This suggests that institutions play a crucial role in facilitating technological advancement and promoting economic development.

Moreover, Kuznets (Kuznets_1973: p.6) emphasizes the importance of tech-innovations in modern economic growth, implying that these innovations depend on the presence of appropriate institutions to ensure their effective implementation. Additionally, Kuznets (Kuznets_1973: p.11) highlights unexpected negative results even within developed countries, which could be attributed to inadequate or unfavorable institutions hindering the full potential of modern technology utilization.

Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3) argue that differences in mortality rates are not the only or main cause of variation in institutions. Instead, they propose that more fundamental institutions have a significant impact on property rights and economic progress. Their findings demonstrate a large effect of institutions on economic performance, with unfavorable institutions being detrimental to investment and overall economic development (Acemoglu et al. 2001: p.27, p.19).

Structural change, rather than institutions, may play a significant role in long-run economic growth. McCloskey (2015) contends that ideas and ideologies, not institutions, were the driving forces behind economic progress in France and Spain, two countries with unfavorable institutional environments but experienced periods of economic growth (McCloskey_2015: p.9). For instance, in France, reactionary parties prospered despite their disinterest in economic growth, yet the country still underwent significant economic development due to ideological influences (McCloskey_2015: p.9).

Furthermore, Bryceson (2002) notes that women's economic and social spheres are influenced by their adherence to traditional hierarchical orders within communities (Bryceson_2002: p.9). This suggests that societal structures, rather than institutions, might shape economic growth by affecting the roles and opportunities of different groups within a society.

In areas where land shortages are not prevalent, such as Njombe, Tanzania, households with restricted land access have resorted to non-agricultural economic activities or migrant labor due to diminishing returns from cash cropping (Bryceson_2002: p.11). This shift in economic focus indicates that structural changes may be prompted by factors other than institutions, leading to growth.

These findings challenge the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, suggesting instead that structural change and societal transformations might play a more significant role in driving economic progress. However, further investigation is needed to fully understand the interplay between institutions, ideas, and structures in shaping economic development.

The argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is further complicated by the role of structural changes and the interplay between ideas, institutions, and societal transformations. Structural changes, such as industrialization and urbanization, have been shown to correlate significantly with economic growth (Austin_2008: p.1). However, it is essential to acknowledge that these changes may not only be a result of strong institutions but also a cause in shaping them.

For instance, the exhaustion of cheap labor due to structural changes like industrialization was found to slow economic growth (Temin_2002: p.4). Moreover, the contribution of African women to economic growth and development was largely dependent on the economic provision of the husband, suggesting that household structures and gender roles play a crucial role in shaping economic outcomes (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.9).

In this context, it becomes evident that institutions, ideas, and structural changes are intertwined and influence each other in shaping long-run economic growth. The British classical economists recognized this interdependence when they sought to explain both long-run distribution trends and economic growth (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1).

Furthermore, the relationship between schooling and Swedish economic growth before World War I demonstrates how societal transformations can impact institutions and economic development (Williamson_1996: p.20). Understanding this interplay is crucial for a comprehensive understanding of the factors driving long-run economic growth.

The relationship between institutions and technological advancement significantly contributes to the understanding of long-run economic growth. Komlos (1998) argues that physical stature during the Industrial Revolution was influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic processes, suggesting a correlation between economic development and biological growth. This implies that institutional changes could have a profound impact on societal transformations, which in turn may drive technological advancement and economic growth.

Austin (2007) further supports this notion, stating that the railway had a fundamental advantage over lorries in terms of cost-effectiveness until the mid-1920s. This suggests that institutions can play a crucial role in facilitating the adoption of new technologies, thus fostering economic growth.

Clark and Jacks (2007) highlight the importance of technological advancement in the coal industry during the Industrial Revolution. They argue that the cliometric account of the coal industry demonstrates how technological improvements allowed for a response to increases in demand, which was vital for economic growth.

Allen et al. (2011) illustrate income disparities between Europe and China during the early 20th century, attributing them to long-run stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe. This indicates that institutional differences can lead to significant variations in economic growth rates, further emphasizing the importance of institutions in driving long-term economic development.

The relationship between institutions and economic growth is further nuanced when considering the impact of climate and environmental factors on human living standards, as suggested by Koepke & Baten (2005). They argue that despite the Roman Empire's economic growth, there was no significant increase in heights among its population, which could indicate stagnant living standards contrary to the common view (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.16). This finding underscores the importance of institutions in driving long-term economic development, as they may have failed to adapt to changing environmental conditions and thus hindered growth.

Moreover, Hopkins (2019) contends that postmodernism, which focused on cultural aspects rather than economic and political change, limited the diversity of independent African viewpoints in studying long-run economic and political change (Hopkins_2019: p.4). This omission may have obscured the role of institutions in Africa's economic growth, further emphasizing their significance in driving development across different regions.

Bolt & van Zanden (2014) suggest that revisions of nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth rates do not fundamentally change our understanding of the great divergence between Europe and other parts of the world (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5). However, they also acknowledge that economic conditions in Java were influenced by both institutional factors and environmental factors such as climate and soil quality (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.15). This implies that while institutions are crucial for long-term economic growth, they do not operate in isolation but interact with other factors such as the environment.

The scholarly consensus on the fundamental role of institutions in long-run economic growth is reinforced by both Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959). Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) emphasizes that institutions, as factors of production, can significantly influence economic growth. He illustrates this through examples such as the Russian peasants' enserfment and the economic cost of slaveholding in the Cotton Belt (Domar_1970: p.4, p.16). Similarly, Bohannan (1959: p.2, p.7) highlights that Western economic institutions had a profound impact on African societies, particularly in areas like trade and status acquisition.

However, both scholars also acknowledge the interplay between institutions and other factors. Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) notes that when labor is tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, suggesting environmental factors can affect institutional development and economic growth. Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.10) acknowledges that the Western impact was not limited to economic institutions but extended to other aspects of society as well.

Despite their contributions, some issues remain unresolved. For instance, while both scholars agree on the importance of institutions, they do not delve deeply into how institutional changes lead to structural change or long-term economic growth. Furthermore, neither scholar provides a comprehensive analysis of how various types of institutions contribute differently to economic growth, leaving room for future research in this area.
