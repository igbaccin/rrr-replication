LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question in the literature review on institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth revolves around understanding the role and impact of institutional structures on a nation's economic development over time. This question holds significant importance, as it can provide insights into why some countries have experienced sustained economic growth while others have struggled.

Several scholars support the notion that institutions are indeed the primary driver of long-term economic growth. Austin (2008) argues that institutions play a crucial role in determining a country's economic position, both historically and in the present day (Austin_2008: p.26). Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) demonstrate how representative government institutions and the common law courts have contributed to economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28). Acemoglu et al. (2001) also support this view, showing that certain institutional structures can either promote or hinder investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27).

However, not all scholars agree that institutions are the sole determinant of long-run economic growth. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate this argument by pointing out the lack of data on Sub-Saharan Africa's economic performance before 1950, which has led to its absence in recent debates about comparative long-term growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance by discussing the challenges of understanding women's economic contributions and the impact of gender roles on economic growth in Africa (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3).

These contrasting viewpoints highlight the complexity of the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth. While some argue that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause, others suggest that other factors, such as historical data or gender roles, may also play significant roles in shaping a nation's economic trajectory over time. This ongoing debate underscores the need for further research to fully understand the intricate interplay between institutions and long-term economic growth.

The role of institutions in shaping long-term economic growth is further substantiated by various scholars. Kuznets (1973) posits that rapid changes in economic structure, which are characteristic of modern economic growth, are associated with rapid changes in other aspects of society (Kuznets_1973: p.4). He also emphasizes the non-conventional costs of economic growth and how these costs can have negative effects even within developed countries while less developed countries struggle to utilize modern technology (Kuznets_1973: p.11).

North & Weingast (1989) argue that the fundamental institutions of representative government, such as multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are crucial for economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28). They focus on the political factors underpinning economic growth and the development of markets, emphasizing not only the rules governing economic exchange but also the institutions governing how these rules are enforced and changed (North&Weingast_1989: p.2).

North (1989) discusses how centralized enforcement mechanisms can impact economic growth. He suggests that increased security rights and a well-developed market can lead to economic growth (North_1989: p.9). Ogilvie (2007) supports this view, arguing that the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework plays a significant role in economic growth (Ogilvie_2007: p.27).

Pamuk (2007) highlights how the Black Death and modern economic growth led to significant changes in wages and incomes during the last millennium, paving the way for the commercialization of agriculture (Pamuk_2007: p.4). These findings underscore the crucial role institutions play in shaping a nation's economic trajectory over time, providing further evidence that they are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

Trade between Africa and Western Europe, as outlined by Austin (2008), provides additional support for the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Austin argues that these trades reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe. This suggests that the institutional frameworks in place during these trading periods significantly influenced the economic outcomes, underscoring their importance in determining long-term growth trajectories.

Austin further highlights that the theoretical conclusion of institutions as 'the' fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is attractive due to its logical economy (Austin_2008: p.24). This implies a strong relationship between institutional structures and economic development, reinforcing the notion that institutions play a crucial role in shaping a nation's economic trajectory over time.

Moreover, Austin emphasizes the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history to reveal relationships that recur due to similar situations or path dependencies (Austin_2008: p.1). This underscores the significance of institutions in determining economic growth patterns over extended periods, providing further evidence for their role as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

The persistence of institutions plays a crucial role in shaping economic performance over time, as demonstrated by Acemoglu and colleagues (2001). Their research reveals that more fundamental institutions, such as property rights and political power structures, significantly impact economic growth. For instance, the authors argue that these institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress in certain regions (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27).

Moreover, Acemoglu and colleagues challenge the notion that mortality rates are the primary cause of variation in institutions. Instead, they suggest that differences in mortality rates might be a consequence rather than a cause of institutional arrangements (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3). This perspective underscores the importance of considering the role of institutions as a fundamental driver of economic growth patterns.

Furthermore, Acemoglu and colleagues provide empirical evidence to support their claims. Their findings indicate that overall, the results show a large effect of institutions on economic performance (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19). This suggests that structural change correlates with growth, further reinforcing the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

Despite evidence suggesting a strong correlation between institutions and economic growth (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19), some sources challenge this thesis by highlighting instances where economic growth occurred without significant institutional reforms. For instance, McCloskey (2015) argues that ideologies and ideas, rather than institutions, played a crucial role in economic growth in France and Spain during certain periods. Similarly, Bryceson (2002) demonstrates that traditional hierarchical orders within communities can influence women's economic and social spheres, suggesting that institutional changes may not always be necessary for economic growth.

Moreover, Stern (2004) questions the Economic Kuznets Curve (EKC) hypothesis, which posits that economic growth leads to environmental degradation but eventually results in improved environmental conditions. Stern argues that this assumption may not hold true, as irreversible environmental damage could potentially reduce future income levels, thereby hindering economic growth.

Simson (2019) also critiques the emphasis on institutional reforms by showing how Kenyan, Tanzanian, and Ugandan state expenditure and deficits expanded during a period of high economic growth in the 1960s and 1970s. Simson suggests that while there may have been spending inefficiencies, their elimination might not have been sufficient to sustain the investment levels of that era or reorient these economies.

These sources challenge the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by providing examples where economic growth occurred without significant institutional reforms or where institutional changes did not necessarily lead to sustained growth. Further investigation is needed to better understand the complex interplay between institutions, ideas, and economic growth.

The complexity of understanding the relationship between institutions, ideas, and economic growth is further nuanced by several factors highlighted in various sources. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) suggest that Sub-Saharan Africa's absence from recent debates about long-run growth can be attributed to a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950, indicating that the region's historical growth patterns may not yet have been fully understood. This underscores the importance of comprehensive data in evaluating the impact of institutions and ideas on economic growth.

Komlos (1998) discusses the concept of modern economic growth and its relationship with physical stature during the Industrial Revolution, implying that biological growth processes were significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic conditions. This finding complicates the argument that institutional changes are the sole cause of long-run economic growth, as it suggests that other factors such as short-term economic cycles might also play a role.

Temin (2002) presents the idea that the exhaustion of cheap labor caused economic growth to slow and that recent theories of economic growth should consider economic transition periods. This perspective complicates the argument by suggesting that the relationship between institutions, ideas, and economic growth may not be linear but rather cyclical, with different factors influencing growth at various stages.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that the task of explaining long-run distribution trends rejoins the search for explanations of growth itself, further emphasizing the interconnectedness between economic growth and income distribution. This finding complicates the argument by suggesting that understanding the role of institutions in promoting economic growth must also consider their impact on income distribution.

Koepke & Baten (2005) explore the relationship between climate and human living standards, which could potentially influence institutional development and, consequently, long-run economic growth. This finding complicates the argument by introducing an environmental factor that might interact with institutions to shape economic growth patterns.

Finally, Bolt & van Zanden (2014) discuss revisions of nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth, emphasizing that these do not fundamentally change our understanding of long-run economic growth. This finding complicates the argument by suggesting that while institutional changes are important, they may not always lead to significant shifts in long-term economic growth patterns.

The contribution of African women to economic growth and development, as highlighted by Akyeampong & Fofack (2014), has been historically undervalued due to their labor being subsumed under household production and often unaccounted for in formal economic analyses. This finding adds nuance to the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, as it underscores the importance of recognizing and valuing the economic roles of all members within a society, particularly women.

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) note that the economic contribution of African women was largely dependent on the economic provision of their husbands. This dependence, coupled with the subsuming of women's labor under household production, has resulted in policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles being undermined. The authors argue that understanding these processes is crucial for creating effective policies that empower women economically.

Moreover, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) highlight that the major historical developments in West Africa, which find parallels elsewhere in Africa, have significant implications for gender and economic growth. By acknowledging these developments and their impact on women's economic roles, we can better understand how institutional changes might lead to shifts in long-term economic growth patterns, particularly in relation to gender equality.

The work of Sokoloff and Engerman (2000) sheds light on the complexities surrounding the relationship between institutions, economic growth, and factor endowments. They propose that unique conditions in individual countries could have significant and lasting impacts on long-term growth patterns.

Sokoloff and Engerman (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4) argue that "conventional economic factors have certainly not been ignored," yet they emphasize the importance of institutions, particularly those relevant to growth such as property rights, contract enforcement, and political stability. Their research indicates that these institutional differences across societies can explain contrasting records in growth.

Moreover, Sokoloff and Engerman (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.12) highlight the divergence in paths between countries like the United States and Canada, which achieved sustained economic growth during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, and others that did not manage to attain this goal until later. This suggests that institutional changes might have played a crucial role in shaping long-term economic growth patterns.

In addition, Sokoloff and Engerman (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.15) note the importance of institutions in fostering economic freedom, which is often associated with economic growth. They argue that "institutions, economic growth and freedom" are interconnected and have historical implications for economic welfare.

By acknowledging these developments and their impact on economic growth patterns, we can further understand how institutional changes might lead to shifts in long-term economic growth patterns, particularly in relation to gender equality. However, more research is needed to examine the specific role of institutions in shaping women's economic roles and opportunities over time.

Institutions, as crucial drivers of long-term economic growth, have been a subject of interest for numerous scholars, with Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) providing valuable insights. Both authors acknowledge the significant role institutions play in shaping economic structures.

Domar (1970) emphasizes the impact of labor-tied institutions on competition among employers, suggesting that such arrangements can hinder growth due to reduced incentives for efficiency and innovation. He also highlights the influence of institutions on the distribution of factors of production, arguing that when labor is tied to land or owners, it can lead to imbalances in economic development (Domar_1970: p.4).

On the other hand, Bohannan (1959) focuses on the impact of Western institutions on African societies, particularly in relation to trade and status acquisition. He notes that these institutions often centered around slave dealing and curing, significantly altering traditional social structures (Bohannan_1959: p.7). However, Bohannan also acknowledges the limitations imposed by institutional structures, such as the restriction of women's roles due to societal norms (Bohannan_1959: p.13).

Despite their shared focus on institutions and economic growth, both authors diverge in their primary areas of interest. Domar primarily explores the impact of labor-tied institutions on competition and factor distribution, while Bohannan examines the influence of Western institutions on African societies, particularly in terms of trade and status.

However, both works leave several questions unanswered. For instance, there is a need for further research to understand how specific institutional changes might impact long-term economic growth patterns, particularly in relation to gender equality (as previously discussed). Additionally, more studies could delve deeper into the interplay between various types of institutions and their effects on different aspects of economic development.
