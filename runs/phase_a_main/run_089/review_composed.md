The question of the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth has been a subject of extensive debate among economists, with institutions emerging as a prominent contender. This review examines literature on the role of institutions in economic growth, focusing on four key sources: Austin (2008), Kuznets (1973), Broadberry & Gardner (2022), Akyeampong & Fofack (2014), Acemoglu et al. (2001), and Komlos (1998).

Austin (2008) supports the thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, arguing that they reinforce and originate economic positions compared to Western Europe. Kuznets (1973) concurs, highlighting the association between rapid changes in economic structure and growth, as well as the impact of social innovations on economic development.

However, not all sources support this position unconditionally. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate the argument by pointing out that the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has led to Sub-Saharan Africa's absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance, emphasizing the need to understand the processes by which women's economic contributions are subsumed under household production and their labor formally unaccounted for.

Acemoglu et al. (2001) support the thesis, demonstrating that institutions have a large effect on economic performance. However, Komlos (1998) complicates the argument by using the concept of modern economic growth in a Kuznetsian sense and questioning the timing of its onset and the Industrial Revolution's conceptualization.

The stakes of this debate are significant, as understanding the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting development in underdeveloped regions. The literature suggests that while institutions play a crucial role in economic growth, other factors such as social innovations and women's economic contributions also require consideration. Future research should continue to explore these complexities to advance our understanding of the drivers of long-run economic growth. The influence of institutions on long-run economic growth is further substantiated by various scholars, notably Douglas North and his collaborators. North & Weingast (1989) assert that the fundamental institutions of representative government, such as an explicit set of multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are intimately related to economic growth and political freedom (p.28). They highlight that these institutions played a crucial role in making possible economic growth and political freedom.

In agreement, Mokyr (2005) posits that elements of a progressive society, such as individualism, man-made formal law, corporatism, self governance, and rules determined through an institutionalized process, were significant contributors to the economic growth in the West (p.56). He suggests that changes in the intellectual realm concerning "useful knowledge" were key factors in the change of the rate and nature of economic growth in the West.

Moreover, North (1989) argues that centralized enforcement mechanisms, such as those employed by Spain, can significantly impact economic growth (p.1). He emphasizes that changes in transaction costs reflect increased security rights, which in turn contribute to market development and long-term economic growth.

The works of Austin (2008), Kuznets (1973), North & Weingast (1989), Mokyr (2005), and North (1989) collectively support the notion that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. These studies underscore the importance of institutions in shaping economic structures, fostering technological innovations, and promoting political freedom, thereby driving economic progress over time.

Institutions play a crucial role in shaping technological advancements, which in turn influence long-run economic growth. Acemoglu et al. (2001) highlight the detrimental impact of institutions that hinder investment and economic progress (p.27). They argue that more fundamental institutions, such as property rights, significantly affect the rate of economic performance (p.19). Furthermore, Acemovitz (1986) suggests that macroeconomic conditions encouraging capital investment are essential for productivity growth (p.7).

These studies underscore the importance of institutions in fostering technological innovations. Acemoglu et al. (2001) assert that differences in mortality rates are not the main cause of variation in institutions, which implies that other institutional factors such as property rights and political structures play a more significant role in driving technological advancements (p.3). Abramovitz (1986) also emphasizes the importance of economic growth on the elaboration of mixed economies, suggesting a reciprocal relationship between institutions and technology (p.20).

The significance of institutions in driving long-run economic growth is further underscored by Acemoglu et al. (2002), who assert that institutions are a "first-order fact" in understanding both economic and political development over the past 500 years (p.49). They also note that their findings on the relationship between institutions and economic growth are novel, as they document a change in the distribution of economic prosperity that has not been observed or documented in other works (p.6). This shift in prosperity is related to institutional changes, suggesting that the development and evolution of institutions play a crucial role in economic growth.

Bryant (2006) supports this notion by highlighting the importance of environments conducive to commercial exchange in fostering economic growth. He argues that a fusion of mercantilist imperialism with technological capitalism was instrumental in the development and growth of early European economies (p.5). This fusion, which led to structural changes, can be seen as an example of how institutions can facilitate technological advancements and economic growth.

Challenging the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, some scholars argue that certain factors associated with colonialism may have hindered economic development in colonized regions. For instance, Stern (2004) posits that economic growth could potentially lead to environmental degradation, rather than being a catalyst for technological advancements and growth as suggested by the proponents of institutional theory (p.1). Similarly, McCloskey (2015) contends that ideologies and interests, particularly those that suppress economic growth in favor of religious conservatism, can hinder economic development (p.9).

Furthermore, Simson (2019) suggests that excessive government spending and deficits during the 1960s and 1970s in certain African countries may have contributed to their economic crisis rather than facilitating growth (p.4). Humphries (2013) also notes that factors such as lower fertility rates in France compared to Britain could have impacted the division of labor, workshop production, and the emergence of the factory system, which are often associated with economic growth (p.18, p.19).

These sources challenge the view that institutions are solely responsible for long-run economic growth by suggesting that factors related to colonialism and ideologies may have negatively influenced economic development in certain regions. However, further research is needed to fully understand the complex interplay between institutions, colonialism, and economic growth.

The relationship between institutions and technology in relation to long-run economic growth is a subject of debate, with some scholars challenging the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of this growth. Clark & Jacks (2007) argue that technological advancements played a crucial role in the coal industry during the Industrial Revolution, enabling it to respond effectively to increases in demand (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.4). This suggests that technology may have been a significant driver of economic growth, rather than institutions.

Similarly, Feinstein (1998) notes that changes in farming and migration patterns were not sufficient to alleviate labor gluts in rural areas until the mid-19th century (Feinstein_1998: p.28). This implies that certain economic shifts may have occurred independently of institutional frameworks, challenging the idea that institutions are the primary catalyst for long-term growth.

Moreover, Clark & Jacks (2007) argue that their account of the coal industry serves as an implicit counterfactual to other theories, suggesting that technological progress could have occurred even in the absence of certain institutional structures (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.27). This challenge to the thesis highlights the potential for economic growth to occur independently of institutions, at least in specific industries and contexts.

Lastly, Feinstein (1998) discusses the long plateau of British economic growth from 1780 to 1850, a period characterized by political stability and demographic indicators consistent with other economic measures (Feinstein_1998: p.2). This stable growth suggests that institutions may not have been the primary driver of economic development during this period, as the thesis posits.

While Feinstein's study (1998) suggests that institutions may not have been the primary driver of economic development during a specific period due to stable growth and consistent demographic indicators, other sources complicate this argument by highlighting the impact of colonialism on long-run economic growth.

Komlos (1998) discusses the concept of modern economic growth and its connection to structural changes, including population growth, urbanization, and income inequality, which could be influenced by institutions established during colonial periods. Furthermore, Komlos implies a relationship between economic processes and height cycles, possibly indicating that short-term fluctuations in economies might have an impact on long-run growth.

Temin (2002) argues that the exhaustion of cheap labor caused economic growth to slow, suggesting that institutions set up during colonialism may have played a role in shaping labor markets and economic development trajectories. Additionally, Temin asserts that the end of the Golden Age was brought about by the return to equilibrium growth paths, which could be influenced by institutional changes following colonialism.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) emphasize the relationship between economic growth and equality, suggesting that initial conditions or factor endowments, including institutions established during colonial periods, could have profound and enduring impacts on long-run growth. They also highlight policymakers' concerns about trade-offs between economic growth and equality, which may be influenced by institutional factors.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) explore the possibility that initial conditions or factor endowments, including institutions established during colonialism, could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run growth. They argue that contrasting records in growth can be traced back to the achievement of sustained economic growth by certain colonies while others did not, suggesting a role for institutional factors in shaping development trajectories.

Frankema (2012) argues that colonial education is a key factor in explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa, with former British colonies growing faster due to their educational lead. This finding suggests that institutions established during colonialism may have played a role in shaping the educational systems and, consequently, economic development trajectories of former colonies.

The literature review further elucidates the significance of institutions in shaping long-run economic growth, particularly in former colonies. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) highlight that Sub-Saharan Africa's (SSA) historical data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has been limited, which complicates understanding the extent to which institutions established during colonialism influenced SSA's economic development trajectories (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1).

Moreover, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that in other regions, new contributions to historical national accounting have provided insights into patterns of growth and development over centuries, rewriting the history of economic divergence and convergence since before the industrial revolution (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). This suggests that further research focusing on SSA may uncover similar patterns and offer a more comprehensive understanding of the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth.

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) contribute to the discussion by emphasizing the role of women's economic contributions in former colonies, which were often subsumed under household production and unaccounted for in formal economic analyses (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). They argue that understanding these historical processes can help inform policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). This nuance underscores the importance of considering not only institutional structures but also their impact on various social and economic groups when examining long-run economic growth.

Skocpol & Sommers (1980) demonstrate that comparisons between cases reveal the particularities of individual instances, which can help establish patterns in the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth (Skocpol&Sommers_1980: p.9). They argue that by increasing the "visibility" of one structure by contrasting it with another, we can gain a more profound understanding of their role in shaping economic development (Skocpol&Sommers_1980: p.8). This method could be applied to former colonies to further elucidate the impact of colonial institutions on long-run economic growth.

The works of Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) and Allen et al. (2011) complicate the understanding of institutions' role in long-run economic growth, particularly in relation to labor markets.

Braudel & Wallerstein (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.30) note a fundamental disagreement regarding the impact of institutions on economic development, suggesting that the "strategy" of one institution can significantly affect others within the market. This interplay between institutions underscores the complexity of their role in shaping economic growth.

Allen et al. (AllenEtAl_2011: p.1) demonstrate income disparities during the early 20th century, attributing them to stagnation in China and industrialization in Japan and Europe. The authors further highlight that labor markets were not only influenced by institutions but also played a role in shaping them. For instance, they show how the rapid growth of the German economy led to rising real wages for workers in Leipzig (Allen et al., 2011: p.20).

These sources underscore the intricate relationship between institutions and labor markets, suggesting that institutions not only shape economic development but are also influenced by it. This reciprocal relationship adds nuance to the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Further exploration could delve into how this interplay varies across different regions and historical periods.

Scholars Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), although discussing different historical contexts, share a common understanding that institutions play a significant role in long-run economic growth. Both authors acknowledge the importance of institutional structures in facilitating competition among employers, which is crucial for economic development (Domar_1970: p.4).

Domar emphasizes the impact of labor-tied institutions on the cessation of employer competition, while Bohannan highlights the role of Western institutions in shaping African societies (Bohannan_1959: p.7). However, they diverge in their focus; Domar primarily examines the economic aspects of institutional structures, whereas Bohannan explores cultural and societal transformations brought about by external institutions.

Despite their insights, several aspects remain unresolved. For instance, neither author delves deeply into how these institutional structures evolved over time or how they might vary across different regions. Furthermore, the relationship between specific types of institutions and economic growth is not extensively explored in either work. Future research could benefit from a more comprehensive analysis of this interplay to provide a more nuanced understanding of the role of institutions in long-run economic growth.
