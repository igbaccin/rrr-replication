The central question under investigation in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, a thesis that has garnered significant attention among economists and historians. The stakes are high as understanding the roots of economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting development and reducing poverty in various regions.

Support for the thesis comes from several sources. Austin (2008) endorses the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history, revealing relationships that recur due to path dependency, reinforcing Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe (Austin_2008: p.24). Kuznets (1973) also supports this view, emphasizing the role of institutions in facilitating technological innovations and societal adjustments during modern economic growth (Kuznets_1973: p.6).

North & Weingast (1989) focus on the political factors underpinning economic growth and the development of markets, highlighting how representative government institutions with multiple veto points played a crucial role in making possible economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). Acemoglu et al. (2001) also support this view, demonstrating that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27).

However, not all sources agree with the thesis. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate the discussion by pointing out that a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has kept Sub-Saharan Africa absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance, noting that understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution becomes subsumed under household production is essential for policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3).

The debate continues as scholars grapple with the complexities of long-run economic growth and the role of institutions in shaping these dynamics. The stakes are high, as understanding the roots of economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting development and reducing poverty in various regions. Future research should aim to address the concerns raised by those offering critiques and nuances, contributing to a more comprehensive understanding of this crucial issue.

The significance of institutions in fostering long-run economic growth is underscored by various scholars, highlighting their role in shaping economic development and market formation (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). North and Weingast (1989) argue that the fundamental institutions of representative government, such as multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are crucial in making possible economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

In a similar vein, Mokyr (2005) suggests that the change in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West can be explained through developments in the intellectual realm concerning "useful knowledge" (Mokyr_2005: p.4). The author emphasizes the importance of man-made formal law, corporatism, self-governance, and rules determined through an institutionalized process in fostering economic growth (Mokyr_2005: p.56).

North (1989) contributes to this discourse by focusing on the role of institutions in reducing transaction costs and increasing security rights, which are essential for market development and economic growth (North_1989: p.9). Furthermore, North highlights that centralized enforcement mechanisms, such as those present in Spain, can hinder economic growth (North_1989: p.1).

Whatley (2018) provides empirical evidence supporting the relationship between institutions and economic growth by demonstrating the impact of gunpowder imports on slave exports in Africa (Whatley_2018: p.13). Abramovitz (1986) supports this argument by suggesting that the elaboration of a mixed economy is a function of economic growth itself (Abramovitz_1986: p.20).

Lastly, Nunn (2008) shows that ethnic fractionalization can have a negative impact on long-term economic growth due to the environment of uncertainty and insecurity it creates (Nunn_2008: p.4). These findings emphasize the importance of institutions in reducing uncertainty and fostering economic development.

Structural change, a shift in the composition of an economy from primary activities such as agriculture and extraction to secondary and tertiary sectors like manufacturing and services, has been identified as a significant factor contributing to long-term economic growth (Allen_2001: p.4). Allen (2001) further supports this argument by stating that wages of building craftsmen can be considered indicative of trends in average earnings, providing some reassurance for the representative nature of the British industrial revolution (Allen_2001: p.4).

Broadberry et al. (2015) also underscore the importance of structural change in economic growth by discussing India's long-run economic performance, suggesting that the country's economy experienced significant transformation since the late sixteenth century (BroadberryEtal_2015: p.2). This transformation, according to Broadberry et al., was characterized by a shift from agriculture to manufacturing and services, which is consistent with the notion that structural change fosters economic development.

Moreover, Bryant (2006) argues that Europe provided more than adequate environments for the growth of commercial exchange, which is a crucial aspect of economic development (Bryant_2006: p.13). This shift towards commercial exchange can be viewed as a structural change, further supporting the claim that such changes are fundamental to long-term economic growth.

Structural changes in trade patterns significantly contributed to long-term economic growth, as evidenced by the emergence of commercial exchange systems (Bryant_2006: p.13). This shift is further supported by the assertion that institutions play a fundamental role in economic development.

Austin (2008) argues that institutions are indeed the primary cause of long-run economic growth. He posits that trade, even significant ones like the Atlantic trade, reinforced rather than originated economic positions, implying that institutional structures were instrumental in shaping these outcomes (Austin_2008: p.24). Furthermore, Austin emphasizes the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history, suggesting a recurring relationship between institutions and economic development over time (Austin_2008: p.1).

Frankema & van Waijenburg (2012) support this notion by demonstrating how West African commercial centers attracted large flows of economic migrants, leading to increased population growth rates that exceeded 3% per annum (1911-1931) in cities like Accra and the Gold Coast (Frankema&vanWaijenburg_2012: p.15, p.17). This influx of labor likely contributed to economic growth by increasing productivity and promoting specialization.

Structural change, such as the shift from agriculture to industry, is suggested as a key driver of economic growth (Humphries_2013: p.18). The emergence of the factory system and increasing division of labor are posited as essential factors in this process. However, it's important to note that these structural changes were not without their challenges. For instance, Humphries (2013) highlights the significant reliance on women and children for work during this period, which could have potentially hindered economic growth due to their limited productivity compared to adult males (Humphries_2013: p.11, p.19).

Similarly, Simson (2019) discusses the expansion of state expenditure and deficits in Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda during the 1960s and 1970s, a period marked by high economic growth. While this increased spending may have contributed to industrial development and educational expansion, it also raised questions about efficiency and the sustainability of such investment levels (Simson_2019: p.4, p.25).

These sources challenge the notion that institutions are the sole cause of long-term economic growth by suggesting that structural changes, such as the emergence of the factory system or increased state spending, also play significant roles. However, further investigation is needed to determine the precise interplay between institutions and these structural changes in driving economic growth over the long term.

The assertion that institutions are the primary drivers of long-term economic growth is further nuanced by various scholarly works. These sources complicate this perspective by highlighting the interplay between institutional development and other factors such as structural changes, factor endowments, and environmental conditions.

For instance, Temin (2002) challenges the notion that institutions are solely responsible for long-term economic growth. He suggests that the exhaustion of cheap labor and the return to equilibrium growth paths may have contributed to the slowdown in economic growth, rather than institutional changes (Temin_2002: p.4). Similarly, Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that long-run distribution trends and economic growth are interconnected, further complicating the relationship between institutions and economic growth (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1).

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) emphasize the role of initial conditions or factor endowments in shaping long-term economic growth paths. They argue that countries with different factor endowments may have experienced varying levels of economic growth, complicating the argument that institutions are the sole determinant of long-term economic growth (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4).

Moreover, Koepke & Baten (2005) explore the relationship between climate and human living standards, suggesting that environmental conditions may also play a role in shaping long-term economic growth patterns (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.20). Hopkins (2019) discusses how Western impositions on other parts of the world inhibited the study of long-run economic and political change, further complicating the argument that institutions are the primary drivers of long-term economic growth (Hopkins_2019: p.10).

The works of Broadberry & Gardner (2022), Komlos (1998), and Bolt & van Zanden (2014) add nuance to the argument that institutions are the primary drivers of long-term economic growth.

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) highlight the data deficiency regarding Sub-Saharan Africa's (SSA) economic performance before 1950, which has been absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. Their findings suggest that understanding SSA's growth patterns and risks could provide insights into potential paths to convergence, contributing to a more comprehensive analysis of global economic development.

Komlos (1998) discusses the impact of physical stature during the Industrial Revolution on economic growth. While not directly addressing institutions, his work implies that biological growth processes were significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic conditions and structural changes, which could indirectly support the argument that institutions play a role in long-run economic growth.

Bolt & van Zanden (2014) focus on revisions of nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth data for various regions, including Europe and Indonesia. Their work underscores the importance of considering regional differences in understanding long-term economic growth patterns. This emphasis on regional specificity aligns with the argument that institutions may vary significantly across geographical locations, influencing their impact on economic growth.

Institutions and colonial economies significantly shaped long-term economic growth patterns, as evidenced by the role of gender dynamics and the creation of alliances between plantation elites and colonial administrations.

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) emphasize that women's economic contributions were often subsumed under household production, making them formally unaccounted for in policies aimed at strengthening their economic roles (p.3). This underscores the complexity of institutional structures during colonial times and their impact on gender equality, which is a crucial factor in determining long-term economic growth patterns.

Moreover, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) highlight that the economic contribution of African women was largely dependent on the economic provision of the husband (p.9). This dependency structure within households could have hindered women's potential to contribute independently to economic growth. The authors also note that these historical developments in West Africa share parallels with other parts of Africa where gender and economic growth are concerned (p.4), suggesting a broader relevance of this finding.

Stoler (1989) discusses the creation of "imagined communities" by colonizers, which helped overcome economic and social disparities between colonizers and the colonized (p.5). This innovation not only shaped the relationships between different groups but also created a more solid alliance between the plantation elite and the colonial administration (p.21), potentially influencing long-term economic growth patterns through the consolidation of power and resources.

These sources complicate the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by shedding light on the role of gender dynamics and the formation of alliances between powerful groups during colonial times. Understanding these factors can provide a more nuanced perspective on how institutions have influenced economic growth in the past, and potentially continue to do so in the present.

The scholarship on the role of institutions in long-run economic growth presents a complex interplay of convergence and divergence among scholars. Both Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) acknowledge the significant impact of institutions, albeit with different focal points.

Domar (1970) emphasizes the structural changes that occur in response to institutional arrangements, such as labor being tied to land or owners, which affects competition among employers and economic growth (p.4). He also highlights the historical examples of Russia's enserfment of peasants and the American South's slaveholding, demonstrating how these institutions stifled growth by limiting labor mobility (pp.4, 16).

On the other hand, Bohannan (1959) focuses on the Western impact on African societies, revealing that it extended beyond economic institutions to include cultural and social aspects like slave dealing and acquisition of status (p.7, p.10). He also notes the limitations in women's numbers and geographical reach before European contact (pp.13, 10), suggesting potential implications for economic growth.

Despite these insights, some key issues remain unresolved. For instance, while both scholars acknowledge the negative effects of certain institutions on growth, they do not extensively explore potential positive impacts or discuss strategies for reforming problematic institutional arrangements to promote growth. Additionally, neither scholar provides a comprehensive analysis of how cultural and social factors intersect with economic institutions to shape long-term growth patterns.
