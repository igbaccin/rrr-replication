However, the argument remains complex and multifaceted, with various factors influencing economic growth beyond institutions alone. The stakes are high, as understanding the role of institutions in economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting long-term development. Further research is needed to fully unravel the intricate relationship between institutions and economic growth.

The role of institutions in fostering long-term economic growth is further substantiated by various scholars. In his work, Kuznets (1973) highlights the association between rapid changes in economic structure and societal aspects during periods of modern economic growth. He emphasizes that while positive achievements are observed, there are also unexpected negative consequences even within developed countries (Kuznets_1973: p.4).

North & Weingast (1989) argue that the fundamental institutions of representative government, characterized by multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are integral to economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28). They further contend that these institutions provide the necessary conditions for market development and enforcement of rules governing economic exchange (North&Weingast_1989: p.2).

Nunn & Wantchekon (2011) demonstrate that both internal and external factors play a significant role in shaping economic growth, but the internal channel is at least twice as large as the external one (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.4). They suggest that this internal channel could be attributed to the impact of institutions on fostering economic development.

Peters (2004) also supports the notion that institutions play a crucial role in promoting economic growth. He emphasizes that the aims of land policy reform, such as economic growth and poverty reduction, are not new but have been staples of proposed 'reform' (Peters_2004: p.7).

These findings collectively underscore the importance of institutions as a key driver of long-term economic growth. Understanding the intricate relationship between institutions and economic growth can provide valuable insights for policy decisions aimed at promoting sustainable development. However, further research is necessary to fully elucidate this complex interplay and identify effective strategies for institutional reform.

Trade between Africa and Western Europe, as highlighted by Austin (2008) and Pamuk (2007), provides significant evidence supporting the thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. According to Austin (Austin_2008: p.26), the theoretical conclusion of institutions being 'the' fundamental cause of long-term economic growth is attractive due to its logical economy. This perspective suggests that trade, including the Atlantic trade between Africa and Western Europe, reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe.

Pamuk (2007) further supports this argument by pointing out that significant changes in wages and incomes during the last millennium were primarily due to modern economic growth and the Black Death. These changes paved the way for the growing commercialization of agriculture, which is a manifestation of institutional development. The population decline in Europe after the Black Death (Pamuk_2007: p.5) also led to labor scarcity, which increased wages and further promoted institutional change.

The persistence of institutions plays a significant role in shaping economic performance, as evidenced by the findings of Acemoglu et al. (2001) and Mokyr (2005). Acemoglu et al. (2001) assert that more fundamental institutions have a substantial impact on property rights, investment, and economic progress. Their research demonstrates that these institutions can either promote or hinder economic growth, with detrimental institutions being particularly harmful (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). Furthermore, they argue that differences in mortality rates are not the primary cause of institutional variation (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3).

Mokyr (2005) supports this notion by emphasizing the importance of intellectual developments and the growth of economic reasonableness in shaping Western societies. He suggests that changes in the rate and nature of economic growth can be attributed to advancements in the realm of "useful knowledge" (Mokyr_2005: p.4). Mokyr also highlights the emergence of elements such as individualism, formal law, corporatism, self-governance, and institutionalized processes that allowed for fair representation and participation (Mokyr_2005: p.56). These developments contributed to a progressive society that fostered economic growth.

The works of Simson (2019) challenge the notion that institutions are the primary cause of long-term economic growth, particularly in East African countries during the 1960s and 1970s. Instead, Simson argues that the emphasis on industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy played a significant role in these economies' growth (Simson_2019: p.27).

Simson also suggests that expanding public employment rather than protecting those already in government jobs was prioritized during this period (Simson_2019: p.4). This shift towards increased spending and deficits contributed to the economic growth witnessed in East Africa during the 1960s and 1970s, suggesting that institutional developments may not have been the sole driving force behind these economies' growth.

Moreover, Simson questions whether eliminating spending inefficiencies, leakages, and costly delays would have been sufficient to sustain the investment levels of the 1960s or reorient these economies (Simson_2019: p.4). These findings challenge the idea that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth in East African countries during this timeframe.

Finally, Simson notes that while there were economic crises in the region during the 1980s and 1990s, these economies recovered in the 2000s (Simson_2019: p.4). This evidence suggests that other factors beyond institutions may have contributed to the region's economic recovery, further challenging the thesis that institutions are the primary cause of long-term economic growth.

The labor market slowdown, a key factor in the economic growth debate, is further examined in the works of Komlos (1998), Temin (2002), and Williamson (1996). These scholars offer insights that complicate the argument that institutions are the primary cause of long-term economic growth.

Komlos (1998) highlights the connection between physical stature and economic growth during the Industrial Revolution, suggesting that biological growth processes were significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economies (p.16). Moreover, he links structural changes such as population growth, urbanization, and income inequality to economic growth (p.17), implying that these factors may have played a significant role in driving long-term economic expansion.

Temin (2002) argues that the exhaustion of cheap labor led to a slowdown in economic growth, challenging the notion that institutions alone could drive sustained economic growth (p.4). He further suggests that the end of the Golden Age was not caused by adverse economic shocks but rather by a return to equilibrium growth paths (p.14), indicating that other factors may have contributed to the slowdown in growth during this period.

Williamson (1996) examines the relationship between schooling and Swedish economic growth before World War I, suggesting that mass migrations reduced the pace of real wage growth in receiving countries by glutting labor markets abroad (p.17). This finding suggests that labor market dynamics may have played a significant role in shaping long-term economic growth trajectories.

The relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth is further nuanced by considering the historical perspectives presented in Lindert & Williamson (1985) and Frankema (2012). These works suggest that while institutions are indeed crucial, other factors may also play significant roles in shaping long-term economic trajectories.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) complicate the argument by emphasizing the intertwined nature of growth and inequality. They argue that the British classical economists and policymakers were aware of this relationship, suggesting a historical precedent for the contemporary understanding of institutions as determinants of economic growth (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.1). Furthermore, they propose that explaining long-run distribution trends is inextricably linked to the search for explanations of growth itself (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.9). This implies that institutions may not solely drive economic growth but might also influence income distribution, a factor that could potentially impact growth trajectories over the long run.

On the other hand, Frankema (2012) provides evidence supporting the idea that colonial factors can contribute to long-run growth disparities. Bolt and Bezemer's (2009) research suggests that colonial education was a key factor in explaining African growth disparities, with former British colonies growing faster due to their educational lead (Frankema_2012: p.2). This finding highlights the potential role of colonial institutions in shaping long-term economic growth patterns, adding another layer of complexity to the relationship between institutions and growth.

Moreover, Frankema (2012) argues that the degree of British "liberalism" was endogenous to the local political and economic context (Frankema_2012: p.19). This suggests that the impact of colonial institutions on long-run growth may not have been uniform across all colonies, further complicating the relationship between institutions and growth.

The works of Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) and Hopkins (2019) add nuance to the thesis by emphasizing the role of country-specific conditions in shaping long-run economic growth.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) argue that initial conditions, or factor endowments broadly conceived, could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth. They suggest that while conventional economic factors have been considered, explanations for contrasting records in growth have most often focused on institutions and the variation across societies in conditions relevant to growth (Sokoloff & Engerman_2000: p.2). Furthermore, they highlight the divergence in paths between countries such as the United States and Canada, which achieved sustained economic growth during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, and other countries that did not manage to attain this goal until late in the nineteenth century (Sokoloff & Engerman_2000: p.12).

On the other hand, Hopkins (2019) points out that the study of long-run economic and political change has been inhibited by 'totalism' and 'essentialism', which were regarded as Western impositions on the rest of the world. He suggests that these perspectives limited the diversity that independent African viewpoints would otherwise have supplied (Hopkins_2019: p.4). Additionally, Hopkins (2019) argues that food exports made very little contribution to economic growth in West Africa during the period of the slave trade (Hopkins_2019: p.13), suggesting that other factors may have played a more significant role in shaping economic growth in specific regions.

The works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), while not directly addressing the central thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, offer tangential insights that contribute to understanding this complex relationship. Both scholars acknowledge the importance of institutions in shaping economic structures, albeit from different perspectives.

Domar (1970) emphasizes the role of labor institutions in promoting competition among employers, arguing that when labor is tied to land or an owner, competition ceases, potentially hindering growth. This aligns with the notion that strong institutions fostering competitive markets can promote economic development. However, Domar's focus on labor institutions diverges from a broader view of institutions as encompassing political, social, and legal structures.

Bohannan (1959) discusses the Western impact on African societies, particularly in terms of economic institutions related to slave dealing and status acquisition. While acknowledging that the Western impact was not limited to economic institutions, this work provides insights into how external forces can shape institutional development, which may indirectly influence economic growth. Yet, Bohannan's focus on the social aspects of these institutions diverges from a strict economic perspective.

Both scholars imply that labor and social institutions play a role in economic growth, but they do not directly address the question of whether institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. The works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) leave unresolved the extent to which specific types of institutions—such as political, social, or economic—are more influential in driving long-term economic growth. Future research could focus on disentangling these relationships to provide a more comprehensive understanding of this important question.
