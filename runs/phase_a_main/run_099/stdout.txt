LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question in the literature on long-run economic growth is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of such growth, a thesis that has garnered significant attention and debate among scholars. This question holds substantial implications for understanding historical economic development and informing contemporary policy decisions.

Supporting this argument, Simon Kuznets (1973) posits that high aggregate growth rates are associated with rapid changes in economic structure, which in turn are linked to broader societal transformations (Kuznets_1973: p.4). Similarly, Douglass North and Barry Weingast (1989) argue that the institutions of representative government, such as multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are integral to economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

On the other hand, scholars like James Acemoglu and his colleagues (AcemogluEtAl_2001) suggest that while institutions play a crucial role in economic growth, they are not the sole determinant. They argue that differences in mortality rates also contribute to variations in institutions (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.4).

Complicating this debate, Austin (2008) argues that while institutions may have been influential in shaping long-term economic growth, they did not necessarily originate it. Instead, he suggests that historical trade patterns, such as the Atlantic trade, reinforced rather than initiated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe and parts of Asia (Austin_2008: p.24).

Broadberry and Gardner (2022) also question the exclusive focus on institutions, arguing that a lack of data before 1950 has kept Sub-Saharan Africa out of recent debates about long-run growth. They suggest that understanding economic growth in this region requires considering the risks of shrinking that have undermined progress in the past (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.18).

Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) further complicate the discussion by highlighting the role of women's economic contributions, which have often been overlooked or subsumed under household production (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3).

Institutions play a pivotal role in fostering long-run economic growth, as evidenced by various scholarly works. Kuznets (1973) emphasizes the association between rapid changes in economic structure and growth, linking these transformations to technological innovations and societal adjustments (Kuznets_1973: p.4). North & Weingast (1989) further underscore this notion by highlighting the importance of representative government institutions in promoting economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

Mokyr (2005) supports these findings, attributing the West's economic prosperity to developments in intellectual realms concerning "useful knowledge" (Mokyr_2005: p.4). North (1989) also contributes to this discourse by discussing how institutions can influence transaction costs and, consequently, market development and economic growth (North_1989: p.9).

Moreover, Ogilvie (2007) points out the interdependency of individual institutions within a broader framework, suggesting that the success or failure of one institution can impact others (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). deVries (1994) reinforces this idea by emphasizing Europe's urban network as a crucial framework for regional economic development during the early modern period, which facilitated industrial growth (deVries_1994: p.5).

These sources collectively demonstrate that institutions are indeed fundamental causes of long-run economic growth. They showcase how different types of institutions—from political to urban networks—can influence economic structure, technological progress, and overall prosperity. Further exploration should delve into the specific mechanisms through which these institutions interact and shape economic growth in various contexts.

Institutional development significantly influences long-term economic growth, as demonstrated in various studies. Acemoglu and his colleagues (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27) highlight the impact of fundamental institutions on property rights, suggesting that detrimental institutions can hinder investment and economic progress. Furthermore, they argue that differences in mortality rates are not the primary cause of institutional variation (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3).

The results from Acemoglu et al.'s research (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19) show a substantial effect of institutions on economic performance, supporting the notion that they are crucial for long-term growth. Similarly, Acemoglu and his collaborators in another study (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49) emphasize the importance of institutions in understanding and evaluating theories of long-run development.

Moreover, Abramovitz (Abramovitz_1986: p.20) suggests that the elaboration of mixed economies is a function of economic growth itself, implying that institutional development can be driven by growth and vice versa. He also notes the importance of macroeconomic conditions encouraging capital investment and effective demand for sustained economic growth (Abramovitz_1986: p.7).

The claim that ethnic fragmentation is a primary cause of unproductive institutions has drawn criticism, as other factors contributing to solidarity and action must be considered (Hopkins_2009: p.22). Hopkins argues that institutions in Africa have been instrumental in supporting communities rather than acting as burdens (Hopkins_2009: p.21), suggesting that ethnic fragmentation may not be the primary cause of unproductive institutions.

Similarly, Bryant (2006) supports the notion that structural change correlates with growth, providing more than adequate environments for commercial exchange (Bryant_2006: p.13). This implies that institutional development, rather than being a hindrance to economic growth, can facilitate it by fostering conditions conducive to commerce and trade.

These findings support the argument that institutions are fundamental causes of long-run economic growth. The evidence suggests that institutions can be drivers of growth and that their role in supporting communities may be more significant than previously assumed. Further examination of the interplay between institutional development, ethnic fragmentation, and economic growth is warranted to deepen our understanding of this complex relationship.

The assertion that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth is challenged by several scholars, who argue that other factors such as industrial development and educational expansion may play a more significant role. For instance, Simson (2019) points out that in Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda, state expenditure and deficits expanded during a period of high economic growth in the 1960s and 1970s (Simson_2019: p.25). Similarly, Stern (2004) questions whether the elimination of spending inefficiencies, leakages, and costly delays to reform would have been enough to sustain investment levels during this period (Stern_2004: p.4). These findings suggest that industrial development and government spending could contribute to economic growth, potentially overshadowing the role of institutions.

Moreover, McCloskey (2015) critiques the claim by arguing that ideologies and ideologies, not interests or institutions, may have been more influential in economic growth in France during two centuries after the Unfinished Revolution of 1789 (McCloskey_2015: p.9). Feinstein (1998) also challenges the thesis by suggesting that changes in farming and migration were sufficient to disperse labor gluts before the 1850s and 1860s, which could have contributed to economic growth without the need for specific institutions (Feinstein_1998: p.28).

These findings suggest that while institutions may play a role in long-run economic growth, other factors such as industrial development, educational expansion, and government spending may also be significant contributors. A deeper understanding of the interplay between these factors and their relative importance is necessary to fully grasp the complex relationship between institutional development, ethnic fragmentation, and economic growth.

The sources provided offer nuanced perspectives on the relationship between institutions, industrialization, and economic growth, complicating the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) argue that initial conditions, or factor endowments, could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth, suggesting a more complex interplay between institutions and industrialization (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4). Their work highlights the contrasting records in growth between countries such as the United States and Canada, which achieved sustained economic growth during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, and others that did not until later (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.12).

Temin (2002) provides a different perspective, suggesting that the slowing of economic growth was due to the exhaustion of cheap labor rather than institutions being the primary cause (Temin_2002: p.4). He also argues that recent theories of economic growth should consider the Golden Age in the context of economic transition, implying a more complex relationship between industrialization and economic growth over time (Temin_2002: p.7).

Komlos (1998) discusses physical stature during the Industrial Revolution, suggesting that short-term fluctuations in economic processes influenced biological growth processes (Komlos_1998: p.16). He also notes that economic processes and associated structural changes were significant during this period, implying a complex interplay between institutions and industrialization (Komlos_1998: p.17).

Austin (2007) discusses the role of the railway in comparison to lorries during the Industrial Revolution, suggesting that the cheaper cost of railways was a fundamental advantage (Austin_2007: p.8). He also notes that few of the capital-deepening and TFP-improving forces of modern economic growth were at work in Ghana, implying a more complex relationship between institutions and industrialization (Austin_2007: p.16).

Williamson (1996) discusses the sources of convergence, suggesting that understanding these sources is fundamental to understanding policy responses (Williamson_1996: p.25). He also notes that mass migrations reduced the pace of real wage growth in receiving countries, implying a complex relationship between institutions, industrialization, and labor markets (Williamson_1996: p.17).

Clark & Jacks (2007) discuss the cliometric account of the coal industry in the Industrial Revolution, suggesting that technological advances enabled the industry to respond to increases in demand (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.4). They also note that prices are deﬂated by a price index for the economy as a whole, implying a more complex relationship between institutions and industrialization at an aggregate level (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.3).

These sources complicate the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by suggesting a more complex interplay between institutions, industrialization, and other factors such as factor endowments, technological advancements, and labor markets. Further investigation into these relationships is necessary to fully grasp the nuances of this relationship over time and across different contexts.

The assertion that institutions are the primary driver of long-term economic growth is not without complications, as highlighted by Austin (2008) and Broadberry & Gardner (2022). Austin (Austin_2008: p.26) acknowledges that while institutions may be a fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, they also reinforce existing economic disparities, such as those observed in the Atlantic trade between Africa and Europe. This suggests that institutions might perpetuate economic stagnation rather than spurring growth in certain contexts.

Broadberry & Gardner (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1) underscore the importance of examining long-term growth theories, arguing that historical data from Sub-Saharan Africa before 1950 has been largely overlooked in recent debates about economic growth. They suggest that understanding the economic performance of this region over a longer period is crucial for determining whether current growth trends are sustainable and can lead to convergence with other regions (Broadberry & Gardner, 2022: p.18). This implies that the relationship between institutions and long-term economic growth may vary significantly across different time periods and geographical contexts, necessitating a nuanced approach to this research.

The relationship between institutions and long-term economic growth is not a straightforward causality, as other factors such as structural change and distribution of wealth also play significant roles in this dynamic (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.1). Lindert and Williamson (1985) argue that the classical economists recognized the importance of institutions in economic growth but often overlooked the complexities involved, such as the trade-offs between economic growth and equality (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.1). Moreover, they suggest that policymakers should consider these trade-offs when addressing issues related to long-term national development, emphasizing the need for an enormous sample of long-term national data to study this relationship effectively (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.1).

Furthermore, Hopkins (2019) points out that the study of long-run economic and political change in Africa has been hindered by ideologies such as 'totalism' and 'essentialism,' which were often perceived as Western impositions on the continent (Hopkins_2019: p.10). Hopkins also notes that food exports made minimal contributions to economic growth during the period of the slave trade in West Africa, suggesting that other factors might have played a more substantial role in driving long-term economic growth in this region (Hopkins_2019: p.13).

Scholars Domar and Bohannan, albeit addressing different contexts, share a common perspective that institutions play a significant role in long-run economic growth. Domar (1970) emphasizes the importance of labor mobility and competition among employers as crucial factors for economic growth, suggesting that when labor is tied to land or owners, such competition ceases, hindering growth. On the other hand, Bohannan (1959) highlights how Western economic institutions, particularly those related to slave trading and status acquisition, had an impact on African societies, although not exclusively economic.

Both scholars converge in recognizing the role of institutions in economic growth but diverge in their focus. Domar primarily focuses on labor mobility and competition, while Bohannan explores the influence of Western economic institutions on African societies. However, both works suggest that institutional structures can either promote or hinder long-term economic growth.

Despite these insights, several questions remain unresolved. For instance, how do specific types of institutions impact economic growth differently? What are the mechanisms through which institutions shape economic growth over time? Furthermore, understanding the interplay between various factors, such as technology, geography, and culture, with institutions in driving long-term economic growth is still a topic of ongoing research.
