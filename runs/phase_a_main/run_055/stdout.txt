The debate surrounding the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth has been a subject of extensive research, with institutions emerging as a key factor. This discourse is significant as it sheds light on the factors that drive economic development and growth in different regions.

Several scholars support the notion that institutions are indeed the primary driver of long-term economic growth. For instance, Austin (2008) argues that institutions play a crucial role in shaping economic performance, particularly in Africa, where trade patterns have reinforced rather than originated the continent's poor economic position compared to Western Europe and parts of Asia. Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) highlight the relationship between representative government, multiple veto points, and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs in promoting economic growth and political freedom.

On the other hand, some scholars offer critiques or complications to this argument. For example, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) point out that the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has led to Sub-Saharan Africa's absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance by discussing how understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution is subsumed under household production can undermine policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles.

As the debate continues, it is clear that institutions play a significant role in long-run economic growth. However, the extent of their influence and the challenges they face, particularly in regions like Sub-Saharan Africa, remain topics of ongoing research and discussion. The stakes are high, as understanding the factors driving economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting development and reducing economic disparities across regions. This review serves as a starting point for further exploration into this complex and important topic.

The significance of institutions in driving long-run economic growth is underscored by various scholars, particularly in the context of agriculture. North & Weingast (1989) posit that representative government, characterized by multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, played a crucial role in fostering economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28). Similarly, Mokyr (2005) contends that the development of institutions such as individualism, man-made formal law, corporatism, self-governance, and rules determined through an institutionalized process were essential for progressive societies and economic growth (Mokyr_2005: p.56).

Furthermore, North (1989) highlights the role of institutions in reducing transaction costs by increasing security rights and enhancing market efficiency, thereby contributing to economic growth (North_1989: p.9). Acemoglu et al. (2002) support this notion, suggesting that the reversal in the distribution of economic prosperity is related to institutions (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5). In a similar vein, deVries (1994) argues that Europe's urban network, which established a framework for regional economic development, was an achievement of the early modern period (deVries_1994: p.5).

These findings suggest that institutions, particularly those that promote market efficiency and rule of law, are essential in fostering economic growth. The agricultural sector, being a significant part of many economies, would likely benefit from such institutional improvements. However, further research is needed to understand the specific mechanisms through which these institutions impact agriculture and overall economic growth.

Institutions, as fundamental drivers of long-run economic growth, play a crucial role in shaping economies and fostering development. This is supported by various studies that have delved into the relationship between institutions and economic growth, providing insights into the mechanisms through which these institutions impact agricultural productivity and overall economic development.

For instance, Nunn (2008) found significant correlations between ethnic fractionalization and long-term economic growth, suggesting that insecure environments during critical periods may have led individuals to develop more effective institutions, thereby promoting economic growth (Nunn_2008: p.4). Similarly, Nunn & Wantchekon (2011) demonstrated that both internal and external institutional factors are important for economic development, with the internal channel being at least twice as significant (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.4).

(2015) also supported this notion by investigating India's long-run economic performance, concluding that institutional factors played a substantial role in shaping the country's development trajectory (BroadberryEtal_2015: p.2). Moreover, Ogilvie (2007) highlighted the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework, emphasizing that the failure of one institution may impact others and potentially hinder economic growth (Ogilvie_2007: p.27).

Peters (2004) further supported this argument by stressing the importance of cultural modes of understanding and social organization in shaping economic institutions, suggesting that understanding these dynamics is crucial for promoting sustainable economic growth (Peters_2004: p.16).

The significance of institutions in shaping long-term economic growth is further emphasized by Austin (2008) and Frankema & van Waijenburg (2012). Austin (Austin_2008: p.26) asserts that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, highlighting their role in reinforcing existing economic positions rather than originating them. This perspective supports the argument that understanding institutional dynamics is crucial for promoting sustainable growth.

Moreover, Frankema & van Waijenburg (Frankema&vanWaijenburg_2012: p.15) provide evidence of high population growth rates in West African commercial centers, such as Accra and Lagos, which they attribute to economic migration. The authors suggest that this migration, driven by the attractiveness of these cities, could be a result of more favorable economic institutions (Frankema & van Waijenburg 2012: p.17). This example underscores how institutions can shape population dynamics and, in turn, influence long-term economic growth.

Institutions and Agriculture present a challenge to the assertion that they are the primary driver of long-term economic growth. Economic growth in various regions, such as East Africa, was accompanied by increased state expenditure and deficits during periods of high economic growth (Simson_2019: p.25). This suggests that institutions may not be the sole determinant of economic growth, as other factors like government spending could also play a significant role.

In addition, some argue that economic growth can lead to environmental degradation rather than being beneficial for the environment (Stern_2004: p.1). This perspective challenges the idea that favorable economic institutions are necessary for long-term economic growth, as it suggests that growth itself could have negative consequences on the environment.

Moreover, historical examples from France and Spain demonstrate instances where conservative ideologies and religious beliefs hindered economic growth despite a material base that could potentially support it (McCloskey_2015: p.9). This implies that institutions may not be the only factor influencing economic growth, as ideas and ideologies can also play a crucial role.

Furthermore, in agricultural communities like those studied by Bryceson (2002), traditional hierarchical order can influence women's economic and social spheres, limiting their participation in non-agricultural activities that could potentially contribute to long-term economic growth (Bryceson_2002: p.9). This underscores the complexity of institutions in shaping economic growth, as cultural norms and traditions can significantly impact the ability of individuals to engage in economic activities conducive to long-term growth.

Institutions, as fundamental drivers of long-run economic growth, are influenced by a multitude of factors that extend beyond cultural norms and traditions, as previously suggested (Bryceson_2002: p.9). The absence of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 in Sub-Saharan Africa has hindered its inclusion in recent debates about comparative long-term growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). However, the authors argue that new contributions to historical national accounting have provided insights into patterns of growth and development over centuries, potentially rewriting the history of economic divergence and convergence since before the industrial revolution.

Moreover, the relationship between climate and human living standards is another fascinating topic in long-run economic history (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.20). Over the period of the Roman Empire, heights did not increase at all, contrasting with the common view that living standards increased during this time due to economic growth (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.16). This suggests that climate may have played a significant role in shaping long-term economic growth, adding nuance to the argument that institutions are the sole determinants of economic development.

Furthermore, colonial education has been argued to be a key factor in explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa (Frankema_2012: p.2). Bolt and Bezemer suggest that the former British colonies grew faster due to their educational lead, potentially complicating the argument that institutions are the primary cause of economic growth.

In addition, the study of labor was once heavily focused on the institutions of the labor market (Goldin_2006: p.3). However, Goldin argues that by the term evolution and the shift to revolution, she means something specific, suggesting that institutional changes may not be the only drivers of economic growth and development.

These sources add nuance to the thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, suggesting that other factors such as climate, colonial education, and shifts in labor market dynamics may also play significant roles in shaping economic development over time.

The connection between institutions and long-run economic growth is further nuanced by considering their impact on agriculture, a primary sector that underpins many early economies. Temin (2002) suggests that the exhaustion of cheap labor led to a slowdown in economic growth, implying that the transition from an agricultural economy may have played a significant role in shaping economic development over time.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) also highlight the importance of initial conditions and factor endowments in determining long-term growth patterns. They argue that the United States and Canada, which had more favorable agricultural conditions, were able to achieve sustained economic growth earlier than other countries.

Hopkins (2019) points out that as economies transitioned away from agriculture, institutions assumed a new importance. This shift could have significant implications for understanding the role of institutions in long-run economic growth.

Skocpol & Sommers (1980) and vanZanden (1999) emphasize the need to compare different societies to understand the impact of institutions on economic growth. By examining the contrasting experiences of Japan, China, and Europe, they demonstrate that Western intrusions influenced the development of institutions in these regions, thereby shaping their long-term economic trajectories.

These sources complicate the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by suggesting that other factors such as agricultural conditions, transitions away from agriculture, and external influences may also play significant roles in shaping economic development over time. Further exploration of these factors could deepen our understanding of how institutions interact with other aspects of society to drive long-term economic growth.

The role of institutions in shaping long-run economic growth is indeed multifaceted, and the impact of colonial economies adds a layer of complexity to this discourse. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) shed light on the significant contributions of African women to economic growth during colonial times, yet their labor was often unaccounted for in formal records.

The authors illustrate how the structural change that occurred with the advent of colonialism correlated with economic growth, but the women's economic roles were frequently subsumed under household production (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.3). This oversight undermines policies aimed at strengthening women's economic positions, highlighting the need for institutions to recognize and incorporate these contributions in their development strategies.

Furthermore, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) reveal that the economic contribution of African women was largely dependent on the economic provision of their husbands (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.9). This dependency suggests that institutions under colonial rule may have reinforced traditional gender roles, potentially limiting women's economic agency and growth potential.

Moreover, these findings are not exclusive to West Africa; the major historical developments and themes concerning gender and economic growth in this region find parallels elsewhere in Africa (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.4). This observation underscores the importance of considering the impact of colonial institutions on gender roles and economic development across the continent.

The works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) provide tangential insights into the relationship between institutions, gender roles, and economic growth, albeit focusing on different contexts. Both scholars acknowledge the significance of institutional structures in shaping long-run economic development.

Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) emphasizes that labor tied to land or owners impedes competition among employers, which can hinder economic growth. This aligns with the argument that strong institutions fostering competition are crucial for sustained economic growth (North & Weingast, 1989). However, Domar's focus is more on the factor market, whereas this literature review concentrates on colonial institutions and their impact on gender roles and economic development in Africa.

Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) discusses the Western impact on African societies, particularly centering on slave dealing, curing, and acquisition of status. This suggests that colonial institutions may have had diverse effects, some potentially negative, on African economies and gender roles. This perspective complements Akyeampong & Fofack's (2014) observation that the impact of colonial institutions on gender roles across Africa is complex.

Despite these insights, there remains a need to address the specific ways in which colonial institutions influenced gender roles and economic development in Africa. For instance, it would be valuable to explore how institutional changes post-colonialism affected these relationships further. Additionally, understanding the variation in the impact of colonial institutions across different African regions is another unresolved issue that warrants investigation.
