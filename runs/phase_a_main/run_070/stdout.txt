LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question in the literature review on institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is whether institutions play a pivotal role in shaping economic development over extended periods. This question holds significant implications for understanding the disparities in economic performance between regions and nations.

Supporting the thesis, Austin (2008) argues that institutions are indeed 'the' fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, as they influence trade patterns and reinforce economic positions, particularly when comparing Africa to Western Europe (Austin_2008: p.24). Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) demonstrate how representative government institutions with multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs are crucial for economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28). Acemoglu et al. (2001) also support this notion, illustrating how certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27).

However, some sources offer critiques or complications to the thesis. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has kept Sub-Saharan Africa absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance to the discussion, highlighting how understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution becomes subsumed under household production is essential for policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3).

These perspectives underscore the complexity of the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth, necessitating a nuanced understanding of the role institutions play in shaping economic development over extended periods. The debate continues, with researchers delving deeper into various aspects of this intricate relationship to unravel its intricacies and implications for policy formulation and economic development strategies.

The relationship between institutions and economic growth is further substantiated by various scholars, as they delve into the cultural factors that influence this relationship. Kuznets (1973) posits that rapid changes in economic structure, often associated with modern economic growth, are accompanied by significant adjustments in other aspects of society, such as family and urbanization structures (Kuznets_1973: p.4). Mokyr (2005) supports this notion, suggesting that the change in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West can be attributed to developments in intellectual realms concerning "useful knowledge" (Mokyr_2005: p.4).

North & Weingast (1989) focus on the political factors underpinning economic growth, emphasizing the role of institutions that govern how rules are enforced and changed (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). They argue that the fundamental institutions of representative government, such as multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, play a crucial role in fostering economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28). This perspective underscores the importance of institutional arrangements in promoting long-term economic development.

Moreover, North (1989) argues that institutions can significantly reduce transaction costs by increasing security rights and facilitating market development, thereby contributing to economic growth (North_1989: p.9). Ogilvie (2007) supports this view, emphasizing the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework and the potential for their failure when confronted with external encroachment (Ogilvie_2007: p.27).

The interplay between institutions and trade is crucial in understanding long-term economic growth. Austin (2008) affirms this view, suggesting that trades, even those as significant as the Atlantic one, reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to western Europe (Austin_2008: p.24). This perspective aligns with the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

Broadberry & Gupta (2006) also support this thesis, arguing that the service sector played a crucial role in economic growth during the early modern period, as in the modern period (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.9). They further assert that British economic growth during the Industrial Revolution was substantially slower than previously thought, emphasizing the importance of institutions in determining economic development.

Moreover, Broadberry et al. (2015) discuss India's long-run economic performance, stating that it has been a subject of enduring controversy (BroadberryEtal_2015: p.2). They suggest that the size of armies can be used as a method to estimate population, providing a starting point for their population estimates (BroadberryEtal_2015: p.4). This approach underscores the significance of institutions in shaping economic growth, as military organization and efficiency often reflect the strength and effectiveness of the ruling institutions.

The impact of institutions on economic growth extends to environmental factors, as demonstrated by various studies. Hopkins (2009) argues that ethnic fragmentation is often considered a hindrance to productive institutions; however, he acknowledges that this assumption has attracted criticism and requires further investigation (Hopkins_2009: p.10). Similarly, Frankema & van Waijenburg (2012) illustrate how West African commercial centers, such as Accra and Lagos, drew large flows of economic migrants due to high land-labor ratios and opportunities for growth (Frankema&vanWaijenburg_2012: p.15). These findings suggest that the environmental impact is significantly influenced by institutions, which facilitate or hinder economic growth and migration.

Moreover, Bryant (2006) emphasizes the role of institutions in creating environments conducive to commercial exchange (Bryant_2006: p.13). He highlights that a fusion of mercantilist imperialism with capitalistic transformation played a crucial role in shaping these institutions, thus underscoring their importance in facilitating economic growth and environmental impact.

Institutional development, while crucial in driving long-term economic growth, may not be the sole factor at play. Cultural factors and ideologies also exert significant influence on a nation's economic trajectory, challenging the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth (Simson_2019: p.27).

For instance, in Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda, state expenditure and deficits expanded during a period of high economic growth in the 1960s and 1970s (Simson_2019: p.25). This spending pattern, which prioritized industrial development, educational expansion, and public employment over protecting existing government jobs, suggests that cultural factors could significantly shape institutional structures and their impact on economic growth.

Moreover, the argument that institutions are the primary driver of long-term economic growth overlooks the role of cultural ideologies in shaping economic outcomes. In France, for two centuries after the Unfinished Revolution of 1789, reactionary parties prospered that were uninterested in economic growth if they could impose a rigid form of Catholicism (McCloskey_2015: p.9). This example illustrates how cultural ideologies can hinder economic development, even when favorable institutional structures are in place.

The relationship between institutions and cultural factors in shaping long-run economic growth is a complex one, as evidenced by various scholarly works. Komlos (1998) suggests that the onset of modern economic growth during the Industrial Revolution was influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic processes and associated structural changes (Komlos_1998: p.16). This implies that while institutions may play a crucial role, they are not solely responsible for economic growth, as other factors such as business cycles and population growth also have significant impacts.

Temin (2002) further complicates this argument by asserting that the slowdown in economic growth was caused by the exhaustion of cheap labor rather than being a cause itself (Temin_2002: p.4). This perspective underscores the importance of understanding historical economic transitions and their impact on long-term growth.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that policymakers often grapple with trade-offs between economic growth and equality, suggesting a potential correlation between inequality and growth (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1). Their work implies that the pursuit of economic growth may not always be aligned with promoting equality, which could have implications for institutional design and policy making.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) emphasize the role of initial conditions or factor endowments in shaping long-run economic growth paths (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4). Their research suggests that while institutions are important, they may interact with unique country-specific conditions to produce different economic outcomes.

Koepke & Baten (2005) explore the relationship between climate and human living standards, suggesting that environmental factors could also influence long-run economic growth (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.20). This finding adds another layer of complexity to our understanding of institutional impacts on economic growth, as it highlights the potential role of external factors in shaping economic trajectories.

Williamson (1996) discusses the relationship between schooling and Swedish economic growth before World War I, underscoring the importance of human capital development in fostering long-run economic growth (Williamson_1996: p.20). This example illustrates how cultural factors such as education can intersect with institutions to influence economic outcomes.

The literature further underscores the complex relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth, particularly in the context of environmental impact. Frankema (2012) argues that colonial education played a significant role in explaining long-term growth disparities in Africa, with former British colonies experiencing faster growth due to their educational lead (Frankema_2012: p.2). However, the degree of British "liberalism" was endogenous to the local political and economic context (Frankema_2012: p.19).

Hopkins (2019) adds nuance to this argument by suggesting that the focus on totalism and essentialism in African Economic History may have inhibited the study of long-run economic and political change, potentially limiting the diversity of independent African viewpoints (Hopkins_2019: p.4). Hopkins also notes that food exports made little contribution to economic growth during the period of the slave trade in West Africa (Hopkins_2019: p.13).

Goldin (2006) complicates the argument by emphasizing the importance of understanding the evolution and revolution in women's choices and decisions, which could have significant implications for economic growth (Goldin_2006: p.1). Clark & Jacks (2007) contribute to this discussion by examining the cliometric account of the coal industry during the Industrial Revolution, suggesting that technological advancements were crucial in enabling the industry to respond to increases in demand (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.4).

Feinstein (1998) offers insights into the long plateau of economic growth before the Industrial Revolution, suggesting that changes in farming and migration patterns played a significant role (Feinstein_1998: p.2). However, Feinstein also notes that direct information on unemployment during this period is lacking, making any measure very rough (Feinstein_1998: p.23).

These sources demonstrate that while institutions are indeed fundamental to long-run economic growth, the specific factors and mechanisms involved can be complex and context-dependent. Further examination of these factors, particularly in the context of environmental impact, could provide valuable insights into the dynamics of long-term economic development.

The relationship between institutions and economic growth is further complicated by the role of gender inequality, as demonstrated by Akyeampong & Fofack (2014). They illustrate that in West Africa, women's contributions to economic development have often been overlooked due to their labor being categorized as household production (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). This oversight can undermine policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). Moreover, the economic contribution of African women has historically been dependent on their husbands' provision (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.9). These findings suggest that institutions play a crucial role in determining gender roles and economic opportunities, which can impact long-term growth.

Allen et al. (2011) provide additional nuance to the relationship between institutions and economic inequality. They argue that income disparities during the early 20th century were due to industrialization in Japan and Europe compared to stagnation in China (AllenEtAl_2011: p.1). This highlights the role of institutional structures in shaping economic growth trajectories, as the differences in labor markets and technology adoption between these regions led to significant income disparities. Furthermore, their research shows that rapid industrialization in Germany translated into rising real wages for workers (AllenEtAl_2011: p.20), demonstrating a potential link between institutional development and wage growth.

The literature on the role of institutions in long-run economic growth presents a convergence on several key points, while also revealing areas of divergence. Both Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) acknowledge the importance of institutions as determinants of economic growth. Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) emphasizes that with labor tied to land or to the owner, competition among employers ceases, which can hinder economic development. Similarly, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.10) notes that the Western impact is not limited to economic institutions but also extends to social and political structures.

However, there are differences in their perspectives. Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) argues that the Russian situation prior to the peasants' enserfment corresponds to a case where labor is tied to land or to the owner, thus impeding growth. Conversely, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) highlights how the arrival of the British led to an increase in slave dealing and the acquisition of status, which may have stimulated economic growth in certain contexts.

Despite these differences, both scholars agree that institutions play a crucial role in economic growth by influencing competition and resource allocation. However, what remains unresolved is the specific nature of the institutional arrangements conducive to sustained economic growth. Further research is needed to identify the optimal institutional structures for different historical and cultural contexts.

In terms of Allen et al.'s (2011) findings on industrialization and wage growth, it suggests a potential link between institutional development and wage growth, which aligns with Domar's (1970) argument that institutions can influence competition among employers. However, more research is needed to establish a causal relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth.
