The central question under investigation in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, a thesis that has garnered significant attention among economists and historians. The stakes are high, as understanding the drivers of economic growth could potentially inform policy decisions aimed at promoting development and reducing poverty in various regions.

Supporting this perspective, North & Weingast (1989) argue that representative government institutions, characterized by multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, play a crucial role in fostering economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28). Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2001) demonstrate that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress, whereas others facilitated economic development (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27).

On the other hand, Austin (2008) complicates this argument by suggesting that the Atlantic trade reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe (and parts of Asia), implying that institutions may not be solely responsible for long-run growth (Austin_2008: p.24). Kuznets (1973) supports the notion that high aggregate growth rates are associated with rapid changes in economic structure, but also acknowledges the negative consequences of modern economic growth (Kuznets_1973: p.11).

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) and Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) further complicate the discussion by focusing on the role of women's economic contribution, revealing that women's labor was often unaccounted for in historical analyses, which undermines policies aimed at strengthening their economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3; Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1).

As the debate continues, it is clear that institutions play a significant role in long-run economic growth, but other factors such as trade patterns and gender roles also need to be considered. The ongoing discourse promises to provide valuable insights into the complex interplay of factors contributing to economic development over time.

Institutions play a pivotal role in long-run economic growth, as supported by various scholarly works. According to Mokyr (2005), the change in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West can be attributed to developments in the intellectual realm concerning "useful knowledge" (p.4). This knowledge encompassed elements such as individualism, man-made formal law, corporatism, self governance, and rules determined through an institutionalized process (p.56). North (1989) emphasizes that institutions, particularly those with centralized enforcement mechanisms, significantly impact economic growth by reducing transaction costs and increasing security rights (p.9).

Ogilvie (2007) highlights the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework as crucial for economic growth. He argues that the efficiency theory, which assumes most people in the economy want the pie to be as big as possible, may not always hold true due to the complexities of institutional change (p.14). Sokoloff and Engerman (2000) propose that initial conditions, or factor endowments broadly conceived, could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth (p.4).

Acemoglu et al. (2002) underscore the importance of institutions in understanding economic and political development over the past 500 years. They note a reversal in the distribution of economic prosperity, which they attribute to changes in institutions (p.5). This reversal, they argue, is related to the institutional environment that fosters or hinders economic growth (p.5).

These findings collectively suggest that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. However, it is important to consider the complex interplay between institutions and other factors such as trade patterns and gender roles for a comprehensive understanding of economic development over time. Institutional development plays a crucial role in fostering long-term economic growth, as supported by various scholarly works. Acemoglu et al. (2001) provide compelling evidence that more fundamental institutions significantly impact property rights and investment, thereby promoting economic progress. The authors argue that differences in mortality rates are not the primary cause of institutional variation, contrary to some beliefs. Instead, they suggest that these institutions often hinder investment and economic advancement.

Abramovitz (1986) also supports this viewpoint by emphasizing the importance of economic systems that encourage capital investment and effective demand for long-term growth. He suggests that the evolution of mixed economies is closely linked to economic growth itself, further underscoring the significance of institutions in promoting development.

Broadberry et al. (2015) discuss India's long-run economic performance, a topic of ongoing debate among scholars. Their research provides valuable insights into the population estimates and economic trends in India, highlighting the country's complex history and the role of institutions in shaping its development trajectory.

The works of North and Weingast (1989) and Bryant (2006) substantiate the assertion that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth, particularly in the context of India.

North and Weingast (1989) emphasize the importance of political institutions in fostering economic growth. They argue that the presence of multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs are crucial for economic development. Their research highlights how these institutions enable economic growth and political freedom, as demonstrated by the case studies from medieval Europe and England (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

Bryant (2006) also supports this view, focusing on the role of mercantilist imperialism and technological advancements in shaping commercial exchange environments. According to Bryant, these factors played a significant role in economic growth, particularly in Europe (Bryant_2006: p.13).

These findings suggest that the evolution of institutions, including political and economic systems, significantly influenced long-term economic growth. The ongoing debate among scholars regarding the population estimates and economic trends in India further underscores the importance of understanding the role of institutions in shaping a country's development trajectory. Future research could focus on examining the specific institutional changes that led to industrialization and economic growth in various regions, providing valuable insights into the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth.

Challenges to the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth emerge from various perspectives, as some authors suggest that other factors, such as structural changes and environmental impacts, may play significant roles in economic development.

For instance, Stern (2004) questions the Environmental Kuznets Curve (EKC) hypothesis, which posits a positive relationship between economic growth and environmental degradation. Instead, he contends that economic growth might eventually lead to environmental improvement (Stern_2004: p.1). Similarly, McCloskey (2015) argues that ideologies and ideas, rather than institutions, have influenced economic growth in certain regions, such as France, where conservative parties hindered economic development despite the successes of other nations like Holland and Britain (McCloskey_2015: p.9).

Moreover, Bryceson (2002) highlights the role of traditional hierarchical order within communities in shaping women's economic and social spheres, suggesting that these structures may impact economic growth differently than institutions (Bryceson_2002: p.9). Additionally, van Zanden (1999) challenges the consensus view that the early modern economy was unable to generate economic growth, arguing instead that pre-industrial economic growth was more complex and varied than previously thought (vanZanden_1999: p.1).

These sources suggest that while institutions may be crucial for long-run economic growth, other factors such as structural changes, environmental impacts, ideologies, and traditional hierarchical orders also play significant roles in shaping economic development. Future research could explore the interplay between these various factors to gain a more comprehensive understanding of their individual and collective effects on long-term economic growth.

The interplay between various factors contributing to long-run economic growth is a complex web of interactions, as evidenced by several notable works in the field. For instance, Komlos (1998) highlights the significance of physical stature during the Industrial Revolution, suggesting that biological growth processes were influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic conditions (Komlos_1998: p.16). This finding complicates the assertion that institutions are solely responsible for long-term economic growth, as it demonstrates the potential role of other factors, such as population growth and urbanization, in shaping economic development.

Temin (2002) provides another perspective by focusing on the relationship between economic transitions and the Golden Age of capitalism. He argues that the exhaustion of cheap labor caused economic growth to slow, suggesting that the role of institutions may be contingent upon broader economic factors (Temin_2002: p.4). This view complicates the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, as it suggests that other economic transitions play a significant role in shaping economic development over time.

Lindert and Williamson (1985) further challenge the idea that institutions are solely responsible for long-term economic growth by exploring the relationship between economic growth and equality. They argue that policymakers have traditionally sought to balance economic growth with equality, suggesting that the distribution of income may play a role in shaping long-run economic growth (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1). This finding complicates the assertion that institutions are the sole drivers of long-term economic growth, as it suggests that income distribution and equality may also play significant roles in shaping economic development.

Koepke and Baten (2005) provide evidence that climate and environmental factors may also play a role in long-run economic growth. For example, their study on the relationship between climate and human living standards during the Roman Empire reveals that heights did not increase at all over the period of the empire, despite common views that living standards increased due to economic growth (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.16). This finding complicates the assertion that institutions are solely responsible for long-term economic growth, as it suggests that environmental factors may also play a significant role in shaping economic development over time.

The assertion that institutions are the sole drivers of long-term economic growth is further complicated by additional perspectives presented in various studies. Austin (2008) argues that while institutions play a significant role, they may not be the exclusive factor shaping economic development over time. He suggests that environmental factors, such as trade patterns, have also contributed to Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to western Europe and parts of Asia (Austin_2008: p.24).

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) point out the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 in Sub-Saharan Africa, which has hindered its inclusion in recent debates about long-term growth. They emphasize that understanding the extent to which past economic shrinkage still poses risks for future progress is crucial (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.18). This underscores the importance of historical data and comprehensive analysis in assessing the role of institutions in long-term growth.

Bolt & van Zanden (2014) discuss revisions of nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth, including for Germany, Sweden, Italy, Greece, Russia, and Bulgaria. These revisions do not fundamentally change our view but highlight the complexity of factors influencing economic growth (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5). This suggests that while institutions are important, they may interact with other factors, such as trade patterns and technological advancements, to shape long-term economic growth.

The impact of institutions on long-term economic growth is further nuanced by their relationship with education, as evidenced by Frankema (2012), Hopkins (2019), and Goldin (2006). These sources suggest that colonial education played a significant role in shaping the economic trajectories of former colonies, particularly in Africa.

Frankema (2012) argues that the British colonies experienced faster growth due to their educational advantage, a factor often overlooked in discussions about long-run growth disparities. However, the degree of British "liberalism" was not uniform across all colonies but rather endogenous to the local political and economic context (Frankema_2012: p.19). Furthermore, other factors such as post-colonial levels of capital investment and rates of economic growth also contributed to the varying outcomes (Frankema_2012: p.2).

Hopkins (2019) highlights that the focus on totalism and essentialism in African Economic History may have inhibited the study of long-run economic and political change. While food exports made little contribution to economic growth during the period of the slave trade, the superstructure, including education, assumed greater importance as the economic base weakened (Hopkins_2019: p.9, p.13).

Goldin (2006) contributes to the discussion by emphasizing the role of institutions in shaping labor markets and women's choices. The study of labor was once focused on institutions within the labor market, but Goldin shifts the focus to revolutionary changes that occurred outside the traditional labor market (Goldin_2006: p.3). This shift underscores the importance of understanding the broader institutional context in which economic growth occurs.

The works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), while tangential to the direct examination of institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, provide valuable insights into the role of labor markets and institutional changes in economic development.

Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) emphasizes that with labor tied to land or an owner, competition among employers ceases, suggesting a potential hindrance to economic growth due to lack of competition. Conversely, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) notes that the Western impact was not limited to economic institutions, indicating that broader institutional changes could drive growth.

Both scholars touch upon structural change as a factor correlated with economic growth. Domar's (Domar_1970: p.4) reference to the Russian situation prior to peasants' enserfment implies that shifts in labor structures may impact growth, while Bohannan's (Bohannan_1959: p.10) mention of the British influence beyond economic institutions suggests that broader structural changes could be significant.

However, both works do not explicitly argue for institutions as the primary driver of long-run economic growth. The unresolved question in this context is the extent to which institutions, labor markets, and structural changes interact and contribute to long-term economic development. Future research should further explore these interconnections to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the relationship between institutions and economic growth.
