LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, particularly in the context of Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA). This question holds significant importance as understanding the drivers of economic growth in SSA can provide insights into potential strategies for promoting development and reducing poverty.

Supporting this thesis, Austin (2008) argues that institutions play a crucial role in long-term economic growth by creating an environment conducive to investment and progress. He contends that the Atlantic trade reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe. Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2001) assert that institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress in various regions.

On the other hand, some authors offer critiques of this perspective. Kuznets (1973) suggests that while modern economic growth is associated with rapid changes in economic structure, it also brings about unexpected negative results. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has kept SSA absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth.

Several sources add nuance or qualifications to this debate. For instance, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) highlight the importance of understanding how women's economic contribution becomes subsumed under household production and is often unaccounted for in policy discussions. Komlos (1998) complicates the issue by suggesting that while institutions play a role, they are not the only factor influencing economic growth.

This review demonstrates a complex landscape of opinions on the role of institutions in long-run economic growth, particularly in SSA. Future research should aim to reconcile these perspectives and provide a more comprehensive understanding of this critical issue. The stakes are high, as finding the root causes of long-term economic growth can inform policies aimed at promoting development and reducing poverty in SSA and other regions.

The relationship between economic growth and societal changes is further substantiated in various works. Kuznets (1973) asserts that rapid structural changes, along with technological innovations, significantly impact both economic and social structure (Kuznets_1973: p.4). These transformations are not only linked to high aggregate growth rates but also associated with unexpected negative outcomes within developed countries, while less developed nations struggle to utilize modern technology effectively (Kuznets_1973: p.11).

Acemoglu et al. (2001) emphasize the role of fundamental institutions in shaping economic progress and investment, suggesting that detrimental institutions can hinder long-term development (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). They also argue that differences in mortality rates are not the primary cause of institutional variations (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3).

Mokyr (2005) underscores the significance of intellectual developments, particularly the growth of economic reasonableness, in explaining the change in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West (Mokyr_2005: p.4). He suggests that this shift must be attributed to advancements in the intellectual realm concerning "useful knowledge" (Mokyr_2005: p.4).

Acemoglu et al. (2002) reinforce the importance of institutions in understanding economic and political development over the past 500 years, emphasizing that the reversal in the distribution of economic prosperity is likely related to institutional changes (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5).

The works of North & Weingast (1989) and North (1989) provide substantial evidence supporting the thesis that institutions are crucial for long-run economic growth. North & Weingast (1989) argue that political factors underpin economic growth, emphasizing the role of institutions in enforcing rules and facilitating change (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). They also contend that absolutist states, such as early modern Spain, created economic conditions that hindered long-term growth due to the lack of constraints on centralized enforcement mechanisms (North&Weingast_1989: p.7).

Similarly, North (1989) posits that institutions play a fundamental role in reducing transaction costs and increasing security rights, which in turn contribute to economic growth (North_1989: p.9). He also suggests that the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework is crucial for economic development (Ogilvie_2007: p.27).

Furthermore, Ogilvie (2007) highlights the importance of bringing institutions inside the tent of economic analysis, recognizing their interdependency and role in long-term economic growth (Ogilvie_2007: p.3). Nunn & Wantchekon (2011) support this view by demonstrating that both internal and external channels influenced institutional development and subsequent economic growth (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.4).

Institutions play a crucial role in fostering industrialization and long-term economic growth, as supported by evidence from Austin (2008) and Allen (2001). Austin (Austin_2008: p.26) posits that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, with the implication being that trade, even the Atlantic one, reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe. This suggests that strong institutional structures can mitigate the negative effects of external factors on a region's development.

Allen (Allen_2001: p.4) supports this notion by highlighting the relationship between structural change and economic growth in the British industrial revolution. The wages of building craftsmen, as an indicator of trends in average earnings, demonstrate a strong correlation with growth during this period. This correlation underscores the importance of institutions in facilitating economic development through structural changes.

Moreover, both authors emphasize the need for long-term historical analysis to reveal recurring relationships that stem from similar situations or path dependencies. Austin (Austin_2008: p.1) advocates for examining growth theories against long-term history, while Allen (Allen_2001: p.15) bases his welfare ratio on the assumption that illness, injury, bad weather, and economic fluctuations prevented work on every permissible day, and that there was no long-run trend in their incidence. These findings underscore the importance of considering historical context when studying the impact of institutions on economic growth.

Structural change, rather than institutions, may be the primary driver of long-run economic growth. This argument is supported by evidence suggesting that economic growth can occur without significant institutional reforms (Simson_2019: p.4). For instance, in the sub-Saharan African region, state expenditure and deficits expanded during a period of high economic growth in the 1960s and 1970s, challenging the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-term economic development (Simson_2019: p.25).

Moreover, the emergence of the factory system and the growing division of labor during the eighteenth century played a crucial role in driving economic growth, as opposed to institutional changes (Humphries_2013: p.18). Similarly, the consensus view in the 1960s and 1970s was that the early modern economy was unable to generate economic growth, casting doubt on the assertion that institutions are the primary determinant of long-term economic development (vanZanden_1999: p.1).

These findings suggest that while institutions may influence economic growth, they may not be the fundamental cause. Further exploration is needed to understand the complex interplay between institutions and structural changes in driving long-run economic growth.

The assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth becomes more nuanced when considering the contributions of various authors. Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) argue that while institutions play a significant role, initial conditions and factor endowments may also have profound and enduring impacts on long-term economic growth paths. This suggests that the interplay between institutions and structural changes might be more complex than initially thought.

Furthermore, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) emphasize the importance of understanding how women's economic contributions have been subsumed under household production, which can undermine policies aimed at strengthening their economic roles. This underscores the idea that institutions may need to address gender disparities to effectively promote long-term economic growth.

Frankema (2012) focuses on colonial education as a key factor in explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa, suggesting that the impact of institutions extends beyond their traditional scope and encompasses educational systems inherited from colonizers. Goldin (2006) and Bolt & van Zanden (2014) also contribute to this nuanced view by highlighting the importance of education, labor market structures, and historical revisions in understanding economic growth patterns.

Williamson (1996) discusses the relationship between schooling and economic growth, emphasizing the need for a clear definition of convergence to understand its sources and implications. This underscores the complexity of the relationship between institutions and economic growth, as understanding convergence is fundamental to comprehending who benefits and who suffers from it.

The relationship between institutions and economic growth is further nuanced by the role of technological advancements, as evidenced in various historical contexts. Komlos (1998) discusses the concept of modern economic growth, highlighting its Kuznetsian sense and the influence of short-term fluctuations during the Industrial Revolution on physical stature (Komlos_1998: p.2). This suggests that while institutions play a crucial role in fostering long-term economic growth, technological advancements may also significantly impact population growth and urbanization, which in turn can contribute to economic development.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) support this notion by emphasizing the correlation between imbalance and inequality in Japan's economic history, implying that technological advancements could lead to greater income variability and structural changes (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.14). Furthermore, they suggest a causal direction from inequality to growth, hinting at the potential for technological advancements to drive both inequality and economic growth (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.8).

Clark & Jacks (2007) provide an example of this relationship in their study of the coal industry during the Industrial Revolution. They demonstrate that technological advancements enabled the coal industry to respond effectively to increases in demand, underscoring the importance of technological innovation in driving economic growth (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.4).

Despite the significant role of technological advancements in driving economic growth during the Industrial Revolution (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.4), it is crucial to acknowledge that institutions play a pivotal role in shaping long-run economic development, particularly in regions such as Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA). As Broadberry & Gardner (2022) note, SSA has been largely excluded from debates about comparative long-term growth due to the scarcity of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1).

This absence of data raises questions about the economic performance in SSA, with Collier and Gunning (1999) observing that African economic performance has been markedly worse than other regions (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). However, it is essential to understand that this poor performance may be linked to the institutions present in SSA, which could have hindered economic growth and convergence.

Moreover, Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) discuss the disagreement among scholars regarding the role of institutions in shaping long-run economic growth. They argue that all actors in the market influence each other's strategies, suggesting a complex interplay between institutions and economic development (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.21). This perspective underscores the importance of understanding the specific institutional contexts within which economic growth occurs, as these can significantly impact the trajectory of long-term development.

One such example of the institutional influence on economic growth is the role of coinage in early European economies (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.7). The standardization and widespread use of coins facilitated trade, enabling economic growth by reducing transaction costs and promoting specialization. In contrast, the absence or poor quality of coinage in other regions could have hindered economic development, as seen in SSA before 1950 (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1).

Scholars Domar, Bohannan, and Bryceson offer tangential insights into the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth. Domar (1970) emphasizes that labor tied to land or an owner hinders competition among employers, which can stifle economic growth. This aligns with the assertion that institutions play a crucial role in promoting growth by reducing transaction costs and fostering specialization.

Bohannan (1959) discusses how Western impact on African societies extended beyond economic institutions, focusing on slave dealing and status acquisition. While this does not directly address long-term economic growth, it highlights the broader influence of institutions on societal structures. Bryceson (2002) explores how traditional hierarchical orders within communities affect women's economic and social spheres, suggesting that institutions can impact gender roles and livelihood diversification.

However, there is a lack of direct evidence from these sources that explicitly supports the claim that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. The relationships between institutions, labor markets, and economic development remain complex and multifaceted, with various factors influencing each other in intricate ways. Further research is needed to fully understand this relationship and its implications for economic growth over extended periods.
