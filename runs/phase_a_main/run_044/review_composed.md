The central question at hand is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth, a claim that has garnered substantial support from various scholars in the field of economics and history. The stakes are high as this understanding can significantly influence policy decisions aimed at promoting economic development, particularly in underdeveloped regions.

Austin (2008) supports the thesis by arguing that institutions play a crucial role in determining long-run growth, reinforcing rather than originating Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe and parts of Asia. Similarly, Kuznets (1973) emphasizes the association between high aggregate growth rates and rapid changes in economic structure, which are also linked to societal innovations that provide necessary adjustments for economic progress.

However, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate the argument by pointing out that Sub-Saharan Africa has been absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth due to the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance, suggesting that understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution becomes subsumed under household production is essential for policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles.

In support of the central thesis, North & Weingast (1989) focus on the political factors underlying economic growth and the development of markets, demonstrating how institutions played a necessary role in making possible economic growth and political freedom. Acemoglu et al. (2001) also support this view by showing that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress.

As the debate continues, it is clear that institutions play a significant role in long-term economic growth. However, understanding the intricacies of these relationships and their implications for policy decisions remains a complex task, requiring further research and analysis. The stakes are high, as policies based on an incomplete understanding could lead to unintended consequences and hinder economic development.

The assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is substantiated by various scholarly works. Douglas North (1989) emphasizes the role of institutions in reducing transaction costs, which in turn contributes to economic growth. He argues that "centralized enforcement mechanisms of Spain" were instrumental in promoting economic growth (North_1989: p.1). Similarly, North highlights how the development of secure property rights and capital markets can foster long-term economic growth (North_1989: p.9).

In a similar vein, Whatley (2018) investigates the relationship between gunpowder imports and slave exports, providing evidence that institutions play a crucial role in shaping these economically significant activities. Whatley's work suggests that when gunpowder is above its long-run equilibrium level, it has implications for slave exports (Whatley_2018: p.11).

Pamuk (2007) supports this view by demonstrating how the Black Death and modern economic growth led to significant changes in wages and incomes during the last millennium. He contends that these changes "paved the way for the growing commercialization of agriculture" (Pamuk_2007: p.24).

Nunn (2008) provides further evidence by examining the relationship between ethnic fractionalization and long-term economic growth. Nunn argues that due to the environment of uncertainty and insecurity at the time, individuals required well-defined property rights to engage in economic activities (Nunn_2008: p.4).

Broadberry et al. (2015) support this perspective by investigating India's long-run economic performance, suggesting that institutions played a crucial role in shaping the country's economic trajectory (BroadberryEtal_2015: p.2).

Lastly, Peters (2004) underscores the importance of institutions in promoting economic growth through his study on land policy reform. He contends that "the aims have been to promote economic growth, encourage more sustainable management, and reduce poverty" (Peters_2004: p.7).

Institutional development plays a pivotal role in fostering long-term economic growth, as evidenced by various scholarly works. Mokyr (2005) posits that the Enlightenment and subsequent intellectual advancements significantly contributed to the West's economic progress by promoting useful knowledge and instilling elements of a progressive society such as individualism, formal law, corporatism, self-governance, and rule of law through institutionalized processes (Mokyr_2005: p.56).

Abramovitz (1986) supports this notion by suggesting that the evolution of mixed economies is a direct result of economic growth itself, with macroeconomic and monetary conditions encouraging capital investment and effective demand playing crucial roles in this process (Abramovitz_1986: p.20). Furthermore, Abramovitz argues that productivity growth has become increasingly important for countries in the modern era (Abramovitz_1986: p.22).

Hopkins (2009) contends that ethnic fragmentation is not solely responsible for unproductive institutions; other factors such as solidarity and motives for action need to be considered before drawing conclusions about the impact of ethnicity on development (Hopkins_2009: p.22). The author also highlights the importance of examining methodology in studies on ethnicity and development (Hopkins_2009: p.10).

Nunn & Wantchekon (2011) provide empirical evidence for the internal and external channels through which institutional development affects economic growth. Their research indicates that both channels are important, but the internal channel is at least twice as large as the external channel (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.4). This finding supports the notion that institutions play a significant role in promoting long-term economic growth by fostering domestic development rather than relying on external factors alone.

The relationship between institutions and technological advancements further supports the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Kuznets (1973) argues that rapid changes in economic structure, often associated with technological innovations, are major factors affecting economic and social structure (Kuznets_1973: p.4, p.6). This suggests that institutions play a crucial role in facilitating the adoption and implementation of new technologies, thereby promoting economic growth.

Ogilvie (2007) also emphasizes the importance of institutions in technological advancements. He points out that economic institutions, which provide the necessary framework for technology to thrive, are critical in determining a society's ability to adapt and prosper (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). This implies that the effectiveness of a country's institutions can significantly impact its capacity to leverage technological innovations for economic growth.

deVries (1994) highlights the role of institutions in establishing a framework for regional economic development, particularly during the early modern period when the Industrial Revolution took place (deVries_1994: p.5). This finding underscores the importance of institutions in facilitating industrial growth and long-term economic development.

Despite the assertion that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth, some argue that technological advancements may also play a significant role in this process (deVries_1994: p.5). This perspective is exemplified in Simson's work from 2019, where he discusses the expansion of industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy during certain periods (Simson_2019: p.27). Although institutions might have facilitated these developments, Simson suggests that technological advancements could have been instrumental in driving them.

Furthermore, Simson challenges the notion that institutional reforms were essential for sustaining investment levels and economic growth during specific decades (Simson_2019: p.4). He argues that while there might have been spending inefficiencies, leakages, and costly delays to reform, their elimination may not have been sufficient to maintain the investment levels of the 1960s or reorient these economies (Simson_2019: p.4).

In addition, Simson's research indicates that state expenditure and deficits expanded in certain regions during a period of high economic growth and global optimism about future prosperity (Simson_2019: p.25). This suggests that technological advancements could have contributed to the expansion of public employment rather than institutional reforms. However, further examination is needed to fully understand the relationship between institutions, technological advancements, and long-term economic growth.

The relationship between institutions, technological advancements, and long-term economic growth is a complex one, with various factors contributing to its intricacies. While some literature suggests that institutions are the primary drivers of long-run economic growth (North & Weingast_1989), others complicate this perspective by highlighting the role of labor market dynamics in shaping growth trajectories.

One such complication comes from the examination of colonial education's impact on long-run growth disparities in Africa. Frankema (2012) argues that the former British colonies experienced faster growth due to their educational lead, which was endogenous to the local political and economic context. This finding suggests that the quality of education provided by colonial powers may have played a significant role in fostering long-term economic growth in these regions.

Moreover, Goldin (2006) emphasizes the importance of understanding labor market evolution and revolution in shaping women's choices and decisions. By examining changes in legal restrictions on women's employment, Goldin reveals that these shifts had profound effects on long-term economic growth by altering the supply and demand dynamics of the labor force.

Additionally, Williamson (1996) discusses how mass migrations reduced the pace of real wage growth in receiving countries, potentially hindering long-run economic growth. This observation underscores the importance of considering labor market adjustments when analyzing institutions' role in fostering long-term economic growth.

Furthermore, Allen et al. (2011) demonstrate that income disparities in the early 20th century were due to stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe. They also highlight the role of labor markets in absorbing non-food items, indicating that institutional factors may have influenced consumption patterns and, by extension, economic growth.

Lastly, van Zanden (1999) challenges the consensus view that the early modern economy was unable to generate economic growth before the industrial revolution. By examining wage trends and living standards in various regions, van Zanden provides evidence suggesting that institutions played a crucial role in shaping long-term economic growth during this period.

The evidence provided by Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicates the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Their study highlights a significant gap in data on aggregate economic performance before 1950, particularly in Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA), which has been absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.1). This lack of data for SSA potentially challenges the universality of institutions as a driving force for economic growth, suggesting that other factors may play a more significant role in this region.

Moreover, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) note that African countries have historically experienced slower economic growth compared to other regions (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.1). This observation implies that institutions may not have been as influential in shaping long-term economic growth in Africa as they were in other parts of the world, further complicating the argument.

On the other hand, Komlos (1998) discusses physical stature during the Industrial Revolution and suggests that economic processes and associated structural changes significantly influenced biological growth processes (Komlos_1998: p.16, p.17). This finding implies that economic growth may not be solely dependent on institutions but could also be influenced by other factors such as population growth, urbanization, and income inequality.

Lastly, Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that the task of explaining long-run distribution trends rejoins the search for explanations of growth itself (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.9). This observation complicates the argument by suggesting that the relationship between economic growth and inequality is intertwined, implying that institutions may not be the sole determinant of long-run economic growth but could be influenced by factors such as income distribution trends.

The relationship between institutions and economic growth in colonial economies is further nuanced by considering the role of gender dynamics and the impact of colonial administrations on local economies.

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) highlight how African women's contributions to economic growth were often overlooked due to their labor being subsumed under household production, undermining policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles (p.3). Moreover, the authors demonstrate that the economic provision of husbands significantly influenced women's contribution to economic growth in West Africa (p.9, p.19). These findings suggest that understanding gender dynamics is crucial for a comprehensive analysis of institutions and their impact on long-run economic growth.

Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) argue that the relationship between colonizers and the local economy was complex, with each influencing the other (p.21). They contend that this interdependence can be found in Marx's writings, implying that the economic strategies of colonizers had profound effects on the institutions and growth patterns of colonial economies (p.21). This perspective complicates the notion that institutions are solely responsible for long-run economic growth by emphasizing the role of external forces such as colonization.

Stoler (1989) contends that colonizers lived in "imagined communities" designed to overcome social and economic disparities between themselves and the local population (p.5). The author also notes that these imagined communities created a more solid alliance between the plantation elite and the colonial administration, which could have significant implications for the institutions and growth patterns of colonial economies (p.21). This finding suggests that understanding the dynamics of colonization is essential to comprehending how institutions shape long-run economic growth in colonial settings.

The works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), while tangential to the direct examination of institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, provide valuable insights that help contextualize this argument. Both scholars acknowledge the role of institutions in shaping economic dynamics, albeit in different contexts.

Domar (1970) focuses on the factors of production, particularly labor, and its impact on economic growth. He argues that when labor is tied to land or an owner, competition among employers ceases, potentially stifling economic progress. This aligns with the assertion that institutions play a crucial role in fostering or hindering economic growth by shaping the competitive landscape.

On the other hand, Bohannan (1959) delves into the cultural and societal implications of Western colonization on African societies, highlighting how these impacts extended beyond economic institutions. He notes that the arrival of the British, for instance, influenced social structures, limiting women's roles and curtailing territorial expansion. This suggests that colonial institutions, in their entirety, had a profound impact on long-term economic growth.

Despite these convergences, both authors leave several questions unresolved. Domar (1970) does not explore how the dynamics he describes might vary across different colonies or over time, nor does he delve into the role of non-labor factors in economic growth. Similarly, Bohannan (1959) does not explicitly link his findings on cultural and societal changes to long-term economic growth patterns.

Therefore, future research could benefit from a more direct examination of how specific institutions shaped by colonization influenced structural change and long-run economic growth in various colonial settings. This would help solidify the argument that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth in these contexts.
