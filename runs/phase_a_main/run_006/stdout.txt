LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The debate on the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth, particularly in the context of Africa, has been a subject of intense scholarly discussion. A central question that arises is whether institutions play a pivotal role in driving economic development over the long run.

The proponents of this view argue that institutions are indeed the primary drivers of economic growth (Austin_2008: p.26, North&Weingast_1989: p.2). For instance, Austin (2008) suggests that institutions as "the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth" offer a logical and attractive explanation for economic disparities between regions. Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) emphasize the importance of representative government and its role in fostering economic growth and political freedom.

On the other hand, some scholars complicate this argument by highlighting factors that may influence or qualify the relationship between institutions and economic growth. For example, Broadberry & Gardner (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1) point out the lack of data on economic performance before 1950 in Sub-Saharan Africa, which has limited the region's inclusion in recent debates about long-term growth. Akyeampong & Fofack (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3) similarly argue that understanding the processes by which women's economic contributions are subsumed under household production is crucial for policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles.

The stakes of this debate are significant, as understanding the fundamental causes of long-term economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting development and reducing economic disparities between regions. The ongoing discussion underscores the need for further research to clarify the role of institutions in economic growth, particularly in light of complicating factors such as regional differences and gender dynamics.

In future analyses, it will be important to consider how institutions interact with other factors, such as technological innovation (Kuznets_1973: p.6) and social structure (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.18), in driving economic growth over the long term. Additionally, a more nuanced understanding of the role of institutions in shaping gender dynamics and women's economic contributions could provide valuable insights for policy makers seeking to promote equitable economic development.

The argument continues as we delve deeper into the complex interplay between institutions, technology, social structure, and gender dynamics in driving long-term economic growth.

The role of institutions in driving long-term economic growth is further substantiated by various scholars, each providing unique insights into this complex interplay. Douglas North and Barry Weingast (1989) argue that the development of representative government, characterized by multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, played a crucial role in promoting economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.2).

In a similar vein, North (1989) emphasizes that institutions are essential for reducing transaction costs and increasing security rights, which in turn foster market development and economic growth (North_1989: p.9). Furthermore, he suggests that the effectiveness of economic institutions is contingent upon their interdependence within a wider framework (Ogilvie_2007: p.27).

The work of Sokoloff and Engerman (2000) highlights the significance of initial conditions, or factor endowments, in shaping long-run economic growth paths across countries (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4). They also demonstrate that contrasting records in growth can be traced back to the achievement of sustained economic growth by certain nations during specific periods (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.12).

Abramovitz (1986) supports this argument by suggesting that the elaboration of mixed economies is a function of economic growth itself, with countries readjusting the balance in favor of productivity growth over time (Abramovitz_1986: p.22). Hopkins (2009), meanwhile, discusses how institutions can support communities rather than acting as a weight on them, thus contributing to economic development (Hopkins_2009: p.21).

Together, these studies underscore the fundamental role of institutions in fostering long-term economic growth and provide valuable insights for policy makers seeking to promote equitable economic development. However, further research is needed to fully understand the nuanced interplay between institutions, technology, social structure, and gender dynamics in this process.

The interplay between institutions and agriculture plays a pivotal role in promoting long-term economic growth, as highlighted by several scholars. Austin (2008) posits that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, with trade reinforcing rather than originating Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe and parts of Asia (Austin_2008: p.24). Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2001) argue that institutions have a substantial impact on economic performance, with detrimental ones hindering investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27).

In the agricultural sector specifically, Pamuk (2007) suggests that changes in agriculture contributed to modern economic growth, particularly during the Industrial Revolution (Pamuk_2007: p.4). This commercialization of agriculture paved the way for increased productivity and prosperity, further underscoring the critical role institutions play in shaping agricultural practices and, consequently, long-term economic growth.

Acemoglu et al. (2002) further emphasize this point by demonstrating that the reversal of economic prosperity over the past 500 years is closely linked to institutional changes (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5). The authors argue that understanding these institutions is crucial for comprehending both historical and contemporary development, as well as evaluating various theories of long-run development (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49).

Institutions and education play a significant role in fostering long-run economic growth, as suggested by Mokyr (2005) and Whatley (2018). Mokyr (Mokyr_2005: p.4) posits that the change in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West can be attributed to developments in the intellectual realm concerning "useful knowledge." He further argues that many elements of a progressive society, such as individualism, formal law, corporatism, self-governance, and rules determined through an institutionalized process, emerged during this period (Mokyr_2005: p.56).

Whatley (2018) supports this perspective by demonstrating the impact of institutions on economic growth through the example of gunpowder imports and slave exports. The author's model indicates that when gunpowder is above its long-run equilibrium level, it correlates with a decrease in slave exports (Whatley_2018: p.11). This relationship suggests that the presence of efficient institutions, as represented by the use of advanced technology like gunpowder, can lead to economic growth and development.

Furthermore, Mokyr (Mokyr_2005: p.54) emphasizes that the growth of economic reasonableness was far less monotonic and irreversible than technological progress, implying that education and institutions play a crucial role in shaping economic growth patterns over time. In conclusion, understanding the relationship between institutions and education is essential for comprehending long-run economic development (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49).

Despite the significant role that institutions are believed to play in shaping economic growth patterns over time, some scholars argue that other factors may also contribute to long-run economic development. For instance, Simson (2019) suggests that spending inefficiencies, leakages, and costly delays to reform might have hindered the ability of certain economies to sustain investment levels during periods of high growth (Simson_2019: p.4). Furthermore, McCloskey (2015) contends that ideologies and ideologies, rather than institutions, may have played a crucial role in economic growth patterns, as demonstrated by the examples of France and Spain (McCloskey_2015: p.8-9). These perspectives challenge the notion that institutions are the sole fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

In Simson's (2019) analysis, he highlights the expansion of state expenditure and deficits in several African economies during the 1960s and 1970s, a period of high economic growth (Simson_2019: p.25). While these investments might have been necessary for industrial development and educational expansion, Simson questions whether their elimination would have been sufficient to sustain the investment levels of the time or reorient these economies towards long-term growth (Simson_2019: p.4).

McCloskey (2015) also presents a counterargument by focusing on the role of ideologies in economic growth, particularly in France and Spain during the 16th and 18th centuries. He argues that conservative ideologies throttled economic growth in these countries despite the examples of economic success in other regions (McCloskey_2015: p.9). This perspective challenges the idea that institutions are the primary drivers of long-run economic development, as it suggests that ideological factors may also play a significant role.

These perspectives challenge the notion that institutions are the sole fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by highlighting the potential impact of other factors such as spending inefficiencies, ideologies, and conservative policies on economic growth patterns over time. Understanding these complex relationships between institutions, ideologies, and economic growth is crucial for a comprehensive understanding of long-run economic development. The assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is further nuanced by acknowledging the historical context and regional disparities in economic development.

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) highlight the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 in Sub-Saharan Africa, which has led to its absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. This suggests that the understanding of institutions' role in economic growth may be incomplete without considering regional differences and historical data (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.1).

Komlos (1998) discusses the physical stature during the Industrial Revolution, implying that biological growth processes were significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic conditions. This complicates the argument by suggesting that economic institutions may not be the sole determinants of long-run economic growth, as other factors such as short-term economic cycles could also play a role (Komlos_1998: p.16).

Temin (2002) argues that the exhaustion of cheap labor caused economic growth to slow, suggesting that institutional changes may not always be the primary factor influencing long-run economic growth. Instead, external factors such as labor availability could also significantly impact growth patterns (Temin_2002: p.4).

Hopkins (2019) discusses how successive generations of African economic historians have been influenced by Western ideologies, potentially limiting the diversity of independent African viewpoints. This complicates the understanding of institutions' role in African economic growth, as the research may have been biased by external influences (Hopkins_2019: p.4).

vanZanden (1999) challenges the consensus view that the early modern economy was unable to generate economic growth, suggesting that the link between institutions and long-run economic growth may not be as straightforward as previously thought (vanZanden_1999: p.1).

The relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth is further nuanced by the consideration of education, as suggested by various studies. Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that the British classical economists and policymakers were conscious of the connection between economic growth and equality, recognizing the need to balance these two aspects in their policies (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1). Furthermore, they propose that understanding long-run distribution trends is crucial for explaining growth itself (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.9).

Frankema (2012) supports this notion by emphasizing the role of colonial education in explaining long-run growth disparities, particularly in Africa. He argues that former British colonies exhibited faster growth due to their educational advantage (Frankema_2012: p.2). However, Frankema also points out that the degree of British "liberalism" was influenced by the local political and economic context (Frankema_2012: p.19), suggesting a more complex relationship between institutions and growth.

Goldin (2006) adds to this discourse by focusing on the evolution and revolution in labor markets. She argues that understanding the distinction between these two concepts is crucial for comprehending women's choices and decisions, which can significantly impact economic growth (Goldin_2006: p.1). Goldin also highlights the importance of legal changes in shaping labor markets over both short and long-term periods (Goldin_2006: p.14), underscoring the role of institutions in facilitating structural change and, consequently, economic growth.

These studies complicate the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by emphasizing the importance of education, local context, and legal changes in shaping labor markets and influencing economic growth. Further research should explore how these factors interact with institutional structures to determine their relative impact on long-term economic development.

Institutional Development and Economic Growth: A Complicated Relationship

The relationship between institutional development and economic growth is not as straightforward as previously thought, as various factors interact to determine long-term economic development. This complexity is further evidenced by the findings of Koepke & Baten (2005), Bolt & van Zanden (2014), and Skocpol & Sommers (1980).

Koepke & Baten (2005) argue that climate, a factor often overlooked in discussions about institutions and economic growth, plays a significant role. For instance, their study on stature and nutrition in the Habsburg monarchy reveals that living standards and economic development did not necessarily increase during the Roman Empire, despite common assumptions (Koepke & Baten_2005: p.16). This finding complicates the argument that institutional structures are the sole cause of long-run economic growth.

Bolt & van Zanden (2014) also contribute to this discourse by highlighting the importance of comparing economic growth across different regions and periods. Their research on early modern economic growth in Europe and Indonesia demonstrates that understanding economic development requires a comprehensive comparison of various structures, rather than focusing solely on institutional development (Bolt & vanZanden_2014: p.15). This comparative approach further complicates the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

Skocpol & Sommers (1980) provide another perspective by emphasizing the role of external factors, such as Western intrusions, in shaping institutional development and economic growth. Their study on social revolutions reveals that comparing cases can increase the visibility of one structure by contrasting it with another (Skocpol&Sommers_1980: p.8). This comparative approach complicates the argument that institutions are solely responsible for long-run economic growth, as it suggests that external factors also play a significant role.

The evidence provided by Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), though tangential to the primary argument, offer insights that both support and challenge the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

Domar (1970) highlights the role of institutions in shaping labor markets, as he discusses the impact of serfdom and slavery on competition among employers, suggesting that when labor is tied to land or owners, such competition ceases (Domar_1970: p.4). This aligns with the argument that institutions can hinder economic growth by limiting labor mobility and competition. However, Domar also acknowledges the external factor of British influence in other areas, as he mentions the economic impact of slave dealing and the acquisition of status (Domar_1970: p.16).

Bohannan (1959) provides a comparative perspective on the role of institutions in economic growth by examining the impact of Western influence on African societies. He notes that the Western impact was not limited to economic institutions but also extended to social and political structures (Bohannan_1959: p.10). This suggests that external factors, such as colonialism, can significantly shape institutional development and, consequently, long-term economic growth.

Despite these insights, both sources do not fully address the question of whether institutions are solely responsible for long-run economic growth. The evidence provided complicates this argument by suggesting that external factors also play a significant role. Furthermore, neither source provides a comprehensive analysis of how institutions interact with other factors to drive economic growth over the long term.
