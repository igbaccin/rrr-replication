LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

Long-run economic growth is a topic of ongoing debate among economists, with institutions being posited as the fundamental cause. This review examines evidence from various sources to assess the validity of this claim and its implications.

The central question at hand is whether institutions are indeed the primary driver of long-term economic growth, or if other factors play a more significant role. The stakes are high, as understanding the causes of economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting development in underdeveloped regions.

Several sources support the thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Austin (2008) endorses the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history, revealing relationships that recur due to path dependency and similar situations. Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2001) argue that institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress in certain regions.

However, other sources complicate the picture. For instance, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) suggest that Sub-Saharan Africa's absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth is due to a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) highlight the challenges in accurately measuring women's contributions to economic growth and development, which undermines policies seeking to strengthen their economic roles.

Komlos (1998) also adds nuance, arguing that while physical stature during the Industrial Revolution correlated with economic growth, other factors such as population growth, urbanization, and income inequality were also significant.

Critiques of the thesis are less common but do exist. Kuznets (1973), for example, notes that while high aggregate growth rates are associated with rapid changes in economic structure, there can be unexpected negative results even within developed countries.

This review has established that institutions are posited as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by some, but others complicate this picture. The stakes are high, as understanding the causes of economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting development in underdeveloped regions. Future research should aim to reconcile these differing perspectives and clarify the role institutions play in long-term economic growth.

Institutions play a pivotal role in fostering long-term economic growth, as suggested by various scholars. Austin (2008) posits that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, emphasizing their importance in shaping trade patterns and reinforcing economic positions (Austin_2008: p.26). Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) argue that political factors underpinning economic growth and the development of markets are crucial, highlighting the role institutions play in enforcing rules and facilitating change (North&Weingast_1989: p.2).

Kuznets (1973) supports this view by linking economic growth with social innovations that provide necessary adjustments (Kuznets_1973: p.6). Mokyr (2005) suggests that the change in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West must be explained through developments in the intellectual realm concerning "useful knowledge" (Mokyr_2005: p.4). North (1989) further supports this perspective by focusing on how institutions affect transaction costs, security rights, and market development, which are all integral to economic growth (North_1989: p.9).

Ogilvie (2007) acknowledges the interdependency of individual institutions within a broader framework, emphasizing their role in shaping economic analysis (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). The consensus among these scholars underscores the significance of institutions in long-term economic growth and development. Future research should delve deeper into understanding the specific mechanisms through which institutions influence growth patterns and how they can be leveraged to promote development in underdeveloped regions.

The significance of institutions in shaping long-term economic growth is substantiated by the findings of Acemoglu et al. (2001, p.27) who assert that more fundamental institutions influence property rights and investments, which are crucial for economic progress. They further argue that differences in mortality rates do not primarily account for variations in institutions, contradicting the Malthusian Trap theory (Acemoglu et al., 2001, p.3).

Acemoglu et al. (2001, p.8) also emphasize the persistence of these institutions due to economic mechanisms, suggesting that once established, they can have long-lasting effects on a region's development trajectory. This persistence is further supported by their observation that detrimental institutions hindered investment and progress (Acemoglu et al., 2001, p.27).

In a similar vein, Acemoglu et al. (2002, p.49) underscore the importance of institutions in understanding economic and political development over the past 500 years. They also note an intriguing change in the distribution of economic prosperity, which they attribute to institutional changes (Acemoglu et al., 2002, p.6). The authors question whether this change is related to institutions, indicating that further exploration of this connection could yield valuable insights into long-term growth patterns (Acemoglu et al., 2002, p.5).

These findings support the argument that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Understanding the mechanisms through which these institutions influence growth patterns and their persistence can provide valuable insights for promoting development in underdeveloped regions.

Structural change, a shift in the economy from agriculture to industry and services, is a crucial mechanism through which institutions influence long-run economic growth (Abramovitz_1986: p.16). This transformation, driven by institutional arrangements that facilitate innovation and investment, leads to increased productivity and economic expansion.

In Abramovitz's analysis, the elaboration of mixed economies is found to be a result of economic growth itself (Abramovitz_1986: p.20). This suggests that institutions evolve over time to adapt to changing economic conditions, fostering structural change and thereby promoting long-term growth.

Moreover, Abramovitz argues that the trend towards a mixed economy is not just confined to the leaders but is a generalized effect on productivity growth (Abramovitz_1986: p.16). This indicates that institutional development, leading to structural change, can have broad implications for economic growth across different regions and countries.

However, it's important to note that institutions can also negatively impact economic growth by reducing profits and the incentives for investment and innovation (Abramovitz_1986: p.16). Therefore, understanding the specific institutional arrangements that foster or hinder structural change is essential for promoting development in underdeveloped regions.

Challenges to the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth can be found in various perspectives. For instance, Stern (2004) argues that economic growth may not always lead to environmental degradation over the course of economic development (Stern_2004: p.3). He suggests that there is a possibility that environmental damage does not reduce economic activity sufficiently to stop the growth process, and any irreversibility is not severe enough to reduce future income levels.

Simson (2019) also presents a counterargument by highlighting the importance given to industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy in certain regions, which may have been prioritized over protecting those already in government jobs (Simson_2019: p.27). According to Simson, this could potentially explain why economic growth was sustained even with spending inefficiencies, leakages, and costly delays to reform.

McCloskey (2015) critiques the notion by stating that ideas and ideologies, not institutions or interests, played a significant role in shaping economic growth in certain countries during specific periods (McCloskey_2015: p.9). For example, in France, economic growth was throttled until very recently due to conservative reasons despite the examples of bourgeois economic and political successes in other regions (McCloskey_2015: p.9).

In addition, Bryceson (2002) challenges the thesis by demonstrating that traditional hierarchical orders within communities can influence women’s economic and social spheres, potentially hindering their participation in non-agrarian activities (Bryceson_2002: p.9). This could suggest that institutional arrangements may not always foster structural change or promote development in underdeveloped regions.

Lastly, vanZanden (1999) argues that the consensus view in the 1960s and 1970s was that the early modern economy was unable to generate economic growth (vanZanden_1999: p.1). This perspective questions the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, as it suggests that economic growth before the industrial revolution may not have been primarily driven by institutional arrangements.

These sources challenge the thesis by suggesting that factors other than institutions, such as ideas, ideologies, and traditional hierarchical orders, can play significant roles in shaping economic growth and development. Further investigation into these factors is necessary to gain a more comprehensive understanding of long-run economic growth.

Institutions and trade play significant roles in shaping long-run economic growth, as they influence the structural changes and factor endowments that are unique to individual countries (Sokoloff & Engerman_2000: p.4). Komlos (1998) argues that physical stature during the Industrial Revolution was significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic processes and associated structural changes (Komlos_1998: p.16, p.17). This suggests that institutional factors may have indirect impacts on long-run economic growth through their influence on population growth, urbanization, income inequality, and variability.

Moreover, the relationship between climate and human living standards has been a fascinating topic in long-run economic history (Koepke & Baten_2005: p.20). However, over the period of the Roman Empire, heights did not increase despite common views that living standards and purchasing power increased due to economic growth (Koepke & Baten_2005: p.16). This finding complicates the understanding of how climate affects long-run economic growth, suggesting that other factors such as institutions may be more influential.

Furthermore, Lindert and Williamson (1985) argue that the task of explaining long-run distribution trends rejoins the search for explanations of growth itself (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.9). They also suggest that Japan’s economic history supports the idea of imbalance and inequality, since neither appeared to be correlated in Japan’s long-run experience (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.14). This implies that institutions may play a role in shaping the relationship between growth and inequality.

In addition, Hopkins (2019) argues that postmodernism removed independent African viewpoints from the study of long-run economic and political change (Hopkins_2019: p.4). This suggests that institutions may have inhibited the study of long-run economic growth in Africa by limiting the diversity of research perspectives.

Finally, Frankema (2012) argues that colonial education is the key to explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa (Frankema_2012: p.2). However, the degree of British "liberalism" was endogenous to the local political and economic context (Frankema_2012: p.19). This implies that institutions may have influenced the effectiveness of colonial education in shaping long-run economic growth.

The role of institutions in fostering long-run economic growth is further nuanced when considering technological advancements, as highlighted by Broadberry & Gardner (2022) and Akyeampong & Fofack (2014).

Broadberry & Gardner (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1) argue that Sub-Saharan Africa's absence in recent debates about long-run growth is due to the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950. This suggests that a comprehensive understanding of institutions' impact on economic growth may be limited by insufficient historical data, particularly for regions such as Sub-Saharan Africa.

Moreover, Broadberry & Gardner (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1) note the observation made by Collier and Gunning (1999) that African economic performance has been markedly worse than other regions, and Sachs and Warner's (1997) statement that Africa has experienced slow growth. These observations imply that the institutions shaping long-run economic growth in Africa may have contributed to its comparatively poor economic performance.

However, Broadberry & Gardner (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.18) also point out the need for understanding the extent to which there are still risks of shrinking that have undermined economic progress in the past. This emphasizes the importance of considering not only the role of institutions in promoting growth but also their potential to hinder it, particularly in light of historical challenges faced by African economies.

Akyeampong & Fofack (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3) discuss the underrepresentation of women's economic contributions, which are often subsumed under household production and unaccounted for formally. This underscores the importance of institutions in recognizing and valuing the work of all contributors to economic growth, including women.

Furthermore, Akyeampong & Fofack (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.4) highlight that major historical developments in West Africa and themes concerning gender and economic growth find parallels elsewhere in Africa. This implies that understanding the role of institutions in shaping long-run economic growth should consider the specific contexts and experiences across different African regions.

The role of institutions in shaping long-run economic growth is further complicated by the influence of labor markets, as suggested by Temin (2002) and Allen (2001). According to Temin (Temin_2002: p.4), the exhaustion of cheap labor was a significant factor that caused economic growth to slow. This finding implies that institutions play a crucial role in determining the availability and cost of labor, which can significantly impact long-term economic growth.

Allen (Allen_2001: p.1) highlights that wages and prices have been central concerns for economic historians due to their implications for economic development, leadership, and standard of living. In this context, institutions can shape wage structures and price levels, thereby influencing the distribution of income and economic growth. For instance, Allen (Allen_2001: p.4) demonstrates that structural change correlates with growth, suggesting that institutional changes leading to structural transformations could potentially drive long-term economic growth.

Moreover, both authors shed light on the importance of considering specific contexts when analyzing the role of institutions in shaping long-run economic growth. Temin (Temin_2002: p.7) argues that recent theories of economic growth should approach the Golden Age in the context of economic growth over the past century or two, which had a large component of economic transition. This suggests that understanding the role of institutions requires considering the historical evolution of economies and their transitions from agrarian to industrial societies.

Similarly, Allen (Allen_2001: p.15) emphasizes the importance of understanding the factors that influence work productivity, such as illness, injury, bad weather, and economic fluctuations. This implies that institutions play a role in mitigating these factors and promoting a more stable and productive labor force, contributing to long-run economic growth.

The literature on the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth indicates a general consensus that institutions play a significant role in promoting productivity and mitigating factors such as illness, injury, bad weather, and economic fluctuations (Domar_1970: p.4, p.5, p.7, p.15, p.16).
