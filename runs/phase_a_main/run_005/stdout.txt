LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The question of whether institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth has been a subject of extensive debate among economists and historians. This discussion is significant as it sheds light on the factors that drive economic development over extended periods, with potential implications for policy formulation and development strategies.

Supporting this thesis, Austin (2008) argues that institutions play a crucial role in long-term growth by establishing relationships that recur due to path dependency and similar situations. Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) demonstrate that representative government institutions with multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs are intimately related to economic growth and political freedom. Furthermore, Acemoglu et al. (2001) highlight that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress, suggesting a direct link between institutions and long-term growth.

On the other hand, some scholars offer critiques or complications to this argument. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) point out that the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has led Sub-Saharan Africa to be absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) argue that understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution becomes subsumed under household production is essential for policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles, suggesting that institutions may not be the sole determinant of growth.

As the literature review progresses, it will become evident that while institutions are indeed a crucial factor in long-run economic growth, other factors such as technological innovation and social changes also play significant roles. The argument continues to explore the nuances and qualifications surrounding this central question, delving deeper into the various perspectives presented by the scholars mentioned above.

The role of institutions in fostering long-run economic growth is further substantiated by several scholars, including Kuznets (1973), North & Weingast (1989), Mokyr (2005), North (1989), Ogilvie (2007), and deVries (1994).

Kuznets (1973) emphasizes the association between rapid economic growth and structural changes, as well as the crucial role of social innovations in facilitating adjustments required for modern economic growth. Mokyr (2005) supports this perspective by highlighting the importance of intellectual developments in shaping the West's economic progress.

North & Weingast (1989) discuss the significance of representative government institutions, such as multiple veto points and common law courts, in promoting economic growth and political freedom. North (1989), in his work, stresses the importance of institutions in reducing transaction costs and enhancing security rights, which are essential for market development and long-term economic growth.

Ogilvie (2007) sheds light on the interdependency of individual institutions within a broader framework, suggesting that the failure of one institution can impact the overall economic system. deVries (1994) underscores the importance of urban networks and regional economic development in facilitating industrial growth.

These scholars' works collectively support the notion that institutions play a fundamental role in long-run economic growth by providing the necessary framework for technological innovation, social change, and market development. However, further exploration is required to understand the nuances and complexities of institutional dynamics in various historical and cultural contexts.

The significance of institutions as a primary driver for long-run economic growth is further substantiated by various scholarly works, such as those by Acemoglu and his colleagues in 2001 and 2002, and Abramovitz's work from 1986.

Acemoglu et al. (2001) emphasize the substantial impact of fundamental institutions on property rights and economic performance. They argue that detrimental institutions were harmful to investment and economic progress, as evidenced by their large effect on economic performance (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19). Furthermore, they assert that differences in mortality rates are not the main cause of variation in institutions (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3). This suggests that while biological factors may play a role in shaping societies, it is the institutional framework that ultimately determines their economic growth trajectory.

Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2002) highlight the significance of institutions in understanding economic and political development over the past 500 years. They argue that the reversal in the distribution of economic prosperity is related to institutions (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5). This demonstrates that institutional dynamics have played a crucial role in shaping the course of history, with positive changes in institutions leading to increased economic growth and prosperity.

Abramovitz (1986) also supports the thesis by pointing out that the elaboration of the mixed economy is a function of economic growth itself (Abramovitz_1986: p.20). This implies that as economies grow, they tend to develop more sophisticated institutional structures, which in turn facilitate further growth. Additionally, Abramovitz emphasizes the importance of macroeconomic and monetary conditions encouraging and sustaining capital investment and effective demand (Abramovitz_1986: p.7), further underscoring the role of institutions in fostering economic growth.

Structural change, a shift in the composition of an economy from primary activities like agriculture to secondary and tertiary sectors such as manufacturing and services, respectively, significantly correlates with economic growth (Allen_2001: p.4). This is further supported by Broadberry & Gupta (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.9) who argue that the service sector played a crucial role in economic growth during both early modern and modern periods.

Allen (Allen_2001: p.15) notes that illness, injury, bad weather, and economic fluctuations prevented work on every permissible day, suggesting that a stable institutional environment, which encourages productivity and economic activity, is essential for long-term growth. This view is echoed by Broadberry & Gupta (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.24) who caution against inferring equal levels of economic development in different regions based solely on grain wages, implying that the institutional framework must be taken into account.

Moreover, Allen (Allen_2001: p.23) points out that both positions—that of rapid industrialization leading to economic growth and a more gradual process—can find support in the indices reported, indicating that institutions play a critical role in shaping the trajectory of economic development. This is consistent with Broadberry & Gupta's (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.9) observation that the recent literature of new economic geography supports the skeptical view of proto-industrialization as a stage on the road to modern economic growth, further underscoring the importance of institutions in fostering economic growth.

Structural change, such as the shift from agrarian to industrial economies, has been suggested as a significant factor in economic growth (Simson_2019: p.25). However, some argue that this transformation may not be as crucial as previously thought. For instance, McCloskey (McCloskey_2015: p.9) contends that ideas and ideologies, rather than institutions or structural changes, played a more substantial role in driving economic growth in certain regions, such as France and Spain.

Moreover, Stern (Stern_2004: p.3) questions the direct relationship between economic growth and environmental degradation, suggesting that economic factors may drive changes in environmental impacts over the course of development, rather than being an inherent consequence of growth itself. This perspective challenges the notion that structural change is a primary driver of long-term economic growth.

Furthermore, Humphries (Humphries_2013: p.18) argues that the division of labor and the rise of workshop production were key factors in 18th-century economic growth. However, he also points out that the relative needs of women and children in these workshops may have hindered overall productivity, casting doubt on the assumption that structural change always leads to increased output.

Lastly, van Zanden (vanZanden_1999: p.1) critiques the consensus view from the 1960s and 1970s that the early modern economy was unable to generate economic growth. However, he also acknowledges that in the mid-18th century, there was still a negative link between output measures of the standard of living and the level of economic development (van Zanden_1999: p.13). This suggests that while structural change may have contributed to economic growth in some instances, it was not necessarily an essential factor across all regions and time periods.

The relationship between institutions and labor provides nuanced perspectives on the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Komlos (1998) explores the concept of modern economic growth, suggesting that it was not only structural changes but also short-term fluctuations in economic processes that influenced physical stature during the Industrial Revolution. This implies that institutions, such as labor regulations and property rights, may have played a role in shaping these fluctuations.

Temin (2002) argues that the exhaustion of cheap labor caused economic growth to slow, suggesting that institutional factors related to labor markets might have impacted economic development. Furthermore, he posits that recent theories of economic growth should consider the historical context of economic transition, implying a complex interplay between institutions and labor in driving long-term economic growth.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) discuss the relationship between economic growth and equality, suggesting that policymakers have often sought to balance these two factors. They also argue that the task of explaining long-run distribution trends rejoins the search for explanations of growth itself, implying a potential causal link between institutions, inequality, and economic growth.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) highlight the variation in conditions relevant to growth across societies, including institutions, and suggest that initial conditions or factor endowments could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth. They also argue that sustained economic growth was achieved by the United States and Canada during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, while others did not manage to attain this goal until late in the nineteenth century, implying a significant role for institutions in shaping economic trajectories.

The role of institutions in shaping economic growth is further nuanced by the impact of education systems, as evidenced by Frankema (2012) and Goldin (2006). According to Frankema (Frankema_2012: p.2), colonial education played a significant role in explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa, with former British colonies growing faster due to their educational advantage. However, the level of British "liberalism" was not solely determined by the colonial power but also influenced by local political and economic contexts (Frankema_2012: p.19).

Goldin (2006) also highlights the importance of institutions in shaping economic growth, focusing on labor markets. The study of labor was once centered around the institutions of the labor market (Goldin_2006: p.3). Goldin (2006) argues that understanding the evolution and revolution of these institutions is crucial for analyzing women's choices and decisions in the labor force.

The relationship between institutions and industrialization, as a fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, is further nuanced by the works of Bolt & van Zanden (2014) and Clark & Jacks (2007).

Bolt & van Zanden (2014) emphasize that revisions of nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth have been made for several countries, including Switzerland, Germany, Sweden, Italy, Greece, Russia, and Bulgaria. These revisions do not fundamentally change the view that institutions play a significant role in economic growth but instead broaden our understanding of its impact (Bolt & van Zanden_2014: p.5). The authors also highlight the importance of technological advancements in the coal industry during the Industrial Revolution, which enabled it to respond effectively to increases in demand (Clark & Jacks_2007: p.4).

Clark & Jacks (2007) further complicate this argument by demonstrating that the cliometric account of the coal industry during the Industrial Revolution was crucial for economic growth. They argue that prices were deflated by a price index for the economy as a whole, suggesting that the coal industry's performance significantly influenced overall economic growth (Clark & Jacks_2007: p.3).

These findings suggest that while institutions are indeed a fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, their impact may be more complex than previously thought. The evolution and revolution of institutions in various industries, such as the coal industry during the Industrial Revolution, appear to play a critical role in shaping economic growth trajectories. Further examination of these institutional changes and their effects on different sectors could provide valuable insights into the relationship between institutions and long-term economic development.

The works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), while tangential, offer valuable insights into the role of institutions in shaping economic growth trajectories. Both scholars agree that institutional changes play a crucial role in fostering long-term economic development.

Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) emphasizes the importance of labor mobility and competition among employers, suggesting that when labor is tied to land or an owner, as in the case of serfdom or slavery, competition ceases, hindering economic growth. On the other hand, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) discusses how Western economic institutions, such as slave dealing and curing, impacted African societies, altering their traditional structures and potentially influencing their economic development paths.

However, both scholars diverge in their focus. Domar's primary concern is the role of labor mobility and competition in economic growth, while Bohannan explores the broader impacts of Western institutions on African societies.

Despite these insights, several issues remain unresolved. For instance, a comprehensive understanding of how specific institutional changes affect different sectors and contribute to long-term economic development requires further examination. Additionally, more research is needed to compare and contrast the effects of various types of institutional changes across diverse regions and time periods.

Phillips' work cited by Domar (Domar_1970: p.16) on the economic cost of slaveholding in the Cotton Belt could provide valuable insights into the economic implications of specific institutional arrangements, such as slavery. Similarly, Bohannan's discussion on the limitations of women's numbers (Bohannan_1959: p.13) and the role of social networks (Bohannan_1959: p.10) could offer insights into how social institutions influence economic growth.

In essence, while Domar and Bohannan provide valuable perspectives on the role of institutions in long-run economic growth, further research is needed to fully understand the complex interplay between institutional changes, sectors, and regional differences.
