The debate on the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, particularly in the context of sub-Saharan Africa (SSA), is a contentious issue among economists and historians. The central question at hand is whether institutions are indeed the primary driver of economic growth over the long term, and if so, what implications this has for understanding the economic disparities between regions such as SSA and Western Europe.

Several scholars support the institutionalist perspective, arguing that the quality and structure of an economy's institutions have a significant impact on its growth trajectory (Austin_2008, AcemogluEtAl_2001). For instance, Austin (2008) asserts that institutions are "the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth" due to their role in shaping economic structures and fostering innovation. Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2001) demonstrate that more effective institutions lead to increased investment and economic progress.

On the other hand, some scholars offer critiques and complications to this argument. Broadberry & Gardner (2022), for example, argue that a lack of data on SSA's economic performance before 1950 has led to its exclusion from recent debates about long-run growth, potentially obscuring important insights into the region's economic development. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also challenge the institutionalist perspective by highlighting the underrepresentation of women's economic contributions and the need for a more nuanced understanding of gender roles in economic growth.

Komlos (1998) further complicates the debate by emphasizing the multifaceted nature of modern economic growth, suggesting that while institutions play an important role, other factors such as population growth, urbanization, and income inequality must also be considered.

The stakes in this debate are high, as understanding the fundamental causes of long-run economic growth is essential for developing effective policies aimed at promoting economic development and reducing disparities between regions. As the literature review suggests, while many scholars support the institutionalist perspective, others offer critiques and complications that require further exploration. Future research should aim to reconcile these differing viewpoints and develop a more comprehensive understanding of the role institutions play in long-run economic growth.

The significance of institutions in promoting long-run economic growth is further underscored by various scholars, as they delve into the intricate relationship between institutions and economic inequality. Austin (2008) emphasizes that institutions are indeed 'the' fundamental cause of long-term economic growth, as they shape economic structures and historical trajectories (Austin_2008: p.24). Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) argue that the institutions of representative government, such as multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are crucial in fostering economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

Kuznets (1973) contributes to this discourse by linking institutions with technological innovations during modern economic growth, suggesting that both play significant roles in shaping economic and social structures (Kuznets_1973: p.6). Mokyr (2005) also supports the argument, positing that the West's economic prosperity can be attributed to intellectual developments concerning "useful knowledge" (Mokyr_2005: p.4).

These scholars collectively highlight that institutions are not only essential for economic growth but also play a role in shaping economic inequality within and between nations. This underscores the importance of understanding the complex interplay between institutions, technological innovations, and socio-economic structures in promoting long-term economic growth. Future research should further investigate these relationships to develop a more comprehensive understanding of institutional development and its impact on economic growth and inequality.

The evidence provided in various studies supports the assertion that institutions are a fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. For instance, Whatley (2018) presents econometric results demonstrating the relationship between gunpowder imports and slave exports in the long run (Whatley_2018: p.3, p.11, p.13). This suggests that institutional structures, such as trade regulations and labor practices, can significantly impact economic growth over extended periods.

Similarly, van Zanden (2009) argues that the skill premium of carpenters and masons affects the relative price of capital goods, contributing to long-term economic growth (vanZanden_2009: p.27). This study emphasizes the role of human capital formation in fostering economic development.

Allen (2001) also supports this notion by highlighting the importance of wages and prices in understanding economic development, economic leadership, and the standard of living (Allen_2001: p.1). The author's analysis indicates that there is a correlation between structural change and growth.

The significance of institutions in shaping long-term economic growth is further emphasized by Acemoglu and his colleagues (AcemogluEtAl_2001, AcemogluEtAl_2002). They argue that the impact of fundamental institutions on property rights and investment plays a crucial role in economic progress.

In their 2001 study, Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27) assert that certain institutions were detrimental to investment, thus hindering economic development. They also find a large effect of institutions on economic performance (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19). This suggests that the correlation between structural change and growth, as highlighted by Allen (2001), can be significantly influenced by institutional factors.

In their subsequent work in 2002, Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49) state that understanding economic and political development over the past 500 years requires acknowledging the fundamental role of institutions. They also note a change in the distribution of economic prosperity, which they attribute to institutional factors (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.6).

These findings support the assertion that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. The works of Acemoglu et al. provide compelling evidence for the importance of institutions in shaping economic structures and driving economic development. Further exploration into the specific mechanisms through which institutions influence growth could deepen our understanding of this relationship.

The argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth faces challenges in light of evidence suggesting that certain institutional arrangements may lead to economic inequality and hinder growth. For instance, Simson (2019) argues that while state expenditure and deficits expanded during a period of high economic growth in Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda in the 1960s and 1970s, spending inefficiencies, leakages, and costly delays to reform may have hindered these economies from sustaining investment levels (Simson_2019: p.4).

Furthermore, McCloskey (2015) asserts that ideologies and ideas, rather than institutions, played a significant role in shaping economic growth in France for two centuries after the Unfinished Revolution of 1789. In Spain, economic growth was throttled due to conservative reasons despite examples of bourgeois economic successes in other countries (McCloskey_2015: p.9).

These sources challenge the notion that institutions are the sole determinant of long-run economic growth by suggesting that certain institutional arrangements may lead to economic inequality and hinder growth. The implications of these findings could potentially shift the focus towards understanding how ideologies, ideas, and other factors interact with institutions to shape economic structures and drive development.

The relationship between institutions and economic inequality, as key factors in long-run economic growth, is a complex one that involves various nuances. Komlos (1998) posits that the onset of modern economic growth during the Industrial Revolution was influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic processes, suggesting that institutional changes may have been cyclical rather than linear (Komlos_1998: p.16). Similarly, Temin (2002) argues that the slowdown in economic growth was due to the exhaustion of cheap labor, implying that institutions play a role in shaping the availability and distribution of resources (Temin_2002: p.4).

Lindert & Williamson (1985) suggest that long-run distribution trends are intertwined with the search for explanations of growth itself, further complicating the relationship between institutions and economic inequality (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.9). Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) argue that initial conditions or factor endowments could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth, implying that the role of institutions may be contextually dependent (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4).

Moreover, Koepke & Baten (2005) explore the relationship between climate and human living standards, suggesting that environmental factors could potentially interact with institutional structures to influence economic growth (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.20). Frankema (2012) argues that colonial education played a significant role in explaining long-run growth disparities, implying that the impact of institutions extends beyond national borders and into the realm of colonization (Frankema_2012: p.2).

These findings suggest that the relationship between institutions and economic inequality is multifaceted and influenced by a variety of factors, including short-term fluctuations, resource availability, initial conditions, environmental factors, and colonialism. This complexity underscores the need for further research to fully understand how ideologies, ideas, and other factors interact with institutions to shape economic structures and drive development.

The literature on the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth further highlights the complexities surrounding this topic, with several sources adding nuance to the thesis. For instance, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) point out that Sub-Saharan Africa has been largely absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth due to a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950. This absence may have skewed our understanding of the role institutions play in shaping economic structures and driving development, as this region is not included in many historical analyses (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.1).

Similarly, van Zanden (1999) challenges the consensus view that the early modern economy was unable to generate economic growth. Instead, he argues that structural change correlates with growth, as seen in mid-eighteenth century Western Europe where a negative link between 'output' measures of standard of living and the level of economic development could still be observed (vanZanden_1999: p.13). This suggests that institutions may have played a role in driving economic growth even before the industrial revolution, adding complexity to the argument that they are the sole cause of long-run economic growth.

These findings underscore the need for further research, particularly in regions like Sub-Saharan Africa and during earlier periods of history, to fully understand how ideologies, ideas, and other factors interact with institutions to shape economic structures and drive development. The complexity of these interactions may help explain why some regions experience sustained economic growth while others do not, providing a more comprehensive understanding of the role institutions play in long-run economic growth. The role of institutions in long-run economic growth is further nuanced when considering their impact on gender roles and economic structures, as highlighted by Akyeampong & Fofack (2014). Their research underscores the significance of understanding how women's economic contributions are often subsumed under household production, making them formally unaccounted for in policy-making aimed at strengthening women's economic roles.

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) demonstrate that the contribution of African women to economic growth and development was largely dependent on the economic provision of their husbands. This dependence on male economic provision may have hindered the potential for sustainable long-term growth, as the economic power remained concentrated in male hands.

Moreover, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) argue that these observations are not unique to West Africa, but rather find parallels elsewhere in Africa where gender and economic growth are concerned. This suggests that understanding the role institutions play in shaping gender roles and economic structures may be crucial for explaining variations in long-term economic growth across different regions of the continent.

By considering these factors, a more comprehensive understanding of the role institutions play in long-run economic growth can be achieved, one that acknowledges the importance of gender roles and economic structures in shaping development outcomes. (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3, p.9, p.19, p.27)

The works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) offer tangential insights into the relationship between institutions, economic structures, and long-run economic growth, albeit with differing focuses. Both scholars acknowledge the influence of labor structures on competition among employers and economic development.

Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) posits that when labor is tied to land or an owner, competition among employers ceases, potentially hindering economic growth. This aligns with Bohannan's (Bohannan_1959: p.7) observation of the centrality of slave dealing in African societies, suggesting that such labor structures may stifle economic development.

However, Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) also discusses instances where labor ties have facilitated growth, as seen in the Russian peasants' enserfment before their emancipation. This contrasts with Bohannan's (Bohannan_1959: p.13) assertion that the limited number of women in some African societies may hinder economic development due to gender roles and population dynamics.

Both scholars imply that external influences, such as Western impacts (Bohannan_1959: p.10) and the economic cost of slaveholding (Domar_1970: p.16), play a role in shaping institutions and economic structures. However, they do not delve deeply into these aspects, leaving unresolved questions about the impact of external factors on long-run economic growth.
