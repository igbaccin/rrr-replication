The central question in the literature review on institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth revolves around understanding the role of institutional structures in shaping economic trajectories across different regions, particularly focusing on Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA). This question holds significant stakes, as it could potentially inform policy decisions aimed at fostering sustainable economic development and reducing poverty.

The majority of sources support the thesis that institutions play a pivotal role in long-term economic growth. Austin (2008) argues that institutions are 'the' fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, while North & Weingast (1989) show how institutions underpin economic growth and political freedom. Acemoglu et al. (2001) also demonstrate a large effect of institutions on economic performance.

However, some sources offer critiques and complications to this thesis. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that the lack of data on SSA before 1950 has prevented it from being part of recent debates about comparative long-run growth. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) highlight how understanding the processes by which women's economic contributions are subsumed under household production is crucial for policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles.

Moreover, 19 sources add nuance or qualifications to the thesis. For instance, Kuznets (1973) discusses how modern economic growth is associated with rapid changes in economic structure and other aspects of society, but also mentions unexpected negative results even within developed countries.

This review sets the stage for a nuanced understanding of the role of institutions in long-run economic growth, particularly in the context of Sub-Saharan Africa. The debate continues on whether institutions are indeed 'the' fundamental cause of long-term economic growth, or if other factors such as geography, culture, or technology play equally significant roles.

The interplay between institutions and labor market dynamics significantly contributes to long-term economic growth, as evidenced by various scholars' works. Kuznets (1973) posits that the succession of technological innovations during modern economic growth necessitates social innovations for adjustment, which are crucial for economic structure changes and growth (Kuznets_1973: p.6).

North (1989) suggests that institutions play a critical role in reducing transaction costs, enhancing security rights, and facilitating market development, all of which contribute to economic growth over the long term (North_1989: p.9). Ogilvie (2007) argues that economic institutions are interdependent and crucial for sustaining growth within a broader framework (Ogilvie_2007: p.27), while deVries (1994) highlights the importance of Europe's urban network in establishing a framework for regional economic development during the early modern period, enabling industrial growth (deVries_1994: p.5).

The centrality of institutions in driving long-run economic growth is further substantiated by the works of North & Weingast (1989) and Acemoglu et al. (2001, 2002). North & Weingast (1989) emphasize that representative government institutions, characterized by multiple veto points and common law courts' primacy over economic affairs, play a crucial role in fostering economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

Acemoglu et al. (2001) support this notion by demonstrating that more fundamental institutions significantly impact property rights and investment, leading to economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19). Their subsequent work in 2002 reveals that the distribution of economic prosperity has changed over time, which they attribute to institutional shifts (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.6).

In addition, Abramovitz (1986) highlights the role of mixed economies in promoting productivity growth and capital investment (Abramovitz_1986: p.20), while Hopkins (2009) argues against the notion that ethnic fragmentation is solely responsible for unproductive institutions, instead suggesting that community support can be a positive factor in economic development (Hopkins_2009: p.21).

Broadberry et al. (2015) contribute to this discourse by examining India's long-run economic performance and providing population estimates for the country, which serve as a foundation for further research on its growth (BroadberryEtal_2015: p.4). These sources collectively underscore the significance of institutions in shaping long-term economic growth.

Structural change, characterized by shifts from agrarian to industrial economies, is a key indicator of long-term economic growth (Allen_2001: p.4). This transformation, as Allen (2001) suggests, is central to economic development and standard of living improvements. The shift from agriculture to industry signifies a move towards more efficient production methods, increased specialization, and higher productivity levels.

Moreover, the service sector played a crucial role in economic growth during both early modern times and the industrial period (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.9). This sector's expansion facilitated the exchange of goods and services, fostering economic integration and promoting long-term growth. The service sector's development also contributed to increased living standards, as the falling relative price of manufactured goods improved the wellbeing of north-west Europeans (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.9).

In addition, the assumption that illness, injury, and economic fluctuations prevented work on every permissible day is crucial in understanding wage patterns and long-term growth (Allen_2001: p.15). This perspective highlights how institutions, such as health systems and economic policies, can significantly impact the pace of economic development by affecting labor productivity.

The evidence presented supports the notion that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. The transformation from agrarian to industrial economies, the expansion of the service sector, and the role of institutions in shaping wage patterns all underscore this argument. Further analysis is required to delve deeper into the intricate relationships between institutions, structural change, and economic growth across different regions and time periods.

The critique of the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is presented in both Simson (2019) and McCloskey (2015). These sources challenge this perspective by highlighting instances where economic growth occurred independently of strong institutions.

In Simson's (2019) analysis, the author points to the economic growth experienced in East Africa during the 1960s and 1970s, a period characterized by expanding public employment and state expenditure (Simson_2019: p.25). Despite questionable spending efficiencies and costly delays to reform, the author suggests that these factors alone may not have been sufficient to sustain the investment levels of this era or reorient the economies (Simson_2019: p.4).

McCloskey (2015) also provides evidence that contradicts the thesis by discussing instances in France and Spain where economic growth was hindered, not promoted, by strong institutions and ideologies. For example, the author mentions that reactionary parties in France during two centuries after the Unfinished Revolution of 1789 were uninterested in economic growth if they could impose a rigid form of Catholicism (McCloskey_2015: p.9).

These findings suggest that while institutions may play a role in shaping economic growth, other factors such as ideologies and external influences might also significantly impact the long-term prosperity of regions. Further examination is required to understand the complex interplay between these various factors and their influence on economic development over time.

The examination of institutions as a primary driver of long-term economic growth is further nuanced by considering their impact on labor markets. Evidence suggests that the exhaustion of cheap labor (Temin_2002: p.4) and the achievement of sustained economic growth by certain countries during specific periods (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.12) can be attributed, in part, to institutional factors influencing labor market dynamics.

Moreover, the role of education, particularly colonial education, has been identified as a key factor in explaining long-run growth disparities, with former British colonies growing faster due to their educational lead (Frankema_2012: p.2). However, it is important to note that the degree of "liberalism" was endogenous to the local political and economic context (Frankema_2012: p.19), suggesting a complex interplay between institutions and external influences.

The study of labor markets also reveals that structural changes often correlate with growth. For instance, as the economic base in Africa appeared to crumble, the superstructure assumed a new importance (Hopkins_2019: p.9). This shift underscores the significance of institutions in shaping economic structures and guiding long-term development trajectories.

The works of Komlos (1998), Koepke & Baten (2005), Bolt & vanZanden (2014), and Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) provide nuanced insights into the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth.

Komlos (1998) delves into the concept of modern economic growth, emphasizing its connection with structural changes such as population growth, urbanization, and income inequality (p.17). However, he also highlights that biological growth processes may be influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic conditions, complicating the causal relationship between institutions and growth.

Koepke & Baten (2005) explore the connection between climate and human living standards, suggesting that while some periods of economic growth might not lead to height increases, as observed during the Roman Empire, other factors such as nutrition could play a significant role in shaping long-term development trajectories.

Bolt & vanZanden (2014) present revisions of nineteenth- and early twentieth-century growth rates for various countries, yet they acknowledge that these adjustments do not fundamentally alter their understanding of the causes of long-run economic growth (p.5). This implies that while institutional development may contribute to growth, other factors such as political history and environmental conditions could also play a role in shaping economic trajectories.

Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) discuss the disagreement between economists and historians regarding the causes of long-run economic growth. They suggest that institutions are essential components of market dynamics, with each player's strategy affecting the others (p.21). This underscores the complex nature of institutional development and its role in shaping economic growth over time.

The sources by Austin (2007) and Allen et al. (2011) provide nuanced perspectives on the relationship between institutions, industrialization, and economic growth.

Austin (2007) highlights that the railway had a significant advantage over lorries in terms of cost until the mid-1920s (p.8). However, he also points out that not all capital-deepening and TFP-improving forces of modern economic growth were present in Ghana (p.16), suggesting that institutional factors might have played a role in hindering its industrialization and subsequent economic growth. Furthermore, Austin notes the faster population growth after 1918, which could have implications for resource allocation and institutional development (p.12).

Allen et al. (2011) demonstrate that income disparities in the early twentieth century were due to long-run stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe (p.1). They also show that rapid growth in the German economy led to rising real wages for workers in Leipzig (p.20), indicating a potential link between industrialization, institutional development, and wage growth.

These findings complicate the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by suggesting that other factors, such as technological advancements and population dynamics, also play crucial roles. The sources underscore the importance of understanding the intricate interplay between these factors in shaping economic growth over time. Further investigation should focus on how specific institutional arrangements influence the adoption and diffusion of technology, population growth, and wage structures during different stages of industrialization.

The literature under review offers insights into the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth, with a particular focus on their influence on structural change, population dynamics, and technological adoption.

Scholars such as Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) provide tangential evidence supporting the notion that institutional arrangements can significantly shape economic growth. For instance, Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) argues that when labor is tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, potentially hindering economic development. Similarly, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) notes that the Western impact on certain societies was not limited to economic institutions but also influenced social structures and trade practices.

On the other hand, Bryceson (2002) offers a more nuanced perspective, focusing on livelihood diversification and its relationship with institutional changes in various communities. For example, Bryceson (Bryceson_2002: p.9) highlights how an adherence to traditional hierarchical order within communities can influence women's economic and social spheres.

While these scholars converge in recognizing the crucial role of institutions in shaping economic growth, they diverge in their focus and the specific aspects of institutional arrangements they investigate. For instance, Domar (1970) primarily explores labor-land relationships, while Bohannan (1959) delves into the impact of Western societies on African communities. Bryceson (2002), on the other hand, focuses on livelihood diversification and its relationship with institutional changes in various communities.

Despite these insights, several questions remain unresolved. For example, further investigation is needed to understand how specific institutional arrangements influence the adoption and diffusion of technology during different stages of industrialization. Additionally, a deeper understanding of the mechanisms through which institutions affect population growth and wage structures would contribute significantly to our understanding of long-run economic growth.
