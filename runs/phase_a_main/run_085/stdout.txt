LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question under investigation in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, a thesis that has garnered significant attention among economists and historians. The stakes are high, as understanding the role of institutions could provide valuable insights into promoting sustainable economic development across different regions.

Support for the thesis is found in various works, such as Austin's (2008) analysis that endorses the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history, revealing relationships that recur due to path dependence. Similarly, North and Weingast (1989) demonstrate how institutions played a necessary role in making possible economic growth and political freedom. Acemoglu et al. (2001) also support the thesis by showing a large effect of institutions on economic performance.

On the other hand, some authors offer critiques or complicate the argument. For instance, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that sub-Saharan Africa's absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth is due to the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) complicate the discussion by highlighting how understanding the processes by which women’s economic contribution becomes subsumed under household production and their labor formally unaccounted for undermines policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles.

As the debate unfolds, it becomes clear that institutions play a crucial role in shaping long-term economic growth. However, the complexity of this relationship necessitates further investigation into various factors, such as the impact of structural changes (Kuznets_1973) and the role of women's economic contributions (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014). The exploration of these aspects could shed light on how to design effective policies that promote sustainable economic growth and development.

The economic growth experienced during the Industrial Revolution was a significant turning point in history, with Britain serving as a prime example (deVries_1994: p.2). This period of rapid industrialization and urban development was made possible by the establishment of an extensive network of cities that facilitated commercial interaction and regional economic development (deVries_1994: p.5).

Moreover, the skill premium, or the difference in wages between skilled and unskilled laborers, has been identified as a crucial factor influencing long-term economic growth (vanZanden_2009: p.24). In industries such as construction, a low skill premium can contribute to economic expansion, while a high skill premium in periods of recession may hinder growth (vanZanden_2009: p.4).

These findings suggest that focusing on the economic history of households or family units could provide valuable insights into economic development (deVries_1994: p.8). By understanding the roles and contributions of individuals within these units, policymakers may be better equipped to design effective strategies promoting sustainable growth.

The role of institutions in fostering long-term economic growth is further emphasized by Austin (2008). He argues that institutions, rather than trade itself, are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. This perspective is supported by his examination of the trade between Africa and Western Europe.

Austin (Austin_2008: p.24) contends that these trades reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to western Europe. This suggests that the institutional structures in place during these trading periods significantly influenced the long-term economic outcomes.

Moreover, Austin (Austin_2008: p.1) emphasizes the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history, revealing relationships that recur due to similar situations and path dependencies. This underscores the significance of institutions in shaping economic trajectories over extended periods.

Structural change, a shift in an economy's composition from one sector to another, is closely linked with economic growth (Kuznets_1973: p.4). This transformation, according to Kuznets, is not only a characteristic of high-aggregate growth rates but also a reflection of broader societal changes. The author emphasizes the role of technological innovations in driving this structural change and economic growth.

Moreover, Kuznets underscores the importance of social innovations that facilitate the necessary adjustments to accommodate these technological advancements (Kuznets_1973: p.6). He suggests that these social innovations are major factors influencing both economic and social structure. This perspective aligns with the notion that institutions, as a set of rules and practices that guide human interaction, play a crucial role in shaping economic trajectories by facilitating technological innovation and societal adaptation.

Furthermore, Kuznets acknowledges the potential negative consequences of modern economic growth within developed countries (Kuznets_1973: p.11). This observation underscores the complex relationship between technological innovations, economic growth, and institutional structures. It suggests that while institutions may facilitate economic growth, they must also be capable of managing and mitigating the negative impacts of such growth.

The literature provided by Simson (2019) challenges the notion that institutions are the primary drivers of long-term economic growth, particularly in the context of industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy. Simson's evidence suggests a focus on expanding public employment rather than protecting existing government jobs, which could imply that institutional structures may not be as crucial for economic growth as initially proposed (Simson_2019: p.27).

Moreover, Simson argues that while there were undoubtedly inefficiencies, leakages, and delays in reform, their elimination might not have been sufficient to sustain the investment levels of the 1960s, let alone reorientate these economies (Simson_2019: p.4). This implies that institutional structures may not be solely responsible for economic growth, as they might not necessarily address underlying inefficiencies.

Furthermore, Simson's work highlights the expansion of state expenditure and deficits in Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda during the 1960s and 1970s, a period of high economic growth (Simson_2019: p.25). This suggests that institutional structures may not be the primary determinant of economic growth, as these economies experienced growth despite potentially inefficient institutional arrangements.

However, it is important to note that Simson's work does not dismiss the role of institutions entirely. Instead, it questions their primacy in long-term economic growth and suggests a more complex interplay between institutional structures, industrial development, educational expansion, military autonomy, and public employment. This calls for further exploration of the intricate relationships between these factors and economic growth.

The discourse on institutions as the primary driver of long-run economic growth gains nuance when considering historical data from Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA). Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that the lack of pre-1950 data has excluded SSA from recent debates about long-term growth. Their research reveals that African economic performance, historically, has been markedly worse than other regions (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.1). This suggests that institutional structures in SSA may have hindered growth more than in other regions, complicating the argument for institutions as the sole cause of long-term economic growth.

Moreover, Temin (2002) posits that the exhaustion of cheap labor, rather than institutions, caused economic growth to slow. He argues that the end of the Golden Age was brought about by countries returning to equilibrium growth paths, not adverse shocks in the 1970s (Temin_2002: p.14). This perspective challenges the notion that institutions are the primary factor influencing long-term economic growth.

Furthermore, Williamson (1996) discusses the relationship between schooling and Swedish economic growth before World War I. His research indicates that understanding the sources of convergence is fundamental to understanding who benefits and loses from convergence (Williamson_1996: p.25). This implies that educational expansion, another factor intertwined with institutional structures, may play a significant role in long-term economic growth, complicating the argument for institutions as the sole cause.

The role of African women in economic growth and development is a significant yet often overlooked aspect, as highlighted by Akyeampong & Fofack (2014). Their contribution, historically subsumed under household production, has been largely unaccounted for in formal economic policies (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). This underscores the complexity of attributing long-term economic growth solely to institutions, as the economic roles and contributions of African women are integral components that should be considered.

Moreover, Akyeampong & Fofock (2014) note that the economic provision of husbands largely determined the contribution of African women to economic growth and development (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.9). This suggests that institutional structures, in this case, the patriarchal norms that dominated African societies, played a role in shaping the economic growth trajectory.

The authors also point out that the major historical developments and themes discussed in West Africa find parallels elsewhere in Africa where gender and economic growth are concerned (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.4). This implies that the argument for institutions as the sole cause of long-run economic growth may need to be expanded to include a broader, regional perspective on gender roles and their impact on economic development.

Finally, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) emphasize that understanding the processes by which women’s economic contribution becomes subsumed under household production is crucial for strengthening women’s economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). This underscores the importance of recognizing and valuing women's contributions to economic growth, a factor that could potentially reshape institutional structures and contribute to sustained economic development.

The argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth gains nuance when considering the historical context provided by Komlos (1998). While acknowledging the importance of institutions, Komlos emphasizes the role of structural change in driving economic growth during the Industrial Revolution.

Komlos argues that structural changes, such as population growth and urbanization, played a significant role in the onset of modern economic growth (Komlos_1998: p.2). He suggests that these changes were not only influenced by institutional structures but also contributed to their evolution. Furthermore, Komlos highlights the correlation between cyclical fluctuations in biological growth processes, such as height, and short-term economic fluctuations, implying a complex interplay between biology and economics during this period (Komlos_1998: p.16).

In addition, Komlos underscores the increase in income inequality and variability during the Industrial Revolution, factors that could potentially reshape institutional structures and influence economic growth patterns (Komlos_1998: p.17). This suggests that while institutions may be fundamental to long-run economic growth, they are not static entities but evolve in response to structural changes and economic fluctuations.

Therefore, a more comprehensive understanding of the relationship between institutions and economic growth would benefit from considering the dynamic nature of both institutions and economic structures. This could lead to a more nuanced view of how institutions shape and are shaped by long-run economic growth.

The works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) offer tangential insights into the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth, shedding light on both convergence and divergence in scholarly perspectives.

Domar's (Domar_1970: p.4) focus on labor tied to land or owners highlights the impact of institutional arrangements on competition among employers, suggesting that rigid labor markets can hinder economic growth. Conversely, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) emphasizes the role of slave dealing and status acquisition in shaping economic institutions, underscoring the influence of external factors on institutional development.

Both scholars acknowledge the dynamic nature of institutions, with Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) mentioning the Russian peasants' enserfment as an example of institution change, and Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.10) noting that the Western impact extended beyond economic institutions. However, they diverge in their emphasis on labor market structures versus external factors influencing institutional change.

Despite these differences, a common thread emerges: both scholars imply that institutions play a crucial role in shaping long-run economic growth. What remains unresolved is the precise mechanism through which this relationship operates and how to account for the dynamic interplay between institutions and economic structures over time. Further research could explore the nuances of this complex relationship, potentially leading to a more comprehensive understanding of the institutional foundations of long-term economic growth.
