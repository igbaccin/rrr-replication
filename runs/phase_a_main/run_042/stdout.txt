LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The debate surrounding the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth has been a subject of intense discussion among scholars, with institutions emerging as a key factor. This review examines various perspectives on this question, drawing from sources such as Austin_2008, Kuznets_1973, Broadberry&Gardner_2022, Akyeampong&Fofack_2014, North&Weingast_1989, and AcemogluEtAl_2001.

The central question at hand is whether institutions are indeed the primary driver of long-term economic growth, and if so, what implications does this have for our understanding of economic development? The stakes are high, as identifying the root cause of economic growth could lead to more effective policies aimed at promoting development in underdeveloped regions.

Austin_2008 argues that institutions play a crucial role in long-run economic growth, asserting that they "are the fundamental cause of long-rungrowth" (p.26). Kuznets_1973 supports this view, suggesting that both technological innovations and social innovations associated with modern economic growth are major factors affecting economic and social structure (p.6). North&Weingast_1989 also supports the thesis, demonstrating how institutions played a necessary role in making possible economic growth and political freedom (p.30).

However, not all scholars agree that institutions are the sole or even primary driver of long-run economic growth. Broadberry&Gardner_2022 complicates the debate by pointing out that a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has led to Sub-Saharan Africa's absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth (p.1). Akyeampong&Fofack_2014 further complicates the issue by highlighting how understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution becomes subsumed under the category of household production undermines policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles (p.3).

The role of institutions in fostering long-run economic growth is further supported by evidence from various sources. Kuznets (1973) highlights that rapid changes in economic structure, often associated with modern economic growth, are accompanied by significant shifts in societal aspects such as family and urbanization patterns (p.4). This suggests that institutional structures play a crucial role in shaping the broader socio-economic landscape during periods of growth.

Moreover, Kuznets (1973) underscores the interplay between technological innovations and social innovations as key factors influencing economic and social structure (p.6). This correlation supports the argument that effective institutions are essential for ensuring the successful implementation and adaptation of new technologies, which in turn drives growth.

North & Weingast (1989) assert that representative government, characterized by multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, is intimately linked to economic growth and political freedom (p.28). The authors argue that these institutions enable efficient conflict resolution and the enforcement of economic rules, thereby fostering long-term growth.

Mokyr (2005) posits that the intellectual developments during the Enlightenment significantly contributed to the West's economic growth by promoting individualism, man-made formal law, corporatism, self-governance, and rule-based decision-making (p.56). These elements can be considered institutional factors that facilitated the West's economic progress.

The assertion that institutions are the primary cause of long-term economic growth is substantiated by various scholarly works. Acemoglu et al. (2001) and (2002) emphasize the significant impact of institutional factors on economic performance. They argue that differences in mortality rates alone do not account for variations in institutions, and these institutions can either foster or hinder investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3, p.27). Furthermore, Acemoglu et al. (2002) suggest that the reversal in the distribution of economic prosperity is associated with institutional changes (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5).

Similarly, Abramovitz (1986) supports this perspective by arguing that the elaboration of a mixed economy is a function of economic growth itself and that macroeconomic conditions encouraging capital investment and demand levels are crucial determinants of economic growth (Abramovitz_1986: p.20, p.7). Van Zanden (2009) also acknowledges the role of institutions in economic growth, stating that the skill premium, which can be influenced by institutional factors, affects the relative price of capital goods and thus contributes to economic growth (vanZanden_2009: p.27).

Allen (2001) and Broadberry & Gupta (2006) further reinforce this idea by focusing on the role of institutions in shaping wages, prices, and economic development. Allen (2001) highlights that wages and prices have long been central concerns for economic historians due to their impact on economic development, economic leadership, and standard of living (Allen_2001: p.1). Broadberry & Gupta (2006) argue that the service sector played a crucial role in economic growth during the early modern period, as it does in the modern period, suggesting that the falling relative price of manufactured goods contributed to economic growth (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.9).

The impact of institutions on economic growth, particularly in the agricultural sector, is further supported by the evidence provided. In West African commercial centers, such as Accra and Lagos, high land-labor ratios and large flows of economic migrants contributed to real wage rises (Frankema&vanWaijenburg_2012: p.15, p.17). This suggests that the agricultural sector's institutional structures played a significant role in facilitating labor mobility and productivity, thereby fostering economic growth.

Similarly, in India during the late sixteenth century, the size of armies provides an indication of population size, which is crucial for understanding long-term economic performance (BroadberryEtal_2015: p.4). The controversy surrounding India's economic history underscores the importance of institutions in shaping a country's economic trajectory over the long run.

Moreover, the existence of environments conducive to commercial exchange demonstrates the role of institutions in facilitating trade and promoting growth (Bryant_2006: p.13). The fusion of mercantilist imperialism with capitalistic transformations marked a significant shift in institutional structures that likely had lasting impacts on economic development.

Structural change, particularly in education and industrial development, may not necessarily be a primary cause of long-term economic growth, as some argue (Simson_2019: p.27). For instance, Simson points out that while there was an expansion of public employment and state expenditure during the 1960s and 1970s in certain regions, it is questionable whether their elimination of inefficiencies would have sustained the investment levels or re-oriented these economies (Simson_2019: p.4). Similarly, Stern suggests that economic growth may lead to environmental degradation during early stages and that any irreversibility might not significantly reduce income in the future (Stern_2004: p.8). Furthermore, McCloskey argues that conservative ideologies and religious beliefs in certain countries such as France and Spain hindered economic growth despite the examples of more prosperous nations (McCloskey_2015: p.9). These sources challenge the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by suggesting that other factors, such as ideologies and environmental impacts, play significant roles in shaping economic development.

The sources under examination complicate the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by introducing factors such as education, ideologies, and environmental impacts into the discourse.

Firstly, Komlos (1998) suggests that physical stature during the Industrial Revolution was significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic processes and associated structural changes, indicating a complex interplay between institutions and biological growth processes. Additionally, Frankema (2012) argues that colonial education played a crucial role in explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa, suggesting that institutional factors like educational systems can have profound effects on economic development.

Moreover, Temin (2002) posits that the exhaustion of cheap labor caused economic growth to slow and that recent theories of economic growth should consider the historical context of economic transition. This perspective complicates the notion that institutions are solely responsible for long-run economic growth by highlighting the role of endogenous factors such as labor markets in shaping economic trajectories.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) further challenge this thesis by suggesting a reciprocal relationship between economic growth and equality, implying that policymakers must carefully consider trade-offs between these two objectives. Similarly, Koepke & Baten (2005) explore the relationship between climate and human living standards, demonstrating that environmental factors can have a significant impact on long-run economic development.

Lastly, Hopkins (2019) discusses how postmodernism inhibited the study of long-run economic and political change in Africa by removing the problem of poverty from scholarly discourse. This observation underscores the importance of considering various ideological perspectives when examining the causes of long-run economic growth.

The works of Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) and Austin (2007) complicate the relationship between institutions and economic performance by shedding light on gender roles and their impact on economic growth in Africa.

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) emphasize that women's contributions to economic growth have often been overlooked due to their labor being categorized as household production, thus going unaccounted for in official records. They argue that understanding this process is crucial for policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.3). Furthermore, they highlight that the economic contribution of African women was largely dependent on their husbands' provision (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.9), suggesting a significant institutional factor influencing gender-based economic disparities.

Austin (2007) adds to this discussion by examining the role of infrastructure, specifically railways and lorries, in economic growth. He notes that until the mid-1920s, railways had a fundamental cost advantage over lorries (Austin_2007: p.8). This observation underscores the importance of infrastructure development as an institutional factor contributing to economic performance.

These sources demonstrate that while institutions are indeed fundamental to long-run economic growth, understanding the role of gender and infrastructure within these institutions is essential for a comprehensive analysis of economic performance in Africa. Further research could explore how these factors intersect and impact each other, providing valuable insights into the complexities of institutional development and economic growth.

The intersection of institutions and agriculture plays a significant role in understanding long-run economic growth, as demonstrated by Clark & Jacks (2007) and Feinstein (1998). These sources complicate the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by shedding light on the intricate relationship between institutional development and agricultural advancements.

Clark & Jacks (2007) provide evidence of the coal industry's response to increases in demand during the Industrial Revolution, which was facilitated by technological advances that improved ventilation (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.4). This technological progress can be viewed as an institutional factor, as it represents a system of rules and practices that enabled the industry to adapt and grow. However, it is also implicitly the counterfactual of early Africa (E.A.), suggesting that the absence of such institutional advancements may have hindered economic growth in these regions (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.27).

Feinstein (1998) supports this notion by discussing changes in farming during the 1850s and 1860s, which were significant enough to disperse the surplus of farm labor in rural areas (Feinstein_1998: p.28). This shift can be attributed to institutional developments that facilitated agricultural advancements, such as improvements in farming techniques or the implementation of policies promoting agricultural growth. However, it is essential to note that these changes occurred later in Britain compared to the Industrial Revolution era, suggesting that institutional development may not always precede economic growth, but may instead coincide with it.

The scholarly consensus on the role of institutions in long-run economic growth is evident, with both Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) acknowledging their significance. Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) contends that institutional changes can impact factors of production, while Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.2) suggests that the Western impact on economic institutions is not limited to economic aspects alone.

However, there are differences in their perspectives. Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) emphasizes the disadvantage of labor being tied to land or owners, arguing that it stifles competition among employers. In contrast, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) focuses on the centrality of slave dealing and status acquisition in the Western impact on economic institutions.

Both scholars also offer insights into the timing of institutional development relative to economic growth. Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) notes that institutional changes occurred before economic growth in some cases, such as Russia prior to peasant enserfment, but later in Britain compared to the Industrial Revolution era. This suggests that while institutions may not always precede economic growth, they can coincide with it. Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.10) points out that before the arrival of the British, people rarely ventured far beyond their kin or close friends, implying a potential slow pace of institutional change in some societies.

Despite these insights, several questions remain unresolved. For instance, what specific institutional changes drive economic growth? How can we measure the impact of institutions on economic growth, and how do we compare the effects across different societies and historical periods? Addressing these questions is crucial for a comprehensive understanding of the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth.
