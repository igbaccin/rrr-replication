The central question under investigation in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth, a thesis that has garnered significant attention among scholars. The stakes are high, as understanding the drivers of economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at fostering development and reducing poverty.

Supporting this thesis, Austin (2008) argues that institutions play a crucial role in long-term economic growth, asserting that they are 'the' fundamental cause. Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) focus on the political factors underpinning economic growth and the development of markets, demonstrating how institutions played a necessary role in making possible economic growth and political freedom. Acemoglu et al. (2001) also support this view, showing a large effect of institutions on economic performance.

However, not all scholars agree that institutions are the sole drivers of long-term economic growth. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate the debate by pointing out the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 in Sub-Saharan Africa, which has been absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance to the discussion, highlighting that understanding the processes by which women’s economic contribution becomes subsumed under the category of household production is essential for policies seeking to strengthen women’s economic roles.

As the debate unfolds, it becomes clear that while institutions are undeniably important in shaping long-term economic growth, other factors such as technological innovation, social structure, and gender dynamics also play significant roles. The ongoing discourse aims to further disentangle these interrelated factors and their impacts on economic development, with implications for policy decisions aimed at promoting sustainable growth and reducing poverty.

The interplay between institutions and trade significantly contributes to long-run economic growth as highlighted by several scholars. Kuznets (1973) posits that economic and social structure undergo rapid changes concurrent with modern economic growth, which are key factors affecting economic development (Kuznets_1973: p.4). Moreover, he emphasizes the role of technological innovations and social innovations in promoting economic growth (Kuznets_1973: p.6).

North & Weingast (1989) shed light on the political factors underlying economic growth and market development, focusing not only on rules governing economic exchange but also on institutions enforcing these rules (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). They assert that representative government with multiple veto points plays a crucial role in fostering economic growth (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

Mokyr (2005) supports the argument by suggesting that developments in intellectual realms, particularly the growth of economic reasonableness, contributed significantly to the West's economic prosperity (Mokyr_2005: p.4). He also highlights the importance of institutionalized processes that allow individuals to be heard and have an impact on rules (Mokyr_2005: p.56).

These findings underscore the fundamental role of institutions in shaping economic growth, as they influence technological progress, social structure, political systems, and intellectual development—all key factors in long-term economic prosperity. The ongoing discourse on this topic offers valuable insights for policymakers aiming to promote sustainable growth and reduce poverty by strengthening and adapting institutions accordingly.

The role of institutions in shaping agricultural development and long-term economic growth is evident from various studies. Acemoglu et al. (2001) argue that fundamental institutions play a significant role in determining property rights, which in turn affects investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). They further assert that differences in mortality rates are not the primary cause of variation in institutions (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3), suggesting that institutional structures have an independent impact on economic performance.

Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2002) emphasize the importance of institutions as a key factor in understanding long-term economic and political development (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49). They also note that there has been a shift in the distribution of economic prosperity, with countries experiencing improvements in their institutional structures seeing significant growth (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.6).

Broadberry et al.'s (2015) study on India's long-run economic performance supports this perspective. They highlight the controversy surrounding India's economic history and the importance of understanding institutional structures in shaping its development (BroadberryEtal_2015: p.2). Their research provides valuable insights into the role of institutions in agricultural transformation, which is crucial for sustainable economic growth.

Institutions and colonialism significantly influenced long-run economic growth, as evidenced by the transformative impact of European imperialism on various regions (Austin_2008: p.24). Austin (2008) argues that trades, such as the Atlantic one, reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe. This suggests that institutional structures established during colonialism played a crucial role in shaping the long-term development of economies.

Similarly, Bryant (2006) highlights the fusion of mercantilist imperialism with capitalistic transformation as a fundamental axis of difference between European and non-European societies during the period of colonization (Bryant_2006: p.5). This fusion led to the creation of institutions that facilitated commercial exchange, which in turn contributed to economic growth in Europe but not necessarily in colonized territories.

Moreover, Austin (2008) emphasizes the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history to reveal relationships that recur due to path dependence and similar situations (Austin_2008: p.1). This approach underscores the enduring impact of colonial institutions on economic development, as path dependencies often persist over time.

The contention that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth faces challenges from several perspectives, particularly in relation to trade and environmental factors. For instance, Stern (2004) argues that economic growth may not necessarily lead to environmental degradation, as some argue, but rather it could be a means to eventual environmental improvement (Stern_2004: p.1). This contrasts the view that institutions play a crucial role in shaping economic growth and its associated impacts on the environment.

Additionally, McCloskey (2015) critiques the idea that institutional factors are the sole determinants of long-run economic growth. McCloskey suggests that ideologies and ideas have played significant roles in shaping economic growth, as seen in the examples of France and Spain where conservative ideologies hindered economic development for centuries despite favorable material conditions (McCloskey_2015: p.9). This perspective challenges the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

Similarly, Bryceson (2002) provides evidence that traditional hierarchical orders within communities can influence women's economic and social spheres, with women primarily involved in agrarian family work efforts rather than diversifying into nonagricultural activities (Bryceson_2002: p.9). This suggests that institutions may not be the primary drivers of economic growth, as they might limit opportunities for economic diversification.

Lastly, Humphries (2013) argues that the division of labor, workshop production, and the emergence of the factory system were key factors in driving eighteenth-century economic growth, rather than institutions (Humphries_2013: p.18). This perspective challenges the idea that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth.

The perspective that institutions are not the primary cause of long-run economic growth is further complicated by evidence suggesting a correlation between trade and institutional development. Komlos (1998) emphasizes the interplay between economic processes, structural changes, and urbanization during the Industrial Revolution, implying that these factors may have been influenced by institutional developments. Similarly, Temin (2002) contends that the return to equilibrium growth paths was brought about by countries' internal dynamics rather than adverse economic shocks in the 1970s, suggesting that institutions played a role in determining growth trajectories.

Moreover, Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that explaining long-run distribution trends is closely related to understanding the causes of economic growth itself. Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) also highlight the variation across societies in conditions relevant to growth such as property rights and political institutions, suggesting a role for institutions in shaping economic trajectories. Bolt & van Zanden (2014) discuss revisions of nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth that have been added based on institutional factors, further supporting the idea that institutions contribute to long-run economic growth.

The relationship between institutions and education as contributors to long-run economic growth is further nuanced by examining the historical contexts of educational systems and their impact on growth trajectories. Koepke & Baten (2005) present a compelling argument that climate, while often considered a determinant of living standards, may not directly correlate with education levels or economic development. For instance, they demonstrate that despite the Roman Empire's long duration and perceived economic growth, heights among its inhabitants did not increase significantly, suggesting stagnant living standards (Koepke & Baten_2005: p.16).

Similarly, Frankema (2012) challenges the notion that colonial education is solely responsible for long-run growth disparities in Africa, arguing instead that the degree of British "liberalism" was endogenous to the local political and economic context (Frankema_2012: p.19). Goldin (2006) highlights the complexity of the labor market's institutional structure, suggesting that changes in labor laws and women's rights have had profound effects on long-term economic growth (Goldin_2006: p.3).

However, van Zanden (1999) emphasizes that while pre-industrial economic growth was once viewed as insignificant, recent research has challenged this perspective (vanZanden_1999: p.1). He notes that in the mid-eighteenth century, Western Europe still exhibited a negative link between output and level of economic development, suggesting that institutional factors may have played a role in shaping these disparities (vanZanden_1999: p.13).

The contributions of African women to economic growth and development, while significant, were often subsumed under household production and went unaccounted for in many historical analyses (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.3). This oversight undermines policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles, as their labor was largely dependent on the economic provision of their husbands (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.9). Furthermore, the parallels between West Africa and other regions in Africa suggest that gender disparities and their impact on economic growth are not isolated phenomena (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.4).

Similarly, the economic base in West Africa during the period of the slave trade appeared to crumble, leading to an increased emphasis on the superstructure (Hopkins_2019: p.9). However, food exports made minimal contributions to economic growth during this period (Hopkins_2019: p.13). These findings complicate the understanding of the role of trade in fostering long-term economic growth in West Africa.

The study of African economic history has been influenced by Western perspectives, leading to a lack of diversity in research and potentially limiting the exploration of novel enquiries (Hopkins_2019: p.4). Moreover, the focus on postmodernism removed the problem of poverty from the academic discourse, which may have inhibited the study of long-run economic and political change (Hopkins_2019: p.10).

Stoler (1989) argues that colonizers lived in "imagined communities" that were consciously created to overcome economic and social disparities. This observation encourages novel enquiries into the nature of these communities, but it also closes off other lines of investigation (Stoler_1989: p.3). Furthermore, the alliance between the plantation elite and the colonial administration was strengthened by certain innovations in the colony (Stoler_1989: p.21).

These sources add nuance to the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. They complicate the understanding of gender roles, trade, Western influence, and social structures in shaping economic development in West Africa. Further exploration of these factors could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the role of institutions in fostering long-term economic growth.

The role of institutions in fostering long-term economic growth is a topic that garners significant attention among scholars, as evidenced by the works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959). Both authors acknowledge the importance of institutions, albeit from slightly different perspectives.

Domar (1970) emphasizes the impact of labor-tied structures on economic growth. He argues that when labor is tied to land or to an owner, competition among employers ceases, potentially stifling growth (p.4). On the other hand, Bohannan (1959) discusses the Western impact on African societies, including economic institutions, but also touches upon cultural and social structures (p.10).

Both authors converge in their recognition of the role of institutions in shaping economic development. However, they diverge in their focus: Domar primarily concentrates on labor-tied structures, while Bohannan explores the broader influence of Western impact.

Despite these insights, some aspects remain unresolved. For instance, a comprehensive understanding of how specific institutional factors contribute to long-term economic growth in different contexts is yet to be fully explored. Furthermore, the interplay between various institutional factors and their collective impact on economic development remains an area for further investigation.

In this regard, future research could delve deeper into the nuances of institutional structures and their role in fostering long-term economic growth, providing a more comprehensive understanding of this complex relationship.
