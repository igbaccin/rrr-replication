The debate surrounding the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth has been a subject of intense discussion among scholars. One of the central questions at hand is whether institutions play a pivotal role in driving economic growth and development, or if other factors such as geographical location, technology, or cultural practices hold more significance.

In support of the institutionalist perspective, authors such as North & Weingast (1989) argue that the fundamental institutions of representative government, including multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are intimately related to economic growth and political freedom. They demonstrate how these institutions enabled the development of markets and protected property rights, fostering economic progress. Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2001) highlight the detrimental effects of institutions that were unfavorable to investment and economic progress.

On the other hand, some scholars offer critiques and complications to this argument. For instance, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) emphasize the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 in Sub-Saharan Africa, which has led to its absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. They suggest that understanding the extent to which there are still risks of economic shrinkage is crucial for determining whether recent growth provides a path to convergence.

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also complicate the discussion by focusing on the contribution of African women to economic growth and development. They argue that understanding the processes by which women's economic contributions have been subsumed under household production and their labor formally unaccounted for is essential for strengthening policies aimed at empowering women economically.

The stakes of this debate are high, as understanding the fundamental causes of long-run economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting development in various regions. The ongoing discussion between supporters and critics of institutionalism provides a nuanced perspective on the complex interplay of factors that drive economic growth and development, ultimately enriching our understanding of this essential topic.

The literature supports the notion that institutions are indeed a fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Austin (2008) asserts that institutions, such as those in Africa, have played a significant role in shaping the economic position of various regions compared to others, reinforcing rather than originating their relative positions (Austin_2008: p.24). North & Weingast (1989) also emphasize the importance of institutions, specifically those of representative government, in fostering economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.30).

Acemoglu et al. (2002) highlight a reversal in the distribution of economic prosperity over time, suggesting that this change may be related to institutions (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5). Whatley (2018) presents econometric results that demonstrate relationships recurring due to similar situations and path dependencies, further supporting the idea that institutions are fundamental to economic growth (Whatley_2018: p.3).

Nunn & Wantchekon (2011) provide evidence showing that both internal and external factors influenced economic development, but the internal factor was at least twice as significant, underscoring the importance of institutions (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.4). Lastly, Bryant (2006) acknowledges the fusion of mercantilist imperialism with a technologically driven capitalistic transformation as a key factor in economic growth, implying that these transformations were institutionally driven (Bryant_2006: p.5).

Technological innovations and social changes are integral components in the long-run economic growth, as supported by Kuznets (1973). The author emphasizes that rapid changes in economic structure are closely linked to significant transformations in society (Kuznets_1973: p.4). This correlation is further reinforced with Kuznets' assertion that technological innovations and social innovations play a crucial role in driving economic growth (Kuznets_1973: p.6).

Moreover, Kuznets highlights the non-conventional costs associated with economic growth, such as environmental degradation and increased inequality (Kuznets_1973: p.5, p.11). These issues underscore the importance of institutions in managing and mitigating these negative consequences, thereby fostering sustainable long-term growth.

The persistence of institutions plays a crucial role in shaping economic performance, as demonstrated by Acemoglu and colleagues (AcemogluEtAl_2001). They assert that the effect of more fundamental institutions on property rights and incentives for investment significantly influences economic progress.

In their analysis, Acemoglu et al. (2001) contend that differences in mortality rates are not the primary cause of variation in institutions. Instead, they argue that the persistence of certain institutional structures can be traced back to historical events, such as colonization and the establishment of political systems (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3). These institutions, once established, can have long-lasting effects on a country's economic development.

Moreover, Acemoglu et al. (2001) suggest that these institutional structures can be either beneficial or detrimental to investment and economic progress. For instance, they find that the institutions prevalent in Western Europe, which promoted property rights, rule of law, and political accountability, fostered economic growth and structural change (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). In contrast, institutions that were detrimental to investment, such as those found in many parts of Africa and the Middle East, hindered economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27).

Furthermore, Acemoglu et al. (2001) present evidence showing a large effect of institutions on economic performance. Their analysis reveals that countries with stronger property rights and more secure institutions have higher levels of income per capita and economic growth (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19). This suggests that institutional quality is a fundamental determinant of long-run economic growth, supporting the notion that institutions are indeed the key driver of economic development.

Evidence from Simson's (2019) study challenges the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, particularly in the context of industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy. The author reveals that during the 1960s and 1970s, economies in focus expanded their state expenditure and deficits, coinciding with a period of high economic growth (Simson_2019: p.25). This suggests that factors such as industrial development and educational expansion might have played significant roles in driving economic growth during this time.

Furthermore, Simson (2019) points out that there was an emphasis on expanding public employment rather than protecting existing government jobs (Simson_2019: p.4). This shift could imply a focus on institutional expansion and development, potentially contributing to economic growth without the need for strong institutions as a primary driver.

Additionally, the author questions whether eliminating spending inefficiencies, leakages, and costly delays would have been sufficient to sustain investment levels of the 1960s (Simson_2019: p.4). This challenge suggests that institutional quality might not be as crucial for long-term economic growth as previously believed.

However, Simson's (2019) study does not offer a conclusive counterargument to the thesis, as it primarily focuses on specific aspects of institutional development in certain regions during particular time periods. Further research is necessary to fully understand the complex relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth.

The assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is further complicated by findings that suggest other factors may also play significant roles in this relationship. For instance, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that Sub-Saharan Africa has been absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth due to the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). This implies that the underdevelopment in this region could be attributed, at least partially, to insufficient historical data rather than only institutional factors.

Moreover, Temin (2002) posits that the exhaustion of cheap labor caused economic growth to slow down in certain regions (Temin_2002: p.4). This contradicts the notion that institutions are solely responsible for long-run economic growth, as it suggests that labor availability and its cost could also have a substantial impact on economic development over time.

Allen (2001) highlights the importance of wages and prices in understanding economic development, economic leadership, and standard of living (Allen_2001: p.1). While this does not directly challenge the role of institutions, it underscores the complexity of factors influencing long-run economic growth, suggesting that wages and prices could be intertwined with institutional structures in shaping economic outcomes.

Austin (2007) suggests that until the mid-1920s, railways had a fundamental advantage over lorries due to their lower cost (Austin_2007: p.8). This implies that technological innovations and infrastructure development could also contribute to long-run economic growth, potentially alongside institutional factors.

Bolt & van Zanden (2014) indicate that recent revisions of nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth have been made for several countries, including Germany, Sweden, Italy, Greece, Russia, and Bulgaria (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5). These revisions might help refine our understanding of long-run economic growth patterns in these regions, potentially revealing the influence of factors other than institutions on their development.

Finally, Allen et al. (2011) argue that income disparities in the early twentieth century were due to long-run stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe (AllenEtAl_2011: p.1). This finding suggests that institutional factors might not be the sole determinant of economic growth, as geographical differences and technological advancements could also play crucial roles in shaping long-run economic outcomes.

The work of Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) sheds light on the role of African women in economic growth, adding nuance to the claim that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Their research underscores the importance of understanding how women's economic contributions have been subsumed under household production and often overlooked in formal accounts (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.3).

In West Africa, for instance, women's contribution to economic growth and development was largely dependent on the economic provision of their husbands (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.9). This suggests that institutional factors may not only influence economic growth directly but also indirectly through gender dynamics within households.

Moreover, Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) argue that the major historical developments in West Africa find parallels elsewhere in Africa where gender and economic growth are concerned (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.4). This implies that the role of women in economic growth may be a pan-African issue, further complicating the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth.

The contribution of African women to economic growth and development, as noted by Akyeampong and Fofack (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.19), is significant. However, their labor often goes unaccounted for in formal economic records due to its association with household production. This underscores the need to reconsider institutional factors to account for these unofficial contributions when studying long-run economic growth.

The industrialization effect, a critical aspect of long-run economic growth, is further elucidated by Komlos (1998). He highlights that the Industrial Revolution not only led to physical changes in population and urbanization but also exhibited cyclical patterns in height growth, which could be linked to short-term fluctuations in economic conditions (Komlos_1998: p.16). Moreover, Komlos underscores that these structural changes were integral to the process of modern economic growth during this period (Komlos_1998: p.17).

These findings suggest that institutional factors, such as industrialization, played a multifaceted role in shaping long-term economic growth. They not only stimulated population and urban expansion but also influenced biological growth processes through short-term economic fluctuations. This nuanced perspective underscores the importance of considering both formal and informal aspects of institutions when studying long-run economic growth.

In addition, Komlos's work implies that the Industrial Revolution might have been characterized by cyclical patterns, which could offer valuable insights into the relationship between institutional changes and economic growth over time. Further examination of these cycles could potentially reveal how institutions respond to and influence economic fluctuations, providing a deeper understanding of long-run economic growth.

However, Komlos does not discuss the timing of the onset of modern economic growth or the conceptualization of the Industrial Revolution (Komlos_1998: p.2). This omission leaves room for future research to explore how these factors might have interplayed with institutional changes during the transition to modern economic growth.

The works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) provide valuable insights into the relationship between institutions, economic growth, and structural change, albeit from different perspectives. Both scholars acknowledge the crucial role of institutions in shaping economic development. Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) asserts that with labor tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, which can hinder economic growth. This notion aligns with Bohannan's (Bohannan_1959: p.7) argument about the impact of slave dealing and the acquisition of status on economic institutions in Africa.

However, Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) emphasizes that labor-tied structures can lead to underutilization of resources, while Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.13) points out the limiting factor of women's numbers in certain societies. This divergence highlights the complexity of institutional arrangements and their effects on economic growth.

Both scholars also touch upon structural change as a driver of growth. Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) suggests that factors of production, including labor, capital, and technology, are interconnected and affect economic growth. Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.10), on the other hand, discusses how the Western impact extended beyond economic institutions to include social structures, which could have indirect effects on economic growth.

Despite these insights, several questions remain unresolved. For instance, the interplay between institutional changes and the Industrial Revolution remains unexplored (Komlos_1998: p.2). Furthermore, the specific mechanisms through which institutions influence structural change and long-term economic growth require further investigation. Future research could delve deeper into these areas to gain a more comprehensive understanding of the relationship between institutions, structural change, and economic growth.
