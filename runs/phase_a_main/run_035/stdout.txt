The debate on the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth centers around the role of institutions, with a significant number of scholars supporting this perspective (Austin_2008, Kuznets_1973). Austin (2008) asserts that institutions are "the" fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, while Kuznets (1973) emphasizes the association between rapid changes in economic structure and societal innovations, which he views as major factors affecting economic and social structure.

On the other hand, some scholars complicate this argument by highlighting instances where historical circumstances and gender roles have influenced economic growth differently (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014, Komlos_1998). For example, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) argue that understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution becomes subsumed under household production is crucial for policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles.

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) and Simson (2019) offer critiques, with the former pointing out the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 in Sub-Saharan Africa, which has led to its absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. Simson (2019) questions whether the elimination of spending inefficiencies and delays would have been enough to sustain investment levels during the 1960s and 1970s in certain regions, suggesting that other factors may also play a significant role in economic growth.

The stakes in this debate are high, as understanding the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting economic development in various regions, particularly those experiencing slow growth. The ongoing discussion underscores the complexity of economic growth and the need for continued research to fully understand its underlying drivers.

The significance of institutions in promoting economic growth, particularly in agricultural sectors, is underscored by various scholars. Austin (2008) asserts that institutions are 'the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth' (Austin_2008: p.26). This assertion is supported by the observation that certain trades, such as the Atlantic one, reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe (and parts of Asia) (Austin_2008: p.24).

Kuznets (1973) also supports this view by highlighting that high aggregate growth rates are associated with rapid changes in economic structure, which in turn influence other aspects of society (Kuznets_1973: p.4). Furthermore, Kuznets notes that the succession of technological innovations characteristic of modern economic growth is a major factor affecting economic and social structure (Kuznets_1973: p.6).

North & Weingast (1989) focus on the political factors underpinning economic growth, emphasizing the role of institutions in making possible economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). They argue that the fundamental institutions of representative government, such as an explicit set of multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are intimately related to the struggle for control over property rights (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

Acemoglu et al. (2001) support this thesis by demonstrating a large effect of institutions on economic performance (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19). They argue that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27).

The role of institutions in fostering urbanization and, consequently, long-term economic growth is further supported by deVries (1994) and Frankema & van Waijenburg (2012). DeVries (deVries_1994: p.2) asserts that the Industrial Revolution, a significant milestone in economic history, was primarily an achievement of early modern Europe's urban network. This network, established by institutions facilitating commercial interaction among cities, provided a foundation for regional economic development and industrial growth.

Moreover, deVries (deVries_1994: p.5) emphasizes the importance of Europe's urban network in establishing a framework for regional economic development. The author believes that this development enabled industrial growth to occur, suggesting that institutions played a crucial role in the Industrial Revolution.

Frankema & van Waijenburg (Frankema&vanWaijenburg_2012: p.15) also support this notion by highlighting the attractiveness of West African commercial centers, which drew large flows of economic migrants due to their urbanization and economic development. The high population growth rates in these cities indicate that institutions fostering urbanization can lead to increased economic activity (Frankema & van Waijenburg_2012: p.17).

Structural change, a key aspect of economic growth, is significantly influenced by institutional development (Abramovitz_1986: p.7). This transformation involves shifts in the economy from agriculture to industry and services, which are critical for long-term economic expansion. Abramovitz (1986) suggests that the elaboration of a mixed economy, characterized by both public and private sectors, is a function of economic growth itself (Abramovitz_1986: p.20). This implies that as economies grow, institutions evolve to foster a balance between state intervention and market forces, thereby promoting sustained economic development.

Moreover, Abramovitz (1986) highlights the importance of macroeconomic and monetary conditions in encouraging and sustaining capital investment and effective demand levels (Abramovitz_1986: p.7). These conditions are crucial for structural change, as they facilitate the reallocation of resources from less productive sectors to more promising ones, ultimately contributing to economic growth.

In addition, Abramovitz (1986) emphasizes that countries are currently readjusting their focus towards productivity growth (Abramovitz_1986: p.22). This shift signifies a recognition of the importance of institutions in fostering long-term economic expansion. Institutions that promote productivity growth, such as those encouraging innovation and technology adoption, can significantly contribute to structural change and, consequently, long-run economic growth.

The assertion that institutions are the primary driver of long-term economic growth faces challenges when considering their impact on agriculture, as suggested by various sources. For instance, Simson (2019) argues that state expenditure and deficits expanded in Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda during a period of high economic growth in the 1960s and 1970s (Simson_2019: p.25). This evidence suggests that institutional reforms may not have been crucial to sustaining investment levels or reorienting these economies towards industrialization.

Furthermore, McCloskey (2015) challenges the notion by providing examples of countries like France and Spain where economic growth was throttled for conservative reasons despite the examples of more economically successful nations (McCloskey_2015: p.9). These instances imply that institutions may not always be the primary factor influencing long-term economic growth, as some countries experienced stagnation even with neighboring success stories.

In addition, Bryceson (2002) argues that traditional hierarchical order within communities influences women's economic and social spheres in agricultural societies, leading to their continued involvement in agrarian work rather than adopting non-agricultural activities for income generation (Bryceson_2002: p.9). This finding challenges the idea that institutional changes promoting productivity growth and technological adoption are necessary for long-term economic expansion, particularly within agriculture.

Finally, Humphries (2013) suggests that the division of labor, workshop production, and the emergence of the factory system were critical factors in driving eighteenth-century economic growth, rather than institutions (Humphries_2013: p.18). This argument challenges the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, as it emphasizes the role of technological advancements and production methods over institutional reforms.

These findings challenge the perspective that institutions are the primary drivers of long-term economic growth by highlighting instances where state expenditure, traditional hierarchies, and technological advancements played significant roles in shaping agricultural economies. Future research should explore these factors further to gain a more comprehensive understanding of the causes of long-run economic growth.

The examination of institutions and agriculture reveals intricate nuances that complicate the notion of long-run economic growth. For instance, the contribution of African women to economic growth was often subsumed under household production, rendering their labor informally unaccounted for (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.3). This omission undermines policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles, suggesting that a comprehensive understanding of long-run growth requires an inclusive perspective that acknowledges and addresses the economic contributions of all members within agricultural societies.

Furthermore, the relationship between institutions and agriculture is not merely a one-way street. The historical developments in West Africa, as discussed by Akyeampong and Fofack (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.4), find parallels elsewhere in Africa where gender and economic growth are concerned. This implies that understanding the role of institutions in shaping agricultural economies necessitates a broader examination of societal structures beyond West Africa, offering valuable insights into the complex interplay between institutions, agriculture, and long-run economic growth.

Moreover, the concept of modern economic growth, as defined by Kuznets (Komlos_1998: p.2), is multifaceted and encompasses various economic processes and associated structural changes. The onset of modern economic growth was not only influenced by technological advancements but also by short-term fluctuations in economic conditions (Komlos_1998: p.16, p.17). This suggests that the role of institutions in shaping agricultural economies must be examined within the context of these broader economic processes to gain a more comprehensive understanding of long-run growth.

In addition, Temin (Temin_2002: p.4) argues that the exhaustion of cheap labor caused economic growth to slow. The end of the Golden Age was brought about by countries returning to equilibrium growth paths rather than adverse economic shocks in the 1970s (Temin_2002: p.14). This implies that institutions, by facilitating or hindering access to labor and resources, play a significant role in determining the pace of long-run economic growth.

Lastly, Lindert and Williamson (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1) highlight that policymakers have traditionally sought to balance economic growth with equality, acknowledging trade-offs between the two. However, the task of explaining long-run distribution trends rejoins the search for explanations of growth itself (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.8). This suggests that understanding the role of institutions in shaping agricultural economies necessitates a holistic approach that considers not only economic growth but also its distributional consequences.

The relationship between institutional development and economic growth is further complicated by the interplay of various factors, as suggested by Bolt & van Zanden (2014). They highlight that revisions to historical growth data for countries like Switzerland, Germany, Sweden, Italy, Greece, Russia, and Bulgaria have not significantly altered the overall understanding of institutional development's role in economic growth (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5). This indicates a persistent influence of institutions on long-term economic growth, even as the specific data may undergo revisions.

Moreover, Bolt & van Zanden (2014) emphasize that understanding institutional development in different regions requires a comprehensive approach that considers various aspects such as economic conditions, trade patterns, and technological advancements (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5). This holistic perspective underscores the multifaceted nature of institutional development and its impact on long-run economic growth.

On a related note, Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) emphasize that institutions are not isolated entities but are interconnected within an intricate web of interactions (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.21). They argue that the strategies of one institution can significantly affect the strategies of others, suggesting a complex dynamic between institutions and economic growth. Furthermore, they note that there has been a shift towards economic and social history at the expense of political history, which could potentially overshadow the role of political institutions in shaping long-term economic growth (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.7).

Despite the shift towards economic and social history, recent debates about long-run growth have largely overlooked Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA) due to a lack of data on its economic performance before 1950 (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). This oversight is significant as Collier and Gunning (1999) observe that African economic performance has been markedly worse than other regions, while Sachs and Warner (1997) note the region's slow growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1).

However, Broadberry and Gardner (2022) argue that understanding the extent to which SSA's recent growth provides a path to convergence requires an examination of the risks of shrinking that have undermined economic progress in the past (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.18). In other regions, new contributions to historical national accounting have provided insights into patterns of growth and development over centuries, rewriting the history of economic divergence and convergence since before the industrial revolution (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1).

This suggests that while there may be a tendency to overlook political institutions in shaping long-term economic growth due to a focus on economic and social history, a more comprehensive understanding of historical economic performance can potentially reshape our perspective. The case of SSA highlights the importance of examining regions often left out of such debates, as their economic performance may provide valuable insights into the role of political institutions in long-run growth.

The works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) offer valuable insights into the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth, albeit from different perspectives. Both scholars acknowledge the significance of institutional structures in shaping economic outcomes. Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) emphasizes that when labor is tied to land or an owner, competition among employers ceases, potentially hindering growth. Conversely, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) highlights how the Western impact, particularly in African societies, reshaped economic institutions, altering patterns of trade and social organization.

Despite their contrasting focuses, both scholars agree on the importance of institutional change as a driver of growth. Domar's (Domar_1970: p.4) discussion of the Russian peasants' enserfment mirrors Bohannan's (Bohannan_1959: p.7) description of how Western impact centered on slave dealing and status acquisition, both of which significantly reshaped economic institutions.

However, there are areas where their perspectives diverge. Domar (1970) primarily focuses on the role of labor markets and competition in growth, while Bohannan (1959) delves into the socio-cultural aspects of institutional change. This difference underscores the complexity of institutions as a factor in economic growth, encompassing not just economic structures but also cultural and social norms.

Despite these insights, the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth remains unresolved. For instance, Domar (Domar_1970: p.16) references Phillips' work on the economic cost of slaveholding in the Cotton Belt, suggesting that even within a single region, the impact of institutions can be multifaceted and nuanced.
