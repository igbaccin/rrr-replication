LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The question of whether institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth has been a subject of intense debate among scholars. This discussion is crucial as it sheds light on the factors that drive economic development and growth, with potential implications for policy-making and development strategies.

Several scholars support the institutionalist perspective. Kuznets (1973) argues that economic growth is associated with rapid changes in economic structure, which in turn are linked to societal innovations. North & Weingast (1989) demonstrate how representative government institutions, characterized by multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, play a crucial role in promoting economic growth and political freedom. Acemoglu et al. (2001) provide evidence that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress, thereby supporting the institutionalist view.

However, not all scholars agree. Austin (2008), for instance, complicates this argument by suggesting that while institutions are important, they may reinforce rather than originate economic positions, as seen in the case of Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) also challenge the institutionalist perspective, arguing that a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has led to Sub-Saharan Africa being absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth.

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) add nuance to the discussion by highlighting the role of women's economic contribution and how it has been subsumed under household production, undermining policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles. This raises questions about the extent to which institutional analyses adequately account for gender dynamics in economic growth.

As the debate continues, it is clear that institutions play a significant role in long-run economic growth. However, the exact nature of this relationship and the extent to which other factors such as geography, culture, or technology influence this relationship remain open questions. Future research should continue to explore these complexities to better understand the drivers of economic development and growth.

Institutions significantly contribute to long-run economic growth, as evidenced by their impact on labor markets and economic structure. Kuznets (1973) highlights that rapid changes in economic structure are associated with societal transformations, such as urbanization and family structures (p.4). These alterations, in turn, facilitate the adoption of modern technology, thereby fostering economic growth (p.6).

North & Weingast (1989) demonstrate that representative government institutions play a crucial role in enabling economic growth by providing the necessary checks and balances for market development (p.2). Institutions governing rule enforcement and change are essential components of economic progress, as they ensure economic freedom and stability (p.30).

Mokyr (2005) supports this argument, stating that progressive societies with formal laws, corporatism, self-governance, and rules determined through an institutionalized process have experienced significant economic growth (p.56). These institutions contribute to the development of economic reasonableness, which is a key factor in long-term economic prosperity (p.54).

Institutions play a crucial role in fostering long-term economic growth, particularly through industrialization. Allen (2001) supports this notion by emphasizing the centrality of wages and prices in economic development, suggesting that they are indicative of trends in average earnings (Allen_2001: p.4). Moreover, the British industrial revolution offers reassurance that building craftsmen's wages may represent broader economic trends (Allen_2001: p.4).

Broadberry & Gupta (2006) also substantiate this view by highlighting the crucial role of the service sector in economic growth during the early modern period, as it did in the modern era (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.9). Furthermore, they argue that observations based on grain wages alone may not accurately reflect equal levels of economic development between Asia and Europe (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.24).

Bryant (2006) supports the argument by suggesting that the fusion of mercantilist imperialism with a technologically driven capitalistic transformation was a fundamental axis of difference in the economic development between Europe and other civilizations (Bryant_2006: p.5). This nascent European approach provided more than adequate environments for the growth of commercial exchange (Bryant_2006: p.13).

The significance of institutions in fostering long-term economic growth is further emphasized by various scholarly works. Acemoglu, Johnson, and Robinson (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27) argue that more fundamental institutions on property rights have a substantial impact on economic performance. They assert that these institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress. Similarly, Acemoglu, Johnson, and Robinson (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49) contend that understanding economic and political development over the past 500 years necessitates recognizing the fundamental role of institutions.

Moreover, Abramovitz (Abramovitz_1986: p.20) supports this view by suggesting that the elaboration of the mixed economy is a function of economic growth itself. He highlights three critical factors contributing to economic growth, one of which is macroeconomic and monetary conditions encouraging and sustaining capital investment and the level and growth of effective demand (Abramovitz_1986: p.7).

These sources collectively underscore the crucial role institutions play in shaping long-run economic growth. The development and evolution of institutions have been instrumental in fostering environments conducive to commercial exchange, investment, and capital accumulation—all essential elements for sustained economic growth (Bryant_2006: p.13). Furthermore, Acemoglu, Johnson, and Robinson (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19) demonstrate a large effect of institutions on economic performance through their empirical analysis.

Challenges to the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth emerge from studies focusing on labor markets and state expenditure. Simson (2019) contends that in certain regions, such as East Africa, state investment levels expanded during periods of high economic growth, suggesting that institutional reforms may not be the sole determinant of economic performance (Simson_2019: p.4). Furthermore, he argues that while there were undoubtedly spending inefficiencies and delays to reform, their elimination might not have been sufficient to sustain investment levels or reorient these economies (Simson_2019: p.27).

McCloskey (2015) also challenges the thesis by highlighting instances where ideologies and conservative beliefs impeded economic growth in France and Spain, despite the examples of prosperous regions like Holland and England (McCloskey_2015: p.8-9). These findings suggest that factors beyond institutions, such as ideologies and cultural influences, may also play significant roles in shaping long-term economic growth.

The examination of institutions as the primary catalyst for long-run economic growth gains nuance when considering labor markets, as revealed by Komlos (1998), Lindert & Williamson (1985), Sokoloff & Engerman (2000), Williamson (1996), Feinstein (1998), and Skocpol & Sommers (1980). These sources suggest that while institutions play a crucial role, they interact with labor market conditions to shape economic growth.

Komlos (1998) highlights the impact of physical stature on economic growth during the Industrial Revolution, implying that labor market structures may have influenced the biological growth process and short-term fluctuations in economies. Lindert & Williamson (1985) propose a connection between growth and inequality, suggesting that policymakers must consider trade-offs between the two when addressing long-term economic development.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) argue that initial conditions or factor endowments have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth, with labor market structures being among these factors. Williamson (1996) explores the relationship between schooling and Swedish economic growth before World War I, demonstrating how labor market conditions can impact a country's economic trajectory.

Feinstein (1998) discusses the role of migration and emigration in dispersing labor gluts, while Skocpol & Sommers (1980) argue that comparisons between Japan and China reveal the particularities of each case, including their respective labor market structures.

These sources complicate the argument that institutions are the sole cause of long-run economic growth by emphasizing the importance of labor markets in shaping economic development. Further examination is needed to understand how institutions and labor markets intersect and influence one another in driving long-term prosperity. (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4, Williamson_1996: p.20)

The interplay between institutions and education, as key factors in long-run economic growth, is further elucidated through the examination of climate's influence on human living standards (Koepke & Baten_2005: p.20). While it has been suggested that the Roman Empire experienced economic growth and improved purchasing power, the authors found no increase in heights over this period, indicating stagnant living standards contrary to common belief (Koepke & Baten_2005: p.16). This finding underscores the complexity of the relationship between institutions, labor markets, and economic development.

Moreover, the role of colonial education in shaping long-run growth disparities is a contentious issue. Bolt and Bezemer (2009) argue that British colonial education was instrumental in explaining African growth disparities, yet Frankema (Frankema_2012: p.2) notes that the degree of British "liberalism" was endogenous to local political and economic contexts. This suggests that the impact of institutions on education may not be uniform across regions, further complicating the relationship between institutions, education, and long-run growth.

The importance of education in driving economic development is also evident in the case of Africa. However, Hopkins (Hopkins_2019: p.3) argues that food exports made minimal contributions to African economic growth during the period of the slave trade, implying that other factors such as institutions may have played a more significant role.

Goldin (Goldin_2006: p.3) highlights the historical focus on the study of labor within the context of labor market institutions. The author suggests that the evolution and revolution in women's choices and decisions are crucial to understanding long-run economic growth, further underscoring the importance of institutions in shaping labor markets.

Institutions and Technological Advancements serve as critical factors in shaping long-run economic growth, as they facilitate the adoption and diffusion of new technologies, thereby fostering structural change and promoting efficiency.

The authors of Bolt & van Zanden (2014) assert that revisions to historical growth data for various countries, including Germany, Sweden, Italy, Greece, Russia, and Bulgaria, have been made, which do not fundamentally alter the overall picture but provide a more nuanced understanding of economic development (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5). This supports the notion that institutions play a crucial role in shaping technological advancements, as revisions to historical data offer insights into how different institutional frameworks have influenced the adoption and spread of new technologies over time.

Clark & Jacks (2007) highlight the importance of technological advancements in the coal industry during the Industrial Revolution. The authors demonstrate that the coal industry was able to respond effectively to increases in demand due to technological innovations, such as improved ventilation systems (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.4). This reinforces the idea that institutions can facilitate the development and dissemination of new technologies, thereby promoting economic growth.

Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) discuss the interplay between institutions, markets, and technological advancements. The authors argue that institutions serve as the "strategy" of various actors in the market, with each actor's actions influencing the strategies of others (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.21). This suggests that institutions play a key role in shaping the dynamics of technological advancements and their impact on economic growth.

Institutions significantly influence long-term economic growth, as they shape the dynamics of technological advancements (Braudel & Wallerstein_2009). Domar's (1970) and Bohannan's (1959) works provide tangential insights into this relationship. Both authors acknowledge the role of institutions in shaping economic structures, although their focus differs.

Domar (1970) emphasizes the impact of institutional arrangements on labor markets, suggesting that when labor is tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, hindering economic growth (p.4). On the other hand, Bohannan (1959) highlights how Western economic institutions, particularly those related to slave dealing and status acquisition, affected African societies (p.7, p.10).

Despite their differences in focus, both authors converge on the idea that institutions play a crucial role in shaping economic structures and, consequently, growth potential. However, what remains unresolved is the extent to which specific institutional arrangements contribute to long-term economic growth and how these arrangements can be modified to foster more favorable conditions for sustained development.

Phillips' work cited by Domar (1970) on the economic cost of slaveholding in the cotton belt could provide valuable insights into this question, as it delves into the economic impact of specific institutional arrangements (Domar_1970: p.16). Further research could explore how such findings can be applied to contemporary contexts and inform policies aimed at promoting long-term economic growth.
