LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question in the literature review on institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is whether institutional factors have a decisive impact on economic development over extended periods. This question carries significant implications for understanding historical and contemporary economic disparities, informing policy decisions, and shaping future economic strategies.

Several scholars support the thesis that institutions are the primary drivers of long-term economic growth. Austin (2008) argues that institutions, particularly those promoting trade and economic exchange, have played a crucial role in shaping Africa's economic position compared to Western Europe and parts of Asia (Austin_2008: p.24). Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) demonstrate how the establishment of representative government institutions has been closely linked to economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.30).

On the other hand, some scholars offer critiques, arguing that while institutions may influence economic growth, other factors such as geography, demographics, and technological innovation also play significant roles. For instance, Kuznets (1973) highlights how rapid changes in economic structure during modern economic growth are associated with both positive achievements and unexpected negative results (Kuznets_1973: p.4). Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate the discussion by pointing out that a lack of data before 1950 has led to Africa's absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1).

Additionally, some scholars add nuance or qualifications to the argument, emphasizing that while institutions are important, their impact may vary depending on specific historical and cultural contexts. For example, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) discuss how understanding the processes by which women's economic contributions have been subsumed under household production can undermine policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3).

The ongoing debate highlights the complexity of the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth. While there is strong support for the thesis that institutions are fundamental, critics and those who add nuance or qualifications emphasize the need to consider other factors and contexts. The argument continues as scholars continue to explore this crucial topic, seeking to uncover the intricate interplay between institutions, economic growth, and development.

The significance of institutions in fostering long-term economic growth is further underscored by various scholars, as they delve into the intricate relationship between institutional development and economic progress. In their article, North & Weingast (1989) argue that representative government institutions, characterized by multiple veto points and a primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, play a crucial role in enabling economic growth and political freedom (p.2). Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2001) demonstrate that the impact of fundamental institutions on property rights significantly influences investment and economic progress (p.27).

Mokyr (2005) emphasizes the importance of intellectual developments in shaping the West's economic growth. He suggests that changes in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West can be attributed to advancements in "useful knowledge" (p.4). North (1989), discussing institutions in Spain, highlights how centralized enforcement mechanisms impacted economic growth by altering transaction costs and capital markets (p.9).

Acemoglu et al. (2002) point out that the distribution of economic prosperity has undergone a significant shift over the past 500 years, which they attribute to changes in institutions (p.6). The studies by Abramovitz (1986) support this notion, as he identifies macroeconomic and monetary conditions encouraging capital investment and effective demand as key drivers of economic growth (p.7).

Institutions and labor markets are intrinsically linked in the long-run economic growth discourse. Kuznets (1973) asserts that structural change, a key aspect of economic growth, is accompanied by rapid changes in other societal aspects, including labor markets (p.4). He further posits that technological innovations, which are major drivers of modern economic growth, necessitate corresponding social innovations to ensure effective adjustments (p.6).

Similarly, Ogilvie (2007) highlights the interdependency of economic institutions within a wider framework, suggesting that changes in institutions can significantly impact labor markets and, consequently, economic growth (p.27). The author also notes that the efficiency theory, which assumes that people generally want the economy to grow as much as possible, may fail when considering the complexities of institutional interdependencies (p.27).

These findings support the argument that institutions are fundamental causes of long-run economic growth by demonstrating their influence on labor markets. The structural changes associated with economic growth necessitate corresponding adjustments in labor markets, underscoring the importance of institutions in facilitating these changes. Furthermore, the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework suggests that changes in institutions can have far-reaching effects on various aspects of an economy, including labor markets and economic growth.

However, the question remains as to how institutions bring about these changes in labor markets. Future investigations could focus on specific institutional arrangements that promote flexible and adaptable labor markets, thereby facilitating structural change and economic growth. Understanding this mechanism could provide valuable insights into the role of institutions in long-run economic development.

The role of institutions in shaping long-run economic growth is further emphasized when considering the impact of colonialism. According to Austin (2008), African trade, including the Atlantic one, reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe (Austin_2008: p.24). This suggests that the institutions established during colonization had a significant and lasting impact on the economic growth of these regions.

Bryant (2006) supports this notion, stating that European fusion of mercantilist imperialism with a technologically driven capitalistic transformation provided more than adequate environments for the growth of commercial exchange (Bryant_2006: p.13). This implies that the institutions established by colonizers facilitated economic growth in their colonies, further supporting the argument that institutions are fundamental to long-run economic development.

However, it's important to note that not all institutions imposed during colonialism had positive effects on economic growth. For instance, Austin (2008) argues that the imposition of forced labor and restrictions on trade limited Africa's ability to participate in global markets and hinder its long-term economic development (Austin_2008: p.25).

While the argument posits that institutions are the primary driver of long-run economic growth, some scholars challenge this perspective by focusing on technological advancements as a significant factor. Clark and Jacks (2007) contend that the coal industry's response to increased demand during the Industrial Revolution was facilitated by technological advancements rather than institutional changes (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.4). They argue that the cliometric account of the coal industry's ventilation improvements played a crucial role in enabling the industry to meet rising demands (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.4). Furthermore, they suggest that these advancements were essential for the industry's ability to adapt and grow (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.3, p.21, p.27).

Similarly, Bryceson (2002) highlights how technological advancements have influenced economic activities in various communities. He observes that women's economic roles have been affected by technological changes, with men's labor time and economic returns from cash cropping diminishing due to these advancements (Bryceson_2002: p.9). Moreover, he notes that in some areas, young households with restricted land access have resorted to non-agricultural economic activities or migrant labor as a result of technological changes (Bryceson_2002: p.11). These findings suggest that technological advancements can significantly impact economic growth and may challenge the assertion that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth.

Despite the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, some scholars argue that economic shocks and structural changes play significant roles in driving growth (Bryceson_2002: p.11). Two such counterarguments come from Feinstein_1998 and Humphries_2013, both of which challenge the thesis by focusing on economic shocks and their impact on economic growth during specific periods.

Feinstein_1998 critiques the assertion by pointing out that in rural areas, significant changes in farming techniques did not occur until the 1850s and 1860s, which combined with migration and emigration helped disperse labor gluts (Feinstein_1998: p.28). Furthermore, Feinstein argues that the long plateau of economic growth in the eighteenth century was consistent with other economic, political, and demographic indicators (Feinstein_1998: p.2), suggesting that institutions may not have been the primary driver of growth during this period.

Humphries_2013 also challenges the thesis by focusing on the division of labor, workshop production, and the eventual emergence of the factory system as key drivers of eighteenth-century economic growth (Humphries_2013: p.18). Humphries further argues that the relative needs of women and children in the workforce were significant factors in determining economic growth during this period (Humphries_2013: p.11, p.19), suggesting that institutions may not have been the primary driver of growth in these cases either.

The debate on the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth becomes more nuanced when considering the case of Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA). As Broadberry & Gardner (2022) point out, the lack of data before 1950 has excluded SSA from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. They argue that understanding the extent to which there are still risks of economic shrinkage in SSA is crucial for determining whether its growth provides a path to convergence (Broadberry & Gardner, 2022: p.1, p.18).

The British classical economists and government thought that institutions were key drivers of long-run growth and distribution trends (Lindert & Williamson, 1985: p.1). However, Lindert & Williamson (1985) also suggest that the task of explaining long-run distribution trends reconnects with the search for explanations of growth itself. They argue that policymakers often need to balance economic growth and equality, which requires an enormous sample of long-term national data (Lindert & Williamson, 1985: p.1).

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) complicate the argument by suggesting that initial conditions or factor endowments could have profound and enduring impacts on long-run growth. They argue that the divergence in paths can be traced back to the achievement of sustained economic growth by the United States and Canada during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, while others did not manage to attain this goal until late in the nineteenth century (Sokoloff & Engerman, 2000: p.12).

Skocpol & Sommers (1980) add nuance by arguing that structural change correlates with growth. They suggest that comparing cases reveals particularities and increases the "visibility" of one structure by contrasting it with another (Skocpol & Sommers, 1980: p.8, p.9). However, they also note that in Skocpol's States and Social Revolutions only the "positive" cases of social revolution receive extensive discussion (Skocpol & Sommers, 1980: p.13).

These sources complicate the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by suggesting that initial conditions, factor endowments, and structural change may also play significant roles in determining economic growth. Furthermore, they highlight the importance of understanding the extent to which there are still risks of economic shrinkage in SSA and the need for an enormous sample of long-term national data to balance economic growth and equality.

The works of Komlos (1998), Austin (2007), and Allen et al. (2011) provide nuanced perspectives on the relationship between institutions, industrialization, and economic growth. These scholars complicate the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by highlighting various factors that influenced industrialization and growth during different periods.

Komlos (1998) emphasizes the importance of understanding the conceptualization of modern economic growth and the Industrial Revolution, noting that these phenomena were not uniformly defined or experienced across regions and time periods. He also points out that biological growth processes were significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic conditions during the Industrial Revolution.

Austin (2007) discusses the role of transportation infrastructure, specifically railways, in facilitating industrialization and economic growth. He argues that until the mid-1920s, railways had a significant advantage over trucks due to their lower cost. However, he also notes that not all capital-deepening and TFP-improving forces of modern economic growth were at work in certain regions, such as Ghana, during this period.

Allen et al. (2011) examine income disparities between China, Japan, and Europe during the early twentieth century. They find that these disparities were due to long-run stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe. Additionally, they highlight the role of urbanization and rising real wages for workers in Leipzig as factors contributing to the rapid growth of the German economy.

Together, these works suggest that while institutions play a crucial role in promoting economic growth, other factors such as transportation infrastructure, income disparities, and urbanization also significantly influence industrialization and long-run economic growth. The influence of colonialism on institutions and their impact on long-run economic growth is a complex issue, as evidenced by various studies. Frankema (2012) suggests that colonial education played a significant role in explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa, with former British colonies growing faster due to their educational lead (Frankema_2012: p.2). However, the degree of British "liberalism" was endogenous to the local political and economic context (Frankema_2012: p.19). This implies that while colonial education may have been a factor in economic growth, its effectiveness varied depending on the specific circumstances of each colony.

Hopkins (2019) underscores the importance of understanding the economic and political changes that occurred during the period of colonization. He argues that postmodernism's focus on poverty and Western impositions may have limited the diversity of African viewpoints in research, potentially overshadowing the impact of other factors such as food exports (Hopkins_2019: p.13). Asthe economic base appeared to crumble, the superstructure assumed a new importance (Hopkins_2019: p.9). This suggests that while the economic base may have declined during colonialism, institutions and policies remained significant in shaping long-term growth patterns.

Stoler (1989) argues that colonizers lived in "imagined communities" that were consciously created to overcome economic and social disparities (Stoler_1989: p.5). This implies that the institutions established during colonialism may have been designed to serve the interests of the colonizers, potentially shaping long-term growth patterns in ways that benefited them more than the colonized population.

These studies complicate the relationship between institutions, industrialization, and economic growth by highlighting the role of colonialism and its impact on institutions. Further examination of these factors and their interactions could provide valuable insights into the complex relationship between institutions, industrialization, and long-run economic growth.

The relationship between institutions, industrialization, and long-run economic growth is a topic of significant interest among scholars, as evidenced by the works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959). Both authors acknowledge the crucial role of institutions in shaping economic outcomes.

Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) emphasizes the impact of labor-tied institutions, arguing that such arrangements can hinder competition among employers, potentially hindering growth. Conversely, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) highlights how Western influence brought about changes in economic institutions, particularly in the context of slave dealing and status acquisition.

However, both authors diverge in their focus. Domar primarily focuses on labor-tied institutions and their implications for competition, while Bohannan explores the broader impact of Western influence on African societies. Both works also touch upon the role of colonialism indirectly, with Domar (Domar_1970: p.16) referencing Phillips' work on the economic cost of slaveholding in the cotton belt, which could be seen as a form of colonial exploitation.

Despite these insights, several aspects of the relationship between institutions, industrialization, and long-run economic growth remain unresolved. For instance, the specific mechanisms through which institutions drive growth are not fully understood. Additionally, the role of cultural factors in shaping institutions and their impact on economic development is an area that requires further exploration.
