LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question in the literature review on institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is whether institutional structures significantly influence a region's economic development over time. This question holds significant implications for understanding historical and contemporary economic disparities between regions, particularly between Western Europe and Africa.

Several authors support the thesis that institutions play a crucial role in determining long-term economic growth. For instance, Kuznets (1973) argues that the succession of technological innovations characteristic of modern economic growth is closely linked to the social innovations necessary for adaptation, which are themselves influenced by institutional structures (Kuznets_1973: p.6). North & Weingast (1989) also support this view, demonstrating how representative government institutions, such as multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, were essential for enabling economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

On the other hand, Acemoglu et al. (2001) contend that certain institutions can hinder investment and economic progress. They argue that detrimental institutions were responsible for variations in economic performance across regions (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27).

Other authors complicate the thesis by suggesting that while institutions are important, they should not be considered as the sole determinant of long-run economic growth. For example, Austin (2008) endorses the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history, revealing relationships that recur due to path dependency rather than institutions being the fundamental cause (Austin_2008: p.1). Similarly, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) suggest that data limitations have hindered understanding the role of institutions in Sub-Saharan Africa's economic growth before 1950 (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1).

The stakes of this debate are high, as understanding the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting economic development in regions lagging behind. The literature review continues to explore the nuances and qualifications surrounding this central question, shedding light on the complex interplay between institutions, historical context, and economic growth. (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3)

The relationship between institutions and technological advancements is a crucial factor in understanding long-term economic growth. According to Kuznets (1973), technological innovations are major drivers of both economic and social structure changes, which in turn contribute to economic growth (Kuznets_1973: p.6). Mokyr (2005) supports this idea by suggesting that the change in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West can be attributed to developments in the intellectual realm concerning "useful knowledge" (Mokyr_2005: p.4).

Furthermore, North & Weingast (1989) argue that representative government institutions with multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs are essential for economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28). This perspective is supported by Ogilvie (2007), who emphasizes the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework, suggesting that the failure of one institution can impact the overall economy (Ogilvie_2007: p.27).

The relationship between institutions and labor markets is further elucidated in various works, offering substantial support for the thesis that institutions are a primary driver of long-run economic growth. Allen (2001) emphasizes the central role of wages and prices in economic development, suggesting that their trends can provide insights into the pace of economic development and standard of living (Allen_2001: p.1).

Broadberry & Gupta (2006) highlight the service sector's crucial role in economic growth during the early modern period, indicating that it played a significant part in economic expansion as it does today (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.9). Furthermore, they argue that observations based on grain wages alone may not accurately reflect levels of economic development across regions (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.24).

Similarly, Bryant (2006) suggests that the fusion of mercantilist imperialism with capitalistic transformation in Europe contributed to structural changes that likely influenced labor markets and economic growth (Bryant_2006: p.5). This perspective underscores the importance of institutions in shaping economic development, as the unique combination of mercantilism and capitalism is a defining feature of European history.

Broadberry et al. (2015) provide insights into India's long-run economic performance, noting that the country's economic growth since the late sixteenth century has been a subject of ongoing debate (BroadberryEtal_2015: p.2). The authors' work suggests that understanding institutional structures and their evolution is crucial for comprehending India's economic trajectory over time.

The fundamental role of institutions in long-run economic growth is further underscored by the findings of Acemoglu and his colleagues (AcemogluEtAl_2001, 2002). In their studies, they demonstrate that more fundamental institutional structures significantly impact property rights, investment, and overall economic performance.

In particular, Acemoglu et al. (2001) reveal that institutions play a crucial role in determining the distribution of economic prosperity over time. They argue that differences in mortality rates are not the main cause of variation in institutions, contrary to some prevailing theories. Instead, they emphasize that these institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). Furthermore, their results show a large effect of institutions on economic performance, suggesting that understanding institutional structures is essential for comprehending long-term economic growth (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19).

Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2002) highlight the importance of institutions in explaining the reversal of economic prosperity over the past 500 years. They question whether this change is related to institutions and offer evidence supporting their claim (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5). Moreover, they note that this shift in economic prosperity distribution is a first-order fact, underscoring the significance of institutional structures for understanding both economic and political development (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49).

These findings collectively support the argument that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. By influencing property rights, investment, and overall economic performance, institutions play a critical role in shaping a nation's economic trajectory over time. Further research should focus on understanding the mechanisms through which institutional structures evolve and their impact on various aspects of economic development.

The assertion that institutions are the primary cause of long-term economic growth faces challenge from scholars who emphasize the role of technology and ideas in driving economic expansion. For instance, McCloskey (2015) argues that in France, economic growth was hindered for two centuries due to conservative ideologies and a lack of interest in economic development, despite the examples of prosperous regions like Holland and England (p.9). Similarly, Simson (2019) suggests that while state expenditure expanded during periods of high economic growth in Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda, it was not necessarily the institutions themselves but external factors that influenced their economic trajectories (p.25, p.4).

Moreover, Humphries (2013) contends that structural change, such as the shift from workshop production to factory systems, played a significant role in 18th-century economic growth, rather than institutions alone (p.18). These perspectives challenge the notion that institutions are solely responsible for long-term economic growth by highlighting the influence of technology and ideas on economic expansion.

However, it is essential to consider that while these factors may contribute to economic growth, they do not necessarily negate the importance of institutions. Further research could explore how institutions interact with technological advancements and ideological shifts to shape a nation's economic trajectory over time.

The relationship between institutions and technology in shaping long-run economic growth is a complex interplay that extends beyond the fundamental role of institutions. The works of various scholars provide nuanced perspectives on this intricate dynamic.

Komlos (1998) emphasizes the significance of physical stature during the Industrial Revolution, suggesting that biological growth processes were influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic conditions (Komlos_1998: p.16). This implies that technological advancements could have played a role in shaping institutional development and economic growth.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) discuss the intertwined nature of economic growth and equality, arguing that policymakers have often had to balance between the two (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1). This suggests that technological advancements may influence institutional development in ways that affect both inequality and economic growth.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) explore how initial conditions, including factor endowments, could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4). This implies that technological advancements, which often result from specific factor endowments, may shape institutional development and economic growth over time.

Koepke & Baten (2005) investigate the relationship between climate and human living standards, suggesting that climate could have influenced technological advancements and subsequently economic growth (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.20). This further complicates the relationship between institutions and technology in shaping long-run economic growth.

Frankema (2012) argues that colonial education, a form of technological transfer, is key to explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa (Frankema_2012: p.2). This suggests that institutional development and economic growth may be shaped by the technology transferred through colonization.

Hopkins (2019) discusses the impact of postmodernism on the study of long-run economic and political change, suggesting that it may have limited the diversity of independent African viewpoints (Hopkins_2019: p.4). This implies that technological advancements in research methods could potentially shape institutional development and economic growth by influencing the perspectives and insights brought to bear on these issues.

The perspectives presented by Austin (2008) complicate the assertion that institutions are the sole cause of long-term economic growth. While acknowledging the appeal of the argument's logical economy, Austin argues that trade, such as the Atlantic trade, may have reinforced rather than originated Africa's economic disparity compared to Western Europe (Austin_2008: p.24). This suggests that while institutions play a significant role in economic growth, other factors like trade relationships and their historical context should also be considered.

Moreover, Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) highlight the complexity of this issue by emphasizing the interconnectedness of various players in the market, stating that "They are all players in the market, the 'strategy' of one affecting the 'strategy' of each of the others" (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.21). This implies that institutions and economic growth may be influenced not only by internal factors but also by external interactions and strategies.

The thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is further nuanced by examining the historical context of Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA) and the early modern economy.

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) suggest that SSA has been largely absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth due to a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950. They emphasize that understanding the extent of risks in SSA's past economic progress is crucial for determining whether its current growth can lead to convergence (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.1, p.18). This indicates that the historical data gap in SSA may complicate the argument that institutions are the sole determinant of long-run economic growth, as other factors such as data availability could also play a significant role.

Similarly, van Zanden (1999) challenges the consensus view from the 1960s and 1970s that the early modern economy was unable to generate economic growth before the industrial revolution. He highlights that in the mid-eighteenth century, there still existed a negative link between output measures of the standard of living and the level of economic development (vanZanden_1999: p.1, p.13). This suggests that while institutions might have been crucial for long-run economic growth, other factors such as technological advancements or resource availability could also have played a role in economic growth during the early modern period.

The evidence from Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) provides tangential insights into the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth, shedding light on both convergence and divergence among scholars.

On the one hand, both authors acknowledge the significance of institutions in economic development. Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) notes that with labor tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, indicating the importance of institutions in facilitating competition and growth. Similarly, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.10) suggests that the Western impact was not limited to economic institutions, implying a recognition of their role in development.

On the other hand, both authors also hint at potential limitations or complexities in the relationship between institutions and growth. Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) discusses the case of the Russian peasants' enserfment, suggesting that institutions might not always be beneficial for economic growth. Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) focuses on slave dealing and the acquisition of status as aspects of Western impact, which may not directly contribute to economic growth.

However, what remains unresolved is the extent to which institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. While both Domar and Bohannan acknowledge their importance, they do not explicitly argue that institutions are the sole or primary driver of economic development. Furthermore, neither author discusses other potential factors such as technological advancements or resource availability in relation to institutions, leaving room for further exploration and debate on this topic.
