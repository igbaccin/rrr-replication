The central question under investigation in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth, a proposition that has garnered substantial attention among economists and historians. The stakes are high, as understanding the root causes of economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting development and alleviating poverty in various regions.

Among the sources examined, there is a diverse range of perspectives on the central question. Some, such as North & Weingast (1989), support the thesis that institutions play a crucial role in fostering economic growth by providing the necessary framework for political freedom and market development. For instance, they argue that "the fundamental institutions of representative government...are intimately related to the struggle for control over property rights" (North & Weingast_1989: p.28).

On the other hand, authors like Austin (2008) complicate the picture by suggesting that while institutions are important, they may not be solely responsible for long-term economic growth. For example, Austin (Austin_2008: p.26) notes that "institutions as 'the' fundamental cause of long-run economic growth" is an attractive conclusion, but argues that historical context and path dependency must also be considered.

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) also add nuance to the debate by highlighting the lack of data on pre-1950 economic performance in Sub-Saharan Africa, which has hindered its inclusion in recent discussions about long-term growth. This lack of data makes it difficult to determine whether institutions are the primary drivers of growth in this region.

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) further complicate the issue by focusing on the role of women in economic growth and development, suggesting that understanding how their labor has been historically subsumed under household production is essential for creating effective policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles.

In contrast, sources like Kuznets (1973) and Acemoglu et al. (2001) offer more explicit support for the thesis that institutions are critical to long-term economic growth. For example, Kuznets (Kuznets_1973: p.6) posits that "the succession of tech-innovations characteristic of modern economic growth and the social innovations that provide the needed adjustments are major factors affecting economic and social structure."

The debate is far from settled, with some sources supporting the thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth, while others complicate the picture by suggesting that historical context, path dependency, and gender roles must also be considered. The argument continues as scholars continue to explore the complex interplay between institutions, economic growth, and development.

The role of institutions in promoting long-run economic growth is further substantiated by several scholars, who highlight their influence on economic structure and societal changes. Kuznets (1973) posits that rapid changes in economic structure are associated with significant shifts in other aspects of society, such as family roles and urbanization (Kuznets_1973: p.4). He also emphasizes the importance of technological innovations in driving economic growth and their accompanying social innovations (Kuznets_1973: p.6).

North & Weingast (1989) support this perspective, focusing on the political factors underlying economic growth and market development. They argue that representative government institutions with multiple veto points and a primacy of common law courts over economic affairs are crucial for economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.2).

Mokyr (2005) extends this argument by suggesting that the change in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West can be explained through developments in the intellectual realm concerning "useful knowledge" (Mokyr_2005: p.4). He emphasizes the importance of individualism, man-made formal law, corporatism, self-governance, and rules determined through an institutionalized process for economic growth (Mokyr_2005: p.56).

North (1989) also supports the argument by discussing how institutions can lower transaction costs and contribute to economic growth (North_1989: p.9). He highlights that the tracts of an economy may be nested, and alterations in them could have significant consequences for economic growth (North_1989: p.6).

Ogilvie (2007) reinforces these findings by pointing out the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework, suggesting that their failure to adapt may lead to economic stagnation (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). He also emphasizes the importance of geographical characteristics in shaping economic development (Ogilvie_2007: p.2).

deVries (1994) supports this perspective by focusing on the role of urban networks and commercial interactions in regional economic development, particularly during the early modern period (deVries_1994: p.5). He also highlights the need for renewed attention to the economic history of the household or family unit (deVries_1994: p.8).

These sources collectively provide strong evidence that institutions play a fundamental role in long-run economic growth by shaping economic structure, promoting technological innovations, and influencing societal changes. The interplay between institutions, economic growth, and development remains a rich area for further exploration.

The impact of institutions on economic growth, particularly in the agricultural sector, is a significant factor that supports the thesis that institutions are fundamental to long-run economic growth. Acemoglu et al. (2001) argue that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress, as seen in their analysis of property rights institutions and their effect on economic performance (p. 27). This study underscores the importance of institutions in shaping the economic structure, which can either hinder or promote growth.

Acemoglu et al. (2002) further highlight the role of institutions in long-run development by noting that the distribution of economic prosperity has changed over time, and this change is related to institutional changes (p. 5). They emphasize the importance of understanding these institutional shifts for a comprehensive understanding of economic and political development over the past 500 years (p. 49).

Broadberry et al. (2015) support this argument by discussing India's long-run economic performance, which has been a subject of controversy due to its varied results. They suggest that the population estimates in their study provide a starting point for understanding India's economic history and the role institutions may have played in shaping it (p. 4).

Structural change, a key aspect of economic development, has been found to correlate with growth (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.2). This shift in economic focus from agriculture to manufacturing and services is often associated with increased productivity and higher income levels. The authors argue that the timing and pace of this transition vary significantly across countries, suggesting that institutions play a crucial role in determining these patterns.

For instance, the United States and Canada experienced structural change during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, which led to sustained economic growth (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.12). On the other hand, countries like India did not achieve this growth until late in the nineteenth century. This divergence can be attributed to differences in institutional structures that influenced the pace and direction of structural change.

The authors also emphasize the need to consider initial conditions or factor endowments when studying economic growth (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4). They argue that these factors, along with institutions, can have profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth. In this context, institutions are not just the rules of the game but also the referees who enforce them, creating a stable environment conducive to economic development.

Moreover, Sokoloff and Engerman highlight that conventional economic factors, such as factor endowments and geography, have often been overshadowed in explanations for contrasting growth records across societies (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.2). However, they argue that institutions are equally important in understanding these differences.

Critics argue that while institutions play a significant role in economic growth, other factors such as environmental conditions and external influences also have substantial impacts on long-term economic development (Simson_2019: p.4). For instance, in the case of Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda, state expenditure and deficits expanded during a period of high economic growth in the 1960s and 1970s (Simson_2019: p.25). Furthermore, McCloskey (2015) contends that ideologies and ideas, rather than institutions, often determined economic growth patterns in countries like France and Spain during crucial periods (McCloskey_2015: p.8, p.9).

Moreover, Bryceson (2002) asserts that traditional hierarchical orders within communities can significantly influence women's economic and social spheres, potentially hindering their participation in non-agrarian activities or migration for work (Bryceson_2002: p.9). Humphries (2013) also suggests that the division of labor, workshop production, and the emergence of the factory system were crucial drivers of eighteenth-century economic growth, rather than institutions alone (Humphries_2013: p.18).

These sources challenge the notion that institutions are the sole fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by highlighting the importance of environmental conditions, ideologies, and societal structures in shaping economic development trajectories.

The relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth is further nuanced when considering environmental conditions. For instance, Komlos (1998) posits that physical stature during the Industrial Revolution was significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic processes and associated structural changes (p.16). This suggests that while institutions may have played a role in driving economic growth, they were not the sole factor, as environmental conditions also impacted development trajectories.

Temin (2002) supports this notion by arguing that the exhaustion of cheap labor caused economic growth to slow (p.4). Additionally, he contends that recent theories of economic growth should consider the historical context of economic transition, which includes agricultural economies with very few exceptions around 1800 (p.7). This implies that environmental conditions and economic structure may have had a substantial influence on long-term growth patterns.

Similarly, Lindert & Williamson (1985) suggest that policymakers between the 18th and 20th centuries grappled with trade-offs between economic growth and equality, indicating that environmental factors played a role in shaping both economic growth and distribution trends (p.1). Furthermore, they argue that the task of explaining long-run distribution trends rejoins the search for explanations of growth itself (p.8), implying that environmental conditions may have been intertwined with institutional arrangements in determining long-term development trajectories.

Koepke & Baten (2005) also highlight the relationship between climate and human living standards, stating that over the period of the Roman Empire, heights did not increase at all, standing in contrast to the common view that living standards increased during this time due to economic growth (p.16). This demonstrates that environmental conditions can have a significant impact on long-term economic growth, challenging the notion that institutions alone are responsible for driving development.

The literature further complicates the assertion that institutions are the sole cause of long-run economic growth by highlighting the significance of historical data and women's contributions in Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA) and West Africa, respectively.

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) emphasize the lack of historical data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 in SSA, which has excluded it from recent debates about long-run growth (p.1). This lack of data could potentially influence the understanding of SSA's growth patterns and its role in global economic development. Furthermore, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that understanding the risks of shrinking economies is crucial for determining whether recent growth provides a path to convergence (p.18). This suggests that the focus on institutions may overlook other factors such as historical economic patterns and potential risks that could impact long-term growth.

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) underscore the importance of understanding how women's economic contributions have been subsumed under household production, often going unaccounted for in policy-making (p.3). Their work reveals that the contribution of African women to economic growth and development was largely dependent on their husbands' economic provision (p.9, p.19). This highlights how institutions, particularly those related to gender roles, can significantly influence long-run economic growth by shaping women's economic roles and contributions.

These sources complicate the thesis by suggesting that historical data and gender institutions play significant roles in long-run economic growth, potentially reshaping our understanding of the factors driving development. Further exploration of these factors could provide a more nuanced perspective on the relationship between institutions and economic growth.

The relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth is further complicated by the influence of colonialism, as suggested by both Austin (2008) and Allen et al. (2011). Austin (Austin_2008: p.24) argues that trades, even those like the Atlantic trade, reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe. This suggests that colonial institutions may have perpetuated existing economic disparities, rather than being the primary cause of long-run growth.

Allen et al. (AllenEtAl_2011: p.1) support this idea by demonstrating income disparities in the early 20th century, attributing them to long-run stagnation in China and industrialization in Japan and Europe. The authors also highlight that colonial economies, such as those in Africa, may have been negatively impacted by the extraction of resources for European industries (Allen et al. 2011: p.20).

These findings reshape the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Instead, it seems plausible to consider that colonial institutions may have exacerbated existing economic disparities or hindered growth in some regions. The literature on the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth presents a complex narrative, with some scholars emphasizing the fundamental role of institutions while others offer more nuanced perspectives that account for various factors.

Scholars such as Domar (1970) argue that certain institutional arrangements can limit competition among employers, thereby hindering economic growth. For instance, Domar highlights the example of labor being tied to land or owners, which ceases competition among employers (Domar_1970: p.4). On the other hand, Bohannan (1959) suggests that while Western economic institutions played a significant role in shaping African societies, they were not solely responsible for economic growth or decline (Bohannan_1959: p.10).

Both Domar and Bohannan touch upon the idea of institutional arrangements limiting competition, but their focus differs. While Domar focuses on labor-land ties within a specific context, Bohannan discusses the broader impact of Western institutions on African societies.

Despite these insights, several aspects of the relationship between institutions and economic growth remain unresolved. For example, Phillips' work cited by Domar (1970) explores the economic cost of slaveholding in the Cotton Belt, suggesting that certain institutional arrangements can have detrimental effects on economic growth (Domar_1970: p.16). However, it is unclear how such negative impacts can be mitigated or overcome, leaving this question open for further research.

In addition, the role of gender in institutions and economic growth is under-explored in both Domar's and Bohannan's works. Bohannan (1959) notes that the number of women is limited, but does not delve into how this might impact economic growth (Bohannan_1959: p.13). Further research is needed to understand the interplay between gender and institutions in shaping long-term economic development.
