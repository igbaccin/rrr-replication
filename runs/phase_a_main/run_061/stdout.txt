LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question in the literature review on institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is whether institutional factors are indeed the primary driver of sustained economic progress. This question carries significant implications for understanding historical and contemporary economic disparities, informing development policies, and shaping global economic discourse.

The majority of sources support the thesis that institutions play a pivotal role in long-term economic growth. Austin (2008), for instance, argues that institutions are "the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth" (p.26). Similarly, Kuznets (1973) posits that both technological innovations and social adjustments associated with modern economic growth are major factors affecting economic and social structure (p.6).

However, some sources offer critiques and complicate the argument. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) suggest that the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has kept Sub-Saharan Africa out of recent debates about comparative long-run growth (p.1). Stern (2004) critiques the Economic Kuznets Curve hypothesis, which suggests that economic growth leads to environmental degradation but eventually results in improved conditions (p.1).

Other sources add nuance or qualifications to the argument. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) discuss the underrepresentation of women's economic contributions and the need for policies that strengthen their economic roles (p.3). Simson (2019) highlights spending inefficiencies, leakages, and costly delays to reform as factors that may have hindered economic growth in certain regions (p.4).

The debate continues as scholars grapple with the complex interplay between institutions, economic growth, and other factors such as technological innovation, environmental degradation, and gender roles. The stakes are high, as understanding the fundamental causes of long-run economic growth is crucial for informing development policies, reducing disparities, and promoting sustainable global economic progress.

The significance of institutions in fostering long-run economic growth is underscored by various scholars, providing robust evidence that supports the assertion. According to Austin (2008), institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth, as they shape and influence the development trajectories of societies over extended periods (Austin_2008: p.26). This theoretical conclusion is further bolstered by the argument that institutions reinforce existing economic positions, such as the case of Africa's relatively poor economic standing compared to Western Europe (Austin_2008: p.24).

Kuznets (1973) also contributes to this discourse by highlighting the interplay between institutional changes and economic growth. The scholar emphasizes that rapid changes in economic structure, often associated with modern economic growth, are accompanied by similar adjustments in other aspects of society (Kuznets_1973: p.4). Furthermore, Kuznets (1973) points out the existence of non-conventional costs of economic growth, which underscores the importance of institutions in mitigating these negative consequences (Kuznets_1973: p.5).

Acemoglu et al. (2001) delve deeper into the role of institutions in shaping long-run economic performance. They argue that more fundamental institutions, such as property rights and political institutions, play a crucial role in determining a country's economic growth (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). The authors also assert that differences in mortality rates are not the main cause of variation in institutions, further emphasizing the significance of institutional factors in economic development (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3).

North and Weingast (1989) focus on the political aspects of institutions and their impact on economic growth. They argue that the political environment, including the enforcement mechanisms and the ability to change rules, significantly influences economic growth and market development (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). The authors also illustrate how the absence of constraints, as in early modern Spain, led to economic conditions that retarded long-run economic growth (North&Weingast_1989: p.7).

Mokyr (2005) discusses the role of intellectual developments and institutions in fostering economic growth in the West. He suggests that changes in the intellectual realm, particularly the growth of economic reasonableness, were essential for the West's economic superiority (Mokyr_2005: p.4). Mokyr also emphasizes that many elements of a progressive society, such as individualism and self-governance, are rooted in institutions (Mokyr_2005: p.56).

North (1989) contributes to the discourse by focusing on the role of institutions in reducing transaction costs, which, in turn, promotes economic growth (North_1989: p.9). The scholar also underscores the importance of understanding the institutional environment in examining economic growth and development (North_1989: p.1).

Institutional impact on economic performance has been challenged by scholars who argue that other factors, such as technological advancements and resource availability, may play a more significant role in driving long-term growth. Stern (2004) posits that economic growth might not always lead to environmental degradation in the long run, contradicting the notion that institutions are the primary cause of economic growth (Stern_2004: p.1). Similarly, McCloskey (2015) contends that ideologies and ideas, rather than institutions, have significantly influenced economic growth in certain regions (McCloskey_2015: p.9).

Simson (2019) argues that spending inefficiencies, leakages, and costly delays to reform may not have been the sole reasons for the slowdown in economic growth in some regions during specific periods (Simson_2019: p.4). This suggests that institutional factors might not be the primary drivers of economic growth in all contexts.

Moreover, Humphries (2013) and vanZanden (1999) question the consensus view that early modern economies were unable to generate sustainable economic growth before the industrial revolution (Humphries_2013: p.18; vanZanden_1999: p.1). This challenges the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth by suggesting that other factors, such as technological advancements and resource availability, might have played a more significant role in driving economic growth during this period.

The perspective of Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicates the assertion that institutions are the primary cause of long-term economic growth by highlighting the significant data gap regarding Sub-Saharan Africa's economic performance before 1950. They emphasize that the region has been absent from recent debates due to this lack of data, which raises questions about the generalizability of the thesis across all regions (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.1).

Moreover, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) point out that African economic performance has historically been markedly worse than other regions (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.1), suggesting that factors beyond institutions might have played a more significant role in shaping Africa's economic trajectory. However, they also acknowledge the need for understanding the extent to which historical risks of economic shrinkage still persist in the region (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.18).

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) contribute nuance to the argument by focusing on women's contributions to economic growth and development in Africa. They argue that understanding the processes by which women's labor has been subsumed under household production is crucial for formulating policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.3). Their work underscores the importance of considering gender dynamics when evaluating the role of institutions in driving long-term economic growth.

Komlos (1998) complicates the thesis by emphasizing the interplay between institutions, economic processes, and structural changes during the Industrial Revolution. He suggests that physical stature during this period was significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic conditions, implying that the relationship between institutions and economic growth might be more complex than previously thought (Komlos_1998: p.16).

Temin (2002) contributes to the discussion by arguing that the exhaustion of cheap labor caused economic growth to slow, suggesting that resource endowments and technological advancements may also play essential roles in long-term economic growth (Temin_2002: p.4).

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) complicate the thesis by exploring the possibility that initial conditions or factor endowments could have profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth. They argue that contrasting records in growth can be traced back to the achievement of sustained economic growth by the United States and Canada during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, while others did not manage to attain this goal until late in the nineteenth century (Sokoloff & Engerman_2000: p.12).

Lastly, Koepke & Baten (2005) add nuance to the argument by investigating the relationship between climate and human living standards over time. Their findings suggest that climate may have played a more significant role in shaping economic growth than previously thought, potentially complicating the thesis that institutions are the primary cause of long-term economic growth (Koepke & Baten_2005: p.20).

The literature reviewed suggests a complex interplay between institutions, labor, and economic growth. Scholars such as Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) touch upon the role of institutions in shaping economic growth, albeit tangentially. Domar's work emphasizes the impact of labor-land ties on competition among employers, potentially affecting economic growth (Domar_1970: p.4). On the other hand, Bohannan discusses how Western economic institutions influenced African societies, although not exclusively focusing on economic growth (Bohannan_1959: p.10).

Bryceson's work (2002) provides insights into livelihood diversification and its correlation with economic growth, particularly in the context of land scarcity and labor shifts. Bryceson highlights how changing labor patterns can impact both men's and women's roles within communities, potentially affecting overall economic growth (Bryceson_2002: p.9).

While these scholars acknowledge the role of institutions in shaping economic growth, they do not uniformly assert that institutions are the primary cause of long-term economic growth. The literature suggests that climate, labor, and other factors may also play significant roles. Furthermore, the impact of institutional changes on economic growth remains a complex issue, with various factors interacting in intricate ways.

The question of whether institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth remains unresolved due to the multifaceted nature of economic development and the interplay between various factors such as climate, labor, and institutions. Future research should aim to disentangle these factors and their respective contributions to long-term economic growth.
