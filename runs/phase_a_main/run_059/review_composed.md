The central question under investigation in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, as posited by some scholars (Austin_2008: p.26). This question holds significant implications for understanding the historical and contemporary economic disparities between regions, particularly with regards to Sub-Saharan Africa (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1).

On one hand, proponents of this view argue that institutions play a crucial role in fostering economic growth by providing the necessary framework for property rights, contract enforcement, and political stability (North&Weingast_1989: p.2; AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3). For instance, North & Weingast (1989) demonstrate how representative government institutions with multiple veto points can facilitate economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28). Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2001) highlight the detrimental impact of institutions that are unfavorable to investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27).

On the other hand, there are scholars who complicate this perspective by pointing out the nuances and qualifications that need to be considered. For example, Austin (2008) acknowledges the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history, revealing relationships that recur due to path dependencies rather than institutions being the sole cause (Austin_2008: p.1). Similarly, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) argue that understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution becomes subsumed under household production is crucial for policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3).

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) further complicate the discussion by noting the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 in Sub-Saharan Africa, which has led to its absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). This underscores the need for more comprehensive historical data to fully understand the role of institutions in economic growth.

The role of institutions in fostering long-run economic growth is a critical aspect that has been extensively studied by various scholars. North & Weingast (1989) assert that the fundamental institutions of representative government, such as an explicit set of multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are intimately related to the struggle for control over economic growth and political freedom (p.2). They further emphasize that these institutions played a necessary role in making possible economic growth and political freedom (p.30).

Acemoglu et al. (2001) also support this notion, highlighting that more fundamental institutions significantly impact property rights and investment, thereby influencing economic progress (p.27). Their findings demonstrate a large effect of institutions on economic performance (p.19).

Mokyr (2005) discusses the development of progressive societies, suggesting that elements such as individualism, man-made formal law, corporatism, self governance, and rules determined through an institutionalized process were essential for economic growth (p.56). He also emphasizes the importance of intellectual developments in explaining the change in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West (p.54).

North (1989) focuses on centralized enforcement mechanisms, asserting that these mechanisms significantly affect transaction costs, security rights, and ultimately economic growth (p.9). Acemoglu et al. (2002) also underscore the importance of institutions in understanding economic development over the past 500 years (p.49).

Structural change in economic systems, a key component of modern economic growth, is significantly linked to societal transformations (Kuznets_1973: p.4). Kuznets (1973) highlights that these changes are not only associated with high aggregate growth rates but also with rapid adjustments in various aspects of society, such as family structure and urbanization. This suggests that institutions play a crucial role in facilitating such transformations, thereby contributing to long-term economic growth.

In addition, the succession of technological innovations characterizing modern economic growth is intertwined with social innovations that provide the necessary adjustments (Kuznets_1973: p.6). This underscores the importance of institutions in managing and implementing these technological advancements effectively, thereby promoting economic development.

Moreover, Ogilvie (2007) emphasizes the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework, suggesting that the failure of one institution can potentially impact other institutions within the system (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). This interdependence underscores the importance of robust and efficient institutions in ensuring sustained economic growth.

The role of colonialism in shaping institutions and their impact on long-term economic growth is a subject of significant debate among scholars. Austin (2008) contends that institutions, rather than trade or geography, are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. He posits that trades, such as the Atlantic one, reinforced Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe and parts of Asia, implying that colonialism may have hindered institutional development in colonized regions (Austin_2008: p.24).

Similarly, Bryant (2006) emphasizes the fusion of mercantilist imperialism with a technologically driven capitalistic transformation as a key difference between Europe and other civilizations during colonial times. This fusion provided environments conducive to commercial exchange, suggesting that European colonization may have facilitated the development of institutions that fostered economic growth in colonized regions (Bryant_2006: p.5).

However, it is important to note that the impact of colonialism on institutions and subsequent economic growth was not uniform across all colonized regions. The specific nature of colonial rule, the resistance of native populations, and the pre-existing institutional structures in these regions all played a role in shaping the outcome (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). Therefore, while colonialism may have influenced institutions in some cases, it is essential to consider the complex interplay of factors that shaped the long-term economic growth trajectories of colonized regions.

Technological advancements, a critical factor in economic growth, are argued to have been constrained by institutions in some cases, challenging the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.4). The coal industry during the Industrial Revolution serves as an example, where technological advancements enabled the industry to respond to increases in demand only because of these innovations (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.4). This suggests that while institutions may have influenced the adoption and dissemination of technology, they did not necessarily drive its creation or development.

Moreover, Bryceson's (2002) research on livelihood diversification in Africa reveals that traditional hierarchical orders within communities can significantly impact women's economic and social spheres. In many cases, women are primarily involved in agrarian family work, and their roles as family provisioners have been undermined by changes in labor time and economic returns from cash-cropping (Bryceson_2002: p.9). This evidence suggests that institutions may not always foster the structural change necessary for long-term economic growth, as they can limit access to certain economic activities and resources.

Despite the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, some sources challenge this notion by highlighting the impact of economic shocks on growth. Feinstein (1998) critiques the thesis by suggesting that certain historical periods experienced a plateau in economic growth due to factors such as wars and political instability (Feinstein_1998: p.5). Additionally, Feinstein points out that during these periods, unemployment was high and structural change was limited (Feinstein_1998: p.2).

Similarly, Humphries (2013) argues that the division of labor, workshop production, and the emergence of the factory system were key drivers of eighteenth-century economic growth, rather than institutions (Humphries_2013: p.18). Furthermore, Humphries suggests that the relative needs of women and children in the workforce may have hindered structural change and economic growth (Humphries_2013: p.11, p.19).

These sources challenge the thesis by suggesting that economic shocks and structural factors, rather than institutions, play a significant role in long-run economic growth. However, further investigation is needed to determine the extent to which these factors interact with institutions and contribute to economic growth over the long term.

Despite the prevailing view that institutions are the primary drivers of long-run economic growth, recent findings suggest that other factors may also play a significant role in this process. For instance, (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1) highlights the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 in Sub-Saharan Africa, which has been absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. This data gap could potentially influence our understanding of the role of institutions in long-term economic growth in this region.

Moreover, (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.8) argues that the task of explaining long-run distribution trends rejoins the search for the explanations of growth itself, complicating the relationship between inequality and economic growth over the long term. Similarly, (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1) points out that the British classical economists and government thought that institutions played a crucial role in economic growth, suggesting a historical perspective on the link between institutions and growth.

(Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4) emphasizes the importance of initial conditions or factor endowments in shaping long-run economic growth. They argue that unique conditions within individual countries could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-term economic development, potentially challenging the exclusive focus on institutions as the primary driver of growth.

Lastly, (Skocpol&Sommers_1980: p.14) presents a case study comparing Japan and China to establish that these countries differed in terms of the Western intrusions to which they were subjected. This comparison reveals particularities of the cases, complicating our understanding of how external factors interact with institutions to influence long-run economic growth.

The evidence provided by Komlos (1998) complicates the understanding of the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth by highlighting the influence of short-term fluctuations in economic processes during the Industrial Revolution on physical stature, population growth, and income inequality (Komlos_1998: p.16, p.17). Austin's (2007) research adds nuance to this argument by demonstrating that the cheaper cost of railways over lorries in the mid-1920s played a significant role in economic growth, and that population growth was faster after 1918 (Austin_2007: p.8, p.12).

Allen et al. (2011) further complicate this perspective by showing the correlation between structural change and growth, as seen in the rapid growth of the German economy translating into rising real wages for workers in Leipzig (AllenEtAl_2011: p.20). These findings suggest that while institutions are fundamental to long-run economic growth, other factors such as technological advancements, population dynamics, and cost differences between transportation methods also play crucial roles.

The evidence provided by these sources demonstrates that the impact of external factors on institutions and, consequently, long-term economic growth is more complex than previously thought. Further investigation into the interplay between these various elements could lead to a deeper understanding of the mechanisms driving economic development over time.

The interplay between institutions and colonialism significantly influences long-term economic growth, as suggested by various scholars. For instance, Frankema (2012) posits that British colonies exhibited faster growth due to their educational advantage, although the level of "liberalism" in these colonies was influenced by local political and economic contexts (Frankema_2012: p.19). Additionally, the control over fertile areas played a crucial role in the British colonies' economic success (Frankema_2012: p.2).

Hopkins (2019) echoes this sentiment by emphasizing that the focus on totalism and essentialism in African Economic History may have inhibited the study of long-run economic and political change, as well as limiting the diversity that independent African viewpoints would otherwise have supplied (Hopkins_2019: p.4). Moreover, Hopkins suggests that food exports made minimal contributions to economic growth during the period of the slave trade in West Africa (Hopkins_2019: p.13).

Stoler (1989) argues that colonizers lived in "imagined communities" designed to overcome economic and social disparities, which could have contributed to the establishment and persistence of certain institutions (Stoler_1989: p.5). Furthermore, Stoler highlights how the plantation economy created a more solid alliance between the plantation elite and the colonial administration (Stoler_1989: p.21).

These findings suggest that the relationship between institutions and economic growth is complex, with colonialism playing a significant role in shaping these relationships. Understanding the intricacies of this relationship could lead to a deeper understanding of long-term economic development in colonized regions.

Institutions, as fundamental drivers of long-run economic growth, have been a subject of extensive discussion among scholars, with Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) providing valuable insights. Both authors agree that institutions play a significant role in shaping economic growth, particularly in colonized regions.

Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) emphasizes the importance of labor mobility as a factor contributing to economic growth. He argues that when labor is tied to land or an owner, competition among employers ceases, potentially stifling growth. On the other hand, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) focuses on the impact of Western institutions, particularly those related to slave dealing and status acquisition, on economic development in colonized regions.

However, both scholars diverge in their focus. Domar's (1970) primary concern is the relationship between labor mobility and economic growth, while Bohannan (1959) examines the broader effects of Western institutions on various aspects of society. This divergence highlights the complexity of the relationship between institutions and economic growth, suggesting that multiple factors must be considered to fully understand this relationship.

Despite their contributions, several issues remain unresolved. For instance, Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) acknowledges that the Russian situation prior to peasants' enserfment corresponds to a case where labor is tied to land or an owner, but he does not explore this connection further. Similarly, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.13) notes the limited number of women in some societies, which could potentially impact economic growth, but does not delve into this aspect.
