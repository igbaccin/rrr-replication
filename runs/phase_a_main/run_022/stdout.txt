The central question in the literature review on institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is whether institutional structures are indeed the primary drivers of economic progress over extended periods. This question carries significant implications for understanding historical and contemporary economic development, particularly in regions such as Africa, where growth rates have been notably lower than other parts of the world.

Supporting this thesis, Austin (2008) emphasizes that institutions play a crucial role in shaping long-term economic growth, pointing out that they influence economic structure and societal aspects. He argues that institutions were key factors in reinforcing Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe and parts of Asia (Austin_2008: p.24). Similarly, Kuznets (1973) notes the association between rapid changes in economic structure and growth, suggesting that institutions provide the necessary adjustments for modern technological advancement (Kuznets_1973: p.6).

On the other hand, Acemoglu et al. (2001) support the argument but add nuance by highlighting that institutional differences can lead to varying economic outcomes. They argue that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate the discussion by pointing out that Sub-Saharan Africa's absence from recent debates about long-run growth is due to a lack of data before 1950, which could potentially shed light on the role of institutions in economic development (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1).

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) further complicate the issue by focusing on the contribution of African women to economic growth and development, suggesting that understanding the processes by which their labor is subsumed under household production could undermine policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). North & Weingast (1989) support the thesis by focusing on political factors underlying economic growth and market development, demonstrating how institutions played a necessary role in making possible economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.2).

The stakes of this debate are high, as understanding the role of institutions in long-run economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting development and reducing poverty in underdeveloped regions. The literature suggests that while institutions play a crucial role in shaping economic outcomes, there are also complexities and nuances to consider, such as the contribution of women's labor and the availability of data for different historical periods. This review sets the stage for further exploration of these issues and their implications for economic development policies.

The significance of institutions in fostering long-run economic growth is further emphasized by various scholars. Kuznets (1973) highlights that economic and social structure undergo profound changes during periods of rapid growth, suggesting a close relationship between the two (Kuznets_1973: p.4). Mokyr (2005) supports this notion, attributing the West's economic prosperity to developments in intellectual realms concerning "useful knowledge" (Mokyr_2005: p.4).

North & Weingast (1989) focus on political factors underlying economic growth and market development, asserting that institutions play a crucial role in enforcing rules and facilitating changes that promote long-term economic expansion (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). Similarly, North (1989) argues that centralized enforcement mechanisms significantly impact economic growth (North_1989: p.1).

(2002) underscore the importance of institutions in understanding economic and political development over the past 500 years, suggesting that the reversal in the distribution of economic prosperity is related to institutional changes (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5). Ogilvie (2007) emphasizes the interdependency of individual institutions within a broader framework, noting that economists have started integrating institutions into their analyses (Ogilvie_2007: p.27).

These sources collectively support the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by demonstrating the crucial role they play in shaping economic structure, enforcing rules, and fostering market development. The implications of these findings for economic development policies warrant further exploration.

The significance of institutions in shaping long-term economic growth is further emphasized by Abramovitz (1986) who posits that institutional structures play a pivotal role in influencing economic incentives and productivity growth. He suggests that the evolution of mixed economies is linked to economic growth itself, implying that institutions foster an environment conducive to profit-making, investment, and innovation (Abramovitz_1986: p.20). Moreover, Abramovitz (1986) raises the question of whether there are generalized effects on productivity growth among leading economies, indicating that institutional development could potentially have widespread impacts on economic performance (Abramovitz_1986: p.16).

Similarly, Broadberry & Gupta (2006) underscore the importance of institutions in fostering economic growth during both early modern and contemporary periods. They argue that the service sector has played a critical role in spurring economic growth, further highlighting the institutional context's significance in shaping market development (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.9). In addition, they acknowledge the slower-than-anticipated economic growth during the Industrial Revolution, emphasizing that institutions play a crucial role in determining the pace of economic progress (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.25).

These findings underscore the fundamental role institutions play in long-run economic growth by shaping economic structures, enforcing rules, and fostering market development. The implications of these insights for economic development policies warrant further exploration to fully understand how institutional development can be leveraged to promote sustainable economic growth.

The centrality of institutions in long-term economic growth is further corroborated by Austin's (2008) work. He posits that institutions are not only crucial but also the fundamental cause of such growth, a claim that resonates with the assertion under review (Austin_2008: p.26).

Austin argues that the trade patterns between Africa and Western Europe, including the Atlantic trade, reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe (Austin_2008: p.24). This suggests that the institutional frameworks in place during these periods significantly influenced the economic outcomes, supporting the notion that institutions are a primary driver of long-term growth.

Moreover, Austin emphasizes the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history to reveal recurring relationships due to similar situations and path dependencies (Austin_2008: p.1). This approach underscores the significance of institutions in shaping economic trajectories over extended periods.

Critiques of the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth emerge from analyses of colonialism's impact on economic development. (Simson_2019: p.27) argues that during the 1960s and 1970s, Kenyan, Tanzanian, and Ugandan state expenditure expanded significantly, contributing to high economic growth in these regions. However, (Simson_2019: p.4) questions whether the elimination of spending inefficiencies, leakages, and costly delays would have been enough to sustain the investment levels of that period. Similarly, (Humphries_2013: p.18) contends that eighteenth-century economic growth in Britain was driven by a growing division of labor, the rise of workshop production, and the emergence of the factory system, rather than institutions alone. This perspective challenges the assertion that institutions are the sole determinant of long-term economic growth, suggesting that other factors, such as technological advancements and industrial organization, may also play significant roles.

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that Sub-Saharan Africa's lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has been a barrier in recent debates about comparative long-run growth. They also note that African economic performance has historically been worse than other regions, which may be due to the region's unique conditions (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.1). However, they emphasize the need for understanding the extent to which there are still risks of economic shrinking that have undermined progress in the past (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.18).

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) highlight the significant contribution of African women to economic growth and development, yet their labor is often unaccounted for due to being subsumed under household production. This understanding undermines policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.3). They also note that the major historical developments in West Africa concerning gender and economic growth have parallels elsewhere in Africa (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.4).

Komlos (1998) complicates the argument by emphasizing the importance of understanding the Industrial Revolution's impact on physical stature during modern economic growth. He also discusses the discovery of 8-year height cycles similar to classical business cycles, suggesting that biological growth processes were significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic conditions (Komlos_1998: p.16).

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) suggest that initial conditions or factor endowments could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth. They argue that the divergence in paths can be traced back to the achievement of sustained economic growth by the United States and Canada during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, while other societies did not manage to attain this goal until late in the nineteenth century (Sokoloff & Engerman_2000: p.12).

Hopkins (2019) argues that successive generations of African economic historians have been influenced by "totalism" and "essentialism," which may have limited the diversity of independent African viewpoints in studying long-run economic and political change. He also notes that food exports made very little contribution to economic growth in West Africa during the period of the slave trade (Hopkins_2019: p.13).

Frankema (2012) contends that colonial education is a key factor in explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa, with former British colonies growing faster due to their educational lead. However, he also notes that the degree of British "liberalism" was endogenous to the local political and economic context (Frankema_2012: p.19).

These sources add nuance to the thesis by suggesting that various factors, such as technological advancements, industrial organization, colonial education, and unique regional conditions, may also contribute significantly to long-term economic growth. Understanding these factors can help provide a more comprehensive explanation of economic growth patterns across different regions and historical periods.

The impact of institutions on long-run economic growth is further nuanced by their relationship with labor, as highlighted in Temin (2002) and Allen et al. (2011). According to Temin (Temin_2002: p.4), the exhaustion of cheap labor was a significant factor causing economic growth to slow. This suggests that institutions play a role in regulating labor markets, influencing wages, and determining the availability of labor.

Moreover, Temin (Temin_2002: p.16) argues that the stagnation of economies during the late 18th century was a result of slow growth rather than its cause. This implies that institutions may have contributed to economic growth patterns by maintaining stable and predictable environments conducive to long-term development.

Allen et al. (AllenEtAl_2011: p.1) demonstrate that income disparities in the early 20th century were due to long-run stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe. This finding underscores the role of institutions in shaping labor markets, as differences in institutional structures likely influenced the pace and success of industrialization across these regions.

This correlation between structural change and economic growth supports the argument that institutions play a crucial role in facilitating long-term economic development by promoting fair labor practices and fostering an environment conducive to increased productivity.

Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) focuses on the impact of Western institutions, particularly in the context of slave dealing and status acquisition.
