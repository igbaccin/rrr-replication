The question of whether institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth has sparked extensive debate among scholars. This discourse is crucial as it sheds light on the factors that drive economic development and prosperity in different regions over time.

Supporting this thesis, Austin (2008) argues that institutions play a pivotal role in determining long-term economic growth, particularly in the context of Africa's economic position compared to Western Europe and parts of Asia (Austin_2008: p.24). Similarly, Kuznets (1973) highlights the association between rapid changes in economic structure and societal innovations as major factors influencing economic and social structure, which can be viewed as a manifestation of institutional development (Kuznets_1973: p.6).

On the other hand, some scholars offer critiques and complicate the argument. For instance, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) point out that the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has hindered recent debates about comparative long-run growth in Sub-Saharan Africa (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance to the discussion by emphasizing the importance of understanding how women's economic contributions have been subsumed under household production, which undermines policies aimed at strengthening their economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3).

Moreover, North & Weingast (1989) provide evidence supporting the argument by demonstrating that the fundamental institutions of representative government, such as multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are intimately related to economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28). Acemoglu et al. (2001) also support this stance by showcasing how certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27).

The ongoing debate underscores the importance of understanding the role of institutions in long-run economic growth. As the discussion continues, it is essential to consider various perspectives, including those that support, critique, and complicate the argument, to gain a comprehensive understanding of this critical issue.

The significance of institutions in long-run economic growth is further supported by various scholars, notably Douglas North and Barry Weingast (North&Weingast_1989) and Daron Acemoglu and colleagues (AcemogluEtAl_2001, AcemogluEtAl_2002). North and Weingast (1989) emphasize the role of political institutions, such as representative government and common law courts, in fostering economic growth and political freedom. They argue that these institutions provide a necessary foundation for economic development by establishing multiple veto points and ensuring the enforcement and change of rules in an efficient manner.

Similarly, Acemoglu and colleagues (2001, 2002) highlight the impact of fundamental institutions on economic growth and prosperity. In their studies, they demonstrate that differences in institutions significantly influence economic performance and long-term development. For instance, institutions that promote secure property rights and limit the power of elites have been associated with increased investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001).

Joseph Mokyr (Mokyr_2005) also supports the argument by focusing on the role of intellectual developments, such as the Enlightenment, in shaping institutions that fostered economic growth. He suggests that changes in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West can be attributed to advances in "useful knowledge" and the development of progressive institutions like individualism, formal law, corporatism, self-governance, and rule of law (Mokyr_2005).

These findings collectively suggest that institutions play a crucial role in long-run economic growth by providing a foundation for secure property rights, promoting investment, and fostering the development of progressive institutions. Future research may focus on understanding how different institutional configurations impact economic growth across various regions and historical periods.

The relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth is further supported by evidence suggesting that trade, particularly the Atlantic trade, reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe (Austin_2008: p.24). This observation aligns with the thesis, as it suggests that the institutional environment played a significant role in shaping the economic trajectory of different regions.

Moreover, the development of Europe's urban network during the early modern period established a framework for regional economic growth, which facilitated the Industrial Revolution (deVries_1994: p.5). This highlights how institutions can foster economic development over time, supporting the argument that they are indeed a fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

Additionally, the economic success of West African commercial centers such as Accra and Lagos can be attributed to their high land-labor ratios and large flows of economic migrants (Frankema&vanWaijenburg_2012: p.15, p.17). These factors suggest that institutions that promote property rights, investment, and migration can lead to economic growth, thus supporting the thesis.

The works of Ogilvie (2007), Peters (2004), and Bryant (2006) further support the notion that institutions are fundamental to long-run economic growth. Ogilvie (Ogilvie_2007: p.2) emphasizes the significance of economic institutions in shaping economic development over thousands of years, as demonstrated by Diamond's work on geographical characteristics and Pomeranz's account of European-China economic divergence since 1750.

Peters (Peters_2004: p.7) highlights that land policy reform efforts often aim for economic growth, sustainable management of land, and poverty reduction, underscoring the importance of institutions in promoting these objectives. Moreover, Peters (Peters_2004: p.16) acknowledges the autonomy and dynamic nature of culture, suggesting that it interplays with economic institutions in driving development.

Bryant (Bryant_2006: p.5) underscores the role of European fusion of mercantilist imperialism and technological capitalism in promoting commercial exchange, further supporting the idea that certain institutional frameworks can foster growth. However, Bryant's work also raises questions about the validity of sunshine as a marker of civilizational depth (Bryant_2006: p.12), which may warrant further investigation into the multifaceted nature of institutions and their impact on economic development.

Challenges to the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth emerge from various perspectives, as some scholars argue that other factors, such as structural changes and environmental impacts, play significant roles in economic development.

For instance, Stern (2004) contends that economic growth may not necessarily lead to environmental degradation, challenging the notion that institutions are solely responsible for long-term prosperity. Instead, he suggests that economic factors drive changes in environmental impacts over the course of economic development (Stern_2004: p.3).

Similarly, McCloskey (2015) argues that ideas and ideologies have influenced economic growth, as seen in France where conservative parties prioritized Catholicism over economic expansion, despite examples of bourgeois success in other regions (McCloskey_2015: p.9).

Moreover, Bryceson (2002) highlights the influence of traditional hierarchical orders on women's economic and social spheres, suggesting that these factors may hinder economic growth in certain communities (Bryceson_2002: p.9). Furthermore, vanZanden (1999) questions the consensus view that early modern economies were unable to generate growth, challenging the assertion that institutions are the primary drivers of long-term prosperity (vanZanden_1999: p.1).

These perspectives raise doubts about the exclusive role of institutions in fostering economic growth, suggesting instead that a multifaceted approach considering various factors may be more accurate in understanding the complexities of long-run economic development.

The works of various scholars offer nuanced perspectives on the role of institutions in long-run economic growth, complicating the thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause. For instance, Temin (2002) posits that the exhaustion of cheap labor and the end of the Golden Age were not solely caused by institutional factors but also by the return to equilibrium growth paths (Temin_2002: p.14). Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) emphasize the importance of initial conditions, such as factor endowments, in shaping long-run economic development, suggesting that institutions might not be the sole determinant (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4).

Moreover, Koepke & Baten (2005) argue that climate and environmental factors can significantly impact human living standards and economic growth, further complicating the argument for institutions as the primary driver of long-run economic development (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.20). Frankema (2012) highlights the role of colonial education in explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa, suggesting that other factors such as British "liberalism" and post-colonial levels of capital investment may also play a crucial role (Frankema_2012: p.2).

Hopkins (2019) points out that the study of long-run economic and political change in Africa was inhibited by totalism, essentialism, and postmodernism, which may have limited the diversity of independent African viewpoints and, consequently, our understanding of the factors contributing to economic growth (Hopkins_2019: p.4). Lastly, Bolt & van Zanden (2014) discuss the impact of climate, environmental factors, and colonialism on long-run economic growth, further complicating the argument for institutions as the sole determinant (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5).

These studies suggest that while institutions may play a significant role in fostering economic growth, other factors such as climate, environmental conditions, initial conditions, and colonialism should not be overlooked. A comprehensive understanding of long-run economic development requires considering these various factors in a multifaceted approach.

Institutional structures and their role in long-term economic growth are further elucidated by various scholars, offering nuanced perspectives that complicate the argument. According to Komlos (1998), the concept of modern economic growth, as defined by Kuznets, is crucial for understanding this phenomenon. He notes that the Industrial Revolution was marked by significant structural changes, including population and urbanization growth, income inequality, and variability (Komlos_1998: p.17).

Lindert & Williamson (1985) emphasize the intertwined relationship between economic growth and equality, suggesting that understanding long-run distribution trends is essential for explaining growth itself. They propose that policymakers often face trade-offs between economic growth and equality, underscoring the importance of considering both aspects in a comprehensive analysis (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1).

Austin (2007) highlights the role of industrialization in driving long-term economic growth by comparing transportation technologies such as railways and trucks. He notes that, until the mid-1920s, railways had a significant advantage over lorries due to their lower cost (Austin_2007: p.8).

Feinstein (1998) discusses the impact of wars on British economic growth during specific periods and notes that changes in farming practices combined with migration and emigration were necessary for alleviating labor surpluses in rural areas (Feinstein_1998: p.28).

Allen et al. (2011) demonstrate the disparities between income levels in early 20th-century China, Europe, and Japan, suggesting that industrialization in Japan and Europe contributed to these income differences (AllenEtAl_2011: p.1). They also highlight the influence of institutional structures on economic growth by examining wage tables in various sectors (AllenEtAl_2011: p.11, p.20).

These sources underscore the importance of considering institutional structures and their impact on long-term economic growth. While factors such as climate, environmental conditions, initial conditions, and colonialism should not be overlooked, a comprehensive understanding of this complex phenomenon requires examining the role of institutions in driving structural changes, population dynamics, income distribution, and technological advancements.

The role of institutions in driving long-run economic growth is a complex and multifaceted issue, as evidenced by the extensive literature on the subject. While it has been widely acknowledged that institutions play a significant role in shaping economic outcomes (North & Weingast, 1989; Acemoglu et al., 2002), recent contributions to historical national accounting have revealed the importance of considering regional variations in this context.

Broadberry and Gardner (2022) highlight that Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA) has been largely absent from debates about long-run growth due to a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950. This underscores the need for further research into the historical institutional development in SSA and its impact on economic growth. Collier and Gunning (1999), as well as Sachs and Warner (1997), both observe that African economic performance has been markedly worse than other regions, suggesting that understanding the role of institutions in SSA is crucial to a comprehensive examination of long-run growth.

Broadberry and Gardner (2022) also emphasize that the extent to which growth in SSA provides a path to convergence remains uncertain. This uncertainty underscores the risks inherent in economic progress in this region, particularly with regards to potential shrinking or reversals of growth that have undermined economic development in the past.

Moreover, Broadberry and Gardner (2022) demonstrate how new contributions to historical national accounting have provided insights into patterns of growth and development over centuries, rewriting the history of economic divergence and convergence since before the industrial revolution. This suggests that a comprehensive understanding of long-run growth requires considering not only institutional development but also population dynamics, income distribution, and technological advancements across different regions.

The literature on the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth, as evidenced by Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), suggests a significant role for institutions in shaping economic trajectories. Both authors acknowledge that institutional development can foster structural change, which in turn promotes growth.

Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) posits that labor tied to land or owners leads to reduced competition among employers, whereas Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) highlights the impact of slave dealing and status acquisition on economic institutions. Both authors imply that institutional arrangements can either hinder or facilitate growth, depending on their specific characteristics.

However, both sources also illustrate divergences in their perspectives. Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) emphasizes the role of labor immobility in stifling competition and growth, while Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.13) focuses on demographic factors such as gender imbalances influencing economic institutions.

Despite these insights, several key questions remain unresolved. For instance, Domar's (Domar_1970: p.16) reference to Phillips' work on the economic cost of slaveholding in the Cotton Belt raises questions about the role of specific institutional arrangements—such as slavery—in hindering long-run growth. Moreover, Bohannan's (Bohannan_1959: p.10) mention of the Western impact not being limited to economic institutions suggests that other factors, such as cultural or political ones, may also play a significant role in shaping long-term economic growth.
