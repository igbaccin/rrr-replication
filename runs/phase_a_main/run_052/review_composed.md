The debate on the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, particularly in relation to institutions, has garnered significant attention among economists and historians. The central question at hand is whether institutions are indeed the primary driver of economic growth over the long term, or if other factors play equally important roles.

Supporting this thesis, Kuznets (1973) argues that high aggregate growth rates are associated with rapid changes in economic structure and society, suggesting a strong link between institutions and growth (Kuznets_1973: p.4). Acemoglu et al. (2001) further strengthen this argument by emphasizing the detrimental impact of poor institutions on investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27).

However, not all agree that institutions are the sole cause of long-run growth. Austin (2008) complicates this argument by suggesting that institutions may reinforce rather than originate economic positions, implying a more complex relationship between institutions and growth (Austin_2008: p.24). Similarly, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) highlight the lack of data on African economic performance before 1950, which limits our understanding of the role of institutions in long-term growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1).

Furthermore, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) argue that understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution is subsumed under household production and their labor formally unaccounted for, undermines policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). This nuance suggests that while institutions may be crucial, other factors such as gender roles also play a significant role in long-run growth.

The evidence from various sources supports the notion that institutions are indeed a fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, particularly through technological advancements. Kuznets (1973) highlights the association between rapid changes in economic structure and society, suggesting that technological innovations characterizing modern economic growth significantly impact economic and social structure (Kuznets_1973: p.4). Similarly, Mokyr (2005) emphasizes the role of technological progress in the Industrial Revolution after 1760 and its eventual influence on GDP per capita in the West (Mokyr_2005: p.3).

North & Weingast (1989) focus on political factors underlying economic growth and the development of markets, emphasizing the importance of institutions governing how rules are enforced and changed (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). They argue that the succession of technological innovations is a major factor affecting economic structure, suggesting that institutional adjustments are crucial for long-term economic success (North&Weingast_1989: p.23).

In addition, Ogilvie (2007) discusses how institutions can arise through stochastic shocks and persist through path dependency, implying that the development of economic institutions plays a significant role in long-term growth (Ogilvie_2007: p.19). Hopkins (2009) also acknowledges that institutions, broadly conceived, establish rules that shape individual and social behavior, impacting economic outcomes over the long run (Hopkins_2009: p.6).

These findings collectively suggest that technological advancements within industries such as coal, as well as institutional factors, are crucial in driving long-run economic growth. However, further research is needed to understand the interplay between these factors and their influence on specific sectors and economies over time.

Institutional persistence plays a significant role in driving long-run economic growth, as evidenced by Acemoglu and his colleagues' research (AcemogluEtAl_2001). The authors argue that certain fundamental institutions, such as property rights, have a profound impact on economic development. They highlight the persistence of these institutions over time, attributing it to various economic mechanisms (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.8).

For instance, Acemoglu and his team explain that institutions that hinder investment and economic progress will likely persist due to self-reinforcing cycles. In such systems, the lack of investment leads to underdevelopment, which in turn justifies and strengthens the very institutions that hindered investment in the first place (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27).

Moreover, the authors challenge the popular belief that differences in mortality rates are the primary drivers of institutional variation. Instead, they suggest that it is the underlying institutions themselves that shape demographic patterns over time (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3). This finding underscores the importance of considering institutional factors when analyzing long-term economic growth.

The authors of Acemoglu et al. (2002) further emphasize the significant role of institutions in shaping economic growth over long periods. They argue that the distribution of economic prosperity has undergone a substantial change, which they attribute to institutional factors (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.6). This change in prosperity distribution is a remarkable observation, as it had not been previously noticed or documented by other works (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.6).

The authors question whether this change in prosperity distribution is related to institutions (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5), suggesting that the underlying institutional structure plays a crucial role in determining economic growth patterns over time. They emphasize that understanding and evaluating various theories of long-term development necessitates considering the impact of institutions (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49).

Moreover, the authors thank the Massachusetts Institute of Technology Entrepreneurship Center for their support, indicating that their work has been conducted within an academic environment that values and encourages research on the topic of economic development (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.1). This further underscores the importance attached to understanding the role of institutions in shaping long-run economic growth.

While institutions are often considered fundamental to long-run economic growth, some argue that technological advancements in specific industries may challenge this notion. For instance, Stern (2004) posits that economic growth, particularly in the coal industry, can lead to environmental degradation rather than improvement (Stern_2004: p.1). He suggests that if the Environmental Kuznets Curve hypothesis were true, economic growth could potentially harm the environment initially but eventually lead to a reduction in environmental damage over time.

Simson (2019) also raises concerns about the role of institutions in driving economic growth, particularly in regions like Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda during the 1960s and 1970s (Simson_2019: p.25). The author suggests that while there were spending inefficiencies and costly delays to reform, their elimination might not have been enough to sustain investment levels or reorient these economies towards growth.

These perspectives challenge the idea that institutions are solely responsible for long-run economic growth by suggesting that technological advancements within certain industries and potential environmental consequences could play significant roles in shaping economic trajectories. Further examination of these factors in various contexts may provide a more nuanced understanding of the interplay between institutions, technology, and economic development.

According to Komlos (1998), physical stature during the Industrial Revolution was significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic processes and associated structural changes (p.16). This suggests that technological advancements in the coal industry may have played a role in shaping these fluctuations, potentially impacting long-term growth trajectories.

Temin (2002) posits that the exhaustion of cheap labor could have slowed economic growth (p.4), implying that advancements in technology, such as those in the coal industry, might have been necessary to maintain growth rates during periods of labor scarcity. Furthermore, Temin argues that the end of the Golden Age was brought about by the return to equilibrium growth paths, not adverse economic shocks (p.14), which could imply that technological advancements were crucial in maintaining economic stability and growth.

In a similar vein, Lindert & Williamson (1985) suggest that the relationship between economic growth and equality is intertwined, and policymakers have often struggled with trade-offs between them (p.1). This could imply that technological advancements in the coal industry, which would likely lead to increased productivity and potentially unequal distribution of wealth, might have played a role in shaping long-term economic growth patterns.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) argue that initial conditions, or factor endowments broadly conceived, could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth (p.4). In the context of the coal industry, technological advancements might have been one such factor endowment, shaping the trajectories of economies that relied heavily on this resource.

Koepke & Baten (2005) discuss the relationship between climate and human living standards, suggesting that technological advancements in the coal industry could have played a role in mitigating the effects of unfavorable climates on economic growth (p.20). Similarly, Frankema (2012) argues that colonial education, which might have been facilitated by technological advancements in the coal industry, could explain long-run growth disparities (p.2).

Trade and institutional change are intertwined factors that significantly contribute to long-run economic growth, as suggested by Austin (2008). He posits that trade, such as the Atlantic trade, reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe (Austin_2008: p.24). This perspective complicates the argument that institutions are the sole cause of long-run economic growth by highlighting the role of trade in shaping economic outcomes.

Austin (2008) further argues that institutional changes, particularly those facilitated by trade, can have lasting impacts on economic development. For instance, the author suggests that the Atlantic trade may have reinforced unfavorable institutions in Africa, contributing to its long-term economic stagnation (Austin_2008: p.24). This finding underscores the importance of considering both institutional changes and trade dynamics when examining long-run economic growth.

Moreover, Austin (2008) emphasizes the need for a historical perspective in growth analyses, arguing that recurring relationships in history can reveal the role of path dependence in economic development (Austin_2008: p.1). This call for long-term analysis adds nuance to the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by emphasizing the importance of understanding historical contexts and their lasting effects on institutional development and economic outcomes.

The work of Broadberry and Gardner (2022) further complicates the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by highlighting the unique case of Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA). They argue that a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has excluded SSA from recent debates about comparative long-run growth.

Broadberry and Gardner (2022) note that scholars such as Collier and Gunning (1999) and Sachs and Warner (1997) have observed poor economic performance in Africa, with SSA being markedly worse than other regions. However, they contend that understanding the historical context of SSA is crucial to determining whether its growth provides a path to convergence or poses risks of shrinking that could undermine future progress.

Moreover, Broadberry and Gardner (2022) emphasize the importance of new contributions to historical national accounting, which have provided insights into patterns of growth and development over centuries in other regions. They suggest that such contributions could potentially rewrite the history of economic divergence and convergence since before the industrial revolution, including for SSA.

In essence, Broadberry and Gardner (2022) underscore the need to consider historical contexts when evaluating the role of institutions in long-run economic growth, especially in regions like SSA where data has been limited. Their work adds nuance to the argument by suggesting that a comprehensive understanding of historical patterns is necessary for a complete analysis of institutional development and its impact on economic outcomes.

The works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), though tangential, offer valuable insights into the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth. Both scholars acknowledge that institutions play a significant role in economic development. Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) posits that institutions are among the factors of production, while Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.2) suggests that Western impact is not limited to economic institutions.

However, they diverge in their focus and scope. Domar's work primarily focuses on historical patterns of institutional development in Russia and the American South, offering insights into how changes in institutions can influence economic outcomes (Domar_1970: p.4, p.14). In contrast, Bohannan delves into the Tiv society of Nigeria, examining the impact of external influences on their economy and institutions (Bohannan_1959: p.7, p.10).

Despite these differences, both scholars converge in highlighting the importance of understanding historical patterns for a comprehensive analysis of institutional development and its impact on economic outcomes. They also acknowledge that institutions can be influenced by external factors (Domar_1970: p.16, Bohannan_1959: p.10).

However, what remains unresolved is the extent to which institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. While both Domar and Bohannan suggest that institutions play a significant role, they do not provide definitive evidence that institutions alone can explain long-term economic growth. Further research is needed to establish a causal link between institutions and economic growth over extended periods.
