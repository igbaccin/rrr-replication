LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question under investigation in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, a thesis put forth by Austin (2008) among others. This question holds significant importance as understanding the driving forces behind economic growth can provide valuable insights for policymakers and economists aiming to promote development and alleviate poverty in various regions.

Supporting this thesis, Austin (Austin_2008: p.26) argues that institutions are "the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth." Kuznets (Kuznets_1973: p.4) also supports this view, stating that high aggregate growth rates are associated with rapid changes in economic structure, which in turn impact other aspects of society. North & Weingast (North&Weingast_1989: p.28) concur, demonstrating how institutions played a necessary role in making possible economic growth and political freedom. Furthermore, Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19) present evidence showing a large effect of institutions on economic performance.

On the other hand, some scholars offer critiques or complications to this thesis. Broadberry & Gardner (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1) argue that the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has led to Africa's absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. Akyeampong & Fofack (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3) also complicate the issue, highlighting how understanding the processes by which women’s economic contribution becomes subsumed under household production is essential for strengthening women's economic roles.

As the literature review continues, it will delve deeper into these arguments and explore the nuances and qualifications surrounding the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth. The stakes are high, as understanding this relationship can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting economic development in various regions around the world.

Institutions, as defined by North & Weingast (1989), play a crucial role in promoting long-run economic growth by providing a framework for political and economic development. They argue that the fundamental institutions of representative government, such as an explicit set of multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are intimately related to economic growth and political freedom (North & Weingast_1989: p.28). This perspective is supported by Mokyr (2005), who suggests that changes in intellectual thought during the Enlightenment were instrumental in fostering a culture of economic reasonableness, which contributed to the West's economic growth (Mokyr_2005: p.56).

North (1989) further supports this argument by emphasizing the importance of institutions in reducing transaction costs and increasing security rights, which are essential for market development and economic growth (North_1989: p.9). DeVries (1994) highlights the role of Europe's urban network in establishing a framework for regional economic development, particularly during the early modern period when the Industrial Revolution took place (deVries_1994: p.5).

Hopkins (2009) and Nunn (2008) also provide evidence supporting the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth. Hopkins (2009) challenges the assumption that ethnic fragmentation is responsible for unproductive institutions, suggesting that other factors such as solidarity and community support may be more significant (Hopkins_2009: p.22). Nunn (2008) demonstrates a correlation between institutional quality and long-term economic growth, particularly in the context of Africa's history (Nunn_2008: p.4).

Structural change, as a result of technological advancements, plays a pivotal role in long-run economic growth. This is substantiated by Kuznets (1973), who posits that modern economic growth is characterized by a succession of technology innovations and social innovations that provide the necessary adjustments to society and economic structure (Kuznets_1973: p.6).

Moreover, Kuznets (1973) acknowledges the association between high aggregate growth rates and rapid changes in economic structure, which in turn influence other aspects of society, such as urbanization and family structures (Kuznets_1973: p.4). This suggests that technological progress triggers structural transformations, leading to long-term economic growth.

Whatley (2018) supports this notion by presenting an econometric model that captures the long-run equilibrium relationship between gunpowder imports and slave exports, indicating a correlation between technological advancements (in this case, gunpowder technology) and economic growth (Whatley_2018: p.13). The model also highlights that none of the variables adjust to deviations from long-run equilibrium, implying a persistent impact of technology on economic growth.

Institutional development plays a crucial role in long-run economic growth, as evidenced by the works of Acemoglu et al. (2001) and Abramovitz (1986). According to Acemoglu et al., more fundamental institutions significantly impact property rights, which in turn affect investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). The authors also argue that differences in mortality rates are not the primary cause of institutional variation (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3), suggesting that other factors, such as institutions, may be more influential.

Furthermore, Acemoglu et al.'s research shows that these institutions were detrimental to economic progress when they hindered investment (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). Conversely, favorable institutions can foster growth, as demonstrated by the large effect of institutions on economic performance observed in their study (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19).

Similarly, Abramovitz (1986) emphasizes the role of institutional development in economic growth. He suggests that the evolution of mixed economies is a function of economic growth itself, indicating that institutions adapt and change in response to economic conditions (Abramovitz_1986: p.20). Furthermore, Abramovitz identifies macroeconomic and monetary conditions encouraging capital investment and effective demand as key factors in the development of productive economies (Abramovitz_1986: p.7).

Other scholars challenge the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Simson (2019) critiques this view by emphasizing the importance of industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy in certain regions during periods of high economic growth (Simson_2019: p.27). Stern (2004) argues that economic growth can lead to environmental degradation rather than being a means to future prosperity, as suggested by the institutions-growth thesis (Stern_2004: p.1). Humphries (2013) questions the role of institutions in the eighteenth-century economic growth, suggesting that the division of labor, workshop production, and factory system were more significant factors (Humphries_2013: p.18). These scholars suggest that while institutions may play a role in economic growth, they are not the sole or primary cause.

The assertion that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth is not uncontested, as other factors such as structural change and initial conditions have been suggested to play significant roles in shaping economic trajectories. Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that the relationship between economic growth and inequality is intertwined, suggesting a potential causal link between the two. Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) posit that initial conditions, including factor endowments, may have profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth. Allen (2001) emphasizes the role of wages and prices in understanding economic development, economic leadership, and standard of living, suggesting that these factors should not be overlooked when considering institutional impacts on growth.

Frankema (2012) points to colonial education as a key factor in explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa, while Hopkins (2019) highlights the importance of understanding the economic and political changes that occurred after the collapse of traditional African economies. Goldin (2006), in her study of labor, acknowledges that the evolution and revolution in women's choices and decisions can impact long-run economic growth. These findings complicate the argument that institutions are the sole or primary cause of long-run economic growth by demonstrating the interplay between various factors and their role in shaping economic trajectories over time. Further investigation into these complex relationships is necessary to fully understand the drivers of economic growth.

The examination of long-run economic growth is further complicated by the historical underrepresentation of certain regions, particularly Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA), due to a lack of data before 1950 (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). Collier and Gunning (1999) and Sachs and Warner (1997) have noted that African economic performance has been significantly worse than other regions, raising concerns about the risks of shrinking growth patterns that could hinder progress in the future (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1).

Recent contributions to historical national accounting have provided valuable insights into long-term growth and development patterns across various regions, such as Switzerland, Germany, Sweden, Italy, Greece, Russia, Bulgaria, and Indonesia (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5, Broadberry et al._2022). However, these revisions do not fundamentally change the understanding of economic growth dynamics, suggesting that institutional development plays a crucial role in shaping economic trajectories over centuries.

Moreover, the disagreement regarding the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is more profound than it may appear (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.30). The interplay between various factors, such as political and social institutions, market forces, and geopolitical strategies, significantly influences the economic trajectories of different regions (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.7, p.21). This complexity underscores the need for a nuanced understanding of the role of institutions in shaping long-run economic growth.

The nuanced understanding of the role of institutions in shaping long-run economic growth is further emphasized by the works of Komlos, Austin, and Feinstein. Komlos (1998) highlights the influence of short-term fluctuations in economics on biological growth processes during the Industrial Revolution, suggesting a complex interplay between economic and biological factors in driving growth.

Moreover, Austin's (2007) research underscores the importance of transportation infrastructure, such as railways, in facilitating modern economic growth. He argues that until the mid-1920s, railways had a significant cost advantage over lorries, implying that the evolution of transportation systems played a crucial role in shaping economic trajectories.

Feinstein's (1998) work sheds light on the gradual changes in farming and labor dispersion in rural areas before the 1850s and 1860s, suggesting that these shifts were necessary for the onset of modern economic growth. He also points out the challenges in measuring unemployment during this period due to a lack of reliable data, underscoring the complexity of understanding long-run economic growth.

These findings complicate the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by demonstrating the interplay between various factors, including biological processes, infrastructure development, and agricultural changes, in shaping economic trajectories over time. Further investigation into these intricate relationships could provide valuable insights into the nuanced role of institutions in driving long-term economic growth. (Komlos_1998: p.16, p.17; Austin_2007: p.8, p.12, p.16; Feinstein_1998: p.28)

The scholarly consensus on the fundamental role of institutions in long-run economic growth is substantiated by both Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), albeit from tangential perspectives. Both authors acknowledge the significance of institutional structures in shaping economic dynamics, with Domar focusing on labor-tied economies and Bohannan examining the impact of Western institutions on African societies.

Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) asserts that when labor is tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, thereby stifling economic growth. Conversely, the advent of structural change, such as the transition from slave-holding economies to free labor markets, correlates with increased growth (Feinstein_1998: p.28).

Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) highlights how Western economic institutions, particularly slave dealing and curing practices, exerted a profound influence on African societies. However, he also notes the limitations of these institutions, such as the restricted number of women available for labor (Bohannan_1959: p.13).

Despite these insights, several questions remain unresolved. For instance, the exact mechanisms through which institutions drive long-term economic growth require further investigation. Additionally, it is essential to understand how cultural, political, and historical factors interact with institutional structures to shape economic trajectories (Austin_2007: p.8).
