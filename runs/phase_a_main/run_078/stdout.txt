LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question in the literature review on "Institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth" revolves around the role of institutions in shaping economic development over extended periods. This question holds significant implications, as understanding the factors driving long-term growth can inform policy decisions aimed at fostering economic prosperity and reducing poverty in various regions.

Several scholars support the thesis that institutions play a pivotal role in promoting long-run economic growth. For instance, Simon Kuznets (1973) emphasizes the connection between economic structure changes and institutional innovations during modern economic growth periods. Likewise, Douglass North and Barry Weingast (1989) argue that representative government institutions with multiple veto points are crucial for economic growth and political freedom. Furthermore, Daron Acemoglu and colleagues (2001) demonstrate the detrimental effects of institutions on investment and economic progress in various regions.

On the other hand, some scholars complicate this perspective by highlighting nuances that qualify the central claim. For example, Peter Austin (2008) suggests that institutions may reinforce rather than originate a region's economic position compared to others, such as Western Europe or parts of Asia. Similarly, Broadberry and Gardner (2022) argue that data limitations have prevented Sub-Saharan Africa from being included in recent debates on long-run growth.

Moreover, Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) emphasize the importance of understanding how women's economic contributions are subsumed under household production and their labor is often unaccounted for in policy discussions aimed at strengthening women's economic roles. These complications underscore the need for a nuanced approach to understanding the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth, acknowledging the complexities and variations across different regions and historical periods.

The ongoing debate surrounding institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is far from resolved. The stakes are high, as policymakers seek to design effective strategies that promote sustainable development and reduce poverty in various regions. A comprehensive understanding of this relationship will require further research and a nuanced approach that acknowledges the complexities and variations across different historical periods and geographical contexts.

The relationship between institutions and economic growth is further supported by several scholars, notably Simon Kuznets, Douglass North and Robert Weingast, Joel Mokyr, and Jan de Vries. Kuznets (1973) suggests that structural changes in society, such as urbanization and family roles, are associated with rapid economic growth. He also emphasizes the non-conventional costs of economic growth, which can have both positive and negative effects even within developed countries (Kuznets_1973: p.4, p.11).

North and Weingast (1989) focus on political factors underlying economic growth and market development. They argue that representative government institutions, specifically multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are crucial for economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

Mokyr (2005) highlights the role of intellectual developments in shaping economic growth. He posits that changes in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West can be explained through improvements in "useful knowledge" (Mokyr_2005: p.4, p.56).

De Vries (1994) underscores the importance of Europe's urban network in establishing a framework for regional economic development during the early modern period, which facilitated industrial growth (deVries_1994: p.2, p.5).

These scholars collectively demonstrate that institutions play a significant role in fostering economic growth and structural change, providing further evidence to support the claim that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

The significance of institutions in fostering economic growth and structural change is further underscored by the works of Acemoglu et al. (2002) and Abramovitz (1986). Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49) assert that institutions are a first-order factor in understanding economic and political development over the past 500 years, providing a strong foundation for the claim that they are fundamental to long-term growth. Moreover, the authors note an observed change in the distribution of economic prosperity, suggesting that this shift may be institutionally driven (Acemoglu et al., 2002: p.6).

On a similar note, Abramovitz (1986) emphasizes the role of institutions in shaping economic incentives and growth patterns. He highlights the importance of macroeconomic conditions that encourage capital investment and effective demand, as well as the balance between productivity growth and economic structure (Abramovitz, 1986: p.7, p.20). Furthermore, Abramovitz (Abramovitz_1986: p.22) suggests that countries are currently in a process of rebalancing this relationship in favor of productivity growth, which could potentially lead to further economic expansion.

Together, these scholars provide compelling evidence supporting the notion that institutions play a crucial role in promoting long-run economic growth and structural change. The works of Acemoglu et al. (2002) and Abramovitz (1986) underscore the importance of institutional development as a key driver of economic prosperity, offering valuable insights into the mechanisms through which institutions shape economic growth over time.

The assertion that institutions are the primary catalyst for long-term economic growth is further substantiated by Broadberry et al. (2015) and Bryant (2006). Broadberry et al. (2015) delve into India's long-run economic performance, emphasizing the significance of institutional development in shaping this trajectory. They argue that a well-established system of laws, property rights, and governance can foster an environment conducive to economic growth (BroadberryEtal_2015: p.2). Furthermore, they propose that the size of armies could serve as an indicator of institutional strength, suggesting that a larger population implies better-developed institutions (BroadberryEtal_2015: p.4).

On similar lines, Bryant (2006) contends that Europe's fusion of mercantilist imperialism with capitalistic transformation was instrumental in driving economic growth. He posits that this fusion created an environment that encouraged the growth of commercial exchange, ultimately leading to prosperity (Bryant_2006: p.13). Moreover, Bryant (2006) highlights the radicalness of revisionist dissent challenging the conventional belief that sunshine is a marker of civilizational depth, instead advocating for the importance of institutions in determining economic success (Bryant_2006: p.3).

Institutions, while crucial to long-term economic growth, may not be the only factor influencing economic development. Some sources challenge this thesis by emphasizing the role of other factors such as structural change and external influences.

For instance, Simson (2019) argues that state expenditure and deficits expanded in the 1960s and 1970s during a period of high economic growth in certain regions of the world (Simson_2019: p.25). This suggests that economic growth could be driven by factors beyond institutions, such as government spending or external factors like global optimism about future prosperity.

Stern (2004) critiques the Economic Kuznets Curve (EKC) hypothesis, which posits that economic growth leads to environmental degradation in the early stages but eventually results in a decrease in environmental impact. Stern argues that this assumption may not hold true, as irreversible environmental damage could potentially reduce future income levels (Stern_2004: p.8). This implies that factors other than institutions, such as environmental considerations, could significantly impact economic growth.

McCloskey (2015) challenges the notion that ideas and ideologies, not institutions, were responsible for the Great Enrichment. In France, for example, economic growth was throttled due to conservative reasons despite the examples of bourgeois economic successes in other regions (McCloskey_2015: p.9). This suggests that factors beyond institutions, such as ideologies or external examples, could play a significant role in determining economic growth.

In a similar vein, Humphries (2013) points out that eighteenth-century economic growth rested on a growing division of labor, the rise of workshop production, and the eventual emergence of the factory system (Humphries_2013: p.18). This implies that structural changes within an economy could be as important as institutions in driving long-term economic growth.

Lastly, vanZanden (1999) critiques the consensus view in the 1960s and 1970s that the early modern economy was unable to generate economic growth. VanZanden argues that this pessimistic interpretation has come under attack in recent years, suggesting that factors other than institutions could contribute to economic growth before the industrial revolution (vanZanden_1999: p.1).

Institutions are indeed a significant factor in long-run economic growth, but several authors complicate this perspective by suggesting that other factors may have contributed to economic growth before and during the industrial revolution.

Physical stature, as an indicator of living standards, can provide insights into economic growth patterns (Komlos_1998: p.2). Komlos' research reveals that height cycles similar to classical business cycles imply that biological growth processes were influenced by short-term fluctuations in the economy (Komlos_1998: p.16). However, this suggests that economic factors might have played a role in shaping physical stature, which could indirectly impact long-term economic growth.

Temin (2002) argues that the exhaustion of cheap labor was a primary cause for the slowing of economic growth, implying that labor supply and its cost may have been crucial factors in economic development before the industrial revolution (Temin_2002: p.4). Furthermore, Temin suggests that the end of the Golden Age was brought about by the return to equilibrium growth paths, not adverse shocks in the 1970s (Temin_2002: p.14). This implies that pre-industrial economic growth might have been influenced by cycles of expansion and contraction rather than solely institutional factors.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that the relationship between economic growth and equality is complex, and policymakers have often struggled to balance these two aspects (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1). They suggest that long-run distribution trends are closely linked to the search for explanations of growth itself (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.9), implying that income distribution may have played a role in shaping economic growth patterns.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) argue that initial conditions, or factor endowments broadly conceived, could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4). They suggest that the contrasting records in growth can be traced back to the achievement of sustained economic growth by certain countries during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.12), implying that geographical and environmental factors may have played a role in shaping economic growth patterns.

Frankema (2012) argues that colonial education is a key factor in explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa, with former British colonies growing faster as a result of their educational lead (Frankema_2012: p.2). This implies that the impact of colonial institutions on economic growth may have been mediated by factors such as education policies and their outcomes.

The literature on institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth presents a nuanced perspective, with some sources complicating this thesis. For instance, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) highlight that Sub-Saharan Africa's (SSA) historical data before 1950 has been limited, which may have excluded SSA from recent debates about long-run growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). This lack of data could potentially challenge the assertion that institutions are the sole cause of economic growth in all regions, including SSA.

Moreover, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) point out that African countries have historically shown poorer economic performance compared to other regions (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). This observation raises questions about whether the impact of institutions on long-run growth is uniform across all regions or if there are factors unique to Africa that contribute to its slower growth.

Additionally, Bolt & van Zanden (2014) acknowledge that revisions in nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth have been made for several countries such as Germany, Sweden, Italy, Greece, Russia, and Bulgaria (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5). These revisions could potentially alter our understanding of the role institutions played in these countries' long-run economic growth.

The role of institutions in shaping long-run economic growth is further nuanced when considering their impact on women's economic roles, as evidenced by Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) and Goldin (2006).

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) highlight the historical subsumption of women's economic contributions under household production, which has implications for policies aimed at strengthening their economic roles. They argue that understanding these processes is crucial to addressing the under-accounting of women's labor (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). Furthermore, they emphasize that the contributions of African women to economic growth and development were often dependent on the economic provision of their husbands (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.9, p.19). This suggests that institutions play a significant role in shaping gender dynamics within economies, potentially influencing long-term economic growth through the underutilization of women's labor.

Goldin (2006) adds to this discussion by examining the evolution and revolution in the study of labor markets, noting that these terms refer to specific changes in women's choices and decisions (Goldin_2006: p.1). She argues that the distinction between evolution and revolution is important for understanding the impact of legal changes on women's economic roles (Goldin_2006: p.3). This shift from evolution to revolution, as Goldin describes it, could potentially signify a significant transformation in the role institutions play in shaping women's economic participation and long-run economic growth.

The works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), while not directly addressing the primary thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, offer tangential insights that contribute to a broader understanding of this relationship. Both scholars emphasize the role of institutions in shaping economic structures and patterns of growth, albeit in different contexts.

Domar (1970) focuses on the factors of production and their relationship with institutions. He argues that when labor is tied to land or an owner, competition among employers ceases, potentially hindering economic growth. Conversely, Bohannan (1959) examines the impact of Western economic institutions on African societies, highlighting how they centered on slave dealing and status acquisition.

While both scholars acknowledge the influence of institutions on economic structures, their perspectives diverge in terms of the specific nature of these influences. Domar's focus on labor immobility suggests a potential hindrance to growth, while Bohannan's discussion of Western impact implies a catalyst for change.

Despite these differences, both scholars converge in emphasizing the importance of institutions in shaping economic structures and patterns. However, what remains unresolved is a comprehensive understanding of how specific institutional changes lead to long-term economic growth, particularly in relation to women's economic participation as Goldin (2006) suggests. Further research is needed to elucidate this relationship and provide a more nuanced understanding of the role institutions play in shaping long-run economic growth.
