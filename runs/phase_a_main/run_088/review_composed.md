The central question under investigation in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. This question holds significant implications for our understanding of historical and contemporary economic development, as well as for policy formulation aimed at promoting sustainable growth and reducing poverty in various regions.

Several scholars support the thesis that institutions play a crucial role in driving long-term economic growth. For instance, Austin (2008) argues that institutions are "the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth" (p.26). Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) demonstrate how representative government and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs contribute to economic growth and political freedom (p.28). Acemoglu et al. (2001) also support this view, highlighting that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress (p.27).

On the other hand, some scholars offer critiques of this perspective. For example, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate the argument by pointing out that data limitations have prevented a thorough examination of Sub-Saharan Africa's long-run growth before 1950 (p.1). Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance to the discussion, focusing on how women's economic contributions have been subsumed under household production and their labor formally unaccounted for, undermining policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles (p.3).

As this brief review demonstrates, institutions are a central factor in long-run economic growth. However, the extent to which they are the "fundamental" cause remains a subject of ongoing debate and further investigation is required to fully understand their role and impact on economic development.

Institutions are posited as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, a claim supported by various scholars. Austin (2008) echoes this sentiment, asserting that institutions are not only crucial but also the primary factor driving long-term growth. He further argues that historical analyses reveal recurring relationships due to path dependence, emphasizing the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history.

Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2002) argue that institutions are a first-order factor in understanding economic and political development over the past 500 years. They suggest that the reversal in the distribution of economic prosperity could be linked to institutional changes, indicating the profound impact institutions have on long-term growth.

Whatley (2018) contributes to this discourse by presenting econometric results that demonstrate the relationship between gunpowder imports and slave exports, suggesting a long-run equilibrium relationship influenced by institutions. Hopkins (2009), in his study of ethnicity and development in Africa, also supports the notion that institutions play a significant role in economic growth.

Nunn & Wantchekon (2011) provide evidence showing that both internal and external channels are important for understanding how institutions impact long-run growth. Their findings suggest that the internal channel is at least twice as large as the external channel, underscoring the crucial role of institutions in shaping economic development over time.

The assertion that institutions are the primary driver of long-run economic growth is further substantiated by the works of North & Weingast (1989) and Acemoglu et al. (2001). North & Weingast (North&Weingast_1989: p.2) emphasize that their research focuses on political factors underpinning economic growth and market development, underscoring the importance of institutions in shaping both economic growth and political freedom. Specifically, they highlight the role of representative government with multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs as crucial for economic growth (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19) present compelling evidence that institutional differences have a substantial impact on economic performance. They demonstrate that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress, while others fostered growth (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). Moreover, they underscore the significance of institutions in shaping property rights, which is a crucial determinant of structural change and long-run economic growth (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27).

Technological innovations and social changes are inextricably linked to economic growth, further supporting the notion that institutions play a pivotal role in long-term economic development (Kuznets_1973: p.6). Kuznets (1973) posits that technological modernization, coupled with societal adaptations, significantly influences economic and social structure.

The correlation between structural change and growth is evident as rapid changes in both the economy and society often coincide (Kuznets_1973: p.4). Moreover, Kuznets emphasizes that the succession of technological innovations characterizing modern economic growth necessitates corresponding social adjustments to facilitate their effective implementation (Kuznets_1973: p.6).

However, it is essential to acknowledge that despite the positive achievements of modern technology in developed countries, there are also unexpected negative consequences even within these nations (Kuznets_1973: p.11). This underscores the importance of institutions in managing and mitigating such adverse effects, thereby fostering sustainable growth.

Industrial Development, Educational Expansion, and Military Autonomy have been emphasized in some cases as key factors for economic growth (Simson_2019: p.27). However, the evidence presented suggests that these elements may not necessarily be the fundamental cause of long-term economic development.

For instance, while there was a significant expansion in state expenditure and deficits during periods of high economic growth in certain regions (Simson_2019: p.25), it is questionable whether eliminating spending inefficiencies and delays would have been enough to sustain the investment levels of previous decades, let alone reorient these economies (Simson_2019: p.4).

Moreover, the focus on expanding public employment rather than protecting existing government jobs (Simson_2019: p.27) might not contribute positively to economic growth in the long run. This could potentially lead to bureaucratic bloat and inefficiencies, which could hinder economic development rather than promote it.

The claim that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is further complicated by findings suggesting that historical data from Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA) has been limited, hindering its inclusion in recent debates about comparative long-term growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). Collier and Gunning (1999) observe that African economic performance has been significantly worse than other regions, highlighting the need for a better understanding of SSA's economic history to determine if current growth patterns offer a path to convergence (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1).

Temin (2002) posits that the exhaustion of cheap labor was the primary cause for economic growth slowing, rather than institutions being the main driver (Temin_2002: p.4). He argues that economic stagnation in the 1970s was not due to adverse shocks but a return to equilibrium growth paths (Temin_2002: p.14). These findings suggest that while institutions may play a role, other factors such as labor availability and market transitions could also significantly impact economic growth.

Allen (2001) emphasizes the importance of wages and prices in understanding long-term economic development, industrial leadership, and standard of living (Allen_2001: p.1). However, he notes that interpreting wage trends can be complex, as both positions find support in his indices (Allen_2001: p.23). This complicates the argument that institutions are the sole cause of long-run economic growth, as other factors such as wages and prices may also play crucial roles.

Bolt & van Zanden (2014) revise nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth data for several countries, but their revisions do not fundamentally change the overall picture (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5). This suggests that while institutions may contribute to economic growth, there are likely other factors at play as well, and improvements in data collection can help refine our understanding of these factors.

Allen et al. (2011) demonstrate that income disparities during the early twentieth century were due to long-run stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe (AllenEtAl_2011: p.1). Their research highlights the complex interplay of factors influencing economic growth, including institutional structures, labor markets, and technological advancements, further complicating the argument that institutions are the sole driver of long-run economic growth.

Stoler (1989) argues that colonizers lived in "imagined communities" to overcome economic and social disparities (Stoler_1989: p.5). This finding suggests that institutions may not always be the primary cause of economic growth, as other factors such as colonial dynamics and societal constructs can also play significant roles.

The contribution of African women to economic growth and development is a significant yet often overlooked aspect, as their labor is frequently subsumed under household production (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). This oversight can undermine policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles.

In West Africa, for instance, the economic contribution of African women was largely dependent on the economic provision of their husbands (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.9). However, this dynamic is not exclusive to West Africa, as similar developments and themes can be found elsewhere in Africa where gender and economic growth are concerned (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.4).

Understanding the processes that subsume women's economic contributions under household production is crucial for policy development. By acknowledging and accounting for women's labor, policies can be designed to empower them economically and potentially contribute to long-term economic growth.

The structural change in African economies correlates with growth, but the role of women in this transformation remains understudied (implied in Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.19). Future investigations should focus on understanding and quantifying the impact of women's contributions to economic development in Africa. This nuanced understanding can help refine our understanding of the relationship between institutions, economic growth, and gender roles in African societies.

The incorporation of Roman economic history theories into the discourse on institutions as the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth offers a fresh perspective. Komlos (1998) delves into the concept of modern economic growth, emphasizing its correlation with structural changes during the Industrial Revolution. He notes that physical stature and urbanization significantly increased during this period, suggesting that such transformations could have played a pivotal role in fostering economic growth in African societies as well.

Komlos (1998) also highlights an intriguing discovery of 8-year height cycles, which parallels classical business cycles. This observation implies that the biological growth process was influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic conditions. In African contexts, understanding how such cycles may have impacted women's contributions to economic development can further refine our understanding of the relationship between institutions, economic growth, and gender roles.

Moreover, Komlos (1998) underscores that inequality and income variability increased during the Industrial Revolution. While these factors could potentially challenge the argument that institutions are the primary drivers of long-term economic growth, they also provide opportunities to explore how different institutional arrangements might have influenced the distribution of resources and, consequently, contributed to varying degrees of economic development in African societies.

By incorporating insights from Roman economic history, the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth becomes more nuanced. The focus shifts towards understanding the specific structural changes and socioeconomic transformations that occurred during periods of significant economic growth, as well as how these factors may have impacted different groups within society, such as women in African societies. This expanded perspective can lead to a deeper understanding of the complex interplay between institutions, economic growth, and societal structures.

The works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) provide tangential insights into the relationship between institutions, economic growth, and societal structures, offering a nuanced understanding of this complex interplay. Both scholars acknowledge the significance of institutions in fostering long-term economic development.

Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) posits that with labor tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, potentially stunting economic growth. Conversely, structural change correlates with growth, as indicated by his discussion on the Russian peasants' enserfment and the economic cost of slaveholding in the Cotton Belt (Phillips, "The Economic Cost of Slaveholding in the Cotton Belt," Pol., cited in Domar_1970: p.16).

Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.10) also recognizes the Western impact on economic institutions but expands the focus to include societal structures, noting that before the British arrival, people rarely ventured far beyond their kinsmen or special friends, potentially limiting interaction and exchange. He further discusses gender dynamics within African societies, suggesting that the number of women may be limited (Bohannan_1959: p.13).

While both scholars agree on the importance of institutions in long-term economic growth, they diverge in their focus on specific factors. Domar emphasizes labor immobility and structural change, while Bohannan explores societal structures and gender dynamics. A key unresolved issue is understanding how these various factors intersect and influence each other within different contexts to drive economic growth over the long run.
