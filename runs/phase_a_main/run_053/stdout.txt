LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question under investigation in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. This question holds significant importance as it sheds light on the factors that drive economic development and prosperity over extended periods.

Several sources support the thesis, arguing that institutions play a crucial role in fostering economic growth. For instance, North & Weingast (1989) demonstrate how representative government institutions with multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs are integral to economic growth and political freedom. Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2001) highlight that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress.

However, not all sources fully endorse this perspective. Austin (2008), for example, complicates the argument by suggesting that while institutions are important, they may reinforce rather than originate certain economic positions, as seen in the case of Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) also add nuance to the discussion, arguing that the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has prevented Sub-Saharan Africa from being included in recent debates about comparative long-run growth.

Moreover, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) complicate the issue by discussing how understanding the processes by which women's economic contributions are subsumed under household production and their labor formally unaccounted for is crucial for policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles. Kuznets (1973), on the other hand, supports the thesis by showing that high aggregate growth rates are associated with rapid changes in economic structure and society, implying a strong link between institutions and long-term economic growth.

The stakes of this debate are high, as understanding the role of institutions in driving economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting development and prosperity. The literature suggests that while institutions are indeed important, they may not be the sole determinant of long-run economic growth, and other factors such as historical context, social innovations, and gender dynamics must also be considered. This review continues to explore these complexities in greater depth.

(Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1; Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3)

The assertion that institutions are the primary driver of long-run economic growth is further substantiated by various studies. For instance, Ogilvie (2007) posits that different frameworks of economic institutions can significantly influence their success or failure (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). He also emphasizes the interdependency of individual institutions within a broader framework (Ogilvie_2007: p.27), suggesting that the effectiveness of an institution is contingent on the overall system in which it operates.

Similarly, Whatley (2018) demonstrates the long-run equilibrium relationship between gunpowder imports and slave exports, indicating the significance of institutional structures in shaping economic dynamics (Whatley_2018: p.13). Hopkins (2009) supports this notion by arguing that ethnic fragmentation is not always detrimental to productive institutions, as it can foster community support and solidarity (Hopkins_2009: p.21).

Moreover, Frankema & van Waijenburg (2012) highlight the role of commercial centers in attracting economic migrants, which could lead to structural change and subsequent growth (Frankema&vanWaijenburg_2012: p.15). Nunn (2008) supports this notion by demonstrating that ethnic fractionalization, a product of institutional structures, can have significant long-term effects on economic development (Nunn_2008: p.4). Lastly, Broadberry et al. (2015) argue that understanding the historical context and evolution of institutions is crucial for comprehending a country's long-run economic performance, particularly in the case of India (BroadberryEtal_2015: p.2).

These studies collectively underscore the importance of institutions in shaping long-term economic growth, emphasizing the need to consider historical context, social innovations, and gender dynamics alongside institutional structures when examining economic development.

The significance of institutions in shaping long-term economic growth is further emphasized by various scholars, providing a comprehensive understanding of their role and impact. North & Weingast (1989) argue that the fundamental institutions of representative government, specifically multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are intimately related to the struggle for economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28). Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2001) demonstrate that more fundamental institutions, such as property rights, significantly influence investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27).

Mokyr (2005) expands upon this notion by focusing on the intellectual developments that led to the growth of economic reasonableness in the West, suggesting that changes in the intellectual realm concerning "useful knowledge" played a crucial role in economic development (Mokyr_2005: p.4). North (1989) highlights the centralized enforcement mechanisms of Spain as an example of how institutions can influence economic growth, while Acemoglu et al. (2002) provide evidence for a reversal in the distribution of economic prosperity that may be related to institutional changes (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5).

Structural change, as defined by Kuznets (1973), is closely linked to economic growth. He argues that technological advancements, a key aspect of modern economic growth, are associated with changes in the economic structure and other societal aspects (Kuznets_1973: p.4). Moreover, Kuznets emphasizes the non-conventional costs of economic growth, suggesting that even within developed countries, negative consequences can arise alongside positive achievements (Kuznets_1973: p.11).

Van Zanden (2009) further supports this notion by highlighting the link between the skill premium and economic growth. He suggests that a low skill premium in the construction industry can contribute to economic growth due to its impact on the relative price of capital goods (vanZanden_2009: p.27). Additionally, he posits that human capital formation, which is influenced by the skill premium, is one of the determinants of long-term economic growth (vanZanden_2009: p.24).

Despite van Zanden's assertion that institutions play a pivotal role in long-run economic growth, Simson (2019) challenges this perspective by focusing on East Africa's economic development during the 1960s and 1970s. He argues that the region experienced high economic growth due to factors such as industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy, rather than institutional development (Simson_2019: p.27).

Simson further contends that the expansion of public employment, rather than protecting existing government jobs, was emphasized during this period (Simson_2019: p.27). He suggests that while there were likely inefficiencies, leakages, and costly delays to reform, their elimination may not have been sufficient to sustain the investment levels of the 1960s or reorient these economies (Simson_2019: p.4).

Furthermore, Simson highlights that state expenditure and deficits expanded in East Africa during a period of high economic growth and global optimism about future prosperity (Simson_2019: p.25). These findings challenge the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, suggesting that other factors such as industrial development, educational expansion, and public employment may play equally significant roles.

The assertion that institutions are the primary cause of long-term economic growth is not universally accepted, as other factors such as industrial development, educational expansion, and public employment may also play significant roles. This perspective is further complicated by the findings presented in various scholarly works.

For instance, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that Sub-Saharan Africa has been overlooked in recent debates about long-term growth due to a lack of data on economic performance before 1950. They suggest that understanding the extent to which there are still risks of economic shrinkage in this region is crucial for determining if its current growth provides a path to convergence (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.1). This implies that the role of institutions may be less influential in regions with limited historical data, and other factors might be more prominent.

Similarly, Temin (2002) posits that the exhaustion of cheap labor caused economic growth to slow, rather than institutions being the primary cause (Temin_2002: p.4). Moreover, he contends that the end of the Golden Age was brought about by the return to equilibrium growth paths of these countries, not by adverse economic shocks in the 1970s (Temin_2002: p.14). These findings suggest that the role of institutions may be less significant during periods of economic transition and industrialization.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) argue that initial conditions, or factor endowments broadly conceived, could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run growth (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4). They highlight the contrasting records in growth between the United States and Canada during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, and other societies that did not manage to attain sustained economic growth until later (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.12). This indicates that factor endowments, such as natural resources or climate, might be equally important in shaping long-term economic growth.

The role of institutions in fostering long-run economic growth is further complicated by various factors, as suggested by the works of Komlos (1998), Lindert & Williamson (1985), Austin (2007), and Goldin (2006).

Komlos (1998) emphasizes that the concept of modern economic growth involves structural changes such as population growth, urbanization, and income inequality. He also highlights the influence of short-term fluctuations in economics on biological growth processes during the Industrial Revolution.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) propose a causal relationship between economic growth and equality, suggesting that policymakers have often sought to balance these two aspects. They argue that understanding long-run distribution trends is crucial for explaining growth itself.

Austin (2007) underscores the importance of transportation infrastructure, such as railways, in contributing to modern economic growth. However, he also points out that not all capital-deepening and TFP-improving forces were present in every context, suggesting a more nuanced understanding of the factors driving growth.

Goldin (2006) discusses the evolution and revolution of institutions in shaping labor markets, particularly focusing on women's choices and decisions. She highlights that legal changes can have both short-term and long-run effects on economic growth, adding another layer of complexity to the relationship between institutions and growth.

These studies collectively suggest that while institutions are indeed fundamental to long-run economic growth, understanding their role requires considering various factors such as population growth, urbanization, infrastructure development, legal changes, and income distribution. The interplay between these factors can significantly reshape the argument on the causal relationship between institutions and economic growth. Further examination of these factors in different historical and geographical contexts could provide a more comprehensive understanding of this relationship.

The works of Austin (2008) and Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) offer nuanced perspectives on the relationship between institutions, trade, and economic growth, complicating the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

Austin (Austin_2008: p.26) acknowledges the logical appeal of the argument that institutions are 'the' fundamental cause of long-run economic growth but challenges this assertion by suggesting that trade, such as the Atlantic trade, may have reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe. This perspective underscores the importance of considering the complex interplay between institutions and trade in shaping economic growth trajectories.

Furthermore, Austin (Austin_2008: p.1) emphasizes the need for growth theories to be examined against long-term history, revealing relationships that recur because of similar situations as well as path dependencies. This perspective underscores the importance of historical context in understanding the relationship between institutions and economic growth.

Braudel & Wallerstein (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.30) argue that there is a fundamental disagreement about the role of institutions in shaping economic growth, suggesting that this disagreement extends beyond the debate over the causal relationship between institutions and trade. They further highlight the interdependence of actors in the market, implying that the strategies of one player can affect the strategies of others (Braudel & Wallerstein 2009: p.21). This perspective underscores the complexity of the institutional environment and its influence on economic growth.

Scholars Domar (1970), Bohannan (1959), and Bryceson (2002) offer tangential insights into the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth. While their primary focus is not exclusively on institutions, their works provide valuable perspectives that converge in certain areas and diverge in others.

Domar (1970) emphasizes the role of labor tied to land or owners in limiting competition among employers, thereby hindering economic growth. Similarly, Bohannan (1959) highlights the impact of Western institutions on African societies, particularly in the context of slave dealing and status acquisition. These scholars converge in their recognition of the institutional factors that can hinder economic development.

On the other hand, Bryceson (2002) provides a more nuanced perspective, focusing on livelihood diversification and its impact on economic growth in African communities. This scholar diverges from Domar and Bohannan by highlighting how changes in labor patterns and economic activities can lead to structural change and, potentially, economic growth.

Despite these insights, what remains unresolved is a comprehensive understanding of the specific institutional factors that drive long-term economic growth. Further research is needed to identify the key institutions that foster growth, the mechanisms through which they operate, and how they interact with other economic factors. This understanding could provide valuable guidance for policymakers seeking to promote sustainable economic development in various contexts.
