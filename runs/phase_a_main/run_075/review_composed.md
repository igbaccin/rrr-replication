The central question in the literature review on the role of institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is whether institutions are indeed the primary driver of economic development, or if other factors play a more significant role. This question holds great importance, as understanding the underlying causes of economic growth can inform policy decisions and aid in the formulation of effective strategies for promoting development.

The thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is supported by several scholars. For instance, Acemoglu et al. (2001) argue that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress, while Kuznets (1973) contends that both technological innovations and social innovations are major factors affecting economic and social structure. Furthermore, Austin (2008) endorses the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history, revealing relationships that recur due to path dependence.

However, other scholars complicate this thesis by suggesting that institutions may not be the sole factor driving economic growth. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) note that Sub-Saharan Africa has been absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth due to a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950, implying that other factors may have played a role in shaping the region's economic trajectory. Similarly, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) argue that understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution becomes subsumed under household production is crucial for policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles.

Komlos (1998) also complicates the thesis by suggesting that while institutions are important, they should be considered alongside other factors such as population growth and urbanization in understanding modern economic growth. For example, he argues that physical stature during the Industrial Revolution was significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic processes and associated structural changes.

The significance of institutions in shaping economic growth is further emphasized by North and Weingast (1989) who assert that these political factors are instrumental in facilitating market development and fostering long-term economic expansion. They argue that absolutist states, such as early modern Spain, hindered economic progress due to unfavorable conditions created by the absence of institutional constraints (North&Weingast_1989: p.7). This suggests that effective institutions, which establish rules for economic exchange and ensure their enforcement, are crucial for promoting sustainable growth.

Similarly, Frankema & van Waijenburg (2012) highlight the role of institutions in driving urbanization and economic development in West Africa. They point out that commercial centers, such as Accra and Lagos, attracted large numbers of economic migrants due to their favorable economic conditions, which were largely shaped by institutional factors (Frankema&vanWaijenburg_2012: p.15). This evidence indicates that the development of institutions played a vital role in creating an environment conducive to urbanization and subsequent economic growth.

Pamuk (2007) also supports this view, demonstrating how structural changes during the Industrial Revolution significantly impacted wages and incomes across Europe. He contends that these changes paved the way for the commercialization of agriculture, which was a crucial step towards modern economic growth (Pamuk_2007: p.24). This suggests that institutional changes facilitated by structural transformations can have long-lasting effects on economic development.

Lastly, Broadberry et al. (2015) discuss the role of institutions in shaping India's long-term economic performance, noting that effective institutions are essential for promoting growth and prosperity. While their study focuses primarily on India, the implications are broadly applicable to other regions as well (BroadberryEtal_2015: p.2).

The role of institutions in fostering long-run economic growth is further substantiated by Mokyr (2005), who argues that the intellectual advancements during the Enlightenment period played a significant role in shaping economic growth in the West. Mokyr posits that the growth of economic reasonableness, rooted in developments within the intellectual realm concerning "useful knowledge," is a key factor explaining the wealth disparity between the West and other regions today (Mokyr_2005: p.4).

Furthermore, Mokyr identifies several elements of a progressive society that emerged during this period, such as individualism, formal law, corporatism, self-governance, and institutionalized processes for determining rules (Mokyr_2005: p.56). These elements, which are fundamental to effective institutions, were instrumental in promoting economic growth and prosperity.

Supporting this perspective, van Zanden (2009) highlights the importance of human capital formation as a determinant of long-term economic growth. The author suggests that the skill premium, or the ratio between the wage rates of skilled and unskilled labor, has an impact on relative prices and can contribute to economic growth (vanZanden_2009: p.24). Similarly, Whatley's (2018) study demonstrates that the adoption of gunpowder technology played a crucial role in shaping economic exchanges between Africa and Europe during the early modern period, further emphasizing the impact of institutions on long-run economic growth.

Institutions significantly influence capital investment, which in turn contributes to long-run economic growth. The study by Acemoglu et al. (2001) provides compelling evidence supporting this assertion. They argue that certain institutions, particularly those that promote secure property rights and reduce the cost of enforcing contracts, foster a conducive environment for capital accumulation (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). These institutions, they suggest, are persistent due to various economic mechanisms (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.8).

Moreover, Acemoglu et al. (2001) contend that detrimental institutions can hinder investment and economic progress. For instance, they note that institutions that limit competition and encourage rent-seeking behaviors can stifle capital accumulation (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). This finding underscores the importance of institutional quality in fostering long-term economic growth.

Nunn's (2008) research also supports the notion that institutions play a crucial role in capital investment. He demonstrates that ethnic fractionalization, which can lead to instability and insecurity, negatively affects property rights and thus reduces capital investment (Nunn_2008: p.4). This finding suggests that institutional quality, as reflected by the degree of ethnic homogeneity, can significantly influence long-run economic growth.

Furthermore, Nunn & Wantchekon's (2011) study reveals that both internal and external institutions impact capital investment. They find that the internal institutional environment, which includes factors such as trust, social norms, and political institutions, has a more substantial effect on capital accumulation than the external institutional environment (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.4). This finding underscores the importance of domestic institutions in shaping long-run economic growth.

Economic growth, rather than being a driving force for environmental improvement as suggested by some (Stern_2004: p.1), may instead lead to increased environmental degradation over the course of economic development (Stern_2004: p.3). This perspective challenges the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, as it implies that economic activities can have detrimental effects on the environment.

According to Stern (2004), the assumption is that environmental damage does not significantly reduce economic activity or future income levels (Stern_2004: p.8). This suggests that institutions promoting economic growth may not necessarily prioritize or effectively manage environmental concerns, potentially leading to long-term negative impacts on both economic and ecological systems.

While the evidence presented by Stern (2004) challenges the idea of a direct link between strong institutions and sustainable long-run economic growth, it does not negate the importance of institutional quality in shaping economic performance after major shocks. Further research could explore how institutions balance economic growth with environmental concerns to better understand their impact on long-term development outcomes.

The relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth is indeed complex, as evidenced by various studies (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000, Frankema_2012, Williamson_1996). Sokoloff and Engerman (2000) argue that initial conditions, or factor endowments, may have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth. They suggest that the United States and Canada achieved sustained economic growth during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, while other societies did not, due to unique institutional conditions (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.12).

Frankema (2012) further complicates this argument by asserting that colonial education could be a key factor in explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa. He argues that the former British colonies grew faster as a result of their educational lead (Frankema_2012: p.2). Williamson (1996) also emphasizes the role of institutions, arguing that understanding the sources of convergence is fundamental to understanding policy responses and who gains or loses from convergence (Williamson_1996: p.25).

However, it's essential to acknowledge that other factors, such as capital investment and economic base, may also contribute to long-term economic growth (Frankema_2012, Williamson_1996). For instance, the degree of British "liberalism" was endogenous to the local political and economic context (Frankema_2012: p.19).

In addition, the impact of institutions on long-term development outcomes may not be solely positive. Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) argue that institutions are interconnected and affect each other's strategies in the market (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.21). This suggests that while institutions can promote economic growth, they may also have unintended negative consequences for environmental sustainability or social equality.

Furthermore, Hopkins (2019) highlights the importance of understanding historical context when studying long-run economic and political change. He argues that successive generations of African scholars have been limited in their diversity due to postmodernism's removal of the problem of poverty from academic discourse (Hopkins_2019: p.4). This underscores the need for a nuanced understanding of the role of institutions in shaping economic growth, taking into account the unique historical and cultural contexts of different societies.

The perspective of institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is further complicated by studies that emphasize the interplay between institutions, industrialization, and historical contexts. For instance, Austin (2008) argues that the Atlantic trade reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe, suggesting that institutions might have played a role in shaping the trajectory of growth but not exclusively as the primary cause.

Komlos (1998) highlights the significance of physical stature during the Industrial Revolution and its connection with economic processes and associated structural changes. His findings imply that while institutions may contribute to long-run economic growth, they might be influenced by other factors such as health and nutrition, which can vary significantly across regions and time periods.

Koepke & Baten (2005) discuss the relationship between climate and human living standards, suggesting that environmental conditions could also impact institutional development and, consequently, long-term economic growth. Their research underscores the importance of considering multiple factors when evaluating the role of institutions in shaping economic outcomes.

Austin (2007) points out that some capital-deepening and TFP-improving forces of modern economic growth were not present in certain regions during specific periods, which complicates the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Similarly, Bolt & van Zanden (2014) emphasize the importance of considering regional variations in economic growth and the impact of various factors on each region's development path.

Allen et al. (2011) demonstrate that income disparities during the early 20th century were due to long-run stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe, indicating that institutions may not be the sole determinant of economic growth and that other factors such as technology and geopolitical conditions play significant roles.

The role of institutions in long-run economic growth is further nuanced when considering the impact of colonialism, as evidenced by Broadberry & Gardner (2022). Their research highlights that Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA), which has historically been absent from debates about comparative long-term growth due to a lack of data before 1950, has exhibited markedly worse economic performance than other regions (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.1).

This poor performance can be attributed to the colonial institutions imposed on SSA. Collier and Gunning (1999) note that African economies were often exploited, leading to slow growth (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.1). The authors also suggest that understanding whether this growth provides a path to convergence requires an examination of the extent to which there are still risks of the shrinking that has undermined economic progress in the past (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.18).

However, it is important to note that some economists have suggested using alternative data to track African economic growth in recent decades (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.10), which could potentially provide a more comprehensive understanding of the role of institutions in SSA's long-term economic development.

In this context, it can be argued that while institutions are crucial for long-run economic growth, the nature and quality of these institutions, as influenced by factors such as colonialism, significantly impact their effectiveness. Further examination of the role of institutions in regions like SSA under different historical and political circumstances is necessary to fully understand the intricate relationship between institutions and long-term economic growth.

The scholarship on the role of institutions in long-run economic growth, as presented in Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), offers valuable insights but also exhibits some divergence. Both authors acknowledge the significance of institutional structures in shaping economic outcomes, with Domar emphasizing the impact of labor-tied institutions that limit competition among employers, and Bohannan highlighting the influence of Western institutions on African societies.

However, they differ in their focus. Domar's analysis primarily revolves around factors affecting production and growth, while Bohannan explores the broader societal impacts of external influences. This divergence underscores the multifaceted nature of institutional development and its effects on economic growth.

Both authors also converge on certain points. For instance, they both suggest that institutions can constrain or facilitate growth by influencing labor mobility and competition (Domar_1970: p.4, p.16; Bohannan_1959: p.7). Furthermore, they acknowledge the historical context as a crucial factor shaping institutional development, with Domar referring to the Russian peasants' enserfment (Domar_1970: p.4) and Bohannan discussing the British impact on African societies (Bohannan_1959: p.10).

Despite these shared insights, several issues remain unresolved. For example, a comprehensive understanding of how institutions drive long-term economic growth requires further examination of the role of institutions in regions like Sub-Saharan Africa under different historical and political circumstances (Domar_1970: p.4, p.15, p.16; Bohannan_1959: p.10). Additionally, the interplay between institutional change and structural change, as well as their mutual influence on economic growth, warrants further investigation.

In conclusion (although not written as such), the scholarship presented in Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) underscores the importance of institutions in shaping long-run economic growth. However, to fully grasp this relationship, it is necessary to delve deeper into specific historical and political contexts, particularly in regions like Sub-Saharan Africa, and examine the interplay between institutional change, structural change, and economic growth.
