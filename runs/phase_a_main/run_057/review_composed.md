The central question in the literature review on institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is whether the structure and functioning of societal organizations play a pivotal role in fostering sustained economic progress. This question carries significant implications for understanding historical economic disparities between regions, informing development policies, and shaping theories about growth and development.

The majority of sources support this thesis. Austin (2008) asserts that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, with their importance revealed through recurring relationships in history due to path dependence (Austin_2008: p.1). Similarly, Kuznets (1973) emphasizes that both technological innovations and social innovations associated with modern economic growth significantly impact economic structure (Kuznets_1973: p.6). North & Weingast (1989) also support this view, demonstrating the relationship between representative government institutions and economic growth (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

However, not all sources wholeheartedly endorse this perspective. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) complicate the argument by highlighting that women's economic contributions in Africa have often been subsumed under household production, undermining policies aimed at strengthening their economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). Broadberry & Gardner (2022) also add nuance to the discussion by pointing out that a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has excluded Sub-Saharan Africa from recent debates about comparative long-run growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1).

As the debate continues, it is crucial to further explore how institutions shape long-term economic growth and development, especially in regions like Africa where data may be limited. Understanding these relationships will provide valuable insights into historical patterns of growth and development, inform more effective policies, and contribute to a deeper understanding of the complex interplay between institutions, economic growth, and societal progress.

The works of North and Weingast (1989) and Acemoglu et al. (2002) provide compelling evidence supporting the notion that institutions are fundamental to long-term economic growth. North and Weingast (North&Weingast_1989: p.2) argue that political factors, particularly the development of representative government and enforcement mechanisms, play a crucial role in fostering economic growth and market development. Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49) assert that institutions have been instrumental in shaping patterns of economic prosperity over the past 500 years.

North and Weingast (North&Weingast_1989: p.30) further emphasize that strong institutions enable political freedom, which in turn contributes to economic growth. Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.6) also highlight the importance of institutional changes in understanding shifts in economic prosperity, suggesting a significant role for institutions in long-term development. These findings underscore the critical interplay between institutions, economic growth, and societal progress.

In addition, deVries (deVries_1994: p.2) emphasizes the Industrial Revolution as a monumental achievement of early modern Europe, which established a framework for regional economic development. Abramovitz (Abramovitz_1986: p.7) supports this view, suggesting that macroeconomic and monetary conditions encouraging capital investment and effective demand are essential for economic growth. Furthermore, Hopkins (Hopkins_2009: p.21) argues that community support structures can contribute to economic development, challenging the notion that ethnic fragmentation necessarily leads to unproductive institutions.

Peters (Peters_2004: p.7) also supports the idea that institutions are crucial for economic growth, noting that land policy reform efforts often aim to promote economic growth, sustainable land management, and poverty reduction. However, Peters (Peters_2004: p.16) cautions against oversimplifying social organization and cultural modes of understanding as fully capitalist, emphasizing the need to consider their unique dynamics of development.

The relationship between institutions and technological advancements is a crucial factor in understanding long-run economic growth. Kuznets (1973) highlights that rapid changes in economic structure, often associated with modern economic growth, are accompanied by adjustments in other aspects of society (p.4). He further emphasizes the significance of technological innovations as major factors affecting economic and social structure (p.6).

Mokyr (2005) supports this perspective, suggesting that developments in intellectual realms concerning "useful knowledge" played a significant role in the change in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West (p.4). He argues that many elements of a progressive society, such as individualism and formal law, have contributed to economic development (p.56).

Ogilvie (2007) underscores the importance of institutions in economic development, stating that different frameworks may resist encroachment, potentially leading to failure (p.27). He also notes the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework (p.27).

Van Zanden (2009) supports this assertion, suggesting that the skill premium of carpenters and masons, who design and make capital goods, affects the relative price of these goods and can contribute to economic growth (p.27). He also argues that human capital formation is one of the determinants of long-term economic growth according to new growth theory (p.24).

These findings collectively suggest that institutions play a fundamental role in fostering technological advancements, which in turn drive long-run economic growth. The unique dynamics of development in various societies should be considered while analyzing their level of capitalist organization.

Institutions play a crucial role in fostering labor productivity, which is an essential factor contributing to long-run economic growth. According to North (1989), strong institutions with centralized enforcement mechanisms can significantly impact economic growth, as evident in the case of Spain (North_1989: p.1). The presence of such institutions reduces transaction costs and provides increased security rights, thus promoting a more efficient capital market (North_1989: p.9).

In addition to this, Nunn (2008) argues that ethnic fractionalization during the pre-colonial era had a significant impact on economic development due to the environment of uncertainty and insecurity at the time. This increased the need for institutions that could provide security and stability, thereby fostering long-term economic growth (Nunn_2008: p.4).

Furthermore, Nunn & Wantchekon (2011) highlight the importance of internal and external channels in shaping labor productivity. Their research shows that the internal channel, which refers to institutions within a society, is at least twice as important as the external channel, which involves interactions between societies (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.4). This suggests that the development of strong and effective institutions within a society is vital for promoting labor productivity and long-term economic growth.

Despite the significant role institutions play in promoting labor productivity and long-term economic growth (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.4), some argue that the emphasis on industrial development, educational expansion, and public employment may not be sufficient to sustain investment levels and reorient economies towards long-term growth (Simson_2019: p.4). For instance, while Kenyan, Tanzanian, and Ugandan state expenditure expanded during a period of high economic growth in the 1960s and 1970s, it is questionable whether eliminating spending inefficiencies, leakages, and costly delays would have been enough to sustain the investment levels (Simson_2019: p.4).

Moreover, some challenge the consensus view that the early modern economy was unable to generate economic growth. In recent years, this pessimistic interpretation of economic growth before the industrial revolution has come under attack. For example, a study by van Zanden (1999) shows that in the mid-eighteenth century, there was still a negative link between 'output' measure of standard of living and the level of economic development (vanZanden_1999: p.13). On theoretical and empirical grounds, this suggests that pre-industrial economic growth may not have been as limited as previously thought (vanZanden_1999: p.2).

These critiques challenge the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by questioning the effectiveness of certain strategies for promoting industrial development and economic growth, and by casting doubt on the limitations of pre-industrial economic growth. Future research should further investigate these challenges to deepen our understanding of the role of institutions in economic growth.

The debate on the role of institutions in long-run economic growth is further nuanced by the works of various scholars, each offering valuable insights that challenge and refine the existing understanding.

Temin (2002) posits that the exhaustion of cheap labor may have contributed to the slowing of economic growth, rather than institutions being its primary cause. He argues that the decline in growth during the 1970s was a return to equilibrium growth paths, not an adverse economic shock (Temin_2002: p.14). Moreover, he suggests that recent theories of economic growth approach the Golden Age in the context of economic transition, which complicates the relationship between institutions and long-run growth (Temin_2002: p.7).

Lindert & Williamson (1985) further complicate the issue by questioning the causal direction between economic growth and equality. They argue that policymakers often face a trade-off between economic growth and equality, and that understanding this relationship is crucial for long-term national development (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1). Furthermore, they suggest that initial conditions or factor endowments could have profound and enduring impacts on long-run growth, challenging the notion that institutions are the sole determinant of economic growth (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.8).

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) explore the possibility that initial conditions, such as factor endowments, could have had a significant impact on long-run economic growth. They argue that the divergence in paths can be traced back to the achievement of sustained economic growth by the United States and Canada during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, while other countries did not manage to attain this goal until late in the nineteenth century (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.12).

Koepke & Baten (2005) introduce climate as a factor that may influence long-run economic growth. They suggest that over the period of the Roman Empire, living standards did not increase significantly, contradicting the common view that economic growth and protection led to improved living conditions (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.16).

Hopkins (2019) points out that postmodernism may have limited the diversity of independent African viewpoints in the study of long-run economic and political change, potentially influencing our understanding of the role of institutions in economic growth (Hopkins_2019: p.4).

Skocpol & Sommers (1980) emphasize the importance of comparing cases to increase the visibility of one structure by contrasting it with another. They argue that in Skocpol's States and Social Revolutions, only the "positive" cases of social revolution receive extensive discussion, which could complicate our understanding of the role of institutions in long-run economic growth (Skocpol&Sommers_1980: p.13).

These works add nuance to the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by challenging the notion that institutions are the sole determinant of economic growth and introducing other factors, such as climate and initial conditions, that may influence long-run economic development. Further research should explore these factors in greater detail to deepen our understanding of the role of institutions in economic growth.

Institutions are indeed a fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, but understanding their role requires consideration of other factors as well. As Broadberry & Gardner (2022) point out, Sub-Saharan Africa's absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth is due to the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). This suggests that the impact of institutions in this region may have been underestimated or overlooked, as data limitations might obscure their role.

Moreover, Collier and Gunning (1999) note that African economic performance has been markedly worse than other regions (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). Understanding the reasons behind this disparity is crucial to understanding the role of institutions in long-run economic growth.

On the other hand, Komlos (1998) argues that physical stature during the Industrial Revolution was significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic processes and associated structural changes (Komlos_1998: p.16, p.17). This implies that institutions may not be the sole determinant of long-run economic growth, as other factors such as demographic shifts could also play a significant role.

Bolt & van Zanden (2014) highlight that revisions of nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth have been made for several countries, but these do not fundamentally change our view of economic growth patterns (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5). This suggests that while institutions are important, they may not be the only factor influencing long-run economic development, and that other factors such as technological advancements or resource endowments could also play a role.

The relationship between institutions, migration, and economic growth is a complex one, as suggested by various studies. For instance, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) highlight the underestimation of women's economic contributions in West Africa due to their labor being subsumed under household production (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). This underscores how institutions, particularly those relating to gender roles, can significantly influence economic growth by affecting the productive capacity of individuals and households.

Similarly, Williamson (1996) discusses the impact of mass migrations on labor markets and economic growth. He argues that by glutting labor markets abroad, these migrations reduced the pace of real wage growth in receiving countries (Williamson_1996: p.17). This finding underscores the importance of considering migration policies as part of the institutional framework that influences long-run economic development.

Feinstein (1998) also touches upon the role of institutions in economic growth, albeit indirectly. He discusses the relationship between structural changes, wars, and economic growth in Britain during the Industrial Revolution (Feinstein_1998: p.28). Although Feinstein does not explicitly focus on institutions, his work suggests that institutional changes, such as those resulting from wars or technological advancements, can lead to significant structural shifts that, in turn, influence economic growth.

The works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), while not explicitly focusing on institutions as the primary cause of long-run economic growth, provide tangential insights that support this thesis. Both authors acknowledge the significance of institutional changes in influencing economic growth.

Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) discusses labor tied to land or owners, suggesting that such arrangements can hinder competition among employers and potentially impede economic growth. Conversely, he also highlights instances where institutional changes, such as the end of serfdom, have led to structural shifts that could foster economic growth (Domar_1970: p.4).

Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) notes the impact of Western institutions on societies, including those related to slave dealing and status acquisition. However, he also acknowledges limitations in these institutions, such as the restriction of women's roles (Bohannan_1959: p.13).

Despite their contributions, both scholars do not provide a comprehensive explanation for long-run economic growth solely due to institutions. The relationship between institutions and economic growth remains an area of ongoing research and debate. For instance, Domar (Domar_1970: p.16) references Phillips' work on the economic cost of slaveholding in the Cotton Belt, suggesting a complex interplay between institutions and economic growth that warrants further exploration.
