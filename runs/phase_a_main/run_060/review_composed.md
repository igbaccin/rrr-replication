The debate surrounding the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, particularly in relation to institutions, has been a subject of considerable interest among economists and historians. This discussion is significant as it sheds light on the factors driving economic development across various regions and periods.

Several scholars support the notion that institutions play a pivotal role in long-term economic growth. Austin (2008) argues that institutions are "the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth" (Austin_2008: p.26). Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) maintain that certain political institutions were instrumental in enabling economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.30). Acemoglu et al. (2001) also highlight the detrimental impact of institutions on investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27).

However, not all agree that institutions are the sole driver of long-run economic growth. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate this argument by stating that the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has kept Sub-Saharan Africa out of recent debates about comparative long-run growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) further add nuance by discussing the subsuming of women's economic contribution under household production, which undermines policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3).

The stakes in this debate are high, as understanding the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting development and reducing poverty. The discussion continues, with scholars debating the extent to which institutions are influenced by other factors such as geography, culture, or historical events. Future research should aim to bridge the data gap in Sub-Saharan Africa and consider the role of women's economic contribution in long-term growth.

The assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is substantiated by various scholarly works. As Austin (Austin_2008: p.26) posits, institutions play a pivotal role in driving long-term economic prosperity, with their influence extending beyond the Atlantic trade reinforcing Africa's economic disparity compared to western Europe. Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49) echo this sentiment, asserting that understanding economic and political development over the past 500 years necessitates focusing on institutions.

Moreover, Nunn & Wantchekon (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.4) demonstrate that both internal and external factors contribute to the formation of institutions, but the internal channel is more significant. This underscores the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history, as advocated by Austin (Austin_2008: p.1). These findings suggest that institutions not only influence economic growth but also persist over time, a factor crucial for understanding long-run development patterns.

Furthermore, Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.6) highlight an intriguing shift in the distribution of economic prosperity that warrants further exploration. Understanding this change and its connection to institutions could provide valuable insights into the dynamics of long-term growth. Similarly, Nunn & Wantchekon (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.7) emphasize the importance of considering the persistence of shocks' effects on institutional development, a factor that could have far-reaching implications for understanding economic growth over extended periods.

The importance of institutions in fostering long-run economic growth is further underscored by North & Weingast (1989) and Acemoglu et al. (2001). North & Weingast (North&Weingast_1989: p.2) emphasize that the focus should extend beyond rules governing economic exchange to encompass institutions that determine how these rules are enforced and altered. They argue that representative government, characterized by multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, plays a crucial role in enabling economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

Acemoglu et al. (2001) also support this assertion by demonstrating a significant impact of institutions on property rights and economic performance. They contend that certain institutions, such as those that were detrimental to investment and progress, have a substantial influence on economic growth (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). Furthermore, their research shows a large effect of institutions on overall economic performance (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19).

These findings underscore the critical role of institutional persistence in economic performance over extended periods. The persistence of institutions can shape the long-term trajectory of economies, as they determine the rules that govern economic exchange and the mechanisms for enforcing these rules (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). Therefore, understanding institutional development and its persistence is essential for comprehending long-run economic growth.

Technological innovations play a significant role in long-run economic growth, serving as catalysts for structural changes within societies (Kuznets_1973: p.6). Kuznets (1973) posits that the succession of technological innovations characterizes modern economic growth, and these advancements necessitate social innovations to facilitate necessary adjustments.

For instance, the adoption of agricultural techniques like irrigation systems or the use of plows revolutionized farming practices, leading to increased food production and population growth (Kuznets_1973: p.5). Similarly, the Industrial Revolution transformed manufacturing processes, resulting in higher productivity levels and economic expansion.

Moreover, Kuznets (1973) highlights that technological innovations can lead to unexpected negative consequences even within developed countries, while less developed countries struggle to leverage modern technology effectively (Kuznets_1973: p.11). Non-conventional costs associated with economic growth, such as pollution and resource depletion, are illustrative of these unintended drawbacks.

The focus on industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy, as well as expanding public employment rather than protecting existing ones (Simson_2019: p.27), challenges the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Simson's work suggests that these policy priorities in certain regions may have contributed to economic growth, questioning the central role of institutions.

Furthermore, while there were undoubtedly inefficiencies, leakages, and costly delays to reform, it is questionable whether their elimination would have been enough to sustain the investment levels of the 1960s (Simson_2019: p.4). This implies that factors beyond institutions might play a significant role in economic growth, contradicting the thesis.

Moreover, Simson's research indicates that during periods of high economic growth and global optimism about future prosperity, state expenditure and deficits expanded in Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda (Simson_2019: p.25). This expansion of public spending might suggest that government intervention, rather than institutional structure, could drive economic growth.

Lastly, the recovery of the sub-region's economic growth in the 2000s challenges the idea that institutions are the sole determinant of long-term economic success (Simson_2019: p.4). While external factors may have played a role in this recovery, the focus on policy priorities and public spending suggests that other factors could also contribute to growth.

The assertion that institutions are the sole determinant of long-term economic success may be overly simplistic, as evidenced by the complexities uncovered in various historical and economic analyses. For instance, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) highlight the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 for Sub-Saharan Africa, which has been absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. This suggests that other factors may have played a significant role in economic development during this period, potentially complicating the argument that institutions are the sole determinant of long-term economic success (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.1).

Similarly, Allen (2001) focuses on wages and prices as central concerns for economic historians, emphasizing their impact on economic development, economic leadership, and standard of living. While the issue of representativeness must be considered, the British industrial revolution provides some reassurance that the wages of building craftsmen are indicative of trends in average earnings (Allen_2001: p.4). However, contrary interpretations can also be strengthened using these indices, potentially complicating the assertion that institutions are the sole determinant of long-term economic success (Allen_2001: p.23).

Moreover, Bolt & van Zanden (2014) indicate that revisions of nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth have been made for several countries, such as Switzerland, Germany, Sweden, Italy, Greece, Russia, and Bulgaria. While these revisions do not fundamentally change the conclusions drawn by the authors, they do add nuance to the discussion, potentially complicating the argument that institutions are the sole determinant of long-term economic success (Bolt & van Zanden_2014: p.5).

Lastly, Allen et al. (2011) demonstrate that income disparities in the early twentieth century were due to long-run stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe. Furthermore, they show that workers in Leipzig experienced rapid growth of the German economy, which translated into rising real wages (AllenEtAl_2011: p.20). These findings suggest that external factors, such as industrialization and economic growth in other regions, may also contribute to long-term economic success, potentially complicating the argument that institutions are the sole determinant of long-term economic success.

The contribution of African women to economic growth and development has historically been undervalued, as their labor is often subsumed under the category of household production (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). This oversight in policy-making can hinder efforts aimed at strengthening women's economic roles.

In West Africa, for instance, the economic contribution of women was largely dependent on their husbands' provision (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.9). However, understanding these processes is crucial in devising policies that address this imbalance and promote gender equality in economic growth.

Moreover, the authors highlight that these findings are not unique to West Africa, as similar historical developments and themes concerning gender and economic growth can be found elsewhere on the continent (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.4). This suggests that addressing the undervaluation of women's contributions to economic growth may require region-specific strategies but shares common ground across Africa.

The authors further emphasize that the contribution of African women to economic growth is significant (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.19). By acknowledging and accounting for their labor, policies can be designed to foster more equitable economic development in Africa. This nuanced perspective adds complexity to the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, as it underscores the importance of recognizing and valuing all forms of productive work, regardless of whether they occur within or outside formal institutions.

The work of Komlos (1998) provides a nuanced perspective on the relationship between institutions and economic growth, particularly in the context of Roman economic history. Komlos argues that the Industrial Revolution, a pivotal period in the development of modern economic growth, was influenced by structural changes rather than solely institutional ones.

Komlos (Komlos_1998: p.2) defines modern economic growth in a Kuznetsian sense, implying a focus on long-term, sustained economic expansion that is characterized by significant shifts in population, urbanization, and income distribution. This perspective complicates the argument that institutions are the sole drivers of long-run economic growth, suggesting that structural changes may also play a crucial role.

Moreover, Komlos (Komlos_1998: p.16) identifies 8-year height cycles similar to classical business cycles in the biological growth process during the Industrial Revolution. This finding implies that short-term economic fluctuations could have significant impacts on long-term economic growth and institutional development.

In addition, Komlos (Komlos_1998: p.17) highlights the importance of structural changes, including population growth, urbanization, income inequality, and income variability, in shaping the Industrial Revolution. This emphasis on structural changes further complicates the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

Scholars Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), while not directly addressing the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, provide tangential insights that contribute to the understanding of this complex issue. Both authors emphasize the role of labor institutions in shaping economic structures.

Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) argues that when labor is tied to land or an owner, competition among employers ceases, which could potentially hinder growth. Conversely, he suggests that the Russian peasants' enserfment and the American South's slaveholding may have impeded economic development (Phillips, "The Economic Cost of Slaveholding in the Cotton Belt," Pol., cited on Domar_1970: p.16).

Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) reveals that the Western impact was not limited to economic institutions but also extended to slave dealing and status acquisition, which could have influenced long-term growth patterns. However, he highlights that restrictions on women's numbers and geographical mobility may have hindered economic development (Bohannan_1959: p.13).

Despite these insights, both authors fall short of establishing institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. The question remains unresolved as to whether institutional changes are more crucial than structural changes, urbanization, income inequality, or income variability in shaping the Industrial Revolution and subsequent economic growth. Further research is necessary to clarify this debate and establish a definitive understanding of the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth.
