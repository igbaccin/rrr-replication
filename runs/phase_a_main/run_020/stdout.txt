LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question under examination in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth. This question holds significant importance as it has implications for understanding historical economic development and informing contemporary policy decisions aimed at promoting economic progress.

Supporting the thesis, Kuznets (1973) posits that the succession of technological innovations during modern economic growth is associated with rapid changes in economic structure, which in turn influence other aspects of society (p.4). North & Weingast (1989) further argue that representative government institutions, characterized by multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are intimately related to economic growth and political freedom (p.28). Acemoglu et al. (2001) also support this view, demonstrating a large effect of institutions on economic performance (p.19).

However, not all sources fully endorse the idea that institutions are the sole cause of long-term economic growth. Austin (2008), for instance, complicates this argument by suggesting that institutions may reinforce rather than originate certain economic positions, such as Africa's relatively poor position compared to Western Europe and parts of Asia (p.24). Broadberry & Gardner (2022) also add nuance by noting the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 in Sub-Saharan Africa, which has led to its absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth (p.1).

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) further complicate the issue by highlighting how understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution becomes subsumed under household production can undermine policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles (p.3). This suggests that a comprehensive understanding of institutions and their impact on long-term economic growth must consider the role of gender in economic development.

As this review illustrates, while some sources support the thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, others complicate this argument by suggesting that institutions may reinforce existing economic positions or highlighting the need to consider other factors such as gender roles in economic development. This leaves room for further exploration and debate on the role of institutions in shaping long-term economic growth.

The role of institutions in shaping long-term economic growth is further substantiated by the works of various scholars. Kuznets (1973) emphasizes that economic growth and structural change are intertwined, with technological innovations playing a significant role in this process (Kuznets_1973: p.6). He also acknowledges the non-conventional costs associated with economic growth (Kuznets_1973: p.11), suggesting that institutions can help mitigate these negative effects.

North & Weingast (1989) focus on the political aspects of institutions, arguing that representative government and a legal system that prioritizes economic affairs are crucial for economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28). Mokyr (2005) supports this view by highlighting the importance of institutionalized processes in shaping progressive societies, including individualism, self-governance, and formal law (Mokyr_2005: p.56).

North (1989) discusses how institutions can reduce transaction costs and increase security rights, which are essential for market development and economic growth (North_1989: p.9). Ogilvie (2007) acknowledges the interdependency of individual institutions within a broader framework, suggesting that a comprehensive understanding of economic institutions is necessary for explaining economic development (Ogilvie_2007: p.27).

deVries (1994) underscores the importance of Europe's urban network in regional economic development during the early modern period, establishing a framework that facilitated industrial growth (deVries_1994: p.5). These findings collectively support the argument that institutions are indeed fundamental causes of long-run economic growth.

However, further exploration is needed to understand how institutions interact with other factors such as geography, technology, and cultural values in shaping economic development over time. This could provide a more nuanced understanding of the role of institutions in long-term economic growth.

Institutions significantly influence long-run economic growth, particularly in the agricultural sector. Acemoglu et al. (2001) and Acemoglu et al. (2002) highlight that institutions play a crucial role in shaping property rights and investment, thereby impacting economic progress.

Acemoglu et al. (2001) argue that certain fundamental institutions have a substantial effect on economic performance (p.19). They also demonstrate that differences in mortality rates are not the primary cause of variation in institutions, as some believe (p.3). A more detailed analysis of the impact of these institutions on property reveals their detrimental effects (p.27).

Acemoglu et al. (2002) assert that understanding economic and political development over the past 500 years necessitates considering the role of institutions (p.49). They also notice a change in the distribution of economic prosperity, which could be linked to institutional changes (p.6).

Abramovitz (1986) supports this argument by emphasizing that the elaboration of mixed economies is a function of economic growth itself (p.20). He also mentions three macroeconomic and monetary conditions encouraging capital investment and effective demand, which are closely related to institutional structures (p.7).

Broadberry & Gupta (2006) suggest that the service sector played a crucial role in economic growth during the early modern period, as in the modern period (p.9). They also acknowledge that British economic growth during the Industrial Revolution was slower than previously thought, indicating the importance of institutions in shaping long-term economic growth (p.25).

These sources collectively support the notion that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, particularly in agriculture. Further exploration into how institutions interact with other factors such as geography, technology, and cultural values could provide a more nuanced understanding of this relationship.

The argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is supported by evidence from various historical contexts. Hopkins (2009) asserts that ethnic fragmentation, often proposed as a hindrance to productive institutions, requires further convincing demonstrations to discount other causes of solidarity and motives for action (Hopkins_2009: p.22). This suggests that the role of institutions in economic growth may not be solely influenced by ethnicity but could be intertwined with other factors such as community support or shared values.

Moreover, Hopkins (2009) critiques the methodology underlying studies that attribute unproductive institutions to ethnic fragmentation (Hopkins_2009: p.10). This critique implies a need for improved research methods to accurately assess the impact of institutions on economic growth in diverse cultural settings.

Bryant (2006) supports this perspective by stating that certain historical environments provided more than adequate conditions for commercial exchange, suggesting that institutions played a significant role in fostering economic growth (Bryant_2006: p.13). Furthermore, Bryant (2006) highlights the fusion of mercantilist imperialism and capitalistic transformation as a fundamental axis of difference, implying that institutional structures were instrumental in driving these historical changes (Bryant_2006: p.5).

These findings support the thesis that institutions are crucial for long-run economic growth, particularly in agriculture. The interplay between institutions, geography, technology, and cultural values merits further exploration to gain a comprehensive understanding of this relationship.

The assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth faces challenges when considering the relationship between institutions and education. According to Simson (Simson_2019: p.27), in several African countries, there was an emphasis on industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy during periods of high economic growth. This suggests that while institutions may play a role, other factors such as industrialization and education could also significantly contribute to economic growth.

Similarly, McCloskey (McCloskey_2015: p.9) argues that in France, economic growth was throttled for conservative reasons despite the examples of economic success in other regions like Holland and England. This implies that while institutions might have been less favorable towards economic growth in France, external factors such as neighboring countries' successes could still influence economic development.

In a similar vein, Humphries (Humphries_2013: p.18) contends that the division of labor, workshop production, and the eventual emergence of the factory system were key to 18th-century economic growth, rather than institutions alone. This suggests that technological advancements and industrial organization might be equally important factors in long-run economic growth.

Finally, vanZanden (vanZanden_1999: p.1) notes that the consensus view in the 1960s and 1970s was that the early modern economy was unable to generate economic growth. However, recent years have seen a shift away from this pessimistic interpretation, implying that factors other than institutions might have played a significant role in pre-industrial economic growth.

The relationship between institutions and education is a crucial aspect that adds nuance to the argument of institutions being the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Education, as Komlos (1998) suggests, can be linked to modern economic growth, with physical stature during the Industrial Revolution showing signs of correlation with economic processes and associated structural changes (Komlos_1998: p.16). This implies that education might have played a role in fostering economic growth, as improved educational standards could lead to increased productivity and innovation.

Moreover, Temin (2002) posits that the exhaustion of cheap labor led to economic growth slowing down, yet he also acknowledges that recent theories of economic growth should consider the historical context, which includes economic transition, and the role of education in this process (Temin_2002: p.7). This suggests that education could have been a significant factor driving the economic transitions that led to long-term growth.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) discuss the relationship between economic growth and equality, highlighting that policymakers have often sought to balance these two factors. They suggest that understanding long-run distribution trends is closely tied to explaining economic growth itself, implying a potential connection between inequality and institutional development that could impact economic growth over time (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1).

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) argue that initial conditions or factor endowments, including institutions, played a profound role in shaping long-run economic growth paths. They suggest that the contrasting records in growth can be traced back to the achievement of sustained economic growth by certain countries during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, with institutions being a key factor in this process (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.12).

Frankema (2012) argues that colonial education was a significant factor in explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa, with former British colonies growing faster due to their educational lead (Frankema_2012: p.2). This indicates that education could have been a crucial driver of economic growth in these regions, as it potentially provided the necessary skills and knowledge for increased productivity and innovation.

The assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is not without complications, as highlighted by various scholars. For instance, Austin (2008) points out that while institutions may have played a crucial role in driving growth, they did not necessarily originate the economic disparities between Africa and Western Europe. Instead, certain trades reinforced these differences, contributing to Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to western Europe.

Similarly, Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) argue that the focus on institutions as the sole driver of growth may oversimplify the complex interplay between various economic and political factors. They emphasize that all players in the market influence each other's strategies, suggesting a more nuanced understanding of the relationship between institutions and economic growth is necessary.

Allen et al. (2011) add another layer of complexity by demonstrating that income disparities in the early 20th century were due to long-run stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe. This finding suggests that while institutions may have played a role in promoting growth, other factors such as technological advancements and geopolitical conditions also significantly influence economic development.

The literature on long-run economic growth further underscores the complex nature of institutions as a driving force for development, with additional nuances provided by Broadberry and Gardner (2022) and Akyeampong and Fofack (2014).

Broadberry and Gardner (2022) challenge the prevailing narrative that Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA) has been absent from discussions about long-term growth due to insufficient data on economic performance before 1950. They highlight that SSA's poor economic performance relative to other regions has been well-documented, with Collier and Gunning (1999) and Sachs and Warner (1997) both noting this trend. However, understanding the extent to which historical growth patterns in SSA provide a pathway to convergence requires an examination of the risks that have hampered economic progress in the past. Moreover, new contributions to historical national accounting have provided insights into long-term growth and development patterns in other regions, rewriting the history of economic divergence and convergence since before the industrial revolution (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1).

Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) contribute to the discourse by focusing on the role of women in African economies, a factor often overlooked in discussions about long-run growth. They argue that understanding the processes by which women's economic contributions have been subsumed under household production is crucial for strengthening women's economic roles. The authors also emphasize that the contribution of African women to economic growth and development has largely been dependent on the economic provision of their husbands (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.9, p.19). Furthermore, they highlight that major historical developments in West Africa, such as the Atlantic slave trade and colonialism, had significant gender-related implications for economic growth that can be paralleled elsewhere in Africa (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.4).

These sources complicate the argument that institutions are the sole cause of long-run economic growth by emphasizing the importance of understanding the role of women in African economies and the historical contexts that have shaped their economic contributions. Additionally, they underscore the need for more comprehensive data on economic performance in regions like SSA to fully understand the complex interplay of factors contributing to long-term growth.

Scholars Domar (1970), Bohannan (1959), and Bryceson (2002) offer valuable insights into the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth, albeit from different perspectives. Domar (1970) emphasizes that when labor is tied to land or an owner, competition among employers ceases, thereby hindering economic growth (p.4). Bohannan (1959) highlights the Western impact on African economies, specifically the establishment of slave dealing and status acquisition institutions, which may have hindered long-term development (p.7). Bryceson (2002) focuses on the impact of traditional hierarchical orders on women's economic and social spheres, suggesting that these structures can limit opportunities for growth (p.9).

Despite their different focal points, all three scholars converge on the idea that institutions play a crucial role in shaping economic outcomes. They diverge, however, in their emphasis on specific types of institutions and their impact on various segments of society.

What remains unresolved is the need for more comprehensive data on economic performance in regions like Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA) to fully understand the complex interplay of factors contributing to long-term growth. As Bryceson (2002) points out, large-scale surveys have failed to capture the groundswell of livelihood diversification taking place (p.2). Phillips' work on the economic cost of slaveholding in the Cotton Belt, as cited by Domar (1970), underscores the need for more detailed historical data (p.16). Therefore, future research should aim to fill these data gaps to better understand the role of institutions in driving long-run economic growth in SSA.
