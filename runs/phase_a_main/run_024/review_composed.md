The central question in the literature review on institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is whether the structure and function of societal organizations play a pivotal role in driving economic development over extended periods. This question holds significant implications for understanding historical patterns of growth and informing contemporary policies aimed at fostering economic progress.

Several scholars support the thesis that institutions are indeed the primary driver of long-term economic growth. For instance, Austin (Austin_2008: p.26) argues that "institutions as the fundamental cause of long-rungrowth" is an attractive conclusion due to its logical economy. Similarly, North & Weingast (North&Weingast_1989: p.28) demonstrate how representative government institutions, such as multiple veto points and common law courts primacy over economic affairs, are integral to economic growth and political freedom.

On the other hand, some scholars offer critiques or add nuance and qualifications to this argument. Broadberry & Gardner (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1) complicate the debate by pointing out that the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has kept Sub-Saharan Africa absent from recent discussions about comparative long-run growth. Akyeampong & Fofack (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3) also add nuance by highlighting how understanding the processes through which women's economic contributions are subsumed under household production is crucial for strengthening women's economic roles.

The stakes of this debate are high, as understanding the role of institutions in long-term economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at fostering development and reducing inequality. As Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19) show, differences in institutions have a significant effect on economic performance, with detrimental institutions hindering investment and progress. Conversely, supportive institutions can facilitate growth, as North & Weingast (North&Weingast_1989: p.30) demonstrate through the example of representative government institutions.

The assertion that institutions are a fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is substantiated by various scholars who have delved into the intricate relationship between political and economic factors in shaping development over extended periods. Douglas North and Barry Weingast, in their seminal work published in 1989, emphasize the significance of representative government institutions with multiple veto points, along with the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, in fostering economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

Similarly, North's individual work from the same year highlights the importance of centralized enforcement mechanisms in shaping economic growth (North_1989: p.1). He argues that increased security rights and a reduction in transaction costs reflect the development of institutions that contribute to long-term economic growth.

John Ogilvie, in his 2007 study, supports this perspective by acknowledging the interdependency of individual institutions within a broader framework and their role in fostering economic growth (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). He also emphasizes that the efficiency theory, which assumes most people want the economy to grow as much as possible, is an essential aspect of understanding institutional development and its impact on long-run economic growth.

Daron Acemoglu, Simon Johnson, and James Robinson, in their 2002 work, provide empirical evidence supporting the argument that institutions play a crucial role in shaping long-term economic prosperity (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49). They demonstrate how changes in institutional arrangements can lead to significant shifts in economic prosperity over time.

Lastly, Abramovitz (1986) and Hopkins (2009) also support the thesis by highlighting the role of institutions in fostering productivity growth and economic development, respectively (Abramovitz_1986: p.20; Hopkins_2009: p.21).

Institutions significantly influence long-term economic growth, as evidenced by their impact on productivity and labor markets. Acemoglu et al. (2001) emphasize the role of fundamental institutions in shaping property rights and investment, highlighting how detrimental institutions can hinder economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3, p.27). The authors demonstrate a large effect of institutions on economic performance through their analysis (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19).

Mokyr (2005) supports this view by focusing on the intellectual foundations that fostered economic growth in the West. He argues that the change in the rate and nature of economic growth can be attributed to developments in the intellectual realm concerning "useful knowledge" (Mokyr_2005: p.4). Mokyr also emphasizes the importance of institutionalized processes that allow for individualism, man-made formal law, corporatism, self governance, and rules determined through an institutionalized process (Mokyr_2005: p.56).

Whatley (2018) provides further evidence by examining the relationship between gunpowder imports and slave exports in the Atlantic economy. The author's econometric results suggest a long-run equilibrium relationship between these variables, indicating that institutions play a crucial role in shaping economic dynamics (Whatley_2018: p.13).

Allen (2001) supports this argument by investigating wages and prices, which are central to understanding economic development, economic leadership, and the standard of living (Allen_2001: p.1). The author's findings suggest that wages of building craftsmen are indicative of trends in average earnings, providing reassurance that these data can be used to study long-term economic growth (Allen_2001: p.4).

Nunn and Wantchekon (2011) also contribute to the discussion by focusing on the effects of institutional quality on economic development in Africa. The authors demonstrate that both internal and external channels are important, but that the internal channel is at least twice as large as the external channel (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.4). This finding underscores the crucial role of institutions in shaping economic growth within a region.

The role of institutions in fostering industrialization and subsequent economic growth is further supported by various studies. According to Austin (2008), institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth. He argues that institutions set the framework for regional economic development, allowing industrial growth to occur (Austin_2008: p.5). This perspective aligns with the thesis, as it emphasizes the internal channel of growth driven by institutional factors.

DeVries (1994) also underscores the significance of institutions in shaping economic growth, particularly during the Industrial Revolution. He highlights the importance of Europe's urban network, which established a framework for regional economic development and facilitated industrial growth (deVries_1994: p.5). This finding supports the assertion that institutions play a critical role in long-run economic growth by shaping the conditions conducive to industrialization.

Broadberry & Gupta (2006) also contribute to this discourse, suggesting that the service sector played a crucial role in economic growth during the early modern period and the Industrial Revolution (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.9). Their work implies that institutions, by creating an environment conducive to service sector development, can foster long-term economic growth.

Despite the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, some scholars challenge this notion, particularly in relation to the labor market. Simson (2019) critiques the emphasis placed on industrial development and educational expansion during the 1960s and 1970s in certain African countries, suggesting that these expansions may not have been sufficient to sustain economic growth or reorient economies (Simson_2019: p.4). Furthermore, Simson questions whether eliminating spending inefficiencies, leakages, and costly delays would have been enough to maintain the investment levels of the 1960s (Simson_2019: p.4).

Humphries (2013) also challenges the thesis by emphasizing the importance of a growing division of labor, the rise of workshop production, and the eventual emergence of the factory system in eighteenth-century economic growth (Humphries_2013: p.18). Humphries further argues that the relative needs of women and children may have hindered economic growth due to their significant dependence on them (Humphries_2013: p.11, p.19).

These findings suggest that while institutions play a role in economic growth, other factors such as labor market dynamics, industrial development, and demographic changes may also be crucial. Koepke & Baten (2005) suggest that over the period of the Roman Empire, living standards did not increase significantly, contrasting the common view that economic growth and protection led to higher living standards (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.16).

The Maddison Project's revisions of nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth rates have added new data for several countries, but they do not fundamentally change our understanding of the causes of long-run economic growth (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5). Additionally, Williamson highlights that mass migrations could reduce real wage growth in receiving countries by glutting labor markets abroad.

However, van Zanden also notes a negative link between output measures of the standard of living and the level of economic development in the mid-eighteenth century, suggesting that structural changes may not always correlate with growth.

The role of institutions in shaping long-run economic growth is a topic that has garnered significant attention among scholars, as evidenced by the works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959). Both authors acknowledge the importance of institutions in determining economic outcomes. Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) posits that with labor tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, thereby impeding growth. On the other hand, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) highlights how Western impact, particularly on economic institutions, had profound effects on societies.

Both authors converge in their recognition of institutions as crucial factors shaping economic growth. However, they diverge in their focus and scope. Domar's work primarily delves into the relationship between labor, land, and competition, while Bohannan explores the impact of Western institutions on African societies.

Despite these insights, several questions remain unresolved. Similarly, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.10) notes that the Western impact was not limited to economic institutions, yet he does not elaborate on other aspects influenced by these interactions.
