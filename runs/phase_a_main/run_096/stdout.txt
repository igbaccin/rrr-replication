LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The debate surrounding the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth, particularly in Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA), has been a subject of intense scholarly discussion. The central question at hand is whether institutions are indeed the primary driver of economic growth over the long run, and if so, what implications does this have for understanding historical and contemporary economic disparities between regions?

On one side, proponents argue that institutions play a crucial role in shaping economic growth (AcemogluEtAl_2001; Kuznets_1973). For instance, Acemoglu et al. (2001) assert that certain institutional structures can either promote or hinder investment and economic progress. Similarly, Kuznets (1973) posits that social innovations, which provide the necessary adjustments for modern technological growth, are major factors affecting economic structure and growth.

On the other hand, some scholars complicate this perspective by suggesting that institutions might not be the sole cause of long-term economic growth, especially in SSA (Austin_2008; Broadberry&Gardner_2022). Austin (2008) argues that examining growth theories against long-term history can reveal relationships that recur due to path dependence rather than institutions being the fundamental cause. Similarly, Broadberry and Gardner (2022) highlight the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 in SSA, which has resulted in its absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth.

Moreover, some authors add nuance or qualifications to the debate by discussing factors such as gender roles and women's contributions to economic growth (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014; Komlos_1998). For example, Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) argue that understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution becomes subsumed under household production is essential for policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles.

The stakes of this debate are significant as they have implications for economic development policy, particularly in regions like SSA where growth has been historically slower compared to other parts of the world. Understanding whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth can provide valuable insights into how to design effective policies aimed at promoting sustainable economic development. The debate continues as scholars grapple with the complex interplay between institutions, historical factors, and contemporary economic realities in shaping economic growth patterns across different regions.

Institutions have been identified as a primary factor in shaping long-term economic growth patterns across various regions. Acemoglu, et al. (2001) assert that the more fundamental institutions on property rights play a crucial role in promoting investment and economic progress (p.27). This is supported by their observation that differences in mortality rates do not significantly contribute to variations in institutions (p.3), suggesting that historical factors may not be the sole determinants of institutional development.

Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) emphasize the importance of political institutions in fostering economic growth and market development (p.2). They argue that absolutist states with centralized enforcement mechanisms retarded long-run economic growth (p.7), while those with institutions enabling rule changes promoted economic progress (p.30).

Mokyr (2005) contends that the growth of economic reasonableness, which includes individualism, formal law, corporatism, self-governance, and rules determined through institutionalized processes, played a significant role in promoting economic development in the West (p.4, p.56). He suggests that this change in intellectual realm concerning "useful knowledge" is crucial to understanding why the West became so much richer today (p.4).

North (1989) also supports the thesis by suggesting that centralized enforcement mechanisms in Spain hindered economic growth, while institutions that reduced transaction costs and increased security rights contributed to market development and economic progress (p.1, p.6, p.9).

Institutions play a crucial role in fostering long-run economic growth, as evidenced by numerous studies on institutional development and its impact on economic progress. Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) argue that initial conditions and factor endowments significantly influenced the economic trajectories of various countries, suggesting that unique institutional arrangements could have had profound and enduring effects on long-term growth. For instance, the United States and Canada achieved sustained economic growth during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, contrasting with other nations that did not attain this goal until later (Sokoloff & Engerman 2000: p.12).

Abramovitz (1986) supports this notion by emphasizing the role of institutions in shaping economic incentives and productivity growth. He suggests that the elaboration of mixed economies is a function of economic growth itself, implying that strong institutions can drive innovation and investment, leading to sustained economic expansion (Abramovitz 1986: p.20).

Pamuk (2007) provides additional evidence by linking the Black Death and institutional changes with modern economic growth. The demographic shifts resulting from the plague led to the commercialization of agriculture, which in turn facilitated economic development (Pamuk 2007: p.4).

Broadberry & Gupta (2006) further support this thesis by highlighting the role of service sectors in early modern period growth and the crucial part institutions played in shaping these sectors. They argue that rural peasants supplementing their agricultural activities with manufacturing contributed to economic growth, underscoring the importance of institutional arrangements that facilitate structural change (Broadberry & Gupta 2006: p.9).

The interplay between institutions and technological advancements significantly contributes to economic growth, as suggested by Kuznets (1973) and Whatley (2018). Kuznets (Kuznets_1973: p.4) posits that rapid changes in economic structure are closely associated with societal transformations, including urbanization and family structures, underscoring the importance of institutional arrangements that facilitate such structural shifts. Moreover, he emphasizes the role of technological innovations as a major factor driving economic growth (Kuznets_1973: p.6).

Whatley (2018) provides empirical evidence supporting this perspective by analyzing the relationship between gunpowder imports and slave exports. His findings suggest that an excess of gunpowder, which can be seen as a technological advancement, leads to increased slave exports (Whatley_2018: p.11). This demonstrates how institutional arrangements can facilitate or impede the adoption and diffusion of technological innovations, thereby impacting economic growth.

The works of Simson (2019) and Humphries (2013) challenge the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, particularly in relation to capital investment.

Simson (2019) argues that while there was a focus on industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy in certain regions during the 1960s and 1970s, it is questionable whether eliminating spending inefficiencies would have been enough to sustain the investment levels of that period (Simson_2019: p.4). Moreover, Simson suggests that economic growth in the sub-region recovered in the 2000s, which could indicate that factors other than institutions played a significant role in driving growth (Simson_2019: p.4).

Humphries (2013) contends that eighteenth-century economic growth was primarily driven by a growing division of labor, the rise of workshop production, and the eventual emergence of the factory system, rather than institutional arrangements (Humphries_2013: p.18). Furthermore, Humphries argues that the assumption that all families had a male head on whom to depend is ahistorical, which could imply that institutional structures were not as crucial for economic growth as previously thought (Humphries_2013: p.14).

These findings suggest that while institutions may play a role in economic growth, other factors such as technological advancements, division of labor, and global optimism about future prosperity could be equally or more important. This challenges the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

Structural change in economic activities, rather than institutions, may play a significant role in long-run economic growth and environmental impact. According to Stern (2004), as economies develop, they tend to shift from agriculture to industry and then to services, which can lead to changes in environmental impacts (Stern_2004: p.3). This structural change, not necessarily the institutions, could be responsible for rising or declining environmental degradation over the course of economic development.

Moreover, Stern (2004) argues that economic growth, contrary to the claims made by the environmental movement, may eventually lead to a reduction in environmental damage (Stern_2004: p.1). The author contends that as economies grow and become more technologically advanced, they are better equipped to manage and mitigate environmental degradation (Stern_2004: p.8). This suggests that institutions might not be the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, but rather the structural changes and technological advancements that occur as a result of growth could play a more significant role.

However, it is important to note that Stern (2004) assumes that environmental damage does not reduce economic activity sufficiently to stop the growth process and that any irreversibility is not so severe that it reduces the level of income in the future (Stern_2004: p.8). This implies that there might be instances where environmental degradation could hinder economic growth, challenging the assertion that structural change alone can explain long-run economic growth.

The assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is further complicated by various factors, as suggested by several studies. For instance, climate and environmental conditions have played a significant role in shaping human living standards over time, which could potentially influence economic growth (Koepke & Baten_2005: p.20). The relationship between climate and economic development is intricate, with periods of extreme weather or environmental degradation having the potential to hinder long-term growth.

Moreover, the correlation between economic growth and income distribution has been a subject of debate among historians (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.9). The historical evidence suggests that both growth and inequality can influence each other, challenging the notion that institutions alone are responsible for long-run economic growth.

In addition, the role of education in explaining long-run growth disparities has been a topic of discussion (Frankema_2012: p.2). Some researchers argue that colonial education played a crucial role in shaping the economic trajectories of former British colonies, with those that received more education growing faster over time. However, other factors such as post-colonial levels of capital investment and rates of economic growth also need to be considered.

Furthermore, the impact of colonialism on long-run economic growth has been a contentious issue (Hopkins_2019: p.4). While some argue that food exports made little contribution to economic growth in West Africa during the period of the slave trade, others suggest that the economic base crumbled, leading to an increased importance of the superstructure.

Lastly, the role of market dynamics and global interactions in shaping long-run economic growth has been highlighted by some scholars (Braudel & Wallerstein_2009: p.21). They argue that all players in the market influence each other's strategies, suggesting a more complex interplay between institutions, markets, and economic growth than previously thought.

The literature on institutions and economic growth further emphasizes the complex interplay between these factors, particularly in the context of long-term development. The data deficiency concerning Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA) before 1950 has been a significant hurdle in debates about comparative long-run growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). This lack of historical data raises questions about the economic performance and potential for convergence in SSA. Furthermore, some economists have suggested alternative data to track African economic growth in recent decades, adding nuance to the ongoing discourse (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.10).

Moreover, understanding the role of women's economic contributions is crucial for formulating effective policies aimed at strengthening their roles in economic growth and development (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). The historical processes that subsumed women's economic contribution under household production and unaccounted labor have significant implications for policy-making (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). Parallels between West Africa and other African regions suggest that gender and economic growth are interconnected issues across the continent (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.4).

These sources complicate the argument by highlighting data deficiencies, the importance of women's contributions to economic growth, and regional variations in gender roles and economic development. These nuances underscore the need for a more comprehensive understanding of the complex interplay between institutions, markets, and economic growth, particularly in regions like Sub-Saharan Africa.

The interplay between institutions and capital investment plays a significant role in the long-run economic growth, as demonstrated by Komlos (1998) and Austin (2007).

Komlos (1998) suggests that structural changes, including population growth and urbanization, were crucial factors during the Industrial Revolution. He highlights the discovery of 8-year height cycles, which implies that biological growth was influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic processes (Komlos_1998: p.16). Furthermore, Komlos emphasizes that these structural changes were associated with modern economic growth (Komlos_1998: p.17).

On the other hand, Austin (2007) underscores the importance of cheaper transportation methods, such as railways, in fostering capital investment and economic growth. He argues that until the mid-1920s, railways had a significant advantage over lorries due to their cost efficiency (Austin_2007: p.8). Additionally, Austin suggests that population growth was faster after 1918, which could have impacted capital investment and economic development (Austin_2007: p.12).

These findings add nuance to the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. They suggest that structural changes, population growth, and cost-effective transportation methods may also play significant roles in this process. In Sub-Saharan Africa, understanding these factors could provide valuable insights into the region's economic development. However, further research is needed to fully elucidate their interplay and impact on long-term economic growth.

The works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) offer tangential insights into the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth, albeit from distinct perspectives. Both scholars acknowledge the crucial role of institutions in shaping economic development, particularly through their impact on labor markets and competition.

Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) posits that when labor is tied to land or owners, employer competition ceases, which can hinder growth by limiting the efficiency of resource allocation. Conversely, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) illustrates how the Western impact on African societies led to the centralization of economic activities such as slave dealing and status acquisition, potentially fostering institutional structures that could either stimulate or impede long-term growth.

Despite these shared observations, there is a divergence in their focus. Domar's (1970) analysis primarily revolves around labor markets and employer competition, while Bohannan (1959) delves into the broader societal impacts of Western influence on African institutions.

Unresolved questions remain regarding the specific mechanisms through which these institutional factors influence long-term economic growth. For instance, it is yet to be fully elucidated how the centralization of economic activities as observed by Bohannan (1959) affects the overall development trajectory of a region like Sub-Saharan Africa. Furthermore, Domar's (Domar_1970: p.4) assertion that labor tied to land or owners can hinder growth requires further investigation into alternative institutional arrangements that could promote competition and resource efficiency.

In this context, future research should aim to provide a more nuanced understanding of the interplay between these factors and their impact on long-term economic growth in various regions, particularly Sub-Saharan Africa. This could potentially offer valuable insights into strategies for promoting sustainable development in the region.
