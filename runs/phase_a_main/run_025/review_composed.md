The central question in the literature review on the role of institutions in long-term economic growth is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of economic development, or if other factors play a more significant role. This question holds substantial implications for understanding and addressing global economic disparities.

Supporting this thesis, Austin (2008) argues that institutions provide the foundation for long-run economic growth, reinforcing rather than originating economic positions compared to Western Europe. Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) demonstrate that representative government institutions with multiple veto points and common law courts over economic affairs are crucial for economic growth and political freedom. Acemoglu et al. (2001) support this notion by highlighting the detrimental effects of certain institutions on investment and economic progress.

On the other hand, some sources offer critiques or complicate the thesis. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) suggest that a lack of data before 1950 has excluded Sub-Saharan Africa from recent growth debates, potentially undermining the generalizability of the institutions-growth relationship. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) complicate the issue by pointing out how women's economic contributions have been subsumed under household production and their labor formally unaccounted for, which may undermine policies aiming to strengthen women's economic roles.

As the debate continues, understanding the role of institutions in long-term economic growth remains a critical area of research, with implications for policy-making and global development strategies. Future studies should consider addressing the critiques raised by Broadberry & Gardner (2022) and Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) to provide a more comprehensive understanding of this complex relationship.

The relationship between institutions and economic growth is further substantiated by several scholars, providing robust evidence for the thesis. Kuznets (1973) asserts that economic growth is not only associated with rapid changes in economic structure but also in other aspects of society (Kuznets_1973: p.4). He emphasizes the role of technological innovations and social innovations in driving economic growth, which are crucial components of institutional development.

Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) argue that the fundamental institutions of representative government, such as multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, play a significant role in promoting economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28). They highlight the importance of institutional design in facilitating market development and ensuring that rules are enforced effectively.

Mokyr (2005) supports this view, suggesting that changes in intellectual developments contributed to the growth of economic reasonableness, which is a key aspect of institutional development (Mokyr_2005: p.56). He argues that the change in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West can be explained through developments in the intellectual realm concerning "useful knowledge."

North (1989) focuses on the role of institutions in reducing transaction costs, which is essential for market development and economic growth (North_1989: p.9). He suggests that institutions that increase security rights and property rights are crucial for promoting long-term economic growth.

Lastly, Ogilvie (2007) emphasizes the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework, suggesting that the failure of one institution can have cascading effects on other institutions and economic growth (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). He underscores the importance of understanding the broader institutional context in which economic growth occurs.

These scholars provide compelling evidence for the thesis that institutions are fundamental causes of long-run economic growth. By promoting market development, reducing transaction costs, and facilitating technological and intellectual progress, institutions play a crucial role in driving economic growth over the long term. Future studies should continue to explore the complex relationship between institutions and economic growth, addressing critiques raised by Broadberry & Gardner (2022) and Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) to provide a more comprehensive understanding of this relationship.

The works of Acemoglu and his colleagues offer compelling evidence that institutions play a crucial role in long-term economic growth, particularly in the agricultural sector. In their 2001 study (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27), they demonstrate that certain fundamental institutions significantly impact property rights, which in turn affect investment and economic progress. This is further supported by their findings that differences in mortality rates are not the primary cause of institutional variations (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3).

In a subsequent study from 2002 (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49), they reiterate that institutions are a fundamental cause of long-term economic growth, stating it as a "first-order fact." They also highlight the reversal in the distribution of economic prosperity, suggesting that this change could be linked to institutional changes (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5). This shift underscores the profound impact institutions can have on economic development over extended periods.

These findings support the argument that institutions are indeed a primary driver of long-term economic growth, particularly in agriculture. The works of Acemoglu and his colleagues provide evidence for the significant role institutional structures play in promoting investment, economic progress, and overall prosperity. As such, further research should focus on understanding the intricate relationship between institutions and agricultural development to gain a more comprehensive understanding of this relationship.

The relationship between institutions and economic growth is further substantiated by the works of Abramovitz (1986) and van Zanden (2009). Abramovitz (Abramovitz_1986: p.7) highlights that macroeconomic and monetary conditions promoting capital investment and effective demand are crucial for fostering economic growth. This notion is echoed by van Zanden (vanZanden_2009: p.4), who suggests that periods of economic growth may lead to a rising skill premium in the construction industry, which in turn impacts the relative price of capital goods and contributes to long-term economic growth.

Moreover, Abramovitz (Abramovitz_1986: p.20) posits that the elaboration of the mixed economy is a function of economic growth itself, implying that institutional development may be an outcome rather than a cause of economic progress. However, this perspective does not diminish the significance of institutions in long-run economic growth; instead, it underscores the importance of understanding the reciprocal relationship between institutions and economic development.

van Zanden (vanZanden_2009: p.1) emphasizes the importance of relative prices as the "DNA of an economy," suggesting that the skill premium, which is influenced by institutional factors such as education policies and labor market regulations, plays a crucial role in shaping the structure of the economy and determining long-term economic growth. This perspective aligns with the thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, as they shape the incentives, institutions, and structures that facilitate or hinder economic progress.

The perspective that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth faces challenges, as some scholars argue that these institutions may contribute to economic inequality and hinder development in certain contexts. For instance, Stern (2004) questions the Environmental Kuznets Curve (EKC) hypothesis, which suggests that economic growth leads to environmental improvement over time. Instead, Stern contends that economic growth can lead to increased environmental degradation, particularly during early stages of development (Stern_2004: p.1).

Similarly, McCloskey (2015) critiques the claim by arguing that ideas and ideologies, not institutions, are crucial for fostering economic growth. McCloskey provides examples of countries like France, which had the potential for economic growth but were hindered by conservative ideologies that prioritized Catholicism over economic progress (McCloskey_2015: p.9).

Moreover, Bryceson (2002) challenges the thesis by highlighting how traditional hierarchical orders within communities can limit women's economic and social spheres, leading to dependence on non-agricultural activities or migrant labor (Bryceson_2002: p.9). Humphries (2013) also critiques the thesis by suggesting that the division of labor, workshop production, and the eventual emergence of the factory system were key factors in 18th-century economic growth, rather than institutions (Humphries_2013: p.18).

These sources challenge the notion that institutions are the primary driver of long-run economic growth by suggesting alternative factors, such as ideas and ideologies, or structural changes like the division of labor, may play a more significant role in fostering economic development. However, further investigation is needed to fully understand the complex interplay between institutions, ideas, and structures in shaping long-term economic growth.

The interplay between institutions and economic inequality in shaping long-term economic growth is a complex issue that requires further exploration. Komlos (1998) posits that physical stature during the Industrial Revolution was significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic processes, suggesting that institutional changes may have played a role in determining growth trajectories. Additionally, Komlos highlights the correlation between increased population and urbanization, greater income inequality, and the variability of income with economic growth (Komlos_1998: p.17).

Temin (2002) argues that the exhaustion of cheap labor caused economic growth to slow, implying that institutions may have influenced the availability and cost of labor, which in turn affected long-term economic performance. Furthermore, Temin suggests that the end of the Golden Age was brought about by the return to equilibrium growth paths, not adverse economic shocks (Temin_2002: p.14).

Lindert & Williamson (1985) contend that policymakers have traditionally sought to balance economic growth and equality, but achieving this balance is challenging due to the enormous sample of long-term national data required for empirical analysis (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1). They also argue that the task of explaining long-run distribution trends rejoins the search for the explanations of growth itself, suggesting a strong connection between institutions and economic inequality in shaping growth trajectories.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) argue that initial conditions, or factor endowments broadly conceived, could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth. They suggest that the divergence in paths can be traced back to the achievement of sustained economic growth by the United States and Canada during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, while other countries did not manage to attain this goal until late in the nineteenth century (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.12). This implies that institutions may have played a significant role in determining which countries were able to achieve sustained economic growth.

Frankema (2012) argues that colonial education is the key to explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa, and that the former British colonies grew faster as a result of their educational lead. The degree of British "liberalism" was endogenous to the local political and economic context (Frankema_2012: p.19). This suggests that institutions may have played a role in shaping the educational policies implemented by colonial powers, which in turn affected long-term growth trajectories.

Institutions and economic shocks have a complex relationship in shaping long-run economic growth, as demonstrated by Bolt & van Zanden (2014) and Braudel & Wallerstein (2009). Bolt & van Zanden (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5) argue that revisions of nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth have been made for several countries, including Switzerland, Germany, Sweden, Italy, Greece, Russia, and Bulgaria. However, these revisions do not fundamentally change the understanding of institutions' role in economic growth.

Furthermore, Bolt & van Zanden (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.15) highlight that structural change correlates with growth, suggesting that institutional changes may have played a significant role in facilitating such transformations. On the other hand, Braudel & Wallerstein (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.30) argue that there is a disagreement about institutions' role in economic growth that is more fundamental than it may seem. They contend that institutions and economic shocks are interconnected, with each affecting the other's "strategy" (Braudel & Wallerstein_2009: p.21).

The work of Broadberry and Gardner (2022) adds nuance to the debate on institutions as a fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by focusing on Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA). They argue that the lack of data on SSA's aggregate economic performance before 1950 has left it out of recent discussions about comparative long-term growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1).

However, they contend that this region's economic performance has been markedly worse than other regions, as observed by Collier and Gunning (1999) and Sachs and Warner (1997) (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). To understand whether the growth in SSA provides a path to convergence, it is crucial to assess the extent of risks associated with the region's past economic shrinkage (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.18).

Moreover, Broadberry and Gardner highlight that new contributions to historical national accounting have shed light on growth and development patterns in other regions over centuries, potentially rewriting the history of economic divergence and convergence before the industrial revolution (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). This suggests that a more comprehensive understanding of institutions' role in long-run economic growth may require examining data from various historical periods, including those beyond the industrial era.

The examination of institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is a topic that has garnered significant attention among scholars, with Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) providing valuable insights. Both authors agree on certain key points. For instance, they both acknowledge the importance of labor institutions in shaping economic outcomes. Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) argues that when labor is tied to land or an owner, competition among employers ceases, potentially hindering growth. Similarly, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) notes that the slave-dealing and status acquisition practices in Western Africa were significant economic institutions.

However, there are also notable differences between their perspectives. Domar's (1970) focus is primarily on labor institutions within a capitalist context, while Bohannan (1959) delves into pre-capitalist African societies. This difference in focus leads to divergent conclusions about the role of institutions in economic growth.

Despite these insights, several aspects remain unresolved. For instance, Domar (Domar_1970: p.16) refers to Phillips' work on the economic cost of slaveholding in the Cotton Belt, but does not elaborate further. Exploring this connection could provide a more comprehensive understanding of how institutions impact growth in different historical and geographical contexts.

Moreover, both authors touch upon the role of gender institutions, with Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) suggesting that when labor is tied to land or an owner, competition among employers ceases, potentially affecting women disproportionately. Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.13) also mentions the limitation of women's number in certain African societies. A more detailed examination of gender institutions and their impact on economic growth could further enrich our understanding of this complex relationship.
