The central question under investigation in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, a thesis that has garnered significant attention among scholars. The stakes are high as understanding the factors driving economic growth could inform policies aimed at promoting development and reducing poverty in various regions.

Support for the thesis comes from several sources. Austin (2008) posits that institutions, particularly those reinforcing Africa's poor economic position compared to Western Europe, play a pivotal role in long-term economic growth. Similarly, Kuznets (1973) argues that technological innovations and social changes characteristic of modern economic growth are major factors affecting economic and social structure.

On the other hand, some scholars offer critiques and complications to this thesis. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) note the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 in Sub-Saharan Africa, which has been absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) highlight the importance of understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution becomes subsumed under household production and their labor formally unaccounted for, undermining policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles.

Moreover, North & Weingast (1989) support the thesis by focusing on the political factors underlying economic growth and the development of markets. They demonstrate how institutions, such as representative government with multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are intimately related to economic growth and political freedom.

However, Acemoglu et al. (2001) emphasize that differences in mortality rates are not the only or even the main cause of variation in institutions. They argue that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress. The debate continues as scholars grapple with the complex interplay between institutions, economic growth, and development.

Institutions play a pivotal role in shaping long-term economic growth, with certain institutional structures either promoting or hindering investment and progress (Mokyr_2005: p.4). Mokyr emphasizes the importance of intellectual advancements in driving economic development, suggesting that the West's economic superiority today can be attributed to the growth of "useful knowledge" (Mokyr_2005: p.4). He further argues that elements such as individualism, formal law, corporatism, and self-governance have been instrumental in fostering a progressive society (Mokyr_2005: p.56).

Similarly, North (1989) highlights the impact of institutions on economic growth by examining the centralized enforcement mechanisms of Spain. He suggests that the development and refinement of institutions can significantly reduce transaction costs and contribute to long-term economic growth (North_1989: p.9).

DeVries (1994) underscores the importance of Europe's urban network in establishing a framework for regional economic development, particularly during the early modern period when the Industrial Revolution took place (deVries_1994: p.5). Furthermore, Hopkins (2009) supports this argument by pointing out that ethnic fragmentation is not necessarily detrimental to economic growth and can even support communities rather than act as a weight on them (Hopkins_2009: p.21).

In the context of Africa, Frankema & van Waijenburg (2012) demonstrate how West African commercial centers attracted large flows of economic migrants, which likely contributed to population growth rates exceeding 3% per annum in cities like Accra (Frankema&vanWaijenburg_2012: p.15). This high land-labor ratio and increased productivity, in turn, led to real wage rises (Frankema&vanWaijenburg_2012: p.17).

Nunn (2008) provides evidence that ethnic fractionalization can have a significant impact on economic development, as individuals required more security during periods of uncertainty and insecurity (Nunn_2008: p.4). This need for protection led to the emergence of institutions designed to ensure safety, which ultimately promoted long-term growth (Easterly & Levine 1997).

Institutions play a pivotal role in promoting long-term economic growth, as evidenced by various studies. Acemoglu et al. (2001) argue that more fundamental institutions on property rights have a substantial impact on economic performance (p.19). These institutions were found to be detrimental to investment and economic progress (p.27). Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2002) highlight the importance of institutions in understanding economic and political development over the past 500 years (p.49). They also note a significant reversal in the distribution of economic prosperity, which they attribute to institutional changes (p.6).

Abramovitz (1986) supports this view by suggesting that the elaboration of the mixed economy is a function of economic growth itself (p.20). He also identifies macroeconomic and monetary conditions encouraging capital investment and effective demand as crucial factors in the development process (p.7). Allen (2001) emphasizes the significance of wages and prices in understanding the pace of economic development, economic leadership, and the standard of living (p.1).

Institutions play a crucial role in shaping long-term economic growth, as highlighted by North & Weingast (1989) and Ogilvie (2007). According to North & Weingast (1989), the development of representative government with multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs is intimately related to economic growth and political freedom. They argue that these institutions facilitate a stable and predictable legal environment, which in turn encourages investment and effective demand (North&Weingast_1989: p.2).

Ogilvie (2007) supports this view, acknowledging the significance of economic institutions in determining economic development. He emphasizes that the efficiency theory, which posits that institutions evolve to maximize productivity and wealth, may not always hold true. Instead, he suggests that individual institutions within a wider framework have interdependencies, with one institution's failure potentially impacting others (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). This underscores the importance of institutional structure in fostering economic growth.

Challenging the assertion that institutions are the primary driver of long-term economic growth, some scholars argue that other factors such as ideas and ideologies may play a significant role (McCloskey_2015: p.8, p.9). For instance, in France during the two centuries following the Unfinished Revolution of 1789, reactionary parties thrived which were disinterested in economic growth if they could impose a rigid form of Catholicism (McCloskey_2015: p.9). Similarly, in Spain, economic growth was throttled until very recently due to conservative reasons despite the examples of economic success in other regions like Holland and Britain (McCloskey_2015: p.9).

Moreover, Simson (2019) contends that while there were spending inefficiencies, leakages, and costly delays to reform in Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda during the 1960s and 1970s, their elimination may not have been enough to sustain the investment levels of that period or reorient these economies (Simson_2019: p.4). This suggests that factors beyond institutional structure might be instrumental in fostering economic growth.

These findings challenge the notion that institutions are the sole determinants of long-term economic growth and instead highlight the potential role of ideas and ideologies in shaping economic trajectories. However, further research is needed to comprehensively understand the interplay between these factors and their impact on long-term growth.

The literature on institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is nuanced and complex, with various factors contributing to economic trajectories. While some studies, such as those by Sokoloff & Engerman (2000), highlight the importance of unique initial conditions and their enduring impacts on long-term growth, others, like Broadberry & Gardner (2022), question the applicability of this theory to regions like Sub-Saharan Africa due to the lack of data before 1950.

Goldin (2006) argues that the study of labor was once focused on institutions within the labor market, but shifts in perspective have led to a more comprehensive understanding of the factors contributing to economic growth. Similarly, Koepke & Baten (2005) explore the relationship between climate and human living standards, suggesting that environmental conditions may also play a significant role in shaping long-term economic growth.

Hopkins (2019) complicates this narrative by pointing out that postmodernism and Western impositions have inhibited the study of long-run economic and political change in Africa, potentially limiting the diversity of independent African viewpoints. Skocpol & Sommers (1980) also provide a comparative analysis of Japan and China, revealing the particularities of each case and the impact of Western intrusions on their respective economic growth.

In light of these findings, it becomes evident that institutions are not the sole determinants of long-run economic growth. Ideas and ideologies, as well as factors such as climate and historical data gaps, may also play significant roles in shaping economic trajectories. A more comprehensive understanding of their interplay is necessary to fully grasp the complexities of long-term growth. (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1, Goldin_2006: p.3, Hopkins_2019: p.10, Koepke&Baten_2005: p.16, Skocpol&Sommers_1980: p.8)

The role of institutions in long-run economic growth is further complicated by factors such as industrialization and economic transitions, as suggested by various studies. Komlos (1998) highlights that physical stature during the Industrial Revolution was significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic processes and associated structural changes, implying a complex interplay between biological growth and economic development. Temin (2002) posits that the slowdown of economic growth was caused by the exhaustion of cheap labor rather than institutions being the primary cause, suggesting a more nuanced perspective on the relationship between institutions and growth. Allen et al. (2011) demonstrate that income disparities in the early 20th century were due to long-run stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe, indicating that economic transitions may also play a significant role in shaping long-term growth patterns. These findings underscore the importance of considering broader factors beyond institutions when examining the causes of long-run economic growth. (Komlos_1998: p.16, Temin_2002: p.4, AllenEtAl_2011: p.1)

The role of institutions in shaping long-term economic growth is indeed a significant factor, but other elements, such as labor and educational policies, also play crucial roles in this process. Lindert & Williamson (1985) suggest that the British classical economists and policymakers recognized the interplay between economic growth and equality, acknowledging the trade-offs involved in balancing these two factors (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1). Furthermore, they argue that understanding long-run distribution trends is closely linked to explaining growth itself (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.9).

Frankema (2012) adds nuance to this argument by highlighting the impact of colonial education on long-run growth disparities, particularly in Africa. He contends that former British colonies experienced faster growth due to their educational lead (Frankema_2012: p.2). However, Frankema also points out that the degree of British "liberalism" was context-dependent and endogenous to local political and economic conditions (Frankema_2012: p.19).

Williamson (1996) further complicates the argument by emphasizing the importance of understanding the sources of convergence in economic growth. He argues that agreeing on the meaning of convergence is fundamental to understanding who benefits and who loses from it, and thus to formulating effective policy responses (Williamson_1996: p.25). Williamson also suggests that mass migrations could reduce the pace of real wage growth in receiving countries by glutting labor markets abroad (Williamson_1996: p.17).

These findings underscore the complexity of factors influencing long-term economic growth and highlight the importance of considering not only institutions but also labor, education, and migration policies when examining this issue. Further research should aim to understand these interrelationships more fully to provide a comprehensive understanding of the causes of long-run economic growth. (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1, Frankema_2012: p.2, Williamson_1996: p.25)

Institutions play a pivotal role in long-run economic growth, as demonstrated by various scholars. Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), while examining different contexts, both emphasize the importance of institutions in shaping economic outcomes.

Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) argues that with labor tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, hindering growth. He also highlights the case of the Russian peasants' enserfment and the economic cost of slaveholding in the Cotton Belt as instances where institutional structures stifled growth (Phillips, "The Economic Cost of Slaveholding in the Cotton Belt," Pol., Domar_1970: p.16).

Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) focuses on African societies and their economic institutions, stating that the Western impact was not solely limited to economic structures but also included slave dealing, curing, and status acquisition. He further notes the influence of social norms on economic behavior, such as the limitation of women's numbers (Bohannan_1959: p.13).

Despite their insights, both scholars' works remain tangential to the direct examination of institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. The interplay between institutions, structural change, and economic growth still requires further investigation to provide a comprehensive understanding of this complex relationship.

In particular, more research is needed to understand how specific institutional arrangements foster or hinder economic growth in different contexts. Additionally, it would be beneficial to explore the role of cultural norms and social structures in shaping institutions and their impact on long-run economic development.
