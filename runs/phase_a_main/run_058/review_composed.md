The central question under investigation in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, a thesis initially proposed by North and Weingast (1989). This question holds significant importance as it directly impacts our understanding of historical and contemporary economic development, particularly in regions such as Sub-Saharan Africa (Broadberry & Gardner, 2022).

Support for the thesis is evident in works like Acemoglu et al. (2001), who argue that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress. For instance, they propose that "differences in mortality rates are not the only, or even the main, cause of variation in institutions" (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3). Kuznets (1973) also supports this view, stating that "the succession of tech-innovations characteristic of modern economic growth and the social innovations that provide the needed adjustments are major factors affecting economic and social structure" (Kuznets_1973: p.6).

However, not all scholars agree. Austin (2008) complicates the argument by suggesting that institutions as the sole cause of long-run growth may oversimplify historical relationships. For example, he argues that "the implication is that these trades, even the Atlantic one, reinforced rather than originated Africa’s relatively poor economic position compared to western Europe" (Austin_2008: p.24). Similarly, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) note that the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has led to Sub-Saharan Africa's absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth.

Furthermore, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) and Komlos (1998) add nuance to the discussion by focusing on the roles of women and physical stature during the Industrial Revolution, respectively. These studies complicate the thesis by demonstrating that other factors, such as gender and biological growth processes, also play significant roles in economic development.

The assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is further supported by several studies. Kuznets (1973) posits that economic and social structure changes, particularly those associated with technological innovations, play a significant role in driving modern economic growth (Kuznets_1973: p.4, p.6). Acemoglu et al. (2001) argue that more fundamental institutions have a substantial impact on property rights and economic progress, while North (1989) emphasizes the role of centralized enforcement mechanisms in fostering economic growth (North_1989: p.1, p.5).

Moreover, Ogilvie (2007) highlights the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework and their impact on economic development (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). Acemoglu et al. (2002) reiterate the importance of institutions, noting that the reversal in the distribution of economic prosperity is likely related to institutional changes (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5). Lastly, Whatley (2018) demonstrates the role of gunpowder and its relationship with slave exports in economic development (Whatley_2018: p.13). These findings collectively suggest that institutions indeed play a crucial role in driving long-term economic growth.

Institutions significantly impacted economic growth, particularly in agriculture, as evidenced by various studies. North & Weingast (1989) highlight the role of political institutions in fostering economic growth and market development (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). They argue that absolutist states with weak constraints on power, such as early modern Spain, created conditions that hindered long-term economic growth (North&Weingast_1989: p.7).

Similarly, Frankema & van Waijenburg (2012) emphasize the importance of institutions in shaping economic development in West Africa. They demonstrate how commercial centers acted as magnets for economic migrants, contributing to increased productivity and wage growth (Frankema&vanWaijenburg_2012: p.15). The presence of strong institutions facilitated the growth of these commercial centers, thereby promoting long-term economic development.

Pamuk (2007) also supports this argument, stating that changes in institutions, such as the Black Death and subsequent demographic shifts, led to significant commercialization of agriculture, paving the way for modern economic growth (Pamuk_2007: p.4). These findings suggest that agricultural growth was closely tied to institutional development.

Broadberry & Gupta (2006) further support this argument by highlighting the crucial role of service sectors in economic growth during the early modern period, similar to their role in the modern era (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.9). They argue that the service sector played a significant role in driving economic growth through the falling relative price of manufactured goods.

The role of institutions in shaping labor markets and fostering economic growth is further emphasized by Mokyr (2005). He posits that the Enlightenment played a pivotal role in cultivating elements conducive to a progressive society, such as individualism, formal law, corporatism, self-governance, and institutionalized decision-making processes (Mokyr_2005: p.56).

Moreover, Mokyr (2005) suggests that the change in economic growth rate and nature in the West can be attributed to developments in the intellectual realm concerning "useful knowledge" (Mokyr_2005: p.4). He contends that this knowledge was instrumental in driving structural change, which in turn spurred economic growth.

The growth of economic reasonableness, however, was not a linear or irreversible process according to Mokyr (Mokyr_2005: p.54). This implies that while institutions have been crucial in shaping labor markets and driving long-run economic growth, the progress has not always been smooth or unidirectional.

Structural change, rather than being a catalyst for long-run economic growth, may instead be a consequence of it according to Stern (Stern_2004: p.3). He argues that economic factors drive changes in environmental impacts, which can lead to rising or declining environmental degradation over the course of economic development. This suggests that the relationship between institutions and long-run growth may not be as straightforward as previously thought.

Moreover, Feinstein (Feinstein_1998: p.28) challenges the notion that changes in farming were sufficient to alleviate labor gluts before the 1850s and 1860s. He implies that institutions might have played a lesser role in driving economic growth during this period compared to other factors such as wars or demographic indicators.

Humphries (Humphries_2013: p.18) also critiques the idea that the division of labor, workshop production, and the emergence of the factory system were primary drivers of eighteenth-century economic growth. He suggests that assumptions about family structures and dependency burdens might need reevaluation.

Lastly, vanZanden (vanZanden_1999: p.1) questions the consensus view that the early modern economy was unable to generate economic growth before the industrial revolution. His work implies that institutions may not have been as crucial in shaping labor markets and driving long-run economic growth during this time as previously believed.

These sources challenge the idea that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, suggesting instead that other factors such as environmental impacts, war, demographic indicators, family structures, and early modern economy might play significant roles in shaping labor markets and driving economic growth.

The works of Simson (2019) and McCloskey (2015) challenge the notion that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth by highlighting other factors such as ideologies, external influences, and historical contexts.

Simson (2019) presents evidence from Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda, arguing that during the 1960s and 1970s, these countries experienced economic growth accompanied by an expansion of state expenditure and deficits. However, he questions whether the elimination of spending inefficiencies and delays would have been sufficient to sustain investment levels and reorient these economies.

McCloskey (2015) also critiques the institutionalist perspective by focusing on the French and Spanish cases during the two centuries following the Unfinished Revolution of 1789. He suggests that reactionary parties, uninterested in economic growth, prospered in both countries, which had significant impacts on their economic development.

These sources suggest that ideologies and external factors may play a more significant role in shaping labor markets and driving economic growth than institutions alone. However, further investigation is needed to fully understand the complex interplay between these factors and long-term economic growth.

The assertion that institutions are the primary cause of long-term economic growth is further complicated by evidence suggesting that other factors, such as structural change and gender roles, may also play significant roles in shaping labor markets and driving economic growth.

For instance, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that Sub-Saharan Africa's absence from recent debates about long-run growth is due to the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). This finding underscores the importance of considering historical data in assessing the relationship between institutions and economic growth.

Moreover, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) highlight the role of women's contributions to economic growth and development, noting that understanding the processes by which women's labor is subsumed under household production is crucial for strengthening women's economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). This insight emphasizes the need to consider gender dynamics in analyzing institutions and long-term economic growth.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) contend that initial conditions, or factor endowments broadly conceived, could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4). Their work suggests that the role of institutions in driving economic growth may be influenced by these initial conditions, necessitating a more nuanced understanding of the relationship between institutions and growth.

Hopkins (2019) discusses how postmodernism inhibited the study of long-run economic and political change in Africa, potentially limiting the diversity of independent African viewpoints (Hopkins_2019: p.4). This finding underscores the importance of considering diverse perspectives in understanding institutions and their role in shaping long-term economic growth.

Institutions and Industrialization serve as crucial elements in shaping long-term economic growth, yet their roles are nuanced and complex, as suggested by Austin (2008) and Braudel & Wallerstein (2009).

Austin (2008) contends that the Atlantic trade reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to western Europe. This finding complicates the assertion that institutions are solely responsible for long-term growth, as it implies external factors can significantly impact a region's economic trajectory. Furthermore, Austin (2008) advocates for examining growth theories against long-term history, revealing relationships that recur due to similar situations and path dependencies.

Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) also highlight the intricate interplay between different players in the market, suggesting that the "strategy" of one actor can affect the strategies of others. This observation underscores the importance of considering the broader economic system within which institutions operate, as well as the potential influence of these systems on institutional development and long-term growth.

These insights from Austin (2008) and Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) reshape the argument by emphasizing the complex interplay between institutions, industrialization, and external factors in shaping long-term economic growth. They suggest that while institutions may be a fundamental cause of long-run growth, understanding their role requires considering the broader context within which they operate and the potential influence of external factors on their development and impact.

The role of institutions in long-run economic growth is further nuanced when considering their interaction with technological advancements. Komlos (1998) suggests that while institutions may be a fundamental cause of modern economic growth, they are intertwined with economic processes and associated structural changes (Komlos_1998: p.17). This implies that the development and impact of institutions might be influenced by technological advancements during the Industrial Revolution. For instance, Komlos (1998) discusses the discovery of 8-year height cycles similar to classical business cycles, implying a potential link between biological growth and economic fluctuations (Komlos_1998: p.16).

Lindert & Williamson (1985) also highlight the correlation between inequality and economic growth, stating that Japan's long-run experience does not show a clear correlation between the two (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.14). This suggests that while institutions may contribute to growth, they might do so differently in various contexts, potentially influenced by technological advancements and their impact on income distribution. Furthermore, Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that the private sector's skills deepening growth could be considered "revolutionary" (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.26), implying a significant role for technological advancements in driving growth and potentially shaping institutions.

These findings complicate the argument that institutions are the sole fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. They suggest that understanding the role of institutions requires considering their interaction with technological advancements and the potential influence of these advancements on the development and impact of institutions. Future research should investigate this interplay in more detail to gain a comprehensive understanding of the relationship between institutions, technological advancements, and long-run economic growth.

The works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) provide valuable insights into the relationship between institutions, labor, and economic growth, albeit tangentially. Both scholars acknowledge the significance of institutions in shaping economic outcomes.

Domar (1970) highlights the impact of institutional arrangements on competition among employers and the labor market, suggesting that when labor is tied to land or an owner, competition ceases, potentially hindering economic growth (p.4). This aligns with Bohannan's (1959) observation of how the Western impact on African societies included the establishment of institutions that restricted labor mobility, such as slave dealing and status acquisition (p.7, p.10).

However, while both scholars recognize the role of institutions in economic development, they diverge in their focus. Domar's work is more theoretical, focusing on the structural aspects of production and growth, whereas Bohannan provides ethnographic evidence from African societies to illustrate the Western impact on institutions.

Despite these contributions, several questions regarding the relationship between institutions, technological advancements, and long-run economic growth remain unresolved. For instance, it is yet unclear how specific institutional arrangements facilitate or hinder technological progress, and vice versa. Future research should investigate this interplay in more detail to gain a comprehensive understanding of the relationship between institutions, technological advancements, and long-run economic growth.
