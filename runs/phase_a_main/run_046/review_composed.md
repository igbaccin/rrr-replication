The question of whether institutions are the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth has been a subject of intense debate among economists and historians. This discussion is crucial as it sheds light on the factors driving economic development across different regions and periods.

Austin (2008) supports the thesis, asserting that institutions play a pivotal role in long-term growth. He argues that these institutions can reinforce or originate economic positions, particularly in the case of Africa compared to Western Europe and parts of Asia (Austin_2008: p.24). Kuznets (1973) also supports this view, linking rapid changes in economic structure with growth and societal innovations (Kuznets_1973: p.6).

However, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate the issue by pointing out that Sub-Saharan Africa has been absent from recent debates due to a lack of data on pre-1950 economic performance. They suggest that understanding the extent to which historical growth provides a path to convergence is essential (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.18).

Acemoglu et al. (2001) provide evidence supporting the thesis, stating that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). Yet, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) complicate the issue further by highlighting how understanding women's economic contribution is crucial for strengthening policies aimed at enhancing their roles in economic growth (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3).

Komlos (1998) also adds nuance to the discussion, emphasizing that physical stature during the Industrial Revolution was significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic processes (Komlos_1998: p.16). This suggests that while institutions may play a role in long-term growth, other factors such as economic cycles might also be significant.

The stakes of this debate are high, as understanding the fundamental causes of long-term economic growth can guide policy decisions aimed at promoting development and reducing poverty. The ongoing discussion among scholars provides valuable insights into this complex issue, with some supporting the central thesis, others complicating it, and yet others adding nuance to the argument. Future research should aim to reconcile these perspectives and provide a comprehensive understanding of the role institutions play in long-term economic growth.

The role of institutions in long-term economic growth is further supported by various scholars. Kuznets (1973) highlights that rapid changes in economic structure, which are often associated with economic growth, also lead to significant alterations in other aspects of society such as urbanization and family structures (Kuznets_1973: p.4). Moreover, he suggests that the non-conventional costs of economic growth can have both positive and negative outcomes (Kuznets_1973: p.11).

Mokyr (2005) argues that the development of intellectual progress played a crucial role in shaping the West's economic growth. He emphasizes that the growth of economic reasonableness was far less monotonic and irreversible than technological progress, suggesting that non-material factors also contribute to long-term economic growth (Mokyr_2005: p.54). Furthermore, Mokyr contends that elements such as individualism, formal law, corporatism, self-governance, and rules determined through an institutionalized process are essential components of a progressive society (Mokyr_2005: p.56).

Ogilvie (2007) supports the idea that economic institutions play a critical role in economic development. He notes that the interdependency of individual institutions within a broader framework is crucial, and failure to consider this could lead to the failure of the entire system (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). Additionally, Ogilvie discusses how economists have attempted to bring institutions into economic analysis, recognizing their importance in understanding long-term growth (Ogilvie_2007: p.3).

Nunn and Wantchekon (2011) provide empirical evidence supporting the role of institutions in economic growth. Their research shows that both internal (within a country) and external (between countries) factors influence long-term economic development, with internal factors being more significant (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.4). They also demonstrate that the effects of institutional changes can persist for nearly as long as the shock itself (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.7).

Finally, Bryant (2006) supports the notion that institutions are fundamental to economic growth by emphasizing that certain environments provided more than adequate conditions for commercial exchange (Bryant_2006: p.13). He also notes a broad agreement among scholars on the key difference between civilizations: the fusion of mercantilist imperialism with technologically driven capitalism (Bryant_2006: p.5).

The argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is further supported by Austin (2008). He emphasizes the role of institutions in shaping economic growth, stating that they are "the fundamental cause" of such growth (Austin_2008: p.26). This perspective aligns with the thesis, underscoring the crucial role of institutions in driving long-term economic development.

Austin (2008) also highlights the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history, revealing recurring relationships that are not only due to similar situations but also path dependence (Austin_2008: p.1). This approach underscores the enduring impact of institutions on economic growth over extended periods.

Moreover, Austin (2008) contends that trade, even significant ones like the Atlantic trade, often reinforced rather than originated the economic positions of regions such as Africa compared to Western Europe (Austin_2008: p.24). This assertion suggests that the institutional frameworks in place significantly influenced the economic trajectories of different regions.

The persistence of institutions plays a significant role in shaping long-term economic growth, as outlined by Acemoglu and colleagues (AcemogluEtAl_2001). They argue that certain institutional frameworks can either facilitate or hinder investment and economic progress. For instance, the authors demonstrate that detrimental institutions, such as those found in some regions of Africa, were persistently unfavorable to economic development (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27).

Furthermore, Acemoglu and colleagues (AcemogluEtAl_2001) argue that there are several economic mechanisms contributing to the persistence of institutions. These include path-dependency, where historical events shape the development of institutions, and self-reinforcing processes, where institutional changes can lead to additional institutional changes that reinforce the original change (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.8).

Moreover, the authors challenge the notion that differences in mortality rates are the primary cause of variation in institutions, suggesting instead that the effect of more fundamental institutions on property rights and political power is a more significant factor (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3). This assertion supports the argument that institutional frameworks have a profound impact on long-run economic growth.

Despite the assertion that institutions are the primary cause of long-term economic growth, Stern (2004) challenges this perspective by highlighting the environmental impact during economic growth. Stern argues that economic growth can lead to environmental degradation in the early stages but may eventually result in improved environmental conditions over time due to structural changes. He claims that there is evidence suggesting that economic growth does not necessarily reduce environmental damage sufficiently to halt the growth process, and any irreversibility is not so severe as to significantly decrease future income levels (Stern_2004: p.8). This perspective challenges the notion that institutions are the sole determinant of long-run economic growth, suggesting instead that economic development might have unintended environmental consequences.

The assertion that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth is challenged by various factors, as revealed in multiple studies. For instance, Komlos (1998) explores the concept of modern economic growth and its relationship with structural changes during the Industrial Revolution, suggesting that biological growth processes were significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic conditions (Komlos_1998: p.16). This implies that other factors, such as economic cycles, may also play a role in determining long-term economic growth.

Furthermore, Koepke & Baten (2005) investigate the relationship between climate and human living standards over time. They find that during the Roman Empire, heights did not increase despite common beliefs of improved living standards due to economic growth (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.16). This evidence complicates the notion that institutions alone can account for long-run economic growth, as it suggests environmental factors may have a significant impact on development.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) also contribute to this discussion by examining long-run distribution trends and their relationship with economic growth. They argue that understanding the causal direction between inequality and growth is essential for a comprehensive explanation of economic development (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.9). This perspective underscores the complexity of the relationship between institutions, inequality, and long-run economic growth.

Lastly, Skocpol & Sommers (1980) compare Japan and China to shed light on the role of Western intrusions in shaping their historical trajectories (Skocpol&Sommers_1980: p.4). Their comparative analysis reveals the particularities of each case, implying that external factors may also influence institutional development and long-run economic growth.

The work by Broadberry and Gardner (2022) sheds light on the lack of data on aggregate economic performance in Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA) before 1950, which has been a significant hindrance in recent debates about comparative long-run growth. This absence of historical data has contributed to the assertion that African economic performance has been markedly worse than other regions (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1).

Moreover, Broadberry and Gardner (2022) emphasize that understanding whether the growth observed in SSA provides a path to convergence requires an examination of the extent to which there are still risks of the shrinking that has undermined economic progress in the past (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.18). This suggests that while institutions may play a crucial role in shaping long-run economic growth, the risk of economic contraction should not be overlooked in the analysis of SSA's development trajectory.

Lastly, Broadberry and Gardner (2022) acknowledge that some economists have proposed using alternative data to track African economic growth in recent decades (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.10). This implies that while historical data may be scarce, there are other means to evaluate the economic performance of SSA and its potential for long-run growth.

Moreover, the historical developments in West Africa reveal parallels with other regions in Africa where gender and economic growth are concerned (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.4). This suggests that understanding the contribution of African women to economic growth and development is crucial for formulating effective policies across SSA.

The growing recognition of women's contributions in both academia and policy circles underscores the importance of their role in long-run economic growth (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). However, the processes by which women's economic contribution becomes subsumed under household production necessitate further examination to ensure that policies effectively address the unique challenges faced by African women in contributing to economic growth.

The literature review on the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth reveals a convergence among scholars that institutions play a crucial role in shaping economic outcomes. Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) both emphasize the importance of labor immobility and institutional structures in limiting competition and hindering economic growth, while Bryceson (2002) highlights how traditional hierarchical orders within communities can influence women's economic and social spheres.

However, there is also a divergence in the focus of these scholars. Domar (1970) primarily discusses labor immobility and its impact on competition among employers, while Bohannan (1959) provides insights into the role of Western impacts on economic institutions in African societies. Bryceson (2002), on the other hand, focuses on how changing economic activities affect gender roles within communities.

Despite these insights, several issues remain unresolved. For instance, while it is clear that institutions play a significant role in shaping economic growth, there is a need for more research to understand the specific mechanisms through which this occurs and the factors that influence the effectiveness of institutions in promoting economic growth. Additionally, further investigation is needed to fully comprehend the unique challenges faced by African women in contributing to economic growth, as well as the impact of changing gender roles on economic development.
