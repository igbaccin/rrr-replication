The central question under examination in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth, a thesis that has garnered significant attention from scholars. The stakes are high as understanding the roots of economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at fostering development and reducing poverty.

Supporting this position, Simon Kuznets (1973) posits that economic growth is associated with rapid changes in economic structure and society, suggesting a crucial role for institutions in facilitating these transformations (Kuznets_1973: p.4). Similarly, Douglas North and Barry Weingast (1989) argue that representative government, characterized by multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, plays a crucial role in promoting economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

On the other hand, some scholars complicate this view. For instance, John Austin (2008) questions whether institutions originated or reinforced Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe and parts of Asia, suggesting that the relationship between institutions and growth may be more complex than initially thought (Austin_2008: p.24). Furthermore, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 in Sub-Saharan Africa has hindered debates about comparative long-run growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1).

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance to the discussion by highlighting how understanding the processes by which women's economic contributions have been subsumed under household production can inform policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3).

The debate is far from settled, with 21 sources supporting the thesis, 5 offering critiques, and 20 adding nuance or qualifications. The argument continues as scholars delve deeper into understanding the intricate relationship between institutions and long-term economic growth.

The intricate relationship between institutions and long-term economic growth is further elucidated in various scholarly works, underscoring the fundamental role institutions play in fostering economic development. North & Weingast (1989) argue that the political institutions of representative government, characterized by multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are crucial for economic growth and political freedom (p.28). Similarly, Ogilvie (2007) emphasizes the interdependency of individual institutions within a broader framework, suggesting that their failure to function effectively can impede economic growth (p.27).

Pamuk (2007) supports this argument by demonstrating how changes in economic institutions paved the way for commercialization of agriculture following the Black Death (p.4), while Nunn (2008) highlights the significant impact of ethnic fractionalization on long-term economic growth (p.4). Nunn & Wantchekon (2011) provide evidence that both internal and external institutional factors contribute to economic development, with the internal factor being at least twice as influential (p.4).

Peters (2004) reinforces this perspective by asserting that cultural modes of understanding play a crucial role in shaping social modes of organization, which in turn impact economic growth (p.16). These findings collectively suggest that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, as they provide the framework for effective governance, rule enforcement, and economic exchange. However, further exploration is needed to better understand the nuances and intricacies of this relationship.

Institutions significantly impact long-run economic growth by providing an effective framework for governance, rule enforcement, and economic exchange (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19, AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49). For instance, detrimental institutions can hinder investment and economic progress, as demonstrated by the analysis of various societies in Acemoglu et al.'s 2001 study (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). Furthermore, the distribution of economic prosperity has significantly changed over time, which may be related to institutional changes (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.6).

The evolution of institutions also plays a crucial role in the economic growth process. Abramovitz's 1986 work suggests that the development of mixed economies is a function of economic growth itself, with countries adjusting their balance in favor of productivity growth (Abramovitz_1986: p.20, p.22). Similarly, Allen's 2001 study highlights the importance of wages and prices in understanding economic development, economic leadership, and standard of living (Allen_2001: p.1).

Institutions play a crucial role in shaping economic growth, as they provide the framework that governs economic interactions and facilitates productivity improvements (North_1989: p.1). The changes in economic structure observed during periods of rapid growth are closely linked to institutional innovations (Kuznets_1973: p.6). For example, the succession of technological innovations characteristic of modern economic growth is accompanied by social innovations that facilitate necessary adjustments and contribute to economic and social structure (Kuznets_1973: p.6).

The development of formal laws, individualism, corporatism, self-governance, and rules determined through institutionalized processes are key elements of a progressive society, which have contributed significantly to economic growth (Mokyr_2005: p.56). The growth of economic reasonableness was far less monotonic and irreversible than technological progress, indicating that the development of institutions is a complex and ongoing process (Mokyr_2005: p.54).

While Mokyr (2005) posits that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, Simson (2019) and Stern (2004) challenge this perspective by emphasizing cultural factors and environmental impacts respectively.

Simson (2019) critiques the focus on industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy in certain regions during the 1960s and 1970s, suggesting that these investments may not have been sufficient to sustain economic growth or reorient economies (Simson_2019: p.4). Furthermore, Simson highlights the expansion of state expenditure and deficits during this period, questioning whether their elimination would have been enough to maintain investment levels (Simson_2019: p.4).

On the other hand, Stern (2004) argues that economic growth could potentially lead to environmental degradation in the early stages of development, challenging the notion that institutions are solely responsible for long-term economic growth without considering the impact on the environment (Stern_2004: p.1). Stern also suggests that changes in the structure and technology of economies may drive changes in environmental impacts over the course of economic development (Stern_2004: p.3).

These sources challenge the assertion that institutions are the sole cause of long-run economic growth by emphasizing cultural factors and environmental impacts, suggesting a more complex relationship between these elements and economic growth.

The sources under examination offer nuanced perspectives on the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth, complicating the assertion that institutions are the sole cause of such growth. Austin (2008) challenges the notion by arguing that historical events, such as the Atlantic trade, reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe and parts of Asia (Austin_2008: p.24). This suggests that institutions may not be the only factor influencing long-term economic growth, but rather interact with historical events to shape outcomes.

Similarly, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) emphasize the significant contribution of African women to economic growth and development, yet their labor was often subsumed under household production and not formally accounted for (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). This underscores the importance of considering cultural factors and social structures in understanding economic growth.

Austin (2007) also complicates the argument by highlighting the role of transportation infrastructure, such as railways, in shaping economic growth (Austin_2007: p.8). This suggests that environmental factors and technological advancements may also play a crucial role in long-run economic growth.

Allen Et Al (2011) further add to the complexity by demonstrating income disparities between China, Japan, and Europe during the early 20th century, which were due to long-run stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe (AllenEtAl_2011: p.1). This implies that institutional differences may not be the sole determinant of economic growth, as other factors such as technological advancements and historical events can also significantly impact economies.

Structural changes and organization played significant roles in long-run economic growth, as evidenced by the impact they had on various aspects of economies. The relationship between climate and human living standards during different periods, such as the Roman Empire, provides a compelling example (Koepke & Baten_2005: p.20). Although living standards were believed to have increased during this time due to economic growth, heights did not follow suit, suggesting that climate may have had a more substantial impact on living standards than previously thought.

Moreover, the growth of population and urbanization, as well as the increase in income inequality and variability, are closely associated with structural changes (Komlos_1998: p.17). These findings imply that institutions were not solely responsible for economic growth but were intertwined with other factors, such as demographic shifts and income distribution patterns.

Furthermore, the relationship between economic growth and equality was a topic of interest among policymakers and economists in the 19th century (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.1). The task of explaining long-run distribution trends became intertwined with the search for explanations of growth itself, suggesting that economic growth and income inequality were intricately linked.

The argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is further nuanced when considering the role of technological advancements, as evidenced by Frankema (2012), Hopkins (2019), and Goldin (2006).

Frankema (2012) posits that colonial education played a significant role in explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa. The British colonies, which controlled most of the fertile areas, experienced faster growth due to their educational lead (Frankema_2012: p.2). However, the degree of British "liberalism" was endogenous to the local political and economic context (Frankema_2012: p.19). This suggests that while institutions were crucial, they were shaped by factors such as geography and historical circumstances.

Hopkins (2019) argues that the study of long-run economic and political change in Africa was hindered by 'totalism' and 'essentialism,' which were perceived as Western impositions. Hopkins also notes that food exports made little contribution to economic growth during the period of the slave trade (Hopkins_2019: p.13). These findings complicate the argument by suggesting that while institutions matter, they may not always be the primary determinant of growth, and other factors such as trade patterns should also be considered.

Goldin (2006) discusses the evolution and revolution in women's choices and decisions, which can be seen as a form of technological advancement in terms of societal norms and expectations. The distinction between the two pertains to three aspects of women’s choices and decisions (Goldin_2006: p.1). This implies that institutional changes, such as legal reforms affecting women's rights, can lead to structural change and economic growth.

The scholarship on the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth demonstrates a convergence in recognizing the fundamental role of institutions. Both Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) highlight that when labor is tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, thereby hindering economic growth (Domar_1970: p.4, Bryceson_2002: p.9). Furthermore, both scholars acknowledge the impact of institutional changes on women's rights and their subsequent influence on structural change and economic growth (Goldin_2006: p.1, Bohannan_1959: p.13).

However, there are divergences in the focus of each scholar. Domar (1970) primarily discusses the factors of production and their relationship with institutions, while Bohannan (1959) focuses on the Western impact on economic institutions in Africa. Bryceson (2002) provides insights into livelihood diversification and its correlation with institutional changes, particularly in relation to women's roles within communities (Bryceson_2002: p.9).

Despite these insights, several aspects remain unresolved. For instance, the specific mechanisms through which institutions drive long-run economic growth require further exploration. Additionally, a more comprehensive understanding of the interplay between different types of institutions and their impacts on various sectors of an economy is needed to fully grasp the role of institutions in promoting sustainable economic growth.
