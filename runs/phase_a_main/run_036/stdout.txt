LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question in the discourse on long-run economic growth is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of such growth, as posited by Austin (Austin_2008: p.26). This question carries significant implications for understanding historical and contemporary economic development, particularly in regions like Africa that have experienced slower growth compared to other parts of the world.

Austin (Austin_2008: p.1) supports this thesis, asserting that institutions play a crucial role in shaping long-term economic growth. He argues that these trades, such as the Atlantic trade, reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe (and parts of Asia). Similarly, Kuznets (Kuznets_1973: p.6) supports this view by emphasizing the role of social innovations in providing the necessary adjustments for modern economic growth.

However, not all scholars agree. Broadberry & Gardner (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1) complicate this argument by pointing out that a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has kept Sub-Saharan Africa absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. Akyeampong & Fofack (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3) also add nuance, suggesting that understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution becomes subsumed under household production is crucial for policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles.

On the other hand, Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27) support the thesis, demonstrating that institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress. Mokyr (Mokyr_2005: p.4) also supports this view, suggesting that changes in the intellectual realm concerning "useful knowledge" were instrumental in the West's economic growth.

The stakes of this debate are high, as understanding the fundamental causes of long-run economic growth is crucial for designing effective policies to promote development and reduce poverty. The argument continues, with scholars still debating the extent to which institutions are the primary drivers of economic growth and the role of other factors such as technology, geography, and culture.

The assertion that institutions are fundamental to long-run economic growth is substantiated by a wealth of evidence. As Acemoglu, Johnson, and Robinson (2002) contend, the distribution of economic prosperity over the past 500 years can be traced back to institutions, suggesting their primary role in development (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49). Similarly, Acemoglu, Johnson, and Robinson (2001) demonstrate that more fundamental institutions on property rights significantly impact economic performance (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19).

Institutional development also plays a crucial role in shaping economic growth through the encouragement of capital investment and effective demand, as Abramovitz (1986) posits (Abramovitz_1986: p.7). Hopkins (2009) further supports this notion by arguing that ethnic fragmentation is not necessarily responsible for unproductive institutions; instead, other causes of solidarity and motives for action should be considered (Hopkins_2009: p.22).

Moreover, Frankema and van Waijenburg (2012) provide evidence of the economic impact of institutional development in West African commercial centers, where large flows of economic migrants contributed to real wage rises (Frankema&vanWaijenburg_2012: p.15). Lastly, Broadberry and Gupta (2006) emphasize the crucial role of the service sector during the early modern period in driving economic growth, suggesting that institutions played a significant part in this process (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.9).

The role of institutions in driving long-term economic growth is further substantiated by various scholars, providing a comprehensive understanding of their impact on economic structure and development. Mokyr (2005) emphasizes the significance of "useful knowledge" in fueling economic growth in the West, suggesting that institutions played a crucial role in its dissemination and application (Mokyr_2005: p.4). North (1989) posits that institutions reduce transaction costs, enhancing market efficiency and fostering economic growth (North_1989: p.9).

Ogilvie (2007) highlights the interdependence of individual institutions within a broader framework, asserting that their failure to align can impede economic development (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). Pamuk (2007) underscores the transformative impact of the Black Death on wages and incomes during the last millennium, suggesting that these changes were facilitated by institutions (Pamuk_2007: p.4).

Nunn (2008) demonstrates that ethnic fractionalization, which is associated with long-term economic growth, necessitated the development of effective institutions to manage uncertainty and insecurity (Nunn_2008: p.4). Lastly, Nunn & Wantchekon (2011) reveal that both internal and external channels are essential for institutional development, with the internal channel proving to be more significant (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.4).

The assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is further supported by various scholars. Austin (2008) endorses this view, emphasizing that institutions play a crucial role in shaping economic development and historical trends (Austin_2008: p.1). Similarly, Kuznets (1973) argues that economic growth is associated with rapid changes in economic structure and society as a whole, implying the significance of institutional factors in driving these transformations (Kuznets_1973: p.4).

North & Weingast (1989) provide evidence supporting this thesis by focusing on the political factors underlying economic growth and market development, emphasizing the role of representative government institutions and the rule of law in fostering economic prosperity (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). Whatley (2018) supports this argument through econometric analysis that reveals the impact of institutional factors on long-term economic trends, such as the relationship between gunpowder imports and slave exports (Whatley_2018: p.13).

Allen (2001) also supports the thesis by highlighting the centrality of wages and prices in understanding economic development, leadership, and standard of living, suggesting that institutional factors play a key role in determining these aspects (Allen_2001: p.1). These scholars collectively underscore the importance of institutions as the fundamental driver of long-run economic growth. However, further investigation is needed to better understand the specific mechanisms through which institutions influence economic development and to identify strategies for promoting institutional change in different contexts.

The works of Simson (2019) and Stern (2004) challenge the notion that institutions are the primary drivers of long-run economic growth by focusing on alternative factors contributing to economic development.

Simson (2019) argues that industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy played significant roles in economic growth in certain regions during specific periods. For instance, he highlights the expansion of state expenditure and deficits in Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda during the 1960s and 1970s (Simson_2019: p.25). Although there were inefficiencies and delays to reform, Simson suggests that their elimination may not have been enough to sustain investment levels or reorient these economies (Simson_2019: p.4).

On the other hand, Stern (2004) questions the Environmental Kuznets Curve (EKC) hypothesis, which suggests that economic growth leads to environmental degradation in the early stages but eventually results in a cleaner environment. Instead, Stern argues that economic factors drive changes in environmental impacts and may be responsible for rising or declining environmental degradation over the course of economic development (Stern_2004: p.3). The assumption is that environmental damage does not reduce economic activity sufficiently to halt growth (Stern_2004: p.8).

These findings suggest that while institutions may influence economic growth, other factors such as industrial development, educational expansion, military autonomy, and environmental impacts also play crucial roles. Understanding the interplay between these factors and institutions is essential for a comprehensive understanding of long-run economic growth.

Institutional development plays a pivotal role in long-run economic growth, but the interplay between institutions and other factors such as climate, educational expansion, military autonomy, and environmental impacts necessitates a comprehensive understanding. This perspective is further enriched by various scholarly works that add nuance to the thesis.

For instance, Koepke & Baten (2005) shed light on the relationship between climate and human living standards over time, suggesting that while economic growth may have occurred during certain periods, it did not necessarily lead to improvements in nutrition and height, as evidenced by their findings on the Roman Empire and Guatemalan children (Koepke & Baten_2005: p.16, p.32). This complicates the notion that institutions alone drive long-term economic growth, implying that environmental factors should also be taken into account.

Frankema (2012) argues that colonial education may have played a key role in explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa, particularly among former British colonies (Frankema_2012: p.2). However, the degree of British "liberalism" was endogenous to local political and economic contexts, further complicating the relationship between institutions and economic growth (Frankema_2012: p.19). This implies that the impact of colonial education on long-term growth may not have been uniform across all regions, suggesting a need for more nuanced analyses.

Hopkins (2019) discusses how the study of African economic history was inhibited by "totalism" and "essentialism," which were perceived as Western impositions on the rest of the world (Hopkins_2019: p.4). This perspective complicates the argument that institutions are the sole cause of long-run economic growth, as it emphasizes the importance of diverse viewpoints in understanding complex historical phenomena.

Bolt & van Zanden (2014) highlight revisions to nineteenth- and early twentieth-century growth rates for several countries, including Germany, Sweden, and Russia (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5). However, these revisions do not fundamentally change the authors' view that institutions play a crucial role in long-term economic growth.

Goldin (2006) examines the study of labor, highlighting the importance of understanding the evolution and revolution in women’s choices and decisions (Goldin_2006: p.1). By doing so, she complicates the argument that institutions are the sole cause of long-run economic growth, suggesting that factors such as gender roles may also play a significant role.

Clark & Jacks (2007) delve into the cliometric account of the coal industry in the Industrial Revolution, emphasizing the technological advancements that enabled the industry to respond to increases in demand (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.4). This complicates the argument that institutions are solely responsible for long-term economic growth, as it underscores the importance of technological innovation in driving economic expansion.

The argument that institutions are the primary cause of long-term economic growth is further nuanced by several factors, as evidenced in various scholarly works. For instance, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) emphasize the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 in Sub-Saharan Africa, which complicates its inclusion in debates about long-run growth. They also acknowledge that understanding the extent to which there are still risks of economic shrinkage is crucial for determining if past growth provides a path to convergence (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.1).

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) highlight the underrepresentation of women's economic contribution in historical analyses, which undermines policies aimed at strengthening their economic roles. They argue that in West Africa, women's economic growth was largely dependent on the economic provision of the husband (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.9). This suggests that institutional factors, such as gender roles, may have a significant impact on long-term economic growth by affecting the distribution of resources and labor within societies.

Komlos (1998) discusses the Industrial Revolution's physical effects on human stature, implying that biological growth processes were significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic conditions (Komlos_1998: p.16). This finding complicates the argument that institutions are solely responsible for long-term economic growth, as it underscores the importance of understanding the relationship between economic processes and structural changes in shaping long-run development.

Temin (2002) suggests that the exhaustion of cheap labor caused economic growth to slow, indicating that labor supply may play a role in long-term economic growth (Temin_2002: p.4). Additionally, he argues that the end of the Golden Age was brought about by the return to equilibrium growth paths rather than adverse economic shocks (Temin_2002: p.14). This implies that external factors, such as labor supply and market equilibriums, may also influence long-term economic growth.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that the task of explaining long-run distribution trends rejoins the search for explanations of growth itself, suggesting a potential link between income inequality and economic growth (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.9). Furthermore, they propose that policymakers must consider trade-offs between economic growth and equality when formulating policies (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.1).

Austin (2007) discusses the advantage of railways over lorries in the mid-1920s, suggesting that technological innovation plays a crucial role in economic growth (Austin_2007: p.8). Additionally, he notes that few capital-deepening and TFP-improving forces of modern economic growth were at work in Ghana before 1950, indicating that institutional factors may have limited the potential for long-term economic expansion (Austin_2007: p.16).

While Sokoloff and Engerman (2000) acknowledge that unique factor endowments have played a significant role in shaping long-term economic growth, they also emphasize the crucial impact of institutions on this process. They argue that sustained economic growth was achieved by the United States and Canada during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, while other countries did not attain this goal until late in the nineteenth century (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.12). This suggests that institutional factors may have been instrumental in determining the timing of economic growth across different regions.

Similarly, Skocpol and Sommers (1980) argue that comparisons between cases reveal their particularities, implying that a focus on institutions can help to highlight differences and similarities that might otherwise be overlooked (Skocpol&Sommers_1980: p.9). They also emphasize the importance of understanding the specific ways in which Western intrusions influenced the development of institutions in different societies, as this could account for the divergent paths of economic growth (Skocpol&Sommers_1980: p.14).

Stoler (1989) adds to this discussion by highlighting the role of imagined communities in shaping colonial administrations and plantation elites' alliances, which could have had significant implications for institutional development and economic growth (Stoler_1989: p.21). This suggests that understanding the social and cultural contexts in which institutions develop is essential for a comprehensive analysis of their impact on long-term economic growth.

The literature on the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth suggests a consensus that institutional development plays a crucial role in economic progress. However, there is divergence in the specific factors considered essential by different scholars.

Domar (1970) emphasizes the importance of labor mobility as a factor influencing economic growth. He argues that when labor is tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, hindering economic development (Domar_1970: p.4). This perspective converges with the notion that institutions promoting labor mobility can foster competition and thereby stimulate economic growth.

On the other hand, Bohannan (1959) focuses on the impact of cultural factors on institutional development. He suggests that the Western impact was not limited to economic institutions but also influenced social structures, such as kinship networks and status systems (Bohannan_1959: p.10). This perspective diverges from the more narrow focus on economic institutions but aligns with the idea that understanding the broader social and cultural context is essential for a comprehensive analysis of institutional impact on long-term economic growth.

Despite these insights, there remains an unresolved question regarding the causality between institutions and economic growth. While many scholars agree that institutions play a significant role in economic development, it is not clear whether institutions cause growth or if economic growth leads to the development of favorable institutions. Further research is needed to clarify this relationship and provide a more complete understanding of the interplay between institutions and long-run economic growth.
