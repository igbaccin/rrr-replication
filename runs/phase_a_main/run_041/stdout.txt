LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question under investigation in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, a thesis that has garnered significant attention among economists and historians. The stakes are high, as understanding the factors driving economic growth can provide valuable insights for policymakers aiming to promote development in various regions.

Supporting this position, Austin (2008) argues that institutions play a crucial role in shaping economic growth, particularly in Africa, where the trade activities reinforced rather than originated the continent's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe (p.24). Similarly, Kuznets (1973) emphasizes the importance of social innovations in providing the necessary adjustments for modern economic growth, alongside technological advancements (p.6).

North & Weingast (1989) focus on the political factors underpinning economic growth and the development of markets, demonstrating how representative government institutions were intimately related to economic growth and political freedom (p.2). Acemoglu et al. (2001) also support this view, highlighting that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress (p.27).

However, not all scholars agree that institutions are the sole determinant of long-run economic growth. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate the argument by pointing out that Sub-Saharan Africa's absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth is due to a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 (p.1). Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance, noting that understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution becomes subsumed under household production is crucial for policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles (p.3).

As the debate continues, it is clear that institutions play a significant role in long-run economic growth. However, further research is needed to understand their interaction with other factors and to develop effective policies aimed at promoting development in various regions. The implications of this research extend beyond academia, as understanding the drivers of economic growth can inform policymaking decisions and ultimately contribute to economic prosperity.

The relationship between institutions and economic growth is further substantiated by several scholarly works. Kuznets (1973) posits that technological innovations, which are characteristic of modern economic growth, are closely linked to social innovations that facilitate necessary adjustments in society (Kuznets_1973: p.6). This suggests that the development and enforcement of effective institutions are crucial for harnessing the potential of technology to drive economic growth.

North & Weingast (1989) argue that representative government, with multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, plays a significant role in fostering economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28). Their research underscores the importance of institutions in enabling economic development by providing a framework for rule enforcement and change.

Mokyr (2005) emphasizes that the West's economic prosperity can be attributed to intellectual developments, such as individualism, man-made formal law, corporatism, self-governance, and rules established through an institutionalized process (Mokyr_2005: p.56). These progressive societal elements, he suggests, were instrumental in fostering economic growth.

Frankema & van Waijenburg (2012) highlight the role of urban centers as magnets for economic migrants, which can contribute to population growth and labor surplus, leading to increased wages (Frankema&vanWaijenburg_2012: p.15). This demonstrates how institutions, in this case, urban centers, can influence economic inequality by shaping migration patterns and labor markets.

The influence of institutions on labor markets is further supported by various studies. Sokoloff and Engerman (2000) argue that unique initial conditions, or factor endowments broadly conceived, have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth. They suggest that the achievement of sustained economic growth by countries like the United States and Canada during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries can be traced back to their institutions (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.12).

Similarly, deVries (1994) emphasizes the role of Europe's urban network in economic development. He posits that the establishment of a framework for regional economic development in which industrial growth could occur was an achievement of the early modern period, primarily due to the interconnectedness of cities through commercial interaction (deVries_1994: p.5).

Whatley (2018) provides evidence that gunpowder imports significantly influenced slave exports, suggesting a relationship between institutions and labor markets. The long-run equilibrium relationship between these variables is captured in the author's model (Whatley_2018: p.13).

vanZanden (2009) highlights the impact of the skill premium on economic growth. He posits that a low skill premium contributes to economic growth by affecting the relative price of capital goods (vanZanden_2009: p.27).

Allen (2001) discusses wages and prices as central concerns in economic history, suggesting that they bear on fundamental issues such as the pace of economic development and standard of living. The author's study provides reassurance that the wages of building craftsmen are indicative of trends in average earnings (Allen_2001: p.4).

These findings collectively support the assertion that institutions play a crucial role in shaping labor markets and influencing long-run economic growth. Future research could delve deeper into specific institutional arrangements and their effects on wage structures, labor productivity, and overall economic development.

The role of institutions in shaping long-run economic growth is further substantiated by several scholarly works. Acemoglu et al. (2001) provide compelling evidence that fundamental institutional arrangements significantly impact economic performance, with detrimental institutions hindering investment and progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). Moreover, they argue that differences in mortality rates are not the primary cause of institutional variation (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3).

Similarly, Abramovitz (1986) posits that economic growth influences the development and elaboration of mixed economies, suggesting a symbiotic relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth (Abramovitz_1986: p.20). He also emphasizes the importance of macroeconomic and monetary conditions in encouraging capital investment and effective demand for sustained economic progress (Abramovitz_1986: p.7).

Hopkins (2009) challenges the notion that ethnic fragmentation is solely responsible for unproductive institutions, stressing the need to discount other factors influencing solidarity and action (Hopkins_2009: p.22). This work underscores the complexity of institutional development and its interplay with various social and economic factors.

Taken together, these studies support the assertion that institutions play a crucial role in long-run economic growth. They demonstrate that fundamental institutional arrangements can have significant effects on investment, productivity, and overall economic performance, emphasizing the importance of understanding and shaping these institutions for sustainable development. Further exploration into specific institutional arrangements and their impacts on wage structures, labor productivity, and economic development could yield valuable insights.

Challenging the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth, some scholars argue that ideologies and cultural factors play a significant role in shaping economic trajectories. For instance, McCloskey (2015) posits that in certain regions, such as France and Spain, conservative ideologies hindered economic growth despite examples of prosperity in other countries like Holland and England (p.8-9). Similarly, Simson (2019) contends that state expenditure and deficits expanded during periods of high economic growth in Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda, suggesting that institutional arrangements may not be the sole determinant of economic development (p.25).

Moreover, Bryceson (2002) challenges the assumption by arguing that traditional hierarchical orders within communities can influence women's economic and social spheres, potentially undermining the role of men as family provisioners (p.9). This suggests that institutions may not always promote wage structures and labor productivity in a manner conducive to sustainable development.

Structural change in industries, such as the coal industry during the Industrial Revolution, may not have been the primary driver of long-run economic growth. Clark & Jacks (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.4) argue that technological advancements enabled the coal industry to respond to increases in demand, but they do not provide evidence that this structural change directly caused economic growth. Instead, it could be argued that the technological advancements themselves spurred growth.

Moreover, Feinstein (Feinstein_1998: p.28) points out that significant changes in farming did not occur until the mid-19th century, which may suggest that structural change in agriculture did not significantly contribute to long-run economic growth during the Industrial Revolution. This is further supported by Feinstein's (Feinstein_1998: p.5) discussion on the impact of wars and other factors on British economic growth during this period, indicating that there were likely multiple contributing factors to economic growth beyond structural change in industries or agriculture.

The examination of institutions and economic inequality further complicates the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth. Komlos (1998) highlights the significant influence of short-term fluctuations in economics on physical stature during the Industrial Revolution, suggesting a correlation between economic processes and structural changes (Komlos_1998: p.17). This implies that institutions may not solely drive long-term growth but could also be influenced by short-term economic cycles.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that the relationship between economic growth and equality is intertwined, with policymakers often grappling with trade-offs between the two (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1). Furthermore, they suggest that long-run distribution trends are crucial in understanding the explanations of growth itself (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.9), implying that economic inequality could potentially play a role in shaping long-term economic growth.

Allen et al. (2011) demonstrate that income disparities during the early 20th century were due to long-run stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe, indicating that economic growth in one region could lead to increased inequality in another (AllenEtAl_2011: p.1). This suggests that while institutions may drive growth in some regions, they might also contribute to economic inequality in others.

The works of Akyeampong & Fofack (2014), Hopkins (2019), and Stoler (1989) add nuance to the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by highlighting the complex roles that gender, colonialism, and cultural perceptions played in shaping economic structures and outcomes.

Akyeampong & Fofack (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3) emphasize that understanding the subsumption of women's economic contributions under household production undermines policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles. This suggests that while institutions may drive growth, they can also perpetuate gender inequalities and limit the full potential of female contributions to economic development.

Hopkins (Hopkins_2019: p.13) presents evidence that food exports made minimal contribution to economic growth in West Africa during the period of the slave trade. This challenges the notion that institutions solely responsible for long-term economic growth, as it indicates that other factors such as trade patterns may have played a significant role.

Stoler (Stoler_1989: p.5) argues that colonizers lived in "imagined communities" to overcome economic and social disparities between them and the native populations. This suggests that institutions established during colonialism were not solely driven by economic growth, but also by cultural perceptions and power dynamics.

These findings complicate the argument that institutions are the sole cause of long-run economic growth. They suggest that other factors such as gender roles, trade patterns, and cultural perceptions may also play significant roles in shaping economic structures and outcomes. Further exploration of these factors could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the complex interplay between institutions and economic growth.

The relationship between institutions and capital investment is a complex one, with various factors influencing its dynamics. Austin (2007) suggests that until the mid-1920s, railways had a cost advantage over lorries, which could have impacted capital investment in transportation infrastructure (Austin_2007: p.8). However, he also acknowledges that population growth was faster after 1918, potentially indicating increased demand for transportation services (Austin_2007: p.12). These findings suggest that the cost advantage of railways and the demographic factors may have played a role in shaping capital investment patterns in the transportation sector.

Bolt & van Zanden (2014) highlight the importance of revisions to nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth figures, which have been made for several countries including Switzerland, Germany, Sweden, Italy, Greece, Russia, and Bulgaria (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5). These revisions could potentially impact our understanding of the relationship between institutions and capital investment during these periods. Furthermore, van der Eng's work on the sources of long-term economic growth in Indonesia sheds light on the role of institutions in shaping capital investment patterns in developing countries (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.14).

Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) argue that there is a fundamental disagreement regarding the role of institutions in economic growth, with some arguing for their centrality and others emphasizing other factors (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.30). This disagreement underscores the complexity of the relationship between institutions and capital investment, suggesting that multiple factors may be at play.

The scholarship on the role of institutions in long-run economic growth presents a complex picture, with some scholars emphasizing their centrality while others highlight other factors (Braudel & Wallerstein_2009). Domar_1970 and Bohannan_1959, although tangential to the main argument, provide valuable insights into this debate.

Domar (1970) argues that the labor-tied institutions in Russia prior to serfdom and the slaveholding system in the American South hindered competition among employers, thereby stifling economic growth. He suggests that structural changes correlate with growth, as seen when these systems were abolished and competition was reintroduced (Domar_1970: p.4, p.16).

On the other hand, Bohannan (1959) focuses on the impact of Western institutions on African societies, particularly in relation to slave dealing and status acquisition. He notes that these institutions disrupted traditional social structures, potentially leading to economic changes (Bohannan_1959: p.7, p.10).

Despite these insights, both scholars do not directly address the question of whether institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. The relationship between institutions and economic growth remains a contentious issue, with some arguing for institutions' centrality while others emphasize other factors such as technology, geography, or cultural norms (Braudel & Wallerstein_2009).

The unresolved question is whether institutions are the primary driver of long-run economic growth or if they interact with other factors in complex ways. Future research should aim to disentangle these interactions and clarify the role of institutions in economic development.
