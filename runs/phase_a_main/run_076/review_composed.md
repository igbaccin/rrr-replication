The central question under investigation in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, a thesis that has garnered significant attention from scholars. The stakes are high, as understanding the factors driving economic growth can inform policies aimed at promoting development and reducing inequality.

Supporting the thesis, Austin (2008) argues that institutions play a crucial role in economic growth, particularly in Africa's economic position compared to Western Europe. He suggests that trades, such as the Atlantic one, reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position. Similarly, Kuznets (1973) posits that both technological innovations and social innovations are major factors affecting economic growth.

On the other hand, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate this view by pointing out that Sub-Saharan Africa has been absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth due to a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950. They suggest that understanding the extent to which there are still risks of shrinking is crucial for knowing whether growth provides a path to convergence.

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance to the debate, highlighting the importance of understanding how women's economic contribution has been subsumed under the category of household production and their labor formally unaccounted for. This, they argue, undermines policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles.

North & Weingast (1989) support the thesis by focusing on the political factors underpinning economic growth and the development of markets. They emphasize that the fundamental institutions of representative government, such as an explicit set of multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are intimately related to economic growth and political freedom.

Acemoglu et al. (2001) further support this view by demonstrating a large effect of institutions on economic performance. They argue that differences in mortality rates are not the only or even the main cause of variation in institutions, and that these institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress.

The relationship between institutions and economic growth is a critical aspect of understanding long-term development efforts. According to North & Weingast (1989), the fundamental institutions of representative government, such as an explicit set of multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are essential for economic growth and political freedom (p.2, p.28). These authors argue that these institutions foster a stable environment conducive to market development by providing effective mechanisms for enforcing rules and facilitating change when necessary.

Similarly, Mokyr (2005) emphasizes the role of intellectual developments in driving economic growth, particularly the expansion of economic reasonableness and the growth of useful knowledge (p.4, p.56). He suggests that these changes were instrumental in making the West economically superior to other regions today.

North (1989) also supports this perspective by highlighting how institutions can reduce transaction costs, enhance security rights, and promote market development, ultimately contributing to economic growth (p.6, p.9). Furthermore, Nunn & Wantchekon (2011) demonstrate that both internal and external factors play a role in shaping economic growth, with the internal channel being at least twice as significant (p.4).

The literature further supports the notion that institutions are a fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, particularly in the context of institutional change and development. Broadberry & Gupta (2006) emphasize the importance of the service sector in economic growth during the early modern period, suggesting that it played a crucial role in driving growth (p.9). They also note that British economic growth during the Industrial Revolution was substantially slower than previously thought, implying that the development of institutions and their impact on economic growth should not be underestimated (p.25).

Broadberry et al. (2015) provide insights into India's long-run economic performance, a topic of enduring controversy. Their work suggests that population estimates play a significant role in understanding economic development, and they argue that India's population was larger than previously thought (p.4). This underscores the importance of institutions in shaping demographic trends, which in turn can influence economic growth.

Bryant (2006) discusses the fusion of mercantilist imperialism with a technologically driven capitalistic transformation as a fundamental axis of difference between Europe and other regions. This shift, he argues, was instrumental in driving long-run economic growth in Europe (p.5). He also notes that certain environments provided more than adequate conditions for the growth of commercial exchange, further supporting the argument that institutions play a crucial role in fostering economic development.

Structural change, rather than institutions, may have played a significant role in economic growth during the Industrial Revolution. McCloskey (2015) argues that ideologies and ideas, particularly in France, could have impeded economic growth despite adequate material conditions. He contends that the success of bourgeois economic and political systems in other regions, such as Holland and England, may have had a detrimental impact on France's economic development (McCloskey_2015: p.8).

Furthermore, Humphries (2013) emphasizes the importance of structural changes like the growing division of labor, workshop production, and the emergence of the factory system in promoting economic growth during the Industrial Revolution. He suggests that factors such as lower fertility rates in France compared to Britain may have reduced the burden of dependency and facilitated economic growth (Humphries_2013: p.19).

These findings challenge the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, suggesting instead that structural changes and ideological factors may have played equally important roles in shaping economic development during the Industrial Revolution.

The assertion that institutions are the primary drivers of long-term economic growth is not without its complexities, as several scholarly works suggest that structural changes and ideological factors may have played significant roles during the Industrial Revolution. For instance, Komlos (1998) argues that physical stature during this period was significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic processes and associated structural changes (p.16). This implies that economic growth might not be solely attributed to institutions but also to these structural transformations.

Similarly, Lindert & Williamson (1985) propose that policymakers often balanced economic growth and equality, suggesting a potential interplay between the two factors (p.1). Their research further complicates the argument by suggesting a possible causal relationship between inequality and growth (p.8), which could imply that institutional arrangements might have influenced both growth and inequality during the Industrial Revolution.

Koepke & Baten (2005) offer another perspective, demonstrating that climate may have had an impact on human living standards during the Roman Empire, a period often associated with economic growth (p.20). This finding suggests that environmental factors could potentially contribute to long-term economic growth, challenging the notion that institutions are the sole determinant.

Lastly, Hopkins (2019) argues that the study of long-run economic and political change in Africa was hindered by 'totalism' and 'essentialism,' which might have overshadowed the role of structural changes during the Industrial Revolution (p.10). This suggests that a more diverse range of viewpoints could reshape our understanding of the factors contributing to long-term economic growth during this period.

The perspective presented by Broadberry & Gardner (2022) offers a nuanced view of the role of institutions in long-run economic growth, particularly by highlighting the absence of Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA) from recent debates due to the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950. This omission potentially oversimplifies our understanding of global economic trends during the Industrial Revolution.

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that SSA's economic growth provides a path to convergence, but understanding the extent of this potential requires an in-depth examination of the risks associated with past economic shrinkage in the region. Furthermore, they suggest that new contributions to historical national accounting have provided valuable insights into patterns of growth and development over centuries, potentially redefining the history of economic divergence and convergence since before the industrial revolution.

Temin (2002) contributes to the discourse by complicating the notion that institutions are the sole cause of long-run economic growth. He suggests that the exhaustion of cheap labor, rather than institutions, may have caused economic growth to slow in some regions. Additionally, he argues that recent theories of economic growth should consider the context of economic transition, which was prevalent during the 1800s and early 1900s.

Williamson (1996) also offers a nuanced perspective by emphasizing the importance of understanding the sources of convergence for a comprehensive understanding of long-run economic growth. He argues that agreement on the meaning of convergence is crucial to understanding who benefits and who suffers from convergence, thus informing policy responses.

Lastly, Stoler (1989) challenges the conventional approach to studying institutions during the colonial period by suggesting that colonizers lived in "imagined communities" that were consciously created to overcome economic and social disparities. This perspective could potentially reshape our understanding of how institutions evolved during the Industrial Revolution and their impact on long-run economic growth.

The perspectives presented in Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) and Austin (2007) complicate the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by highlighting the significant, yet often overlooked, contributions of women to economic development.

This suggests that the creation and evolution of institutions during the Industrial Revolution may have been influenced by societal norms that marginalized women's economic contributions, thereby hindering long-term growth.

Similarly, Austin (Austin_2007: p.8) points out that until the mid-1920s, railways had a fundamental cost advantage over lorries, which could have significantly impacted economic growth. This finding challenges the assumption that institutions like infrastructure were solely responsible for driving long-term economic growth during this period.

Furthermore, Bolt & van Zanden (2014) and Allen Et Al (2011) provide evidence of regional differences in economic growth, suggesting that the evolution and impact of institutions may have varied across different regions.

Allen Et Al (AllenEtAl_2011: p.1) also demonstrate income disparities between China, Japan, and Europe during the early 20th century, which were due to long-run stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe. This finding suggests that while institutions played a role in economic growth, other factors such as technological advancement and resource availability also significantly influenced economic outcomes.

Institutions, as fundamental drivers of long-run economic growth, have been extensively studied by economists such as Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959). Both scholars acknowledge the significant role institutions play in economic development, yet they diverge in their focus and approach.

Domar (1970) primarily examines labor markets, emphasizing that with labor tied to land or to an owner, competition among employers ceases, hindering growth. He also discusses the relationship between institutions and structural change, suggesting that a shift towards manufacturing is crucial for economic development (Domar_1970: p.4).

On the other hand, Bohannan (1959) explores the impact of Western economic institutions on African societies, focusing on slave dealing, curing, and status acquisition. He notes that these institutions had far-reaching effects beyond economic growth, influencing social structures and dynamics (Bohannan_1959: p.7).

Despite their differences, both scholars converge in recognizing the importance of institutions in long-term economic growth. However, several issues remain unresolved. For instance, Domar's work does not delve deeply into the specific institutional factors that contribute to structural change or the mechanisms by which such changes lead to growth. Similarly, Bohannan's focus on Western impact leaves open questions about the role of indigenous institutions in African economic development.
