The central question of this literature review revolves around the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth. This question holds significant implications for understanding economic development across different regions and historical periods, as it could potentially guide policy-making and development strategies.

Supporting this thesis, Austin (2008) argues that institutions play a crucial role in economic growth, particularly in the context of Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe and parts of Asia (Austin_2008: p.24). Kuznets (1973) also supports this view, suggesting that economic growth is associated with rapid changes in economic structure and society, which are facilitated by institutions (Kuznets_1973: p.6). North & Weingast (1989) further reinforce this position, emphasizing the role of institutions in making possible economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

On the other hand, Acemoglu et al. (2001) highlight that certain institutions can be detrimental to investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate the issue by suggesting that a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has led to Africa's absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance by discussing how understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution is subsumed under household production could undermine policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3).

As this review demonstrates, while many authors support the thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth, others complicate the issue by highlighting factors such as data limitations and gender dynamics that could influence the relationship between institutions and economic growth. The ongoing debate underscores the need for further research to fully understand the complex interplay between institutions, economic growth, and development. The assertion that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth is substantiated by the examination of trade relationships between Africa and Western Europe, as outlined in Austin's (2008) work. According to Austin, these trades did not originate Africa's economically disadvantaged position compared to Western Europe; instead, they reinforced it (Austin_2008: p.24). This perspective aligns with the thesis that institutions play a crucial role in shaping economic growth over extended periods.

Trade between Africa and Western Europe during the Atlantic era was characterized by an imbalance of power and resources, which had profound effects on African economies. Austin (2008) argues that this trade relationship reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe (Austin_2008: p.24). This observation underscores the significance of institutions in shaping long-term economic growth, as the power dynamics and resource imbalances inherent in these trade relationships were likely influenced by the institutional frameworks governing them.

Moreover, Austin (2008) highlights that structural change correlates with growth (Austin_2008: p.1). In the context of Africa's trade with Western Europe, this can be observed in the shift from subsistence agriculture to cash crop production for export. While such a transformation may have initially increased revenue and stimulated economic growth, it also led to the concentration of wealth and power in the hands of a few, ultimately undermining the development of broad-based institutions that could foster sustainable long-term growth (Austin_2008: p.4).

According to Kuznets (1973), economic growth is characterized by rapid changes in economic structure and society, including urbanization and family structures (Kuznets_1973: p.4). These structural changes are significant factors contributing to long-term economic growth. Furthermore, Kuznets emphasizes the role of technological innovations in driving modern economic growth, which necessitates social adjustments for continued development (Kuznets_1973: p.6).

However, it's essential to acknowledge that these positive achievements of modern economic growth can also lead to negative consequences, even within developed countries (Kuznets_1973: p.11). Kuznets discusses the non-conventional costs associated with economic growth in his work on the subject (Kuznets_1973: p.5). This suggests that while institutions may stimulate economic growth, they must also adapt to manage these potential negative outcomes effectively.

North and Weingast (1989) emphasize the crucial role of institutions in fostering economic growth and political freedom. They argue that representative government, characterized by multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, is integral to this process (North&Weingast_1989: p.28). This suggests that effective institutions can manage the non-conventional costs associated with economic growth, as previously discussed by Kuznets (1973) (Kuznets_1973: p.5).

Moreover, North and Weingast (1989) assert that their work focuses on political factors underlying economic growth and market development, encompassing not just the rules governing economic exchange but also those determining how these rules are enforced and altered (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). This implies that a comprehensive understanding of institutions' role in long-term economic growth necessitates consideration of both their regulatory and enforcement aspects.

The assertion that institutions are the primary drivers of long-term economic growth faces challenge when considering the environmental impact and economic growth, as suggested by Simson (2019). In East Africa during the 1960s and 1970s, rapid industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy were prioritized over environmental protection (Simson_2019: p.27). This shift in focus led to increased state expenditure and deficits, which expanded during a period of high economic growth (Simson_2019: p.25).

While there may have been spending inefficiencies, leakages, and costly delays to reform, it is questionable whether their elimination would have been enough to sustain the investment levels or re-orient these economies towards a more sustainable growth path (Simson_2019: p.4). These findings challenge the notion that institutions are solely responsible for long-run economic growth, as they illustrate how environmental considerations can be neglected in pursuit of rapid industrialization and development.

Further investigation is needed to determine whether the disregard for environmental protection during this period had lasting negative effects on these economies' long-term growth prospects. However, the evidence presented by Simson (2019) raises doubts about the claim that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, particularly when environmental factors are taken into account.

Clark and Jacks (2007) reveal how technological advancements in the coal industry during the Industrial Revolution enabled it to respond effectively to increases in demand, thus fostering economic growth (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.4). This technological progress may have been driven by institutions but was also a critical factor in long-term growth, suggesting that environmental factors and technological innovations cannot be overlooked in the discussion of institutions' role in economic development.

Feinstein (1998) further supports this notion by highlighting the significance of agricultural changes and labor migration during the 1850s and 1860s in rural areas, which dispersed excess farm labor and contributed to economic growth (Feinstein_1998: p.28). The author also points out that other factors, such as wars, may have had an impact on British economic growth during the Industrial Revolution (Feinstein_1998: p.6), indicating that multiple factors might contribute to long-term economic development and not just institutions.

Despite the assertion that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth, it is crucial to acknowledge that other factors, such as structural change, may also play a significant role in this process. This perspective is supported by Broadberry & Gardner's (2022) analysis, which highlights the importance of structural change in understanding economic growth patterns across various regions.

Broadberry & Gardner (2022:1) argue that Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA) has been overlooked in debates about long-term growth due to a lack of data on pre-1950 aggregate economic performance. However, they contend that new contributions to historical national accounting have provided insights into patterns of growth and development over centuries, even before the industrial revolution (Broadberry & Gardner 2022:1). This suggests that understanding structural change in SSA could offer valuable insights into its long-term economic development.

Moreover, Broadberry & Gardner (2022:1) point out that African economic performance has been significantly worse than other regions, as noted by Collier and Gunning (1999) and Sachs and Warner (1997). While the reasons for this disparity are complex and multifaceted, understanding structural change in SSA could potentially help identify key factors contributing to its slower growth.

However, it is essential to acknowledge that structural change alone may not be sufficient to ensure economic growth. Broadberry & Gardner (2022:18) emphasize the need to understand the extent to which there are still risks of shrinking that have undermined economic progress in the past. This underscores the importance of considering both institutions and structural change when examining long-term economic development.

The work of Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) adds nuance to the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by highlighting the underrepresentation of women's contributions in economic development, particularly in West Africa. They argue that women's labor has often been subsumed within household production, making it unaccounted for in formal economic contexts (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). This undermines policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles.

Moreover, Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) demonstrate that the economic contribution of African women has been largely dependent on the economic provision of their husbands (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.9). This suggests that institutional structures can significantly impact the economic power dynamics within a society, potentially hindering long-term economic growth.

The authors also note that while West Africa serves as the primary focus of their study, similar historical developments and themes regarding gender and economic growth can be found elsewhere in Africa (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.4). This underscores the importance of considering the role of institutions in shaping gender dynamics across various regions, further emphasizing the significance of institutions in determining long-term economic development.

However, the contribution of African women to economic growth and development, as noted by Akyeampong and Fofack (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.19), warrants further examination. Understanding the extent to which institutional structures either empower or hinder women's economic roles could provide valuable insights into the complex relationship between institutions, gender, and long-term economic growth.

Domar (1970) emphasizes the role of labor tied to land or owners in limiting competition among employers, suggesting that institutional structures can hinder economic growth. On the other hand, Bohannan (1959) discusses the Western impact on institu-tions, albeit not solely economic ones, and notes the limitation of women's numbers in certain societies. Bryceson (2002) highlights the influence of traditional hierarchical order on women's economic and social spheres, as well as the diminishing role of men as family provisioners due to structural change.

Scholars converge in acknowledging that institutions play a significant role in shaping economic growth and gender roles. However, they diverge in their focus areas, with Domar primarily discussing labor markets, Bohannan exploring cultural aspects, and Bryceson delving into community dynamics and livelihood diversification.

What remains unresolved is the understanding of how specific institutional structures either empower or hinder women's economic roles, and consequently, their impact on long-term economic growth. Further research should aim to bridge these gaps by examining diverse institutional contexts and their effects on gender equality and sustainable development.
