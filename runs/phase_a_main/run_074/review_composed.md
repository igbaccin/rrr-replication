The central question in the literature review on institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is whether institutions play a decisive role in shaping economic trajectories over extended periods, particularly in Africa. This question holds significant stakes given the persistent economic disparities between regions, with Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA) often lagging behind other parts of the world.

Supporting this thesis, Austin (Austin_2008: p.26) asserts that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. He argues that economic trades, such as the Atlantic one, reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe. Similarly, Kuznets (Kuznets_1973: p.4) posits that high aggregate growth rates are associated with rapid changes in economic structure and other aspects of society, implying a crucial role for institutions in driving these transformations.

On the other hand, Broadberry & Gardner (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1) complicate this argument by noting the lack of data on SSA's aggregate economic performance before 1950, which has led to its absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. Akyeampong & Fofack (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3) further add nuance by discussing how understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution becomes subsumed under household production can undermine policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles.

Notably, North & Weingast (North&Weingast_1989: p.28) and Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19) provide strong support for the thesis. North & Weingast argue that representative government institutions are intimately related to economic growth and political freedom, while Acemoglu et al. demonstrate a large effect of institutions on economic performance.

The ongoing debate highlights the need for more comprehensive data on SSA's historical economic performance and a nuanced understanding of how institutions shape women's economic roles in Africa. The stakes are high, as resolving this question could provide valuable insights into strategies for promoting sustainable economic growth and reducing disparities between regions.

The assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is further supported by various scholarly works. Douglas North's study in 1989, titled "Institutions, Institutional Change, and Economic Performance," emphasizes the role of institutions in shaping economic growth. North argues that the establishment of centralized enforcement mechanisms, such as those seen in Spain, plays a crucial role in fostering economic growth (North_1989: p.1). Additionally, he highlights how institutions that reduce transaction costs by increasing security rights and property rights contribute to economic growth (North_1989: p.9).

In a similar vein, Whatley's 2018 work explores the relationship between gunpowder imports and slave exports in Africa. By investigating the long-run equilibrium relationship between these variables, Whatley demonstrates how institutions can influence economic dynamics (Whatley_2018: p.13). Furthermore, his findings suggest that the model is not one of co-integration, indicating a persistent impact of institutions on economic growth.

Nunn's 2008 research focuses on the relationship between ethnic fractionalization and long-term economic development in Africa. Nunn argues that during periods of uncertainty and insecurity, individuals required strong institutions to facilitate commercial exchange, which is essential for economic growth (Nunn_2008: p.4).

Lastly, Nunn & Wantchekon's 2011 study examines the impact of internal and external factors on institutional development in Africa. Their findings indicate that both channels are important but that the internal channel is at least twice as large as the external channel (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.4). This suggests that local institutions play a more significant role in shaping long-run economic growth compared to external factors.

Bryant's 2006 work provides further evidence supporting the thesis by highlighting how European fusion of mercantilist imperialism and capitalistic transformation created environments conducive to commercial exchange, which is crucial for economic growth (Bryant_2006: p.13).

These studies collectively demonstrate that institutions play a significant role in shaping economic growth, particularly in Africa. Understanding the mechanisms through which institutions influence economic roles and performance could provide valuable insights into strategies for promoting sustainable economic growth and reducing disparities between regions.

The persistence of institutions significantly impacts economic performance, as evidenced by the works of North & Weingast (1989) and Acemoglu et al. (2001). North & Weingast (North&Weingast_1989: p.2) emphasize that political factors, particularly representative government with multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are crucial for economic growth and market development. They further argue that these institutions played a necessary role in making possible economic growth and political freedom (North & Weingast 1989: p.30).

Acemoglu et al. (2001) also support the notion of institutional persistence influencing economic performance. They contend that more fundamental institutions, such as property rights and political institutions, have a substantial impact on economic growth (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). Specifically, they argue that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27), while overall, the results demonstrate a large effect of institutions on economic performance (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19).

The argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is further supported by the analysis presented in Austin (2008). Austin's work focuses on the trade between Africa and Western Europe, a significant historical interaction that provides valuable insights into this debate.

Austin (Austin_2008: p.24) suggests that these trades reinforced rather than originated Africa’s relatively poor economic position compared to western Europe. This implies that the institutions in place during these trade interactions played a crucial role in shaping the long-term economic growth trajectory of both regions.

Moreover, Austin (Austin_2008: p.26) endorses the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history, revealing relationships that recur because of similar situations and path dependencies. This emphasis on historical context supports the assertion that institutions are a fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, as they often persist over time and influence subsequent development.

In addition, Austin (Austin_2008: p.1) states that certain theories of economic growth, such as those focusing on structural change, should be evaluated against long-term history to reveal recurring relationships. This suggests that institutions are a key factor in understanding structural change, which in turn correlates with economic growth.

The sources provided by Simson (2019) challenge the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, particularly in East Africa, by focusing on industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy as key factors driving economic growth during the 1960s and 1970s.

Simson (Simson_2019: p.25) highlights that state expenditure and deficits expanded during this period of high economic growth in Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda. However, it is questioned whether the elimination of spending inefficiencies, leakages, and costly delays would have been enough to sustain the investment levels of the 1960s (Simson_2019: p.4). This suggests that while these factors may contribute to economic growth, they might not be the sole determinant.

Moreover, Simson (Simson_2019: p.27) points out that the emphasis was placed on expanding public employment rather than protecting those already in government jobs, and there was a considerable ambition towards reorienting these economies. This focus on industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy challenges the notion that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth.

However, it is essential to consider that these sources primarily focus on a specific time period and region, which may limit their applicability to broader contexts. Further research could examine whether similar trends hold true in other regions and over longer periods to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the role of institutions in long-run economic growth.

The literature on institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth presents a compelling narrative, particularly in Western contexts (North&Weingast_1989). However, this review expands upon the argument by considering other regions and periods that may complicate the applicability of these findings.

Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA), for instance, has been largely absent from recent debates due to a lack of data on economic performance before 1950 (Broadberry&Gardner_2022). Collier and Gunning (1999) observe that African economic performance has been markedly worse than other regions, while Sachs and Warner (1997) note the slow growth in Africa. The lack of data and understanding of SSA's historical economic growth trajectory raises questions about the role of institutions in this context.

Moreover, Temin (2002) challenges the notion that economic growth was primarily driven by institutional changes. Instead, he suggests that the exhaustion of cheap labor caused economic growth to slow. This perspective complicates the argument that institutions are the sole drivers of long-run economic growth, as it implies that other factors, such as labor supply, also play significant roles.

Furthermore, Bolt and van Zanden (2014) highlight revisions of nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth in various countries, but these do not fundamentally change our understanding of the role of institutions. However, their work underscores the importance of updating and revising historical data to gain a more accurate understanding of economic growth patterns over time.

Lastly, Stoler (1989) argues that colonizers lived in "imagined communities" that helped overcome economic and social disparities between them and the colonized. This finding complicates the notion that institutions are solely responsible for long-run economic growth, as it suggests that relationships and alliances between different groups can also play a crucial role in shaping economic outcomes.

The contributions of African women to economic growth and development, as highlighted by Akyeampong & Fofack (2014), are noteworthy in understanding the complexities of long-run economic growth. This omission undermines policies aiming to strengthen women's economic positions, as they play a significant role in economic growth and development (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.19).

Moreover, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) reveal that the economic contributions of African women were largely dependent on the economic provision of their husbands (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.9). This dependence complicates the notion that institutions are solely responsible for long-run economic growth, as it suggests that relationships and alliances between different groups can also significantly shape economic outcomes.

The findings by Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) demonstrate that historical developments in West Africa share parallels with gender and economic growth concerns elsewhere in Africa (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.4). This suggests that the relationship between institutions, women's contributions, and long-run economic growth may be more universal than previously thought.

The industrialization process, a critical aspect of modern economic growth (Komlos_1998: p.2), further underscores the significance of institutions in long-run economic growth. Komlos (1998) highlights that structural changes, including population growth and urbanization, were prevalent during the Industrial Revolution (Komlos_1998: p.17). These structural shifts, driven by institutional arrangements, spurred economic growth.

Moreover, Komlos suggests a correlation between the Industrial Revolution and cyclical fluctuations in physical stature, implying that economic processes significantly influenced biological growth during this period (Komlos_1998: p.16). This finding underscores the interconnectedness of institutions, industrialization, and long-term economic development.

However, Komlos' work complicates the thesis by not explicitly addressing the causal relationship between institutions and economic growth during the Industrial Revolution. Nevertheless, his findings imply that institutional arrangements played a crucial role in facilitating the structural changes that drove economic growth during this period.

Furthermore, Komlos (1998) notes that these structural changes led to increased income inequality and variability (Komlos_1998: p.17). This observation suggests that while institutions may have been instrumental in promoting industrialization and economic growth, they also contributed to social disparities, a complex aspect of the relationship between institutions and long-run economic development.

The literature suggests a complex relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth, with scholars agreeing on the importance of institutions in promoting industrialization but diverging on their impact on social disparities.

Domar (1970) emphasizes the role of institutions in structuring labor markets, arguing that when labor is tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, hindering economic growth (p.4). This aligns with Bohannan's (1959) observation that the Western impact on African societies was not limited to economic institutions but also included practices like slave dealing and status acquisition (p.10).

However, both authors also acknowledge the potential for institutions to exacerbate social disparities. Domar points out that in situations where labor is tied to land or owners, the distribution of income becomes more unequal (p.4). Similarly, Bohannan notes that the number of women is limited in societies affected by Western influence, suggesting a potential for gender inequality (p.13).

Despite these insights, the literature does not provide a consensus on how to balance the promotion of economic growth with the mitigation of social disparities. For instance, Domar references Phillips' work on the economic cost of slaveholding in the Cotton Belt, suggesting that understanding the economic impact of certain institutions may help address disparities (p.16). However, this does not provide a clear solution for the complex relationship between institutions and long-run economic development.
