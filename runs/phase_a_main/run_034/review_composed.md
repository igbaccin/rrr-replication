The central question under examination in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, a thesis posited by Austin (2008) and supported by North & Weingast (1989), Acemoglu et al. (2001), and Kuznets (1973). This question carries significant implications for understanding the economic development of regions such as Sub-Saharan Africa (Broadberry & Gardner, 2022) and the role of women in economic growth (Akyeampong & Fofack, 2014).

Austin (2008) complicates the argument by suggesting that while institutions play a crucial role, they may reinforce rather than originate economic positions, as seen in the case of Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) add nuance to this discussion by noting the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 in Sub-Saharan Africa, which has hindered its inclusion in recent debates about long-run growth.

Kuznets (1973) supports the thesis by emphasizing the role of technological innovations and social innovations in driving modern economic growth. However, he also acknowledges the non-conventional costs associated with such growth, particularly within less developed countries. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) further complicate the issue by highlighting how women's economic contributions have often been subsumed under household production and their labor formally unaccounted for, which undermines policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles.

North & Weingast (1989) provide a strong endorsement of the thesis, demonstrating how institutions play a necessary role in making possible economic growth and political freedom. Acemoglu et al. (2001) support this view by showing a large effect of institutions on economic performance, with certain institutions being detrimental to investment and economic progress.

The stakes of this debate are high, as understanding the role of institutions in long-run economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting economic development, particularly in regions such as Sub-Saharan Africa. The ongoing discussion among scholars suggests that while institutions are indeed a crucial factor, other factors such as technological innovations and social structures also play significant roles. Further research is needed to fully understand these interrelationships and their implications for economic growth.

Institutions play a pivotal role in shaping long-term economic growth, as highlighted by various scholars (North&Weingast_1989, AcemogluEtAl_2001, Mokyr_2005, North_1989, AcemogluEtAl_2002). These researchers focus on the political and economic factors that underpin growth and the development of markets.

North & Weingast (1989) emphasize the significance of representative government institutions in fostering economic growth. They argue that the presence of multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs are crucial for economic progress and political freedom. Acemoglu et al. (2001) also support this notion, demonstrating a large effect of institutions on economic performance.

Mokyr (2005) suggests that developments in intellectual reasoning concerning "useful knowledge" were essential to the growth of economic reasonableness in the West and subsequent prosperity. He highlights the role of individualism, man-made formal law, corporatism, self governance, and rules determined through institutionalized processes in promoting progressive societies.

North (1989) discusses how centralized enforcement mechanisms can influence economic growth, while Acemoglu et al. (2002) examine the historical reversal of economic prosperity and its potential connection to institutions. They argue that this change is a first-order fact in understanding development over the past 500 years.

Abramovitz (1986) supports this perspective, suggesting that the elaboration of the mixed economy is a function of economic growth itself. He emphasizes the importance of calculating direct effects of systems on economic incentives and the role of macroeconomic and monetary conditions in encouraging capital investment and effective demand.

The evidence presented in various studies supports the notion that institutions are indeed a fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Ogilvie (2007) emphasizes the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework, suggesting that the failure of one institution can significantly impact the economy (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). He also highlights that most people in an economy might want it to be as big as possible, aligning with the efficiency theory (Ogilvie_2007: p.14).

Whatley (2018) presents econometric results demonstrating the long-run equilibrium relationship between certain factors and economic growth, further supporting the argument that institutions play a crucial role in shaping economic trajectories (Whatley_2018: p.3, p.11, p.13).

Frankema & van Waijenburg (2012) discuss the economic explanations for real wage rises, attributing them to high land-labor ratios and the influx of economic migrants into commercial centers, implying that institutions play a significant role in labor market dynamics (Frankema&vanWaijenburg_2012: p.15, p.17).

Nunn (2008) demonstrates the relationship between ethnic fractionalization and long-term economic growth, suggesting that insecure environments at certain historical periods required individuals to develop institutions for their own protection (Nunn_2008: p.4).

Peters (2004) insists on the autonomy and dynamic of development of culture, implying that cultural institutions play a significant role in economic growth (Peters_2004: p.16). Bryant (2006) highlights the radicalness of revisionist dissent suggesting that nascent European fusion of mercantilist imperialism with capitalistic transformation significantly impacted economic growth (Bryant_2006: p.5, p.3).

These studies collectively suggest that institutions, whether they are economic, cultural, or political, play a significant role in shaping long-run economic growth. The interdependency of these institutions within a wider framework is crucial to understanding the dynamics of economic development.

Institutional frameworks, encompassing economic, cultural, and political aspects, significantly influence long-term economic growth (North&Weingast_1989). Technological advancements are integral to this relationship, as they shape the structure of economies and society at large.

Kuznets (1973) highlights that rapid changes in economic structure often coincide with technological innovations, which drive modern economic growth. For instance, the succession of tech-innovations is a major factor affecting both economic and social structures (Kuznets_1973: p.6). Furthermore, Kuznets discusses the nonconventional costs associated with economic growth, emphasizing the need for institutions to adapt to these changes (Kuznets_1973: p.5).

Van Zanden (2009) supports this perspective by demonstrating that the skill premium of carpenters and masons, who design and create capital goods, significantly impacts the relative price of these goods. A low skill premium can contribute to economic growth through new growth theory, which posits human capital formation as a determinant of long-term economic development (vanZanden_2009: p.24).

Institutional development, as a primary driver of long-run economic growth, has been challenged by some scholars. The evidence presented in Simson (2019) suggests that industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy, rather than institutions, may have played significant roles in the economic growth of certain regions during specific periods (Simson_2019: p.27). For instance, in Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda, state expenditure and deficits expanded during a period of high economic growth in the 1960s and 1970s (Simson_2019: p.25).

McCloskey (2015) also questions the institutional development's role in long-term economic development, arguing that ideas and ideologies, not institutions, were crucial factors influencing economic growth in France and Spain during certain historical periods (McCloskey_2015: p.9). The author suggests that the examples of economic success in the Anglosphere may have had a significant impact on France's economic development trajectory (McCloskey_2015: p.8).

These findings challenge the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by suggesting that other factors, such as industrial development and ideological shifts, may play equally important roles in shaping economic trajectories. Future investigations should consider these factors in conjunction with institutional development to gain a more comprehensive understanding of the drivers of long-term economic growth.

The argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is further complicated by several factors highlighted in various scholarly works. For instance, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) point out that Sub-Saharan Africa's economic performance before 1950 has been largely overlooked due to a lack of data, which raises questions about the extent to which institutional development played a role in this region's economic growth trajectory.

Moreover, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) shed light on the underappreciated contributions of African women to economic growth and development. Their research reveals that women's labor was often subsumed under household production and formally unaccounted for, which undermines policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles.

Temin (2002) adds a historical perspective by suggesting that the exhaustion of cheap labor caused economic growth to slow, implying that institutional factors might not be the sole drivers of long-term economic growth. Similarly, Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that the task of explaining long-run distribution trends rejoins the search for explanations of growth itself, suggesting a potential interplay between economic growth and inequality.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) emphasize the importance of initial conditions or factor endowments in shaping long-run economic growth paths, complicating the argument that institutions are the sole determinants of economic growth. Lastly, Bolt & vanZanden (2014) highlight revisions to historical growth data for various countries, which could potentially lead to a reassessment of the role institutional development played in shaping these nations' long-term economic trajectories.

The role of institutions in shaping long-term economic growth is a complex and multifaceted phenomenon, as evidenced by various scholarly works. Komlos (1998) delves into the concept of modern economic growth, highlighting its connection to structural changes such as population growth, urbanization, and income inequality during the Industrial Revolution (Komlos_1998: p.2). This suggests that institutional development played a significant role in these shifts, potentially influencing long-term economic trajectories.

Frankema (2012) further complicates this understanding by examining the impact of colonial education on long-run growth disparities in Africa. The author argues that former British colonies exhibited faster growth due to their educational lead, which was influenced by the degree of British "liberalism" that was endogenous to the local political and economic context (Frankema_2012: p.2). This implies that institutional development, in this case, colonial policies, significantly affected the economic growth trajectory of African nations.

Goldin (2006) offers insights into the labor market institutions, suggesting that they have evolved over time, influencing women's choices and decisions (Goldin_2006: p.3). The distinction between evolution and revolution in this context pertains to three aspects of women’s choices and decisions (Goldin_2006: p.1), further emphasizing the role of institutions in shaping economic growth by influencing labor market dynamics.

Allen et al. (2011) provide evidence of income disparities during the early 20th century, attributing them to long-run stagnation in China and industrialization in Japan and Europe (AllenEtAl_2011: p.1). The rapid growth of the German economy during this period is also noted, as it translated into rising real wages for workers (AllenEtAl_2011: p.20). This indicates that institutional differences between countries played a crucial role in shaping their economic growth trajectories.

The relationship between institutions and economic growth is further nuanced by considering the impact of climate and environmental factors, as well as the role of superstructures in times of economic decline.

Koepke & Baten (2005) argue that while economic growth during the Roman Empire may have led to increased purchasing power, this did not translate into improvements in nutrition or stature, suggesting that institutional differences played a complex role in shaping living standards (p.16). Similarly, in the Habsburg monarchy, the standard of living and economic development were influenced by various factors, including climate and environmental quality (p.33). Furthermore, their study on Guatemalan children reveals that growth variability and environmental quality are interconnected, adding another layer to the discussion on institutional development and economic growth (p.32).

Hopkins (2019) complicates this argument by emphasizing the importance of superstructures during periods of economic decline. As the economic base crumbled, these superstructures took on a new significance (p.9). Additionally, Hopkins points out that food exports made little contribution to economic growth in West Africa during the period of the slave trade, suggesting that the role of institutions may have been overshadowed by other factors (p.13).

Austin (2007) contributes to this discourse by highlighting the advantage of railways over lorries in terms of cost-effectiveness during the mid-1920s (p.8). However, Austin also notes that many capital-deepening and TFP-improving forces of modern economic growth were not at work in Ghana, indicating that institutional development may have been insufficient for driving growth in certain contexts (p.16).

These sources underscore the complexity of the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth. While institutions undeniably play a crucial role, other factors such as climate, environmental quality, and superstructures also significantly impact economic development. Thus, a comprehensive understanding of this relationship requires careful consideration of these various influencing factors.

Institutions, as key drivers of long-run economic growth, have been a subject of interest for several scholars. Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) provide valuable insights into this relationship, albeit from slightly different perspectives.

Domar (1970) emphasizes the role of institutions in shaping labor markets. He argues that when labor is tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, potentially hindering economic growth. Conversely, structural change—a sign of a dynamic economy—correlates with growth (Domar_1970: p.4). Domar also highlights the impact of slaveholding on economic development in the Cotton Belt, suggesting that such institutions can negatively affect growth (Domar_1970: p.16).

Bohannan (1959) focuses on the influence of Western institutions on African societies, particularly those related to trade and status acquisition. He notes that these institutions could have both positive and negative effects on economic development, depending on factors such as the balance of gender ratios (Bohannan_1959: p.13).

Despite their differences, both scholars acknowledge the significance of institutions in shaping economic growth. However, they do not provide a comprehensive explanation for why some institutions promote growth while others hinder it. Moreover, they do not address how environmental factors and superstructures interact with institutions to influence long-run economic development.

Thus, while there is agreement on the fundamental role of institutions in economic growth, more research is needed to understand the specific mechanisms through which institutions impact growth and the conditions under which certain institutions are more likely to promote or hinder economic development.
