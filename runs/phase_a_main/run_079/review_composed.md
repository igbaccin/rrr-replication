The central question under investigation in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, a thesis that has garnered significant attention among scholars. The stakes are high, as understanding the drivers of economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting development and reducing poverty.

Supporting this thesis, Austin (2008) argues that institutions play a crucial role in long-term economic growth, particularly in Africa. He contends that these institutions shape the economic structure and reinforce existing economic positions, either positively or negatively, compared to other regions. Kuznets (1973) also supports this view, emphasizing the relationship between technological innovations, social innovations, and economic growth.

On the other hand, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate the issue by pointing out that a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has led to Sub-Saharan Africa's absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance, highlighting the role of women's economic contribution and how it is often subsumed under household production, undermining policies aimed at strengthening their economic roles.

Notably, North & Weingast (1989) and Acemoglu et al. (2001) provide strong support for the institutional view of long-run economic growth. North & Weingast (1989) focus on the political factors underpinning economic growth and the development of markets, demonstrating how institutions play a necessary role in making possible economic growth and political freedom. Acemoglu et al. (2001) show a large effect of institutions on economic performance, suggesting that differences in institutions, not just mortality rates, are significant drivers of variation in economic outcomes.

This review suggests a robust debate surrounding the role of institutions in long-run economic growth. While many scholars support the thesis that institutions are fundamental, others complicate the issue by pointing out factors such as data limitations and the role of women in economic growth. The argument continues as scholars delve deeper into understanding the intricate relationship between institutions and long-term economic growth.

The scholarship on institutions and long-run economic growth further strengthens the argument that institutions are indeed fundamental to economic development. For instance, Whatley (2018) presents a model demonstrating the relationship between gunpowder imports and slave exports in the context of deviations from long-run equilibrium (Whatley_2018: p.11). This suggests that institutions play a crucial role in shaping trade patterns and economic growth over time.

Similarly, van Zanden (2009) argues that the skill premium, which can be influenced by institutional factors, affects relative prices and thus contributes to long-term economic growth (vanZanden_2009: p.24). This supports the notion that institutions have a significant impact on an economy's DNA, as relative prices are considered its fundamental structure.

Moreover, Hopkins (2009) discusses the role of ethnic fragmentation in shaping institutions and economic growth (Hopkins_2009: p.21). However, the author emphasizes the need for a more convincing demonstration that other causes of solidarity and motives for action can be discounted, suggesting that the relationship between ethnicity and institutions is complex and warrants further exploration.

The persistence of institutions is crucial in understanding long-run economic growth. North & Weingast (1989) assert that representative government, characterized by multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, plays a vital role in fostering both economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28). They emphasize that these institutions are not merely rules governing economic exchange but also the mechanisms enforcing them and facilitating changes (North&Weingast_1989: p.2).

Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2001) highlight the significant impact of fundamental institutions on property and overall economic performance. They argue that differences in mortality rates do not account for variations in institutions, and these institutions can either hinder or promote investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3, p.27). Moreover, Abramovitz (1986) supports this perspective by emphasizing the role of institutions in fostering growth. He suggests that countries are now readjusting their economic systems to favor productivity growth, which is a critical component of long-term economic prosperity (Abramovitz_1986: p.22). Abramovitz also highlights the importance of macroeconomic and monetary conditions in encouraging capital investment and effective demand, all of which are essential for promoting economic growth (Abramovitz_1986: p.7).

The sources provided by Simson (2019) challenge the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, particularly in East Africa, during the 1960s and 1970s. Simson's evidence suggests that industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy were prioritized over institutional reform (Simson_2019: p.27). Furthermore, the author argues that while there might have been spending inefficiencies, eliminating them may not have been enough to sustain the investment levels of the 1960s (Simson_2019: p.4).

Additionally, Simson (2019) notes an expansion of state expenditure and deficits during a period of high economic growth in the region (Simson_2019: p.25), which could imply that factors other than institutions played significant roles in driving this economic growth. The author also mentions a recovery of the sub-region’s economic growth in the 2000s, but does not specify whether institutional reforms were key contributors to this recovery (Simson_2019: p.4).

These findings suggest that while institutions might have been important for long-term economic growth in some contexts, other factors such as industrial development, educational expansion, military autonomy, and public employment could have played equally significant roles during specific periods, particularly in East Africa during the 1960s and 1970s. Further research is needed to understand the interplay between these factors and their relative importance in driving long-term economic growth.

The works of various scholars provide nuanced perspectives that complicate the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth. For instance, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) highlight the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 in Sub-Saharan Africa, which has been absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. This suggests that the role of institutions might have been underestimated or overlooked in certain regions during specific periods.

Temin (2002) argues that the exhaustion of cheap labor caused economic growth to slow, implying that resource availability could play a significant role in shaping long-term economic trajectories. Furthermore, he posits that recent theories of economic growth should consider the context of economic transition, which complicates the notion that institutions are solely responsible for driving long-term growth.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) suggest that the task of explaining long-run distribution trends rejoins the search for the explanations of growth itself. This implies a potential interplay between income distribution and economic growth, which may challenge the notion that institutions are the sole drivers of long-term growth.

Skocpol & Sommers (1980) compare Japan and China to establish patterns in their responses to Western intrusions, revealing particularities of the cases. This comparative approach suggests that external factors might have played a role in shaping institutional development and subsequent economic growth.

Lastly, Stoler (1989) argues that colonizers lived in imagined communities, which were consciously created to overcome economic and social disparities. This finding underscores the importance of understanding the sociopolitical context in which institutions develop and operate, further complicating the notion that institutions are solely responsible for long-term economic growth.

These works collectively demonstrate that while institutions play a significant role in driving long-term economic growth, other factors such as resource availability, income distribution, external influences, and sociopolitical contexts may also have profound effects on economic trajectories over time. Therefore, a comprehensive understanding of the interplay between these factors is crucial for a more nuanced appreciation of the causes of long-run economic growth.

The contribution of African women to long-run economic growth is a complex and multifaceted issue, as highlighted by Akyeampong & Fofack (2014). While their economic roles have been substantial, they have often been overlooked or subsumed under household production (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). This oversight can undermine policies aimed at strengthening women's economic positions.

Moreover, the economic contribution of African women has historically been dependent on the economic provision of their husbands (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.9). This dependence, coupled with the marginalization of women in formal economic activities, has limited their potential to significantly contribute to long-term economic growth.

However, it is important to note that the major historical developments and themes discussed by Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) find parallels elsewhere in Africa where gender and economic growth are concerned (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.4). This suggests that the issue of women's contribution to long-run economic growth is not unique to West Africa, but rather a broader African phenomenon.

Understanding the historical and cultural contexts in which these gender dynamics have evolved is crucial for designing effective policies aimed at promoting women's economic empowerment and contributing to sustainable economic growth. The interplay between institutions, political contexts, and gender roles requires careful examination to fully appreciate the complexities of long-run economic growth in Africa.

The role of institutions in fostering long-run economic growth becomes even more intricate when considering the Industrialization Effect, as highlighted by Komlos (1998). In his study, Komlos emphasizes that the concept of modern economic growth, a key aspect of this thesis, is crucial for understanding the Industrial Revolution. He argues that structural change correlates with growth (Komlos_1998: p.2), suggesting that the transformation of economies from agrarian to industrial societies played a significant role in spurring long-term economic expansion.

Furthermore, Komlos underscores the connection between physical stature and economic development during the Industrial Revolution (Komlos_1998: p.16). He discovers 8-year height cycles similar to classical business cycles, implying that biological growth processes were influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic conditions. This finding underscores the interplay between institutions, economic growth, and societal factors during industrialization.

Lastly, Komlos delves into the structural changes associated with the Industrial Revolution, including population growth, urbanization, income inequality, and income variability (Komlos_1998: p.17). These transformations, driven by institutions, further complicate the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth, as they can both hinder and facilitate economic progress depending on their nature and implementation.

The works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), though tangential to the direct link between institutions and long-run economic growth, provide valuable insights that contribute to a comprehensive understanding of the subject. Both scholars emphasize the role of institutions in shaping economic structures, albeit in different contexts.

Domar (1970) highlights how institutional arrangements can impact the factors of production, particularly labor. He argues that when labor is tied to land or an owner, competition among employers ceases, potentially hindering economic growth (p.4). Conversely, Bohannan (1959) discusses the transformative influence of Western economic institutions on African societies, noting their impact extends beyond economics (p.10).

Despite these insights, both scholars diverge in their focus. Domar's analysis is grounded in a more macroeconomic perspective, while Bohannan focuses on micro-level cultural and social changes induced by external economic institutions. This divergence underscores the complexity of understanding the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth, as it encompasses various levels and aspects of society.

Remaining unresolved is the question of how to assess the nature and implementation of institutions to accurately predict their impact on long-term economic growth. While both Domar and Bohannan offer valuable insights, a comprehensive understanding requires further exploration into the qualitative characteristics of institutions that facilitate or hinder economic progress.
