The central question under investigation in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. This question holds significant importance as understanding the factors that drive economic growth can provide valuable insights for policymakers and economists aiming to promote sustainable development and alleviate poverty in various regions.

Several scholars support the assertion that institutions play a pivotal role in fostering long-term economic growth. For instance, Austin (2008) argues that institutions are "the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth" (p.26), and Kuznets (1973) posits that economic innovations and societal adjustments are major factors affecting economic structure and growth. Additionally, North & Weingast (1989) demonstrate how representative government institutions contribute to economic growth and political freedom, while Acemoglu et al. (2001) highlight the detrimental impact of institutions that hinder investment and economic progress.

However, not all scholars agree on the primacy of institutions in promoting long-run economic growth. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate the argument by pointing out that the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has kept Sub-Saharan Africa absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance to the discussion by emphasizing the importance of understanding how women's labor and economic contributions have been subsumed under household production, which can undermine policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles.

As this brief review reveals, there is a strong body of evidence supporting the idea that institutions are crucial for long-run economic growth. However, it also highlights the need for further research and data to fully understand the complex interplay between institutions, economic growth, and other factors such as technological innovation and societal changes. The ongoing debate underscores the importance of continued investigation into this critical question, with implications for policymaking, development strategies, and poverty alleviation efforts worldwide.

The significance of institutions in fostering long-run economic growth is further emphasized by scholars such as North and Weingast (1989), Mokyr (2005), North (1989), Ogilvie (2007), Pamuk (2007), and Nunn (2008). These researchers highlight the crucial role institutions play in promoting economic growth, political freedom, and market development.

North and Weingast (1989) focus on the political factors that underpin economic growth and the development of markets. They argue that the fundamental institutions of representative government, specifically an explicit set of multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are intimately related to economic growth and political freedom (North & Weingast_1989: p.2).

Mokyr (2005) suggests that changes in intellectual developments concerning useful knowledge played a significant role in the change in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West, making it much richer today than other regions (Mokyr_2005: p.4). He emphasizes that elements such as individualism, man-made formal law, corporatism, self-governance, and rules determined through an institutionalized process were key contributors to a progressive society.

North (1989) explores the relationship between institutions and economic growth by examining centralized enforcement mechanisms in various historical contexts. He argues that increased security rights and decreased transaction costs are fundamental to market development and long-term economic growth (North_1989: p.9).

Ogilvie (2007) acknowledges the importance of institutions, stating that the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework is crucial for their success (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). He also points out that the efficiency theory's assumption of individuals wanting the pie to be as big as possible may not always hold true in real-world scenarios.

Pamuk (2007) demonstrates how structural changes, such as growing commercialization of agriculture, were a direct result of significant events like the Black Death and modern economic growth (Pamuk_2007: p.4).

Nunn (2008) explores the relationship between ethnic fractionalization and long-term economic growth. He argues that during periods of uncertainty and insecurity, individuals required written records to secure property rights and promote economic development (Nunn_2008: p.4).

These scholars' findings collectively support the thesis that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Their research provides valuable insights into the political, intellectual, historical, and social factors that contribute to the development and success of institutions in promoting economic growth. Future investigations could further explore specific institutional mechanisms and their impact on various economic sectors to gain a more comprehensive understanding of this critical relationship.

Institutions play a crucial role in promoting economic growth, particularly in urban areas. The works of various scholars shed light on this relationship, highlighting specific institutional mechanisms and their impact on urbanization.

Kuznets (1973) notes that rapid changes in economic structure, including urbanization, are associated with modern economic growth (p.4). He further emphasizes the role of technological innovations in driving these changes, which provide the necessary adjustments for economic and social development (p.6). Kuznets also points out that while high aggregate growth rates can lead to positive achievements, there may be unexpected negative results even within developed countries (p.11).

Similarly, deVries (1994) underscores the importance of Europe's urban network in establishing a framework for regional economic development during the early modern period, setting the stage for industrial growth (p.5). He also suggests that renewed attention to the economic history of the household or family unit is warranted (p.8).

Hopkins (2009) supports this argument by emphasizing the role of institutions in supporting communities rather than acting as a weight on them (p.21). However, he also acknowledges that the assumption linking ethnic fragmentation to unproductive institutions requires more convincing evidence (p.22).

Frankema & van Waijenburg (2012) provide evidence of high population growth rates and economic migration in West African commercial centers, which could contribute to real wage rises due to high land-labor ratios (p.15, p.17).

Bryant (2006) supports the idea that institutions provide adequate environments for the growth of commercial exchange (p.13). He also highlights the radicalness of revisionist dissent challenging traditional views on the origins and nature of modern civilization (p.3).

These findings suggest that institutions play a significant role in promoting economic growth, particularly in urban areas, by facilitating structural changes, establishing frameworks for regional development, supporting communities, encouraging migration, and fostering commercial exchange. Further exploration of these institutional mechanisms could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth.

The institutional development and economic growth relationship is further supported by the works of Acemoglu, et al., and Abramovitz. Acemoglu et al. (2001) argue that more fundamental institutions significantly impact property rights and investment, ultimately affecting economic performance (p.19, p.27). They also assert that differences in mortality rates do not primarily cause variations in institutions (p.3).

In a subsequent study, Acemoglu et al. (2002) emphasize the crucial role of institutional changes in understanding economic and political development over the past five centuries (p.49). They also point out that the reversal in the distribution of economic prosperity may be linked to institutions (p.5).

Abramovitz (1986) supports this perspective by suggesting that the evolution of mixed economies is a function of economic growth itself (p.20). He highlights three factors contributing to economic growth: technological change, structural change, and macroeconomic conditions encouraging capital investment and demand levels (p.7). Furthermore, Abramovitz notes that countries are currently readjusting the balance in favor of productivity growth (p.22).

These findings underscore the critical role institutions play in long-term economic growth. By shaping property rights, fostering structural change, and encouraging investment, institutions can significantly influence a nation's prosperity over time. Further exploration of these mechanisms could provide a more comprehensive understanding of their impact on economic development.

Institutional factors may not be the sole cause of long-term economic growth, as some argue that ideologies and ideas can significantly influence a nation's prosperity over time. McCloskey (2015) critiques this notion by presenting examples of countries like France and Spain, where conservative ideologies hindered economic growth despite a material base for development (McCloskey_2015: p.9). Furthermore, Bryceson (2002) challenges the thesis by demonstrating that traditional hierarchical orders within communities can limit women's economic and social spheres, thus impacting urbanization and economic growth (Bryceson_2002: p.9, p.11). Additionally, Humphries (2013) argues that the division of labor, workshop production, and the emergence of the factory system were key factors in eighteenth-century economic growth, rather than institutions (Humphries_2013: p.18). These sources suggest that ideologies, social structures, and technological advancements may also play significant roles in shaping a nation's long-term economic growth.

The relationship between institutions and urbanization provides additional nuances to the argument that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth. Komlos (1998) suggests that physical stature during the Industrial Revolution was significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economics and associated structural changes, such as rapid population growth and urbanization (Komlos_1998: p.17). This implies that urbanization, a product of institutions, may have played a role in fostering economic growth.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that the task of explaining long-run distribution trends reconnects with the search for explanations of growth itself (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.8). They further suggest that policymakers often face trade-offs between economic growth and equality, implying that institutional structures influencing urbanization may have both positive and negative effects on long-term economic growth.

Frankema (2012) discusses the degree of British "liberalism" being endogenous to the local political and economic context (Frankema_2012: p.19). This suggests that the institutional structures imposed by colonial powers, which led to urbanization, may have had varying effects on long-run economic growth depending on the specific context.

Hopkins (2019) points out that as the economic base appeared to crumble, the superstructure assumed a new importance (Hopkins_2019: p.9). This shift in focus from production to institutions may have contributed to long-term economic growth by facilitating adaptation and innovation.

Goldin (2006) highlights that the study of labor was once mired in the institutions of the labor market (Goldin_2006: p.3). This indicates that understanding the role of institutions in shaping labor markets is crucial for comprehending long-run economic growth.

Clark & Jacks (2007) demonstrate that technological advancements, such as ventilation in the coal industry, allowed for a response to increases in demand during the Industrial Revolution era (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.4). This suggests that while institutions are important, technological advancements may also play a role in fostering economic growth by enabling structural changes.

These sources complicate the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by suggesting that ideologies, social structures, and technological advancements may also significantly impact a nation's long-term economic growth through their influence on urbanization and other institutional structures.

The role of institutions in fostering long-term economic growth is further nuanced by considering their interplay with climate and the impact of colonialism, as suggested by Sokoloff & Engerman (2000), Koepke & Baten (2005), Skocpol & Sommers (1980), and Stoler (1989).

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) argue that initial conditions, including factor endowments, have played a significant role in shaping long-run economic growth. They propose that unique country-specific circumstances could have had lasting impacts on growth trajectories, complicating the traditional view of institutions as the primary driver of economic development.

Koepke & Baten (2005) delve into the relationship between climate and human living standards over long periods, suggesting that this factor might also influence economic growth patterns. Their research reveals that while economic growth was observed during the Roman Empire, there was no corresponding increase in stature or nutrition, adding complexity to our understanding of how climatic conditions affect economic development.

Skocpol & Sommers (1980) emphasize the importance of comparing cases to reveal their particularities and highlight differences in Western intrusions as a factor influencing economic growth. They argue that focusing on positive cases of social revolution, such as Japan and China, provides valuable insights into the dynamics of institutional change and its impact on long-term economic growth.

Stoler (1989) argues that colonizers lived in "imagined communities" to overcome economic and social disparities, suggesting that colonialism might have played a role in shaping institutions and, consequently, long-run economic growth. The alliance between the plantation elite and the colonial administration created under such conditions could have had lasting impacts on institutional structures and economic development.

Institutions and Industrialization played significant roles in shaping long-run economic growth, as evidenced by the development of railways and population dynamics in various regions. Austin (Austin_2007: p.8) notes that until the mid-1920s, railways had a cost advantage over lorries, suggesting that institutional structures supporting railway development could have fostered economic growth. However, Austin (Austin_2007: p.12) also acknowledges that population growth was faster after 1918, implying that uncontrolled population expansion might have impeded the efficiency of these institutions and, consequently, long-run economic growth.

Allen et al. (AllenEtAl_2011: p.1) demonstrate income disparities between China, Japan, and Europe during the early 20th century, which were due to long-run stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe. This finding complicates the argument that institutions are the sole cause of long-run economic growth, as it suggests that other factors such as industrialization may also play a crucial role. Furthermore, Allen et al. (AllenEtAl_2011: p.20) highlight that rapid growth in the German economy was translating into rising real wages for workers in Leipzig, indicating that institutional structures supporting wage growth could contribute to economic development.

The examination of institutions as the primary driver of long-term economic growth is a theme that resonates in both Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959). Both scholars acknowledge the crucial role of institutions in shaping economic outcomes. Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) posits that with labor tied to land or owners, employer competition ceases, implying a detrimental effect on economic growth. Conversely, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) emphasizes the influence of Western institutions, particularly in slave dealing and status acquisition, which may have spurred economic development.

However, there is a divergence in their focus. Domar (1970) primarily concentrates on factors affecting labor mobility and competition, while Bohannan (1959) delves into the impact of Western institutions on African societies. Both scholars, though, acknowledge the significance of institutional structures in shaping economic growth trajectories.

Despite these insights, several questions remain unresolved. For instance, Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) suggests that a lack of labor mobility and competition can hinder economic development, but he does not elaborate on how institutions could be reformed to promote such mobility and competition. Similarly, Bohannan (1959) highlights the influence of Western institutions on African societies but does not discuss the potential implications for long-term economic growth.
