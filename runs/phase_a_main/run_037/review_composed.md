The question of whether institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth has been a subject of extensive debate among scholars, with various perspectives presented in the literature review. The central question at hand is whether the institutional environment significantly influences economic development over extended periods and, if so, to what extent.

The stakes of this discussion are high, as understanding the underlying factors driving long-term growth can provide crucial insights for policymakers aiming to foster economic progress in different regions. Acknowledging institutions' role in economic growth could lead to targeted interventions that strengthen institutional frameworks and, consequently, promote sustainable development.

Several scholars support the thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. For instance, Austin (2008) argues that institutions play a crucial role in determining long-term economic performance, stating, "AJR’s theoretical conclusion, of institutions as ‘the’ fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, is attractive in its logical economy" (p.24). Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) demonstrate that the development of representative government and common law courts contributes to economic growth and political freedom (p.28).

However, not all scholars agree unanimously with this perspective. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate the argument by highlighting data limitations for Sub-Saharan Africa before 1950, which has hindered its inclusion in recent debates about comparative long-run growth (p.1). Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance to the discussion by examining the role of women's economic contributions and how they have been historically subsumed under the category of household production (p.3).

The role of institutions in fostering long-run economic growth is further substantiated by various scholars, as evidenced by their focus on the interplay between institutions and education. Kuznets (1973) emphasizes that economic growth is associated with rapid changes in societal aspects, including family structure and urbanization (p.4). He also highlights how modern technology, a key driver of economic growth, can have unanticipated negative consequences, particularly within less developed countries (p.11).

North & Weingast (1989) argue that the fundamental institutions of representative government, such as multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are crucial for enabling economic growth and political freedom (p.28). Mokyr (2005) supports this notion by linking the development of a progressive society with elements like individualism, formal law, corporatism, self-governance, and rules determined through an institutionalized process (p.56).

Furthermore, North (1989) contends that centralized enforcement mechanisms significantly contribute to economic growth by reducing transaction costs and increasing security rights (p.9). Ogilvie (2007) underscores the interdependency of individual institutions within a broader framework, suggesting that the failure of one institution can impact the overall economic system (p.27).

Collectively, these sources suggest that strong institutions play a pivotal role in fostering long-run economic growth by promoting technological advancements, shaping societal structures, and facilitating the efficient functioning of various economic systems. The interplay between institutions and education is particularly noteworthy, as it contributes to the development of progressive societies and the widespread adoption of useful knowledge (Mokyr_2005: p.4).

The relationship between institutions and agriculture is a crucial factor in understanding long-term economic growth. Acemoglu et al. (2001) argue that certain fundamental institutions have a significant impact on property rights, which in turn affect investment and economic progress. They found that "institutions were detrimental to investment" (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27), suggesting that agricultural development may be hindered by poor institutions.

Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2002) emphasize the role of institutions in economic prosperity, stating that "this is a first-order fact" for understanding long-run development (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49). They also note that a reversal in the distribution of economic prosperity may be related to institutional changes (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5), implying that improvements in agricultural productivity could potentially result from positive institutional shifts.

Broadberry et al.'s (2015) work on India's long-term economic performance highlights the importance of institutions, as they discuss the "controversy surrounding India's economic performance" (BroadberryEtal_2015: p.2). The authors provide evidence suggesting that population estimates in India have been underestimated, which could have implications for understanding agricultural development and growth over time.

Bryant (2006) asserts that certain environments provided "more than adequate environments for the growth of commercial exchange" (Bryant_2006: p.13), suggesting that agricultural surpluses may have been essential for economic progress. The author also notes a radical revisionist dissent in the understanding of civilizational depth, which could potentially be related to the role of institutions in agricultural development and long-term growth.

The role of institutions in fostering economic growth and trade is further substantiated by various scholarly works. Austin (2008) posits that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, emphasizing their reinforcing effect on trade, such as the Atlantic trade, which potentially contributed to Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe (Austin_2008: p.24). This perspective aligns with the assertion that institutions play a crucial role in shaping economic development over extended periods.

Frankema & van Waijenburg (2012) provide additional support for this claim by demonstrating how West African commercial centers attracted large flows of economic migrants, leading to high land-labor ratios and subsequently boosting real wages (Frankema&vanWaijenburg_2012: p.15, p.17). This dynamic further underscores the importance of institutions in promoting long-term growth by facilitating labor mobility and economic activity.

Broadberry & Gupta (2006) also support this argument by highlighting the crucial role of institutions in shaping economic growth during the early modern period, particularly through the service sector (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.9). Their work suggests that the service sector played a significant role in fostering economic growth, reinforcing the notion that well-established institutions can lead to sustained development.

Challenging the notion that institutions are the primary cause of long-term economic growth, some scholars argue that factors such as ideologies and educational expansion may have played a more significant role in shaping economic trajectories.

Simson (2019) critiques the emphasis on industrial development and educational expansion in certain regions during the 1960s and 1970s, suggesting that spending inefficiencies and delays to reform might have hindered sustained investment levels (Simson_2019: p.4). Furthermore, McCloskey (2015) posits that in France, economic growth was throttled for conservative reasons, despite the examples of economic success in other regions such as Holland and England (McCloskey_2015: p.9). These findings suggest that ideologies and educational expansion may have played a crucial role in shaping economic growth, potentially challenging the centrality of institutions in this process.

Moreover, Humphries (2013) discusses the role of the division of labor, workshop production, and the factory system in 18th-century economic growth, which might imply that these factors, rather than institutions, were more critical for economic expansion (Humphries_2013: p.18). However, further examination is needed to fully understand the interplay between institutions, ideologies, education, and economic development.

The interplay between institutions, education, and economic development is a complex issue that necessitates further examination to fully understand its implications on long-run growth. Several scholars have provided insights into this relationship, complicating the initial assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

Komlos (1998) focuses on physical stature during the Industrial Revolution and discovers 8-year height cycles similar to classical business cycles. This finding suggests that biological growth processes were significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economics, indicating a relationship between institutions and economic processes (Komlos_1998: p.16). Furthermore, Komlos notes that the rapid growth of population and urbanization, increased income inequality, and variability during the Industrial Revolution may be linked to institutions and structural changes (Komlos_1998: p.17).

Lindert & Williamson (1985) explore the relationship between economic growth and equality and suggest that policymakers have often had to balance these two factors, implying a connection between institutions and distribution trends (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1). They also argue that the task of explaining long-run distribution trends rejoins the search for explanations of growth itself, further emphasizing the interplay between institutions and economic development (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.8).

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) examine the contrasting records in growth across societies and attribute this divergence to unique initial conditions or factor endowments that had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth. They also highlight the variation across societies in conditions relevant to growth, such as institutions (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.2).

Frankema (2012) argues that colonial education is a key factor in explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa, with former British colonies growing faster due to their educational lead (Frankema_2012: p.2). However, the degree of British "liberalism" was endogenous to the local political and economic context, indicating that institutions may play a role in shaping education policies (Frankema_2012: p.19).

These sources complicate the thesis by suggesting that while institutions are crucial for long-run economic growth, other factors such as education, endowments, and even biological processes may also play significant roles. Further examination is needed to understand the intricate interplay between these factors in different historical contexts.

Despite the central role of institutions in long-run economic growth, other factors such as education, endowments, and biological processes may also play significant roles. The case of Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA), for instance, has been underrepresented in recent debates due to a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). Collier and Gunning (1999) have observed that African economic performance has been markedly worse than other regions, while Sachs and Warner (1997) note the slow growth in SSA which has undermined economic progress in the past (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). However, understanding the extent to which there are still risks of shrinking that have plagued SSA's economic development is crucial for determining whether its current growth provides a path to convergence (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.18).

In other regions, new contributions to historical national accounting have provided insights into patterns of growth and development over centuries, rewriting the history of economic divergence and convergence since before the industrial revolution (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). This suggests that a comprehensive understanding of long-run economic growth requires examining data from various historical contexts, including SSA.

Moreover, understanding the processes by which women’s economic contribution becomes subsumed under the category of household production and their labor formally unaccounted for is crucial in policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). The contribution of African women to economic growth and development has largely been dependent on the economic provision of the husband (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.9, p.19). However, major historical developments in West Africa find parallels elsewhere in Africa where gender and economic growth are concerned (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.4), indicating that the role of women in economic growth may be a common theme across different African regions.

The Maddison Project has made numerous revisions to nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth, including for Germany, Sweden, Italy, Greece, Russia, and Bulgaria (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5). These revisions do not fundamentally change our view of economic growth patterns but provide a more detailed understanding of the processes involved (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5). This highlights the importance of continuous research and refinement in understanding long-run economic growth, as new data and methodologies can lead to a deeper understanding of the complex interplay between various factors.

The works of Clark and Jacks (2007) provide nuanced insights into the role of institutions in long-run economic growth, particularly in the context of the Industrial Revolution. They argue that technological advancements, such as ventilation in coal mines, played a crucial role in enabling the coal industry to respond effectively to increases in demand during this period (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.4). This suggests that institutions, in the form of technological knowledge and practices, can significantly impact economic growth by facilitating the efficient production and distribution of goods.

Moreover, Clark and Jacks (2007) highlight the importance of considering the broader economic context when analyzing specific industries. For instance, they defer prices using a price index for the economy as a whole, emphasizing the interconnectedness of different sectors in driving growth (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.3). This underscores the notion that institutions operating at various levels can contribute to long-run economic growth.

Braudel and Wallerstein's (2009) work further complicates our understanding of the relationship between institutions and trade in promoting economic growth. They argue that different players in a market influence each other's strategies, suggesting that institutional arrangements within markets can shape the trajectory of economic development (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.21). This perspective emphasizes the dynamic nature of institutions and their role in shaping market dynamics over time.

Feinstein's (1998) work also contributes to this discourse by highlighting the interplay between institutions, economic growth, and other factors such as wars and migration. For example, he notes that changes in farming practices and labor dispersal were crucial for economic growth in rural areas during certain periods (Feinstein_1998: p.28). This underscores the importance of understanding the historical context and specific institutional arrangements to fully grasp their impact on long-run economic growth.

The scholarly discourse on the role of institutions in long-run economic growth presents a blend of convergence and divergence. On one hand, both Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) acknowledge the significance of institutional arrangements in shaping labor markets and economic structures. For instance, Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) discusses how labor tied to land or owners can inhibit competition among employers, while Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) points out that Western impact centered on slave dealing and status acquisition, which significantly influenced local economic institutions.

On the other hand, these scholars diverge in their focus and scope. Domar (1970) primarily examines the factors of production within a broader economic context, while Bohannan (1959) focuses more on the social and cultural aspects of institutional change in African societies. Additionally, Bryceson (2002) contributes to the discourse by emphasizing the impact of livelihood diversification and changing gender roles resulting from land shortages and structural changes.

Despite these contributions, several key issues remain unresolved. For instance, a comprehensive understanding of how specific institutional arrangements lead to long-term economic growth is still lacking. Additionally, there is a need for more research on the interplay between cultural, social, and economic factors in shaping institutions and their impact on economic development over extended periods. Furthermore, exploring the role of institutions in rural areas during different historical periods requires further investigation to fully grasp their significance in long-run economic growth.
