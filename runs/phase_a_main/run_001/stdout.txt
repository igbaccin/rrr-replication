The debate surrounding the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, particularly in relation to institutions, has been a subject of extensive discussion among scholars. The central question at hand is whether institutions are indeed the primary driver of economic growth over the long term, and if so, what implications does this have for understanding economic development in various regions?

Austin (2008) complicates the argument by suggesting that while institutions play a crucial role, they may not be solely responsible for long-term economic growth. He argues that trades, such as the Atlantic one, reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe. On the other hand, Kuznets (1973) supports the thesis by emphasizing the role of institutions in facilitating technological innovations and social adjustments characteristic of modern economic growth.

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) also contribute to the discussion by complicating the argument, highlighting the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 for Sub-Saharan Africa. This has resulted in its absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) similarly add nuance to the debate by discussing how understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution is subsumed under household production is crucial for policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles.

North & Weingast (1989) support the thesis, demonstrating that representative government institutions with multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs are intimately related to economic growth and political freedom. Acemoglu et al. (2001) also offer support, showing a large effect of institutions on economic performance and indicating that institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress in certain cases.

The stakes of this debate are significant, as understanding the role of institutions in long-term economic growth can inform policies aimed at promoting development and reducing poverty in various regions. The ongoing discussion suggests that while institutions are indeed a fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, other factors such as technological innovations and social adjustments also play important roles. Further research is needed to fully understand these complex relationships and their implications for economic development.

Institutional structures significantly contribute to long-term economic growth, as evidenced by various studies. Kuznets (1973) notes that rapid changes in economic structure are associated with corresponding transformations in societal aspects such as family and urbanization patterns (Kuznets_1973: p.4). North (1989) underscores the importance of institutions in reducing transaction costs, thereby facilitating economic growth (North_1989: p.9).

Furthermore, Ogilvie (2007) highlights that economic institutions can create an interdependent framework that is crucial for sustained economic development (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). Acemoglu et al. (2002) argue that the distribution of economic prosperity has undergone a significant shift, which they attribute to changes in institutions (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.6).

These findings collectively support the notion that institutions play a pivotal role in fostering long-run economic growth. However, it is essential to recognize that while institutional development is crucial, other factors such as technological innovations and social adjustments also contribute significantly to economic performance. A comprehensive understanding of these complex relationships remains an area for further research.

According to North & Weingast (1989), political institutions play a crucial role in promoting economic growth and the development of markets. Acemoglu et al. (2001) also emphasize the importance of institutions in shaping economic performance. They demonstrate that more fundamental institutions have a substantial impact on property rights, investment, and overall economic success (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19).

Hopkins (2009) acknowledges the role of institutions in supporting communities rather than acting as a burden, although he notes that further research is needed to fully understand the relationship between ethnic fragmentation and unproductive institutions (Hopkins_2009: p.21).

The relationship between institutions and technological advancements is further emphasized in various studies, supporting the assertion that institutions are crucial for long-term economic growth. Similarly, Peters (2004) highlights the aims of land policy reform as promoting economic growth, sustainable management, and poverty reduction, indicating a direct connection between institutions and economic advancements (Peters_2004: p.7).

Moreover, Bryant (2006) notes that a nascent European fusion of mercantilist imperialism with technologically driven capitalism marked a significant shift in the institutional landscape, creating environments conducive to commercial exchange and economic growth (Bryant_2006: p.5). This fusion, according to Bryant, was radical and transformative, further underscoring the importance of institutions in fostering technological advancements and subsequent economic growth.

Despite the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, some scholars challenge this perspective. Nicholas Stern (2004) argues that economic growth can lead to environmental degradation in the early stages, contradicting the notion that institutions solely foster technological advancements and subsequent economic growth. Stern suggests that changes in economic factors drive changes in environmental impacts, which may be responsible for rising or declining environmental degradation over the course of economic development (Stern_2004: p.3).
