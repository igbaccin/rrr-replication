The question of whether institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth has been a subject of intense debate among scholars, with various perspectives offering support, critiques, or nuanced qualifications to the thesis. This review will delve into the central arguments presented in several key works, highlighting their implications for our understanding of economic development.

One of the most compelling pieces of evidence supporting the institutional growth theory comes from Austin's (2008) study. He argues that institutions play a crucial role in shaping long-term economic growth, particularly in Africa, where they have reinforced rather than originated the continent's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe and parts of Asia. The implication is that examining growth theories against long-term history can reveal relationships that recur due to path dependencies (Austin_2008: p.1).

Kuznets (1973) also offers support for the institutional growth theory, emphasizing the role of social innovations in facilitating modern economic growth. He argues that these innovations provide the necessary adjustments for tech-innovations to be effectively utilized, thereby influencing both economic and social structure (Kuznets_1973: p.6).

However, not all scholars wholeheartedly endorse this perspective. Broadberry & Gardner's (2022) work complicates the institutional growth theory by highlighting the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 in Sub-Saharan Africa, which has led to its absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. They suggest that understanding the extent to which there are still risks of shrinking undermining economic progress is essential for determining whether growth provides a path to convergence (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.18).

Similarly, Akyeampong & Fofack's (2014) work underscores the complexity of women's economic contribution in Africa and how understanding the processes by which their labor is informally unaccounted for can undermine policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles. They note that women's contributions to economic growth and development were largely dependent on the economic provision of the husband (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.9).

On the other hand, North & Weingast (1989) provide strong support for the institutional growth theory by focusing on the political factors underpinning economic growth and the development of markets. They argue that the fundamental institutions of representative government, such as an explicit set of multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are intimately related to economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.2).

Acemoglu et al.'s (2001) study also supports the institutional growth theory by demonstrating that institutions have a significant effect on economic performance. They argue that differences in mortality rates are not the only, or even the main, cause of variation in institutions, and that these institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3).

The assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is further substantiated by various scholars. Mokyr (2005) suggests that developments in the intellectual realm concerning "useful knowledge" were essential for the change in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West (Mokyr_2005: p.4). He highlights that many elements of a progressive society, such as individualism, man-made formal law, corporatism, self-governance, and rules determined through an institutionalized process, contributed to economic progress (Mokyr_2005: p.56).

Abramovitz (1986) supports the thesis by highlighting the role of institutions in encouraging capital investment and maintaining effective demand levels.
