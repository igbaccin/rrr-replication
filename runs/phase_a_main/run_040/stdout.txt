LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, a proposition that has garnered significant attention among scholars. This inquiry delves into the economic development and growth patterns across various regions, with a particular focus on Africa, and evaluates the role of institutions in shaping these trajectories.

The stakes are high as understanding the factors driving long-term economic growth is crucial for policymakers, investors, and researchers alike. If institutions indeed play a pivotal role in fostering economic growth, then focusing on improving institutional quality could be a key strategy for promoting development and reducing poverty. Conversely, if other factors are more influential, this would necessitate alternative approaches to stimulating economic growth.

The literature presents a nuanced picture of the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth. On one hand, scholars like North & Weingast (1989) argue that representative government institutions with multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs are essential for fostering economic growth and political freedom. Similarly, Kuznets (1973) emphasizes the importance of social innovations in providing the necessary adjustments for modern economic growth.

On the other hand, some authors complicate this picture. Austin (2008), for instance, suggests that institutions may reinforce rather than originate certain economic positions, such as Africa's relatively poor position compared to Western Europe. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has led to Sub-Saharan Africa's absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) highlight the underestimation of women's economic contribution and its impact on policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles.

The role of institutions in fostering long-run economic growth is further substantiated by various scholars. Kuznets (1973) highlights the association between rapid changes in economic structure and societal aspects, such as urbanization and family dynamics, during periods of modern economic growth (Kuznets_1973: p.4). These structural shifts are not only linked to high aggregate growth rates but also to both positive and negative outcomes within developed and less developed countries (Kuznets_1973: p.11).

North & Weingast (1989) emphasize the significance of representative government institutions, particularly multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, in promoting economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28). They argue that these institutions provide the necessary foundation for market development and rule enforcement (North&Weingast_1989: p.2).

Acemoglu, Johnson, and Robinson (2001) demonstrate that more fundamental institutions significantly impact property rights and economic performance (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19). They argue that detrimental institutions hinder investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27).

Mokyr (2005) suggests that the change in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West can be attributed to developments in intellectual realms concerning "useful knowledge" (Mokyr_2005: p.4). He argues that elements such as individualism, man-made formal law, corporatism, self-governance, and rules determined through an institutionalized process played crucial roles in promoting economic progress (Mokyr_2005: p.56).

Structural change, a shift in the economic composition of a country from agriculture to industry and services, plays a significant role in promoting long-run economic growth (BroadberryEtal_2015: p.4). This transition is often associated with increased productivity, higher wages, and overall economic development. For instance, India's economic performance since the late sixteenth century has been a subject of debate, but it's evident that structural change was instrumental in its long-run growth (BroadberryEtal_2015: p.2).

Moreover, the existence of environments conducive to commercial exchange significantly contributed to economic progress (Bryant_2006: p.13). The fusion of mercantilist imperialism with a technologically driven capitalistic transformation was radical and marked a shift towards industrialization, which is often linked to long-term economic growth. This shift, however, was not universally accepted, as some questioned the use of sunshine as a marker of civilizational depth (Bryant_2006: p.12). Nevertheless, the radicalness of this revisionist dissent underscores the importance of structural change in driving economic growth.

Structural change, as a critical factor in economic growth, is further supported by Abramovitz (1986). He suggests that the evolution of mixed economies is linked to economic growth itself, implying that institutional changes are integral to this process (Abramovitz_1986: p.20).

Moreover, Abramovitz (1986) emphasizes the importance of macroeconomic and monetary conditions in fostering capital investment and effective demand, which are key drivers of structural change (Abramovitz_1986: p.7). This underscores the role of institutions in shaping economic growth by creating an environment conducive to investment and demand expansion.

Furthermore, Abramovitz (1986) points out that countries are currently readjusting their economies towards productivity growth, indicating a shift towards more efficient institutions (Abramovitz_1986: p.22). This ongoing change highlights the dynamic nature of institutions and their role in long-term economic development.

Structural change, such as expanding public employment and industrial development, may not necessarily lead to long-term economic growth, as evidenced by the experiences in Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda (Simson_2019: p.25). Despite increased spending and deficits during a period of high economic growth, these economies failed to sustain investment levels or reorient towards productivity.

Moreover, economic factors driving changes in environmental impacts may not always lead to improved conditions over the course of economic development (Stern_2004: p.3). The assumption that environmental damage does not significantly impact economic activity is questionable, as irreversible damages could potentially reduce future income levels.

In some cases, ideologies and interests have hindered economic growth, such as in France after the Unfinished Revolution of 1789 where reactionary parties prioritized rigid Catholicism over economic growth (McCloskey_2015: p.9). Additionally, structural changes like the division of labor and the rise of workshop production did not always lead to sustained economic growth (Humphries_2013: p.18).

Lastly, in certain societies, traditional hierarchical orders have influenced women's economic and social spheres, limiting their participation in non-agrarian activities (Bryceson_2002: p.9). This limited role can undermine the provisioning capacity of men and contribute to economic instability.

These sources challenge the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by demonstrating instances where structural changes did not lead to sustained growth or improvements in environmental conditions, and cases where ideologies and societal norms hindered economic development. Further examination is needed to understand the complex interplay between institutions, economic factors, and cultural influences on long-term economic growth.

Institutions play a pivotal role in shaping long-run economic growth, but their impact is influenced by various factors including labor conditions and cultural influences. The works of Austin (2008), Komlos (1998), Temin (2002), Lindert & Williamson (1985), Sokoloff & Engerman (2000), and Koepke & Baten (2005) provide nuanced perspectives on this complex interplay.

Austin (2008) supports the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history, revealing relationships that recur due to path dependency rather than being solely caused by institutions. This suggests that while institutions are fundamental, their effects may be influenced by historical contexts and preceding conditions.

Komlos (1998) delves into the physical stature during the Industrial Revolution, implying that biological growth processes were significantly affected by short-term fluctuations in economic conditions. This finding complicates the argument that institutions are solely responsible for long-run economic growth, as it indicates that external factors can have profound impacts on growth trajectories.

Temin (2002) posits that the exhaustion of cheap labor caused economic growth to slow, suggesting that labor market conditions play a crucial role in shaping long-term economic development. This perspective challenges the notion that institutions are the sole determinants of economic growth.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that policymakers between economic growth and equality face trade-offs, indicating that the relationship between institutional structures and economic growth may be more complex than previously thought. They suggest that understanding this relationship requires an enormous sample of long-term national data, further complicating the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) explore the possibility that initial conditions, or factor endowments broadly conceived, could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth. They suggest that while conventional economic factors have been considered, explanations for contrasting records in growth have most often focused on institutions and highlighted variations across societies in conditions relevant to growth. This suggests that while institutions are important, initial conditions may also play a significant role in shaping long-term economic development.

Koepke & Baten (2005) investigate the relationship between climate and human living standards over time. Their findings indicate that while economic growth may have positive effects on living standards, this is not always the case, as demonstrated by the lack of increase in heights during the Roman Empire despite economic growth and protection. This complicates the argument that institutions are solely responsible for long-run economic growth, as it suggests that external factors such as climate can also impact living standards and economic development.

The impact of institutions on long-run economic growth becomes more complex when considering the role of colonialism, as evidenced by Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) and Frankema (2012).

Akyeampong & Fofack (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3) highlight that understanding the processes by which women's economic contributions were subsumed under household production undermines policies aiming to strengthen women's economic roles. This suggests that institutions, in this case gender-based norms, can hinder economic growth by limiting women's participation and recognition in the formal economy.

On a similar note, Frankema (Frankema_2012: p.2) argues that colonial education played a significant role in explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa. The British colonies, which controlled most of the fertile areas, experienced faster growth due to their educational lead. This implies that external factors like colonial policies can influence institutional development and, consequently, economic growth.

However, it's important to note that the degree of British "liberalism" was endogenous to the local political and economic context (Frankema_2012: p.19). This nuance indicates that while colonial policies may have shaped institutions, these policies were not universally applied and were influenced by local factors.

In West Africa, gender and economic growth were intertwined, with women's contributions largely dependent on the economic provision of their husbands (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.9). This highlights that understanding institutional development must consider the gendered aspects of economy and society.

The argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is further nuanced when considering the historical context of Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA). According to Broadberry & Gardner (2022), SSA has been largely absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth due to a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950.

Collier and Gunning (1999) note that African economic performance has been markedly worse than other regions, while Sachs and Warner (1997) observe that "African countries... have tended to grow more slowly" (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). This suggests that institutional development in SSA may have hindered long-term economic growth.

Moreover, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that understanding the extent to which there are still risks of shrinking that have undermined economic progress in the past is crucial for determining whether current growth provides a path to convergence. This implies that institutional changes in SSA must be examined not only in terms of their impact on growth but also in terms of their potential risks and consequences.

In addition, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) highlight that new contributions to historical national accounting have provided insights into patterns of growth and development over centuries in other regions, rewriting the history of economic divergence and convergence since before the industrial revolution. This underscores the importance of comprehensive data and analysis in understanding institutional development and its impact on long-run economic growth, especially in SSA.

Scholars Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), while not directly addressing the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, provide tangential insights that contribute to the understanding of this complex phenomenon, particularly in Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA).

Both authors highlight the role of institutions in shaping economic structures. Domar (1970) argues that when labor is tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, potentially hindering growth. In contrast, Bohannan (1959) discusses how Western impact on African societies influenced economic institutions, such as the centralization of slave dealing and status acquisition.

Despite these insights, both authors' works do not explicitly link institutional development to long-term economic growth. Domar (1970) focuses on the factors of production, while Bohannan (1959) delves into social aspects of African societies.

What remains unresolved is a comprehensive analysis that clearly demonstrates how specific institutions drive long-run economic growth in SSA. This requires further research incorporating Domar's (1970) factors of production and Bohannan's (1959) insights into social structures, to provide a holistic understanding of the relationship between institutions and economic growth in SSA.
