LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question under investigation in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, a thesis put forth by Austin (2008) among others. This question holds significant importance as understanding the factors driving economic growth could potentially inform policy decisions aimed at promoting development and reducing poverty in various regions.

Several scholars support this position. Kuznets (1973), for instance, argues that the succession of technological innovations characteristic of modern economic growth and the social innovations required for their implementation are major factors affecting economic and social structure (Kuznets_1973: p.6). Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) demonstrate how representative government institutions with multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs played a crucial role in enabling economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

However, not all scholars are convinced that institutions alone can account for long-term economic growth. Austin (2008) complicates the argument by suggesting that while institutions are important, they may reinforce rather than originate certain economic positions, particularly in Africa (Austin_2008: p.24). Broadberry & Gardner (2022) also offer a nuanced perspective, pointing out that the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has led to Sub-Saharan Africa's absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1).

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) further complicate the issue by highlighting how understanding the processes by which women's economic contributions have been subsumed under household production could undermine policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). Acemoglu et al. (2001) also add a layer of complexity by showing that differences in mortality rates are not the only, or even the main, cause of variation in institutions (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.4).

The relationship between institutions and education is a crucial factor in understanding long-term economic growth. Kuznets (1973) highlights that economic growth is associated with rapid changes in societal aspects, including economic structure and urbanization (Kuznets_1973: p.4). These transformations are linked to technological innovations, which provide the necessary adjustments for economic and social structure (Kuznets_1973: p.6). North & Weingast (1989) emphasize that representative government institutions play a significant role in fostering economic growth and market development (North&Weingast_1989: p.2).

Mokyr (2005) supports this notion by arguing that the change in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West can be attributed to developments in intellectual realms, particularly concerning "useful knowledge" (Mokyr_2005: p.4). North (1989) adds that centralized enforcement mechanisms and secure property rights contribute to reduced transaction costs, which are essential for market development and economic growth (North_1989: p.9).

Ogilvie (2007) also acknowledges the interdependency of individual institutions within a broader framework, suggesting that the failure of one institution can impact others and hinder overall economic growth (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). Thus, it is evident that well-functioning institutional frameworks, including those promoting education, are vital for long-term economic development.

Agriculture, as a primary sector, is significantly influenced by institutions and their effectiveness in promoting growth. The works of Acemoglu and his colleagues provide compelling evidence supporting this relationship.

In the study published in 2001 (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27), the authors demonstrate that more fundamental institutions, particularly those concerning property rights, play a crucial role in agricultural productivity. They argue that detrimental institutions were responsible for hindering investment and economic progress in many regions. Furthermore, they present evidence showing a large effect of institutions on overall economic performance (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19).

The following year, Acemoglu and his team continued their investigation, focusing on the structural change that occurred over time in various economies (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49). They found a correlation between this change and economic growth, emphasizing the importance of institutions in facilitating such transformation. The authors also highlight an interesting shift in the distribution of economic prosperity that they attribute, at least in part, to institutional changes (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.6).

These findings underscore the notion that well-functioning institutions are essential for long-term agricultural and economic growth. By promoting investment, fostering structural change, and ensuring fair property rights, effective institutions can significantly contribute to a country's prosperity and development. However, the reversal in economic fortunes observed by Acemoglu and his colleagues (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5) raises questions about the resilience of these institutions and the factors that may lead to their decline or improvement. Further exploration into these aspects could provide valuable insights for policymakers aiming to promote sustainable economic growth.

Institutions are crucial for long-term economic growth, as they facilitate interactions and provide the framework for economic development (deVries_1994: p.5). The Industrial Revolution, a significant monument in economic history, was made possible by Europe's urban network that connected cities, fostering commercial interaction and regional economic development (deVries_1994: p.2). This urbanization played a vital role in the onset of the Industrial Revolution, which subsequently led to unprecedented economic growth.

Furthermore, vanZanden (2009) highlights that the skill premium, which reflects the relative price of skilled labor, significantly impacts economic growth. A low skill premium can contribute to economic growth by reducing the cost of capital goods, as carpenters and masons—designers and makers of capital goods—with a lower skill premium tend to produce these goods at a cheaper rate (vanZanden_2009: p.27). This, in turn, stimulates investment and promotes economic growth.

According to several sources, institutions may not be the sole cause of long-run economic growth as the focus on industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy does not necessarily guarantee economic prosperity (Simson_2019: p.27). For instance, in Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda, state expenditure and deficits expanded during a period of high economic growth in the 1960s and 1970s, yet external factors contributed significantly to their subsequent crisis (Simson_2019: p.25).

Moreover, McCloskey (2015) suggests that ideologies and ideas, not institutions, played a crucial role in economic growth. In France, for example, conservative parties throttled economic growth despite the examples of bourgeois successes in other regions (McCloskey_2015: p.9). Humphries (2013) also challenges the thesis by emphasizing that the division of labor, workshop production, and the factory system were key drivers of eighteenth-century economic growth rather than institutions (Humphries_2013: p.18).

Furthermore, vanZanden (1999) critiques the consensus view in the 1960s and 1970s that the early modern economy was unable to generate economic growth. However, even in regions with positive 'output' measures of standard living, such as Galicia and Sweden, wage levels were still lower than in England (vanZanden_1999: p.13). These findings challenge the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

The evidence provided complicates the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by highlighting the role of education, climate, and colonial influence in shaping economic outcomes.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) suggest that initial conditions and factor endowments, including educational systems, may have had profound and enduring impacts on long-term economic growth. They argue that the United States and Canada achieved sustained economic growth during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries due to unique conditions, which contrasts with other countries that did not attain this goal until later (Sokoloff & Engerman_2000: p.12).

Frankema (2012) supports this notion by arguing that colonial education was a key factor in explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa. British colonies grew faster due to their educational lead, which may have been an unintended consequence of the British control over fertile areas (Frankema_2012: p.2).

However, Koepke & Baten (2005) challenge this perspective by demonstrating that climate can also play a significant role in shaping living standards and economic development. They found that over the period of the Roman Empire, heights did not increase, contrasting with the common view that living standards increased due to economic growth (Koepke & Baten_2005: p.16).

The exploration of institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is further nuanced by several scholarly works. Austin (2008) contends that while trade, such as the Atlantic one, reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe and parts of Asia. This perspective complicates the argument by suggesting that institutions played a role in shaping the economic trajectories of different regions, including Africa, during the colonial era.

Bolt & van Zanden (2014) support the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history, as revealed by relationships that recur because of similar situations or path dependencies. They also add revisions to nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth data for various countries, which do not fundamentally change their view but provide a more comprehensive understanding of economic development during this period.

Clark & Jacks (2007) argue that technological advancements, such as ventilation in the coal industry, enabled the industry to respond to increases in demand during the Industrial Revolution era. This finding suggests that institutions and technological innovations interact to drive long-term economic growth.

Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) emphasize the interconnectedness of economies and the impact of one strategy on others, which further complicates the argument by highlighting the complexity of institutional dynamics in shaping economic growth. Allen et al. (2011) demonstrate that income disparities in the early twentieth century were due to long-run stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe, suggesting that institutions played a role in shaping the relative economic performance of different countries.

The assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is further nuanced by highlighting disparities in economic performance across regions, such as Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA). According to Broadberry & Gardner (2022), SSA has been overlooked in recent debates about long-term growth due to a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950. Collier and Gunning (1999) and Sachs and Warner (1997) both observed that African economic performance has been worse than other regions, suggesting potential institutional factors influencing this disparity.

Moreover, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) emphasize the importance of understanding whether current growth patterns in Africa provide a path to convergence or still pose risks of stagnation, as seen in the past. They also note that recent contributions to historical national accounting have provided insights into economic divergence and convergence patterns in other regions, which could potentially be applied to SSA with further research.

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) add complexity to this discussion by focusing on the role of African women in economic growth and development. They argue that understanding how women's economic contributions have been subsumed under household production and largely dependent on their husbands' economic provision is crucial for strengthening women's economic roles. This finding suggests that institutional factors, such as gender norms, play a significant role in shaping economic performance in Africa.

The works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), although tangential, offer insights that contribute to the understanding of institutions as fundamental causes of long-run economic growth. Both scholars acknowledge the significance of institutional factors in shaping economic performance, albeit with different focuses.

Domar (1970) emphasizes the role of labor institutions in economic development. He argues that when labor is tied to land or an owner, competition among employers ceases, hindering growth due to lack of incentives for efficiency (Domar_1970: p.4). This aligns with the review's argument, suggesting that institutional arrangements can indeed impact long-term economic growth.

On the other hand, Bohannan (1959) discusses how Western economic institutions, particularly those related to slave dealing and status acquisition, had an impact on African societies (Bohannan_1959: p.7). However, his focus on social structures rather than economic ones diverges from the primary thesis.

Despite these insights, both authors fail to explicitly address the role of gender norms as a significant institutional factor shaping economic performance in Africa, which was highlighted earlier in this literature review. This omission leaves an unresolved aspect in understanding the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth, particularly in the African context.
