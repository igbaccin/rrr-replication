LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question of this literature review revolves around the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth. This thesis, put forth by Austin (2008), posits a direct relationship between institutional structures and sustained economic development over time.

The stakes are high, as understanding the underlying drivers of economic growth could potentially lead to more effective policies for promoting development in various regions, including Sub-Saharan Africa (Broadberry & Gardner, 2022). However, not all scholars agree that institutions are the sole or primary cause of long-term economic growth.

Austin (2008) argues that institutions as 'the' fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is an attractive conclusion due to its logical economy. He further suggests that trade, such as the Atlantic trade, reinforced rather than originated Africa’s relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe.

Kuznets (1973) supports this view, stating that rapid changes in economic structure are associated with modern economic growth and social innovations. However, he also acknowledges the non-conventional costs of economic growth, which could potentially undermine long-term development.

Broadberry & Gardner (2022), on the other hand, complicate this thesis by pointing out that a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has led to Sub-Saharan Africa's absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. They suggest that understanding the extent to which there are still risks of shrinking is crucial for determining whether current growth provides a path to convergence.

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance, noting that understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution becomes subsumed under household production is important for policies seeking to strengthen women’s economic roles. They argue that the major historical developments in West Africa find parallels elsewhere in Africa where gender and economic growth are concerned.

Komlos (1998) and Temin (2002) also complicate the thesis by discussing factors such as the exhaustion of cheap labor causing economic growth to slow (Temin, 2002), and the discovery of height cycles implying that biological growth was significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economics (Komlos, 1998).

This literature review sets out to explore the extent to which institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth, and how other factors such as trade, social innovations, gender roles, and labor market dynamics might influence this relationship. The findings could have significant implications for policy-making aimed at promoting sustainable economic development.

The relationship between institutions and technological advancements is crucial in understanding long-term economic growth. As Austin (2008) posits, institutions serve as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, with their impact on trade reinforcing rather than originating economic disparities (Austin_2008: p.24). Similarly, Kuznets (1973) emphasizes that rapid changes in economic structure and technological innovations are major factors affecting economic and social structure (Kuznets_1973: p.6).

North & Weingast (1989) provide a compelling argument on the political factors underpinning economic growth, highlighting the importance of representative government institutions with multiple veto points in promoting economic development (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). Acemoglu et al. (2001) support this notion by demonstrating that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27).

Mokyr (2005) suggests that developments in the intellectual realm concerning "useful knowledge" played a significant role in the growth of economic reasonableness, which in turn contributed to long-term economic growth (Mokyr_2005: p.4). North (1989) further underscores the importance of institutions by arguing that they reduce transaction costs and increase security rights, thereby promoting economic growth (North_1989: p.9).

The significance of institutions in promoting long-term economic growth is further emphasized by deVries (1994) and vanZanden (2009). DeVries (1994) asserts that the Industrial Revolution, a pivotal moment in economic history, was facilitated by Europe's urban network, which established a framework for regional economic development (deVries_1994: p.5). This suggests that institutions, represented here by the urban network, played a crucial role in enabling industrial growth.

Moreover, vanZanden (2009) posits that the skill premium, a measure of the relative wage cost of skilled labor, affects economic growth (vanZanden_2009: p.24). A low skill premium, according to vanZanden, can contribute to economic growth by reducing the relative price of capital goods, thereby stimulating their production (vanZanden_2009: p.27). This underscores North's argument that institutions reduce transaction costs and increase security rights, which in turn promote economic growth (North_1989: p.9).

Whatley's (2018) study further supports the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth. By examining the impact of gunpowder imports on slave exports in early modern Europe, he demonstrates how institutional changes can influence structural change, which in turn drives economic growth.

Equation 2 of Model 2 in Whatley's (Whatley_2018: p.13) research tells us that when gunpowder is above its long-run equilibrium level, it leads to an increase in slave exports. This suggests that the availability and affordability of advanced technology, such as gunpowder, can be facilitated by strong institutions that reduce transaction costs and provide security rights, thereby promoting economic growth.

Moreover, Whatley (Whatley_2018: p.11) notes that none of the variables in his model adjust to deviations from long-run equilibrium, indicating that the model is not one of co-integration. This implies that institutional changes can have a lasting impact on economic growth, as they alter the long-term relationship between key economic factors.

Institutions may not be the sole cause of long-run economic growth, as technological advancements and cultural factors can also play significant roles in shaping a nation's prosperity. The evidence suggests that certain ideologies and traditional hierarchical orders can hinder economic development, particularly in agricultural communities (Bryceson_2002: p.9, p.11). In some cases, excessive state expenditure and deficits during periods of high economic growth may not be sustainable, casting doubt on the importance of institutional reforms for maintaining investment levels (Simson_2019: p.4). Furthermore, it is argued that in certain historical contexts, the examples of successful bourgeois economies in countries such as Holland and England may have had a more substantial impact on economic growth than institutional changes (McCloskey_2015: p.8). These findings challenge the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by highlighting the potential influence of technological advancements and cultural factors on a nation's prosperity.

The assertion that institutions are the primary drivers of long-run economic growth is further nuanced by examining the interplay between institutions and technological advancements, as depicted in various scholarly works. For instance, Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) argue that initial conditions, or factor endowments broadly conceived, could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth. They suggest that the divergence in paths can be traced back to the achievement of sustained economic growth by the United States and Canada during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, while other nations did not manage to attain this goal until late in the nineteenth century (Sokoloff & Engerman_2000: p.12). This implies that technological advancements, which were likely more prevalent in these developed countries, played a significant role in their economic growth.

Moreover, Frankema (2012) argues that colonial education is a key factor explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa. The British colonies grew faster as a result of their educational lead, which may have facilitated technological advancements and knowledge transfer (Frankema_2012: p.2). Komlos (1998) also highlights the importance of understanding the influence of economic processes on physical stature during the Industrial Revolution, suggesting that short-term fluctuations in the economy significantly impacted biological growth processes (Komlos_1998: p.16).

These sources complicate the argument by emphasizing the potential role of technological advancements in driving long-run economic growth. While institutions remain crucial for facilitating and sustaining these advancements, they are not the sole determinants of a nation's prosperity. Instead, a combination of institutional quality, technological advancements, and cultural factors likely contributes to long-term economic growth.

Although institutions are crucial for long-term economic growth, other factors such as environmental conditions and technological advancements also play significant roles. Temin (2002) argues that the exhaustion of cheap labor was a key factor in slowing down economic growth during certain periods (Temin_2002: p.4). This suggests that institutions alone may not always be sufficient to sustain economic expansion, as other resource constraints could become limiting factors.

Moreover, Temin (2002) also contends that the end of the Golden Age was brought about by a return to equilibrium growth paths rather than adverse economic shocks in the 1970s (Temin_2002: p.14). This implies that institutional quality alone may not guarantee sustainable economic growth, as external factors such as resource availability and global economic conditions can also impact long-term development.

Koepke & Baten (2005) highlight the relationship between climate and human living standards, emphasizing that over the period of the Roman Empire, heights did not increase despite common beliefs about rising living standards due to economic growth (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.16). This underscores the importance of environmental factors in shaping long-term economic growth and suggests that institutions alone may not be enough to ensure prosperity in adverse environmental conditions.

The assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is nuanced by evidence suggesting that other factors, such as structural change and environmental conditions, play significant roles in economic development.

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that Sub-Saharan Africa's absence from recent debates about long-term growth is due to the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950. This data gap hinders understanding whether economic progress in the region provides a path to convergence or poses risks of shrinking, as observed in the past (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.1). Thus, while institutions may influence growth, they might not be sufficient in adverse environmental conditions or regions with limited historical data.

Furthermore, van Zanden (1999) complicates the argument by challenging the consensus view that the early modern economy was unable to generate economic growth before the industrial revolution. He presents evidence suggesting a negative link between economic development and certain 'output' measures of standard living in mid-eighteenth century Western Europe (vanZanden_1999: p.13). This finding underscores the importance of considering structural change as a factor that correlates with long-term growth, alongside institutions.

The scholarship on the role of institutions in long-run economic growth reveals a convergence on the fundamental significance of these factors, yet some discrepancies persist. Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), while discussing different historical contexts, emphasize the impact of institutional structures on economic development.

Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) highlights that with labor tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, impeding growth. However, he also acknowledges (Domar_1970: p.4) that in situations where labor is not tied, such as in the Russian peasants' enserfment case, competition can stimulate economic growth.

Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) notes that the Western impact on African societies extended beyond economic institutions, focusing on slave dealing and status acquisition. He also mentions the limited number of women in these societies as a factor affecting economic development (Bohannan_1959: p.13).

Despite these insights, several aspects remain unresolved. For instance, Domar's (1970) work does not delve deeply into the specific mechanisms through which labor mobility fosters competition and spurs growth. Similarly, Bohannan (1959) fails to explore fully how the gender imbalance affected economic institutions and long-term growth in African societies.
