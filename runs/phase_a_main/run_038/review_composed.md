The question of whether institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth has been a subject of extensive debate among economists and historians. This discourse is significant as it sheds light on the factors that drive economic development, with potential implications for policy formulation and economic strategies in various regions.

Several scholars support the thesis that institutions play a pivotal role in long-term economic growth. For instance, Austin (2008) posits that institutions are "the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth" (p.26). Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) argue that representative government institutions, such as multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are crucial for economic growth and political freedom (p.28). Acemoglu et al. (2001) also suggest that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress (p.27).

However, not all agree. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate the argument by pointing out the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 in Sub-Saharan Africa, which has led to its absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth (p.1). Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance by discussing the role of women's economic contribution and how it has been subsumed under household production, undermining policies aimed at strengthening their economic roles (p.3).

The stakes in this debate are high, as understanding the primary drivers of long-term economic growth can inform policy decisions and development strategies. The argument continues, with scholars still debating the extent to which institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, or if other factors such as geography, culture, or technology play equally important roles.

The assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is further supported by various scholars. Kuznets (1973) emphasizes the relationship between economic structure and society, suggesting that rapid changes in both are associated with modern economic growth (Kuznets_1973: p.4). He also acknowledges the non-conventional costs of economic growth, which underscores the importance of institutions in managing these negative effects (Kuznets_1973: p.11).

North & Weingast (1989) argue that representative government, with its multiple veto points and common law courts primacy over economic affairs, plays a crucial role in fostering economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28). North (1989) also supports this view, stating that institutions can reduce transaction costs and promote market development, thereby contributing to economic growth (North_1989: p.9).

Ogilvie (2007) highlights the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework, suggesting that a comprehensive understanding of economic growth requires considering these institutions together (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). Whatley's (2018) econometric analysis reveals correlations between institutions and economic indicators, offering empirical evidence for the role of institutions in long-run economic growth (Whatley_2018: p.3).

Pamuk (2007) discusses how structural changes, such as those brought about by the Black Death, led to significant wage increases and economic growth (Pamuk_2007: p.4). These findings underscore the importance of institutions in facilitating such transformations and promoting long-term economic growth.

The role of institutions in facilitating long-term economic growth is further highlighted by Austin's (2008) analysis of trade between Africa and Western Europe. He argues that these trades, rather than originating Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe, actually reinforced it (Austin_2008: p.24). This suggests that the institutional structures in place in Africa were not conducive to promoting growth and development through trade, further emphasizing the crucial role of institutions in long-run economic growth.

Moreover, Austin (2008) supports the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history, revealing relationships that recur because of similar situations as well as path dependence (Austin_2008: p.1). This approach underscores the significance of institutions in shaping economic outcomes over time, providing further evidence for their fundamental role in long-run economic growth.

The persistence of institutions plays a crucial role in shaping long-run economic growth, as demonstrated by Acemoglu and colleagues (2001). Their research reveals that fundamental institutions significantly impact property rights, investment, and economic progress. For instance, they argue that differences in mortality rates are not the primary cause of variation in institutions but rather the institutions themselves (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3).

Moreover, Acemoglu et al. (2001) contend that these institutions can be detrimental or beneficial to economic development. They find evidence of a large effect of institutions on economic performance, as shown in their analysis of various countries (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19). Furthermore, they highlight the importance of structural change, suggesting that it often correlates with growth (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27).

While Acemoglu and his colleagues argue that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, other scholars challenge this perspective. Simson (2019) suggests that in Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda, state expenditure and deficits expanded during a period of high economic growth in the 1960s and 1970s (Simson_2019: p.25). This expansion occurred despite spending inefficiencies, leakages, and costly delays to reform, casting doubt on whether their elimination would have been enough to sustain the investment levels of the period (Simson_2019: p.4).

Moreover, Stern (2004) questions the Environmental Kuznets Curve (EKC) hypothesis, which suggests that economic growth leads to environmental degradation in the early stages but eventually results in environmental improvement (Stern_2004: p.1). Stern argues that this assumption may not hold true as irreversible environmental damage could reduce economic activity insufficiently to stop the growth process and potentially lower future income levels (Stern_2004: p.8).

McCloskey (2015) also critiques the notion that institutions are the primary driver of economic growth by highlighting instances where ideologies and interests, rather than institutions, hindered economic development. For example, in France after the Unfinished Revolution of 1789, reactionary parties prospered which were uninterested in economic growth if they could impose a rigid form of Catholicism (McCloskey_2015: p.9).

Lastly, Bryceson (2002) argues that structural change may not always correlate with economic growth due to traditional hierarchical orders within communities influencing women's economic and social spheres (Bryceson_2002: p.9). In some areas, young households with restricted land access have become more dependent on non-agricultural economic activities or resorting to migrant labor, undermining men's role as family provisioners (Bryceson_2002: p.11).

These sources challenge the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by highlighting factors such as state expenditure, ideologies, and traditional hierarchies that may also play significant roles in shaping economic performance.

The assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is complicated by various factors highlighted in the sources provided. For instance, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) point out that Sub-Saharan Africa has been absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth due to a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950. They also mention that African economic performance has been markedly worse than other regions, suggesting that factors beyond institutions may contribute to economic growth in these regions (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.1).

Komlos (1998) further complicates the argument by discussing the relationship between climate and human living standards, stating that over the period of the Roman Empire, heights did not increase at all, contrasting with the common view that living standards increased during this time due to economic growth (Koepke & Baten_2005: p.16). This suggests that environmental factors may also play a significant role in shaping economic performance.

Temin (2002) argues that it was the exhaustion of cheap labor that caused economic growth to slow, implying that labor supply and demand might be crucial determinants of long-run economic growth (Temin_2002: p.4). Additionally, Lindert & Williamson (1985) suggest a causal relationship between economic growth and inequality, complicating the notion that institutions are the sole cause of long-run economic growth (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.8).

The work of Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) adds nuance to the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, particularly in the context of African women's contribution to colonial economies. Their research underscores the importance of recognizing the role of gender in shaping economic structures, challenging the notion that institutions alone drive long-term growth.

Akyeampong and Fofack (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3) highlight how the economic contributions of African women have historically been subsumed under household production, making their labor formally unaccounted for. This oversight has significant implications for policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles. The authors also note that the contribution of African women to economic growth and development was largely dependent on the economic provision of the husband (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.9).

Moreover, Akyeampong and Fofack (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.4) argue that major historical developments in West Africa, which they discuss, find parallels elsewhere in Africa where gender and economic growth are concerned. This suggests that the role of institutions in shaping long-term economic growth may be influenced by gender dynamics, complicating the assertion that institutions are the sole drivers of economic growth.

Finally, Akyeampong and Fofack (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.19) state that understanding the contribution of African women to economic growth is crucial for any comprehensive analysis of long-term economic development in Africa. This underscores the importance of considering gender dynamics when examining the role of institutions in driving economic growth.

The work of Sokoloff and Engerman (2000) adds nuance to the assertion that institutions are the primary driver of long-run economic growth by acknowledging the role of initial conditions or factor endowments in shaping economic trajectories. They argue that the United States and Canada experienced sustained economic growth during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, while other countries did not, due to differences in institutional structures (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.12).

Moreover, Sokoloff and Engerman (2000) emphasize that conventional economic factors have been overshadowed in explanations for contrasting growth records across societies, with a focus on institutions and the variation in conditions relevant to growth such as property rights, political stability, and rule of law (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.2). They also acknowledge that structural change correlates with economic growth, suggesting that institutional changes leading to structural transformation could play a significant role in fostering long-term economic development (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4).

However, the work of Sokoloff and Engerman (2000) also complicates the argument by highlighting the importance of initial conditions as determinants of economic growth. They argue that certain structural features, such as factor endowments, may have profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic development, suggesting that institutions alone may not be sufficient to explain all differences in economic growth across countries (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4).

The studies by Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), while not directly addressing the central argument of institutions as the primary driver of long-run economic growth, offer tangential insights that contribute to a broader understanding of the topic. Both scholars emphasize the significance of institutional structures in shaping economies, albeit focusing on different contexts and aspects.

Domar (1970) discusses the role of institutions in regulating labor markets, arguing that when labor is tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, potentially hindering economic growth (p.4). This perspective aligns with the institutional view, suggesting that institutions can indeed play a crucial role in shaping economic outcomes.

On the other hand, Bohannan (1959) focuses on the impact of Western institutions on African societies, particularly in the context of the slave trade. He notes that these institutions affected not only economic but also social and political structures (p.10). This perspective underscores the multidimensional nature of institutions and their potential to influence various aspects of society, thereby contributing to economic growth or hindrance.

Despite these converging views on the importance of institutions, both studies leave unresolved the question of whether institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. While they suggest that institutions can significantly shape economies, they do not provide conclusive evidence that institutions alone can explain differences in economic growth across countries. Further research is needed to address this question more directly and to disentangle the complex interplay between institutions, factor endowments, and other factors influencing long-run economic development.
