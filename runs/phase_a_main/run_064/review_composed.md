The fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is a topic that has garnered significant attention among scholars, with institutions emerging as a key factor. This review explores the literature on this subject, highlighting the perspectives of various authors and the implications of their findings.

The central question at hand is whether institutions are indeed the primary driver of long-term economic growth, and if so, what specific aspects of these institutions contribute to such growth. The stakes are high, as understanding the causes of economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting development in various regions.

Several authors support the thesis that institutions play a crucial role in economic growth. For instance, North & Weingast (1989) argue that representative government and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs are intimately related to economic growth and political freedom. Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2001) demonstrate that institutions significantly impact economic performance, with detrimental institutions hindering investment and progress.

However, not all scholars agree on the primacy of institutions. Austin (2008), for example, complicates the argument by suggesting that while institutions are important, they may reinforce rather than originate economic positions, as seen in the case of Africa's relatively poor economic standing compared to Western Europe. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) also add nuance to the discussion by pointing out that a lack of data on pre-1950 economic performance in Sub-Saharan Africa has limited its inclusion in recent growth debates.

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) further complicate the issue by highlighting the underrepresentation of women's economic contributions and the challenges in understanding their roles in growth and development. Kuznets (1973), on the other hand, supports the thesis by linking rapid changes in economic structure to modern economic growth and social innovations.

The works of North & Weingast (1989) and North (1989) provide substantial support for the thesis that institutions are a primary cause of long-run economic growth. North & Weingast's study focuses on the political factors underpinning economic growth, emphasizing the role of representative government and common law courts in enforcing and changing rules essential for market development (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). They argue that these institutions played a necessary role in making possible economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.30).

Similarly, North (1989) discusses the relationship between transaction costs, security rights, and market development, suggesting that centralized enforcement mechanisms can hinder economic growth (North_1989: p.1). He also highlights how institutions can influence economic incentives, contributing to the long-term growth of effective demand and capital investment (Abramovitz_1986: p.7).

Ogilvie's (2007) work supports the thesis by acknowledging the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework and the importance of efficient institutions for economic growth (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). He also addresses the Achilles heel of the efficiency theory, emphasizing that most people in an economy may want the overall size of the economic pie to be as large as possible (Ogilvie_2007: p.14).

The significance of institutions in promoting long-run economic growth is further emphasized by Acemoglu, et al. (2001) and Pamuk (2007). These scholars provide compelling evidence that supports the thesis, focusing particularly on the role of institutions in agriculture.

Acemoglu, et al. (2001) argue that more fundamental institutions have a substantial impact on property rights, which in turn influences economic performance. They assert that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress, highlighting the importance of institutional quality for long-term economic growth (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). Furthermore, they demonstrate a large effect of institutions on economic performance through their analysis of various countries (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19).

Pamuk (2007) supports this notion by focusing on the commercialization of agriculture as a crucial factor in economic growth. He notes that changes in agriculture paved the way for modern economic growth and were preceded by significant demographic shifts, such as the Black Death (Pamuk_2007: p.4). This commercialization of agriculture led to increased productivity and economic development (Pamuk_2007: p.24).

The role of institutions in fostering long-run economic growth is further emphasized by Mokyr (2005) and Whatley (2018). Mokyr (Mokyr_2005: p.4) suggests that the intellectual developments in the realm of "useful knowledge" played a significant role in explaining why the West is so much richer today compared to other regions. He highlights the growth of economic reasonableness, which was not monotonic or irreversible but still contributed to the progression towards modern economic growth (Mokyr_2005: p.54).

Moreover, Mokyr (Mokyr_2005: p.56) points out that many elements of a progressive society, such as individualism, man-made formal law, corporatism, self governance, and rules determined through institutionalized processes, were crucial factors in the development of economic growth. These aspects promoted economic development by encouraging productivity, innovation, and stability.

Similarly, Whatley (2018) provides evidence for the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth by analyzing the econometric implications of gunpowder imports and slave exports in the early modern period (Whatley_2018: p.3). The author's model indicates that when gunpowder is above its long-run equilibrium level, it leads to an increase in slave exports (Whatley_2018: p.11, p.13), suggesting that the presence of institutions and technology can drive economic growth through trade and industrialization.

Structural change, such as the transition from agrarian to industrial economies, is not necessarily a prerequisite for long-run economic growth (vanZanden_1999: p.14). For instance, in the mid-eighteenth century, certain regions like Galicia and Sweden were less developed than England but still managed to maintain a relatively high standard of living without undergoing significant structural change.

Moreover, some argue that economic growth before the industrial revolution was limited due to factors such as low population growth and insufficient technological advancements (vanZanden_1999: p.1). The optimistic interpretation of pre-industrial economic growth has gained traction in recent years, challenging the consensus view that the early modern economy was incapable of generating sustained growth.

Furthermore, economic growth can lead to environmental degradation rather than being a means to eventual environmental improvement (Stern_2004: p.1). In the early stages of economic development, economic activities often have detrimental effects on the environment. However, as economies develop and technological advancements occur, environmental impacts may decrease over time due to changes in the structure or technology of the economy (Stern_2004: p.3, p.8).

In addition, it has been suggested that the division of labor and the rise of workshop production were not significant factors contributing to eighteenth-century economic growth in Europe (Humphries_2013: p.18). The relative needs of women and children, as well as lower fertility rates in France compared to Britain, may have limited the potential for economic growth through increased labor division (Humphries_2013: p.11, p.19).

These sources challenge the thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by suggesting that other factors, such as environmental impacts and structural change, may play a more significant role in determining economic development over time.

The assertion that institutions are the primary cause of long-term economic growth is challenged by a variety of factors as suggested by several sources. For instance, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that Sub-Saharan Africa's absence in recent debates about long-run growth is due to a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950. They highlight that African countries have experienced slower growth compared to other regions (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). This implies that factors such as environmental impacts or structural changes might have played a more significant role in determining economic development over time in this region.

Similarly, Komlos (1998) complicates the argument by suggesting that physical stature during the Industrial Revolution was influenced by short-term fluctuations in the economy, indicating the interplay between biological growth processes and economic transitions (Komlos_1998: p.16, p.17).

Temin (2002) proposes that the exhaustion of cheap labor caused economic growth to slow, suggesting that economic shocks in the 1970s may not have been the sole cause of the end of the Golden Age (Temin_2002: p.14). Furthermore, Allen (2001) highlights that wages and prices have long been central concerns for economic historians, implying that they bear on fundamental issues such as the pace of economic development and standard of living (Allen_2001: p.1).

Lastly, Hopkins (2019) argues that 'totalism' and 'essentialism', which were considered Western impositions on other parts of the world, inhibited the study of long-run economic and political change in Africa. This suggests that independent African viewpoints might have contributed to a more nuanced understanding of economic growth (Hopkins_2019: p.10).

These sources add nuance to the thesis by suggesting that other factors, such as environmental impacts, structural changes, and regional differences, may play a significant role in determining long-term economic growth. The debate remains open for further examination of these factors and their relative importance in shaping economic development over time.

The role of initial conditions and factor endowments in shaping long-term economic growth is a significant aspect that has been explored by scholars (Sokoloff & Engerman_2000). Sokoloff and Engerman suggest that unique conditions within individual countries, such as resource abundance or geographical location, may have had profound and enduring impacts on economic development. For instance, the United States and Canada achieved sustained growth during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, while other regions did not (Sokoloff & Engerman_2000: p.12).

However, it's essential to acknowledge that while initial conditions play a role, institutions are also crucial determinants of economic growth. Sokoloff and Engerman emphasize the variation across societies in conditions relevant to growth such as property rights, political stability, and rule of law (Sokoloff & Engerman_2000: p.2).

Furthermore, climate can also influence long-run economic history. Koepke and Baten argue that the relationship between climate and human living standards is a fascinating topic (Koepke & Baten_2005: p.20). For example, over the period of the Roman Empire, heights did not increase, which contrasts with the common view that living standards and purchasing power increased during this time due to economic growth (Koepke & Baten_2005: p.16).

In addition, institutional development is intertwined with regional differences in economic growth. Bolt and van Zanden argue that revisions of nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth have been made for several countries, but these do not fundamentally change the understanding of long-term economic growth (Bolt & vanZanden_2014: p.5).

Lastly, Skocpol and Sommers highlight the importance of comparing cases to increase the visibility of one structure by contrasting it with another (Skocpol&Sommers_1980: p.8). This comparative approach can help reveal particularities in the cases under examination, as demonstrated in their work on Japan and China (Skocpol & Sommers_1980: p.14).

The interplay between institutions and education is a crucial factor in understanding long-run economic growth, as evidenced by several scholarly works. Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that the British classical economists recognized the importance of institutions in driving growth, suggesting a historical link between institutional structures and economic distribution trends (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1). Furthermore, they assert that policymakers often face trade-offs between economic growth and equality, necessitating an enormous sample of long-term national data to address such complexities in empirical economics (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1).

Frankema (2012) adds nuance to this discussion by focusing on the role of colonial education in shaping long-run growth disparities, particularly in Africa. Frankema argues that former British colonies exhibited faster growth due to their educational lead, with the degree of British "liberalism" being endogenous to local political and economic contexts (Frankema_2012: p.2, p.19). However, it is important to note that the British controlled many fertile areas, potentially influencing this outcome (Frankema_2012: p.2).

Goldin (2006) highlights the significance of institutions in shaping labor markets and women's choices, arguing that a shift from evolution to revolution in these structures can have profound effects on economic growth (Goldin_2006: p.3, p.1). For instance, Goldin discusses how legal changes can impact women's decisions and choices over time (Goldin_2006: p.14).

These sources complicate the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by demonstrating the interplay between institutions, education, and other factors such as colonial influence and legal reforms. Future research may delve deeper into these relationships to further elucidate the complexities involved in institutional development and its impact on long-term economic growth.

The literature on the role of institutions in long-run economic growth, as evidenced by Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), highlights a shared consensus that institutions play a pivotal role in shaping economic trajectories. Both scholars acknowledge the impact of institutional structures on labor markets, with Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) pointing out how labor tied to land or owners inhibits competition among employers, and Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) noting the centrality of slave dealing in African societies.

However, divergences also emerge. Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) posits that enserfment of peasants in Russia impeded economic growth, while Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.10) suggests that the Western impact on African institutions extended beyond economic ones. Furthermore, Domar (Domar_1970: p.16) references Phillips' work on the economic cost of slaveholding in the Cotton Belt, implying a link between institutions and slavery that Bohannan (1959) does not explicitly address.

Despite these insights, several questions remain unanswered. For instance, the specific mechanisms through which institutional changes lead to long-term economic growth require further exploration. Additionally, the interplay between various factors such as colonial influence and legal reforms necessitates more detailed analysis. Lastly, the impact of institutions on gender dynamics, as hinted by Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.13), calls for deeper investigation.
