LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question in the literature review on the role of institutions in long-term economic growth is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of such growth, or if other factors play a more significant role. This question holds substantial stakes as understanding the primary drivers of economic growth can inform policies aimed at promoting development and reducing poverty.

Several scholars support the notion that institutions are crucial for long-term economic growth. For instance, Austin (2008) argues that institutions are "the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth" (p.26), a conclusion he finds attractive due to its logical economy. Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) focus on the political factors underpinning economic growth and development, demonstrating how institutions played a necessary role in making possible economic growth and political freedom (p.30).

On the other hand, some scholars offer critiques and complications to this perspective. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that Sub-Saharan Africa's absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth is due to a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950, suggesting that the role of institutions in economic growth may be more nuanced (p.1). Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) highlight that understanding the processes by which women's economic contributions are subsumed under household production and their labor formally unaccounted for is essential to strengthening policies aimed at promoting women's economic roles (p.3).

The works of North & Weingast (1989) and North (1989) provide substantial support for the notion that institutions are fundamental to long-run economic growth.

Furthermore, Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49) support this perspective by stating that understanding economic and political development over the past 500 years necessitates an acknowledgement of the central role institutions play in shaping prosperity. They argue that the reversal in the distribution of economic prosperity is related to institutional changes, emphasizing the significance of institutions in long-term development.

In addition, Abramovitz (Abramovitz_1986: p.20) suggests that the elaboration of mixed economies is a function of economic growth itself, implying that institutions evolve and adapt alongside the development of an economy. Hopkins (Hopkins_2009: p.21) also supports this perspective by claiming that certain institutions can support communities rather than act as weights on them, highlighting their essential role in promoting economic growth.

Lastly, Nunn & Wantchekon (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.4) demonstrate that both internal and external channels of institutional change are important for long-run economic growth, with the internal channel being at least twice as large as the external one. These findings underscore the critical role of institutions in shaping economic development over time.

The relationship between institutions and technological advancements is a crucial aspect in understanding long-run economic growth. Kuznets (1973) emphasizes that economic and social structure changes are associated with rapid technological innovations, which are key drivers of modern economic growth (Kuznets_1973: p.6). Acemoglu et al. (2001) also underscore the significance of institutions in shaping economic progress, stating that certain institutions can either facilitate or hinder investment and technological advancements (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27).

Kuznets (1973) further explains that the non-conventional costs of economic growth, such as environmental degradation and social inequality, can arise from rapid technological advancements (Kuznets_1973: p.5, p.11). This suggests that institutions play a vital role in managing these negative consequences and ensuring sustainable economic growth.

Whatley (2018) illustrates this point by demonstrating how gunpowder imports and slave exports were influenced by institutional factors in the early modern period (Whatley_2018: p.11, p.13). The author's findings indicate that institutions can significantly impact technological advancements and economic growth patterns over time.

Van Zanden (2009) supports this argument by highlighting the role of human capital formation in determining long-term economic growth (vanZanden_2009: p.24). The author suggests that skill premiums, which can be influenced by institutions, have a direct impact on the relative price of capital goods and, consequently, economic growth (vanZanden_2009: p.27).

Institutions, as key drivers of long-term economic growth, are integral to the development and dissemination of useful knowledge (Mokyr_2005: p.4). The Enlightenment, a period marked by intellectual progress and the growth of economic reasonableness, played an essential role in this process (Mokyr_2005: p.54). This era saw the development of individualism, man-made formal law, corporatism, self governance, and rules that were determined through institutionalized processes (Mokyr_2005: p.56).

The growth of economic reasonableness was a significant factor in the Industrial Revolution, which is considered the most important monument in economic history (deVries_1994: p.2). The development of Europe's urban network connected cities in systems of commercial interaction, establishing a framework for regional economic development that facilitated industrial growth (deVries_1994: p.5).

Moreover, the fusion of mercantilist imperialism with technologically driven capitalism was a unique characteristic of early modern Europe that fostered an environment conducive to commercial exchange (Bryant_2006: p.5). This nascent European fusion provided more than adequate environments for the growth of commerce and industry, contributing significantly to long-term economic growth.

Institutional development and economic growth have been widely debated, with some scholars challenging the notion that institutions are the primary drivers of long-term economic growth. McCloskey (2015) critiques this argument by pointing out instances where ideas and ideologies, rather than institutions, significantly influenced economic growth.

For instance, in France during the two centuries following the Unfinished Revolution of 1789, reactionary parties prospered that were uninterested in economic growth if they could impose a rigid form of Catholicism (McCloskey_2015: p.9). Similarly, in Spain, economic growth was throttled until very recently for conservative reasons despite the examples of the Dutch and British (McCloskey_2015: p.9).

Moreover, McCloskey (2015) suggests that the Great Enrichment occurred independently of institutions, with a material base having an influence on economic growth without reducing mind to matter or indulging in a tough-guy affection for realism in international relations (McCloskey_2015: p.9).

Furthermore, Bryceson (2002) challenges the thesis by demonstrating that in some communities, women's economic and social spheres are influenced by a stronger adherence to traditional hierarchical order within the community, which restricts their involvement in non-agrarian activities (Bryceson_2002: p.9). In areas like Njombe, Tanzania, young households with restricted land access have become more dependent on non-agricultural economic activities or resorting to migrant labor due to diminished returns from cash cropping (Bryceson_2002: p.11).

These sources suggest that while institutions may play a role in economic growth, other factors such as ideologies and social structures also significantly influence economic development. This challenges the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

Institutional development may not be solely responsible for long-term economic growth, as technological advancements and structural changes in industries also play significant roles. The coal industry during the Industrial Revolution serves as a prime example of this interplay between technology and economic growth (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.4). The authors argue that technological improvements enabled the coal industry to meet increased demand, thus contributing to industrialization and economic development (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.4).

Moreover, Feinstein (1998) challenges the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by highlighting the importance of structural changes in farming and rural areas during the Industrial Revolution era. The author demonstrates that changes in farming practices, combined with migration and emigration, significantly impacted labor distribution and contributed to economic growth (Feinstein_1998: p.28).

Subramanian and Deaton's (1998) work on physical stature during the Industrial Revolution adds nuance to the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. While Feinstein (1998) demonstrates changes in farming practices and migration as significant contributors to economic growth, Subramanian and Deaton highlight a correlation between height and economic development during this period. They argue that taller individuals were more likely to be found in regions with greater economic opportunities, suggesting that structural change may also play a role in long-term growth.

Broadberry and Gardner (2022) complicate the thesis by focusing on Sub-Saharan Africa's absence from recent debates about long-run growth due to a lack of data before 1950. They suggest that understanding economic performance in this region is crucial for understanding convergence, as it has been markedly worse than other regions (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.1). This raises the question of whether institutions in Sub-Saharan Africa have hindered or promoted long-term growth.

Temin (2002) offers a perspective on economic growth over the past century, arguing that it had a large component of economic transition. He suggests that the exhaustion of cheap labor caused economic growth to slow (Temin_2002: p.4). This implies that changes in institutions related to labor may have significant impacts on long-term economic growth.

Sokoloff and Engerman (2000) argue that initial conditions, or factor endowments broadly conceived, could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth. They suggest that contrasting records in growth can be traced back to the achievement of sustained economic growth by the United States and Canada during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries (Sokoloff & Engerman_2000: p.12). This implies that institutions may have played a role in facilitating or hindering economic growth in these regions during this period.

Skocpol and Sommers (1980) use comparisons to reveal particularities of cases, increasing the visibility of one structure by contrasting it with another (Skocpol & Sommers_1980: p.8). This method could be applied to institutions in different regions during the Industrial Revolution era, allowing for a more nuanced understanding of how institutions contributed to long-term economic growth.

These sources complicate the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by adding evidence from various perspectives and regions. They suggest that structural change, factor endowments, and labor conditions may also play significant roles in long-term economic growth. Further investigation into these factors could provide a more comprehensive understanding of how institutions contribute to long-term economic growth during the Industrial Revolution era.

The role of institutions in fostering long-run economic growth is a complex and multifaceted issue, as evidenced by various scholarly works. Lindert & Williamson (1985) suggest that the British classical economists and government thought institutions played a significant role in both economic growth and inequality distribution (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1). Furthermore, they argue that understanding long-term distribution trends is crucial for explaining the causes of economic growth itself (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.9).

Frankema (2012) supports this notion by highlighting the impact of colonial education on long-run growth disparities, particularly in Africa. He posits that former British colonies experienced faster growth due to their educational advantage (Frankema_2012: p.2). However, Frankema also notes that the level of British "liberalism" was influenced by the local political and economic context (Frankema_2012: p.19), suggesting a more nuanced relationship between institutions and growth.

Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) further complicate this understanding by emphasizing that institutions are interconnected and influence each other's strategies (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.21). They argue that the focus on economic and social history at the expense of political history may oversimplify the role of institutions in long-term economic growth (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.7).

These sources add nuance to the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth during the Industrial Revolution era. They suggest that understanding the interplay between institutions, education, and local contexts could provide a more comprehensive perspective on this complex issue. Further exploration into these factors could potentially reveal intricate relationships that contribute to long-term economic growth.

The relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth is further nuanced when considering the factors of climate, nutrition, and economic base. Koepke & Baten (2005) argue that while the Roman Empire experienced economic growth, this did not translate into increased living standards as indicated by the lack of height increase among its population (p.16). This suggests that institutional development may be crucial for translating economic growth into improvements in living standards and human well-being.

Moreover, Hopkins (2019) contends that postmodernism and Western impositions on African studies have inhibited the understanding of long-run economic and political change in Africa (p.10). Consequently, the role of institutions in shaping African economic growth may have been underestimated due to limited diversity in independent African viewpoints.

Bolt & van Zanden (2014) highlight that revisions of nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth rates have been made for various countries, including Switzerland, Germany, Sweden, Italy, Greece, Russia, and Bulgaria (p.5). These revisions underscore the complexity of understanding long-run economic growth, as it is not solely dependent on institutional development but also on a multitude of factors such as climate, nutrition, and economic base.

The works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) offer valuable insights into the relationship between institutions, economic growth, and structural change, although their perspectives slightly diverge. Both scholars acknowledge that institutional development plays a crucial role in long-run economic growth. Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) emphasizes the importance of labor mobility and competition among employers, suggesting that these factors are hindered when labor is tied to land or owners. On the other hand, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) discusses how Western economic institutions, such as slave dealing and curing, significantly impacted African societies.

However, while both authors agree on the importance of institutions, they diverge in their focus. Domar's work primarily concentrates on the economic aspects of institutions, particularly labor mobility and competition, whereas Bohannan provides a more ethnographic perspective, discussing the Western impact on African societies beyond economic institutions.

Despite these contributions, several issues remain unresolved. For instance, Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) mentions that with labor tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, but he does not delve into how this situation can be overcome for sustainable economic growth. Similarly, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.13) points out the limitation of women in African societies, yet does not explore the implications of this gender imbalance on economic development.
