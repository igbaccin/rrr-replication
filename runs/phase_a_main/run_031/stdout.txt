LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question under investigation in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, a thesis that has garnered significant attention among economists and historians. The stakes are high, as understanding the drivers of economic growth could inform policy decisions aimed at promoting development and reducing poverty in various regions.

Supporting this view, Austin (2008) argues that institutions play a crucial role in long-term economic growth, citing historical evidence from Africa that reveals recurring relationships due to path dependency (Austin_2008: p.1). Similarly, Kuznets (1973) posits that both technological innovations and social innovations are major factors affecting economic and social structure, which in turn contribute to long-run growth (Kuznets_1973: p.6). North & Weingast (1989) further support this stance by emphasizing the role of representative government institutions in enabling economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

On the other hand, some scholars offer critiques or complications to this thesis. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has resulted in Sub-Saharan Africa being absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also complicate the issue by highlighting how understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution becomes subsumed under household production is crucial for policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3).

These conflicting viewpoints underscore the need for further research and debate on the role of institutions in long-run economic growth. The stakes are high, as policymakers must understand the complexities involved in promoting development and reducing poverty effectively. Future studies should aim to address the limitations identified by scholars such as Broadberry & Gardner (2022) and Akyeampong & Fofack (2914), providing a more nuanced understanding of the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth.

The relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth is further substantiated by various scholars, including Douglass North (1989), Robert C. Allen (Ogilvie_2007), Kenneth Pomeranz (Pamuk_2007), James A. Robinson (Nunn_2008), and Nunn & Wantchekon (2011).

In North's (1989) work, he argues that the evolution of institutions significantly influences economic growth by reducing transaction costs. He emphasizes that the development of centralized enforcement mechanisms, such as those in Spain, can foster long-term economic growth (North_1989: p.1).

Ogilvie (2007) supports this view by highlighting the interdependency of individual institutions within a broader framework, which is crucial for economic growth. He acknowledges that the efficiency theory's Achilles heel lies in its assumption that most people want the economy to grow as much as possible (Ogilvie_2007: p.14).

Pamuk (2007) contributes to this discussion by demonstrating how changes in institutions, such as the growing commercialization of agriculture, played a significant role in modern economic growth following the Black Death. These changes paved the way for increased economic efficiency and long-term growth (Pamuk_2007: p.4).

Nunn (2008) supports this perspective by investigating the relationship between ethnic fractionalization and long-term economic growth. He finds that in uncertain and insecure environments, individuals required well-defined property rights and institutions to ensure economic development (Nunn_2008: p.4).

Lastly, Nunn & Wantchekon (2011) provide evidence that both internal and external institutional factors contribute to long-run economic growth, with the internal factor being at least twice as significant (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.4).

Institutions play a crucial role in shaping long-term economic growth, as suggested by various scholars. Austin (2008) supports this notion, arguing that institutions are indeed "the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth" (Austin_2008: p.26). He further endorses the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history to reveal recurring relationships and path dependencies (Austin_2008: p.1).

Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2001) found that more fundamental institutions have a significant impact on property and economic progress, with detrimental institutions hindering investment (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27, p.3). In their subsequent work, Acemoglu et al. (2002) highlight the importance of institutions in understanding economic and political development over the past 500 years (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49). They also note a change in the distribution of economic prosperity that appears to be related to institutional changes (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.6).

Supporting this perspective, Abramovitz (1986) suggests that the elaboration of the mixed economy is a function of economic growth itself (Abramovitz_1986: p.20). Allen (2001), in his study on wages and prices, also finds indications supporting the connection between institutions and long-term economic development (Allen_2001: p.1, p.4, p.15, p.23).

The economic structures and organizations of a society significantly contribute to long-term economic growth, as suggested by various scholars. Kuznets (1973) argues that rapid changes in economic structure are associated with social innovations, which provide the necessary adjustments for modern economic growth (Kuznets_1973: p.4). Furthermore, he emphasizes that these structural changes can lead to both positive and negative results within developed and less developed countries (Kuznets_1973: p.11).

North & Weingast (1989) focus on the political factors underlying economic growth and market development, highlighting the role of institutions in enforcing rules and facilitating changes (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). They demonstrate that representative government, with multiple veto points and a primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, plays a crucial role in promoting economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

Mokyr (2005) supports this perspective by suggesting that the change in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West can be attributed to developments in the intellectual realm concerning "useful knowledge" (Mokyr_2005: p.4). He emphasizes that elements such as individualism, formal law, corporatism, self-governance, and institutionalized processes were crucial for the development of a progressive society (Mokyr_2005: p.56).

Structural change in the economy, particularly in agriculture and labor, is posited as a critical factor for long-run economic growth (Mokyr_2005: p.56). However, some scholars challenge this perspective, arguing that structural changes may not have been as significant during the Industrial Revolution as previously thought.

Clark & Jacks (2007) critique the cliometric account of the coal industry's role in the Industrial Revolution, suggesting that technological advancements enabled the coal industry to respond effectively to increased demand (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.4). This implies that the structural change in the coal industry might not have been the primary driver of economic growth, as Mokyr suggests.

Similarly, Feinstein (1998) questions the impact of structural changes in farming and labor during the Industrial Revolution. He notes that significant changes in farming practices and labor dispersion occurred only from the 1850s onwards (Feinstein_1998: p.28). This suggests a delayed response to the structural changes Mokyr identifies as essential for long-run economic growth.

Moreover, Clark & Jacks argue that prices were deﬂated by an economy-wide price index, potentially underestimating the actual growth in the coal industry during the Industrial Revolution (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.3). This could imply that the structural change in the coal industry was more substantial than previously thought, but not necessarily a primary cause of long-run economic growth.

The work of Simson (2019) challenges the notion that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth, particularly in East Africa during the periods of industrial development and educational expansion. According to Simson (Simson_2019: p.27), the emphasis on these areas did not directly lead to increased efficiency or sustainable investment levels.

Simson (Simson_2019: p.4) further argues that while there may have been spending inefficiencies, eliminating them would not have necessarily sustained the investment levels of the 1960s, let alone re-orientate these economies. The author also highlights that state expenditure and deficits expanded during a period of high economic growth in the 1960s and 1970s (Simson_2019: p.25), suggesting that factors other than institutions might have played a more significant role in driving economic growth.

However, it remains unclear whether Simson's critique extends to long-term economic growth or is limited to specific periods and regions. Further examination of the role of institutions in different historical and geographical contexts could provide additional insights into this complex relationship.

The role of institutions in fostering long-term economic growth is a complex issue that extends beyond specific periods and regions, as previously suggested. A more nuanced understanding can be gained by examining various historical and geographical contexts, which may reveal intricate relationships between institutions and economic development.

For instance, the contribution of African women to economic growth and development has been historically underestimated due to their labor being subsumed under household production (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). Moreover, women's economic roles were often dependent on the economic provision of their husbands (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.9), which highlights the importance of considering gender dynamics in the study of institutions and growth.

In a different context, the railway system had a significant advantage over lorries in terms of cost during the mid-1920s (Austin_2007: p.8). However, few of the capital-deepening and TFP-improving forces of modern economic growth were at work until much later (Austin_2007: p.16), suggesting that institutions play a crucial role in facilitating such transformations.

The Maddison Project revisions of nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth have been added for several countries, but these do not fundamentally change our understanding of the role of institutions in economic development (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5). This underscores the need to examine multiple cases and periods to gain a comprehensive understanding of this relationship.

Lastly, the income disparities of the early twentieth century were due to long-run stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe (AllenEtAl_2011: p.1). This example emphasizes that institutions can significantly impact a country's economic growth trajectory, as seen in the contrast between China and Japan during this period.

The argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth becomes more nuanced when considering cultural and political factors, as evidenced by various scholarly works.

Temin (2002) complicates this perspective by emphasizing the role of labor exhaustion in slowing economic growth. He suggests that the end of the Golden Age was brought about by a return to equilibrium growth paths rather than adverse economic shocks in the 1970s (Temin_2002: p.14). Furthermore, Temin argues that recent theories of economic growth must consider the context of economic transition, which had a significant impact on national economies around 1800 (Temin_2002: p.7).

Sokoloff and Engerman (2000) also contribute to this discourse by exploring the possibility that initial conditions or factor endowments could have profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth. They highlight that the divergence in paths can be traced back to the achievement of sustained economic growth by the United States and Canada during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, while others did not manage to attain this goal until late in the nineteenth century (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.12).

Skocpol and Sommers (1980) offer comparative analyses of Japan and China, revealing particularities that shed light on the differences between these two countries. They argue that an increase in the "visibility" of one structure can be achieved by contrasting it with another (Skocpol&Sommers_1980: p.8).

Stoler (1989) adds to this discussion by focusing on the role of colonizers living in "imagined communities" that were consciously created to overcome economic and social disparities (Stoler_1989: p.5). This work suggests that cultural factors played a significant role in shaping the economic trajectories of colonized regions.

The economic structures and organization also play a significant role in long-run economic growth, as suggested by various studies. Komlos (1998) emphasizes that modern economic growth is characterized by specific features such as rapid population growth, urbanization, and income inequality (Komlos_1998: p.2, p.7). Furthermore, he points out that the Industrial Revolution was associated with physical stature changes, suggesting a connection between economic processes and biological growth (Komlos_1998: p.16, p.17).

Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that the relationship between long-run distribution trends and economic growth is intertwined, as policymakers have often sought to balance economic growth with equality (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1, p.8). They suggest that understanding this causal direction can provide valuable insights into historical patterns of inequality and growth (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.8).

Koepke & Baten (2005) explore the relationship between climate and human living standards, finding that during the Roman Empire, heights did not increase despite common beliefs suggesting economic growth and improved purchasing power (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.16, p.20). These findings complicate the notion that economic growth is always associated with improvements in nutrition and health.

These sources underscore the multifaceted nature of long-run economic growth. While cultural factors, as discussed earlier, contribute significantly to shaping economic trajectories, economic structures and organization also play a crucial role. The relationship between economic growth and variables such as population growth, urbanization, income inequality, climate, and nutrition requires further exploration to fully understand the complexities of long-run economic development.

The relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth is a topic of significant interest among scholars, as evidenced by the works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959). Both authors acknowledge the crucial role of institutions in economic development, but their perspectives diverge in certain aspects.

Domar (1970) emphasizes the impact of institutional arrangements on labor markets, arguing that when labor is tied to land or an owner, competition among employers ceases, hindering growth (p.4). He also highlights the role of institutions in determining the distribution of resources and factors of production, stating that with labor fixed, structural change cannot occur (p.4).

On the other hand, Bohannan (1959) focuses on the Western impact on African societies, particularly the introduction of economic institutions such as slave dealing and curing (p.7). He notes that these new institutions altered social structures, leading to changes in population dynamics and gender roles (p.13). However, he also acknowledges the limitations imposed by traditional African institutions, such as the restriction on travel beyond one's kin or friends' areas (p.10).

Despite their differences, both authors converge in recognizing the fundamental role of institutions in economic growth. They suggest that institutional arrangements can either hinder or facilitate structural change and resource allocation, thus influencing long-term economic development.

However, several issues remain unresolved. For instance, while Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) provide insightful perspectives on the role of institutions in economic growth, further research is needed to understand how specific institutional arrangements impact growth across various contexts. Additionally, the relationship between institutions, population growth, urbanization, income inequality, climate, and nutrition requires further exploration to fully understand the complexities of long-run economic development.
