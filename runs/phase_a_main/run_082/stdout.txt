LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question under investigation in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, a thesis first proposed by North and Thomas (1973). This question holds significant implications for understanding the economic development of various regions, particularly Africa, which has historically lagged behind other continents.

Supporting the thesis, Austin (2008) argues that institutions are essential in explaining long-term growth patterns, emphasizing their role in shaping economic structures and reinforcing economic positions between different regions. Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) demonstrate how representative government institutions play a crucial role in promoting economic growth and political freedom. Acemoglu et al. (2001) also support this view by highlighting the detrimental effects of certain institutional structures on investment and economic progress.

However, not all scholars agree that institutions are the sole cause of long-run economic growth. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate the argument by pointing out the lack of data on African economic performance before 1950, which has limited the region's inclusion in recent debates about comparative long-run growth. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance to the discussion by exploring how women's economic contributions have been historically subsumed under household production, undermining policies aimed at strengthening their economic roles.

As this brief review suggests, the debate over the role of institutions in long-run economic growth is complex and multifaceted. The stakes are high, as understanding the underlying causes of economic development can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting growth and reducing poverty in various regions. Further research is needed to clarify the relationship between institutions and economic growth, particularly in understudied regions like Africa.

The relationship between institutions and economic growth is a key area of interest in understanding long-term development, as highlighted by various researchers (North&Weingast_1989, Mokyr_2005, North_1989, Ogilvie_2007, AcemogluEtAl_2002). The works of North & Weingast (1989) and Mokyr (2005), in particular, provide valuable insights into this connection.

North & Weingast (1989) argue that the institutions of representative government—with multiple veto points and a primary role for common law courts in economic affairs—are crucial to both economic growth and political freedom. They demonstrate how these institutions emerged from historical struggles, enabling economic development by reducing the risks associated with property ownership and investment.

Mokyr (2005) emphasizes the importance of intellectual developments during the Enlightenment as a catalyst for economic progress in Western societies. He suggests that changes in "useful knowledge" contributed significantly to the growth of economic reasonableness, leading to increased prosperity over time.

These findings support the notion that institutions play a fundamental role in long-run economic growth. The works of North & Weingast (1989) and Mokyr (2005) provide evidence for the importance of political and intellectual institutions in fostering economic development, offering valuable insights into policy decisions aimed at promoting growth and reducing poverty in various regions. Further research could focus on investigating the specific institutional factors that have contributed to economic success in different historical contexts and geographical locations.

The assertion that institutions are the primary driver of long-run economic growth is further substantiated by various scholarly works. According to Austin (2008), the theoretical conclusion that institutions are 'the' fundamental cause of long-term economic growth holds logical appeal due to its simplicity and consistency with historical evidence (Austin_2008: p.24). This perspective is supported by deVries (1994) who argues that Europe's urban network, connecting cities in systems of commercial interaction, was a significant achievement of the early modern period, establishing a framework for regional economic development (deVries_1994: p.5).

Moreover, Frankema & van Waijenburg (2012) highlight the role of West African commercial centers in attracting large flows of economic migrants, suggesting that these regions were able to foster growth due to their institutional structures (Frankema&vanWaijenburg_2012: p.15). In the case of Accra and the Gold Coast, population growth rates exceeded 3% per annum, leading to real wage rises (Frankema&vanWaijenburg_2012: p.17).

These studies collectively suggest that institutions play a crucial role in shaping economic growth by facilitating trade, promoting urban development, and attracting migration. Further investigation could delve into the specific institutional factors that have contributed to economic success in different historical contexts and geographical locations, providing insights into effective strategies for promoting growth and reducing poverty.

The impact of institutions on long-run economic growth can be observed in various sectors, particularly agriculture. According to Acemoglu and colleagues (2001), certain institutional factors have significantly influenced the economic success of different historical contexts. For instance, they argue that more fundamental institutions, such as property rights and political power, played a crucial role in shaping agricultural practices and fostering economic growth. Specifically, detrimental institutions were found to be harmful to investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27).

In the European context, Pamuk (2007) provides evidence that changes in institutional structures during the late Middle Ages contributed to the commercialization of agriculture. The demographic shifts following the Black Death led to increased real wages and incomes, creating a conducive environment for agricultural growth (Pamuk_2007: p.4).

Similarly, in India, the long-run economic performance was shaped by institutional factors. Broadberry and colleagues (2015) note that the controversy surrounding India's economic history stems from discrepancies in population estimates and economic data, which are heavily influenced by institutional structures. For example, the size of armies can serve as an indicator for population estimates (BroadberryEtal_2015: p.4).

These findings suggest that effective institutions can lead to structural changes that correlate with long-term economic growth, particularly in agriculture. Further examination of these institutional factors and their impact on agricultural development across different geographical locations could provide valuable insights into strategies for promoting growth and reducing poverty.

The works of Simson (2019), Stern (2004), and McCloskey (2015) challenge the notion that institutions are the primary drivers of long-term economic growth by focusing on alternative factors such as industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy.

Simson's (2019) research highlights the importance given to these areas in various regions during periods of high economic growth. For instance, Kenyan, Tanzanian, and Ugandan state expenditure and deficits expanded significantly in the 1960s and 1970s, contributing to economic growth (Simson_2019: p.25). Furthermore, Simson questions whether the elimination of spending inefficiencies, leakages, and costly delays would have been enough to sustain investment levels during these periods.

Stern (2004) argues that economic growth may not always lead to environmental improvement, contradicting the idea that institutional development fosters sustainable long-term growth. Stern claims that while economic growth usually leads to environmental degradation in the early stages, it might eventually result in improved environmental conditions (Stern_2004: p.3).

McCloskey (2015) suggests that ideologies and ideologies, not institutions, have played a significant role in shaping economic growth throughout history. For example, in France after the Unfinished Revolution of 1789, reactionary parties thwarted economic growth due to their conservative religious views (McCloskey_2015: p.9).

These sources suggest that while institutions may play a role in economic development, other factors such as industrial development, educational expansion, military autonomy, and ideologies should also be considered when examining the long-term causes of economic growth. Further examination of these factors across different geographical locations could provide valuable insights into the complex interplay between various drivers of economic growth.

The assertion that institutions are the primary cause of long-term economic growth gains nuance when considering the perspectives presented in various scholarly works. For instance, Komlos (1998) emphasizes the role of physical stature during the Industrial Revolution as an indicator of economic processes and associated structural changes (Komlos_1998: p.793). This suggests that while institutions may not be the sole determinant, they could influence other factors such as population growth, urbanization, and income inequality, which in turn contribute to economic growth.

Temin (2002) complicates this argument by arguing that economic growth can slow due to the exhaustion of cheap labor, implying that institutions play a role in shaping the availability and cost of labor, thus impacting economic growth (Temin_2002: p.4). Furthermore, Temin suggests that the end of the Golden Age was brought about by a return to equilibrium growth paths, not adverse economic shocks (Temin_2002: p.14). This implies that institutions may help maintain or disrupt long-term economic growth trajectories.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) also contribute to the discussion by suggesting a link between long-run distribution trends and explanations of growth itself, implying that institutions play a role in shaping income distribution, which can influence economic growth (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.9).

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) argue that initial conditions or factor endowments could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth, suggesting that institutions are part of these initial conditions (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4). They further suggest that contrasting records in growth can be traced back to the achievement of sustained economic growth by some countries while others did not, which could be attributed to differences in institutions (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.12).

Bolt & van Zanden (2014) discuss revisions of nineteenth- and early twentieth-century growth that have been added to various national datasets, implying a need for comprehensive institutional analysis across different geographical locations to fully understand long-term economic growth (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5).

Goldin (2006) argues that the study of labor was once mired in the institutions of the labor market, suggesting that institutions play a significant role in shaping labor markets and thus economic growth (Goldin_2006: p.3).

Institutions and Education as Factors Shaping Economic Growth

Goldin's (2006) assertion that institutions play a significant role in shaping labor markets and thus economic growth is further supported by the works of Koepke & Baten (2005), Frankema (2012), and Hopkins (2019). These scholars provide nuanced perspectives on how institutions, particularly those related to education, have influenced long-run economic development.

Koepke & Baten (2005) delve into the relationship between climate and human living standards, suggesting that even under favorable climatic conditions, such as during the Roman Empire, there was no significant increase in stature or nutrition levels, indicating stagnant living standards (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.16). The authors argue that this could be due to institutional factors hindering economic growth and development, despite favorable environmental conditions.

Frankema (2012) focuses on the role of colonial education in shaping long-run growth disparities in Africa. The scholar argues that British colonies experienced faster growth due to their educational lead, which was a result of the degree of British "liberalism" being endogenous to the local political and economic context (Frankema_2012: p.19). However, it is essential to note that the British controlled most of the fertile areas, which could have influenced this educational advantage.

Hopkins (2019) discusses how the study of long-run economic and political change in Africa was hindered by 'totalism' and 'essentialism,' which were considered Western impositions on the rest of the world (Hopkins_2019: p.10). As a result, independent African viewpoints may have been limited, potentially impacting our understanding of the role of institutions in shaping economic growth.

The role of institutions in shaping economic growth is further nuanced when considering their impact on labor markets, as evidenced by Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) and Williamson (1996).

Akyeampong & Fofack (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3) highlight the importance of understanding the processes that subsume women's economic contributions under household production, which can undermine policies aimed at strengthening their economic roles. The authors also emphasize that the contribution of African women to economic growth and development was largely dependent on the economic provision of the husband (Akyeampong & Fofack, 2014: p.9). These findings suggest that institutions play a significant role in determining the economic agency of women, which can have far-reaching implications for long-term economic growth.

On the other hand, Williamson (1996) explores the relationship between schooling and Swedish economic growth before World War I (Williamson_1996: p.20). By examining the sources of convergence, he underlines the importance of understanding who gains and loses from convergence, thus shedding light on policy responses (Williamson_1996: p.25). Williamson also notes that mass migrations could reduce the pace of real wage growth in receiving countries by glutting labor markets abroad (Williamson_1996: p.17). These insights underscore the complex ways in which institutions can shape labor markets and, consequently, economic growth.

Institutions play a crucial role in shaping long-run economic growth, as evidenced by the works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959). Both scholars acknowledge the significant impact of institutions on labor markets and economic development.

Domar (1970) argues that when labor is tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, potentially hindering growth (p.4). He also emphasizes the importance of structural change in promoting economic advancement, suggesting a correlation between the two (p.15, p.16). On the other hand, Bohannan (1959) focuses on the Western impact on economic institutions, particularly in Africa, where he observes that the British arrival led to changes in trade patterns and status acquisition (p.7, p.10).

Both scholars converge in their recognition of the institutional factors influencing labor markets and growth. However, they diverge in their focus: Domar primarily discusses labor-land ties, while Bohannan explores the influence of Western impact on African economic institutions.

Despite these insights, several aspects remain unresolved. For instance, Domar's (1970) assertion that structural change correlates with growth requires further investigation to establish causality and identify specific mechanisms driving this relationship. Similarly, Bohannan (1959) could have delved deeper into the long-term effects of Western impact on African economies.
