LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The debate on the primary cause of long-term economic growth has garnered significant attention, with institutions emerging as a key contender. This discussion revolves around the assertion that institutions are fundamental to long-run economic growth (Austin_2008: p.26).

The central question at hand is whether institutions play a decisive role in shaping economic growth over extended periods, and if so, what implications does this have for underdeveloped regions such as Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA)?

Supporting this thesis, Austin (2008) argues that institutions are crucial to understanding long-term economic growth. He contends that these institutions not only reinforce but also originate the economic position of regions like Africa compared to Western Europe (and parts of Asia) (Austin_2008: p.24). Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) demonstrate how representative government institutions, specifically multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are intimately related to economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

On the other hand, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate this argument by highlighting the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 in SSA, which has led to its absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance by discussing the underestimation of women's economic contribution and how this undermines policies aimed at strengthening their roles in economic growth (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3).

As the debate unfolds, it becomes clear that understanding the role of institutions in long-term economic growth is crucial, particularly for regions like SSA where growth has been markedly worse than other regions (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). The stakes are high, as identifying the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth could lead to more effective policies aimed at promoting development and reducing economic disparities between regions. This exploration of institutions as the primary driver of long-term economic growth is a significant contribution to the ongoing discourse on economic development and growth.

The literature provides compelling evidence supporting the notion that institutions are fundamental to long-run economic growth. In this context, institutions refer to the rules and norms that govern economic activities, including labor markets.

Jonathan Mokyr (2005) posits that the intellectual realm, particularly the growth of economic reasonableness, played a significant role in the West's economic growth (Mokyr_2005: p.54). He suggests that changes in the intellectual realm concerning "useful knowledge" were instrumental in explaining why the West is so much richer today (Mokyr_2005: p.4).

Douglass C. North (1989) emphasizes the role of institutions in reducing transaction costs, which in turn contributes to economic growth (North_1989: p.9). He also highlights the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework, underscoring their collective impact on economic development (North_1989: p.27).

Similarly, Jan de Vries (1994) argues that Europe's urban network, connecting its many cities in systems of commercial interaction, established a framework for regional economic development, which was crucial for industrial growth (deVries_1994: p.5).

These findings suggest that the institutional mechanisms affecting labor markets significantly contribute to long-term economic growth by facilitating trade, promoting innovation, and reducing transaction costs. The literature further supports this notion by demonstrating the interdependency of institutions within a wider framework, indicating their collective impact on economic development.

The significance of institutions in fostering long-run economic growth is further substantiated by the works of North & Weingast (1989) and Acemoglu et al. (2001, 2002). North & Weingast (1989) emphasize the role of representative government with multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts in enforcing economic rules as crucial factors for economic growth and political freedom (p.28). These institutions, according to them, facilitate the development of markets by underpinning economic exchange and regulating changes to these rules.

Acemoglu et al. (2001) also support this notion by demonstrating the impact of fundamental institutions on property rights and economic performance. Their research indicates that institutions play a significant role in determining the level of economic prosperity, with detrimental institutions hindering investment and economic progress (p.27). Furthermore, Acemoglu et al. (2002) highlight how the distribution of economic prosperity has changed over time, suggesting that this shift may be related to alterations in institutions (p.5).

In addition, Abramovitz (1986) and Hopkins (2009) provide evidence supporting the interdependency of institutions within a broader framework. Abramovitz (1986) argues that the elaboration of mixed economies is a function of economic growth itself, while Hopkins (2009) contends that ethnic fragmentation is not necessarily responsible for unproductive institutions when other factors are taken into account (p.22).

Lastly, Frankema & van Waijenburg (2012) demonstrate how the economic development of West African commercial centers was influenced by migration, population growth rates, and land-labor ratios (p.15, p.17). These factors, in turn, contributed to real wage rises and economic expansion within these cities.

The role of institutions as a primary driver of long-term economic growth is further substantiated by the works of Austin (2008) and Bryant (2006). Austin (Austin_2008: p.26) posits that institutions are indeed 'the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth', echoing the argument presented earlier. He further reinforces this claim by stating that African trades, including the Atlantic trade, reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe (Austin_2008: p.24).

Similarly, Bryant (Bryant_2006: p.5) supports this thesis by highlighting the fusion of mercantilist imperialism with capitalistic transformation as a fundamental axis of difference between Africa and Europe. This fusion, he suggests, played a significant role in shaping the economic trajectories of these continents.

Moreover, both authors emphasize the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history (Austin_2008: p.1) and acknowledging the radicalness of revisionist dissent (Bryant_2006: p.3). These assertions underscore the need to delve deeper into the historical context to understand the role of institutions in economic development.

Challenging the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth, Simson (2019) presents evidence from East Africa that suggests other factors may have played a significant role in economic development during the 1960s and 1970s. The author highlights an emphasis on industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy, as well as increased public employment, which could imply that these elements might have contributed to the region's economic growth (Simson_2019: p.27).

Furthermore, van Zanden (1999) critiques the consensus view in the 1960s and 1970s that early modern economies were unable to generate economic growth. Instead, the author argues that a negative link between output measures of standard living and economic development was still prevalent in the mid-eighteenth century (vanZanden_1999: p.1). This finding raises questions about the role of institutions during this period and their impact on long-term economic growth.

These sources challenge the thesis by suggesting that factors other than institutions, such as industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy, might have played a significant role in economic growth during specific periods. Additionally, van Zanden's critique of the consensus view in the 1960s and 1970s raises questions about the role of institutions during early modern economies and their impact on long-term economic growth. Further examination of these factors and their interplay with institutional development could offer valuable insights into the relationship between institutions and economic growth.

The literature suggests that institutional mechanisms, particularly within labor markets, play a significant role in shaping long-term economic growth. Komlos (1998) highlights the impact of structural changes on physical stature during the Industrial Revolution, implying a correlation between these transformations and economic growth. Furthermore, he emphasizes the influence of economic processes and associated structural changes on height cycles, which bear resemblance to classical business cycles (Komlos_1998: p.16).

Temin (2002) posits that the exhaustion of cheap labor was a key factor causing economic growth to slow down. This perspective complicates the argument by suggesting that institutions, such as those governing labor markets, might have played a role in determining the availability and cost of labor, thereby impacting long-term economic growth (Temin_2002: p.4).

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) argue that initial conditions or factor endowments could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth. They point out the contrasting records in growth between the United States and Canada, compared to other countries, during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.12). This suggests that institutions, particularly those related to factor endowments, might have played a role in shaping economic growth trajectories.

Williamson (1996) discusses the relationship between schooling and Swedish economic growth before World War I. His work underscores the importance of understanding the sources of convergence for understanding policy responses, thus implying that institutions play a crucial role in this process (Williamson_1996: p.25).

Skocpol & Sommers (1980) compare Japan and China to understand the impact of Western intrusions on these societies. Their work suggests that differences in institutional responses to Western influence might have contributed to divergent economic growth trajectories (Skocpol&Sommers_1980: p.14).

These sources complicate the argument by emphasizing the role of institutional mechanisms within labor markets and factor endowments in shaping long-term economic growth. Further examination of these factors and their interplay with institutional development could offer valuable insights into the relationship between institutions and economic growth.

The examination of long-run economic growth, as influenced by institutions, markets, and factor endowments, is further nuanced when considering regional disparities. For instance, Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA) has been historically underrepresented in debates about economic growth due to the lack of data before 1950 (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). This suggests that a more comprehensive understanding of SSA's long-term economic growth may require additional historical data and research.

Moreover, the relationship between institutions and economic growth is not solely determined by the presence or absence of specific institutional arrangements. Instead, it is influenced by various factors such as colonialism and its aftermath, which have had profound effects on African economies (Hopkins_2019: p.10). For example, the results of a detailed enquiry concluded that food exports made very little contribution to economic growth in West Africa during the period of the slave trade (Hopkins_2019: p.13). This finding complicates the notion that institutions alone are responsible for long-run economic growth and highlights the need for more nuanced analyses that account for historical contexts.

Additionally, the relationship between economic growth and equality has been a topic of interest among policymakers and economists (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1). However, understanding this relationship is complex due to the trade-offs involved and the immense sample size required for empirical research (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1). This complexity underscores the importance of careful consideration when discussing the role of institutions in long-run economic growth.

Furthermore, the disagreement over the fundamental causes of long-run economic growth is not limited to contemporary debates but extends to historical perspectives as well (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.30). This disagreement emphasizes the need for a more comprehensive understanding that accounts for various models and perspectives, including those found in Marx's writings (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.21).

The influence of institutions on long-run economic growth is further elucidated by examining their impact on education, as highlighted in Frankema (2012) and Goldin (2006).

Frankema (2012) posits that colonial education played a pivotal role in explaining long-term growth disparities in Africa. The author argues that the former British colonies experienced faster growth due to their educational advantage, a result of the British control over fertile regions (Frankema_2012: p.2). However, it is essential to note that the degree of British "liberalism" was not uniform across all colonies and was influenced by local political and economic contexts (Frankema_2012: p.19). Furthermore, post-colonial levels of capital investment and growth rates also contributed significantly to long-term economic growth (Frankema_2012: p.2).

Goldin (2006) emphasizes the importance of understanding the evolution and revolution in institutions that govern labor markets. The author argues that the distinction between evolution and revolution pertains to three aspects of women's choices and decisions (Goldin_2006: p.1). By examining these distinctions, it becomes clear that institutional mechanisms affecting education can have profound effects on long-run economic growth, particularly when considering the role of women in the labor force.

These qualifications reshape the argument by demonstrating that the impact of institutions on long-run economic growth is complex and multifaceted. The influence of colonial education, local political and economic contexts, post-colonial investment levels, and gender roles in the labor market all play crucial roles in shaping long-term growth patterns. Understanding these intricacies is essential for a more comprehensive understanding of the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth.

Scholars Domar (1970), Bohannan (1959), and Bryceson (2002) offer valuable insights into the relationship between institutions, labor markets, and long-term economic growth. All three authors acknowledge that institutions play a significant role in shaping economic growth patterns (Domar_1970: p.4, Bryceson_2002: p.9).

Domar (1970) emphasizes the importance of labor being tied to land or owners, suggesting that this restricts competition among employers and may hinder growth (Domar_1970: p.4). On the other hand, Bohannan (1959) discusses how Western economic institutions impacted traditional societies, but also highlights factors beyond economics that were affected (Bohannan_1959: p.10). Bryceson (2002) explores the impact of livelihood diversification on economic growth, particularly focusing on the roles of men and women in these changes (Bryceson_2002: p.9).

While these scholars agree on the importance of institutions, they diverge in their focus and perspectives. Domar's work is primarily focused on labor markets and competition, while Bohannan considers a broader range of Western impacts, and Bryceson focuses on livelihood diversification and gender roles.

Despite these insights, several questions remain unresolved. For instance, further research could investigate how different types of institutions impact long-term economic growth in various contexts. Additionally, understanding the interplay between multiple factors—such as labor markets, gender roles, and Western impacts—in shaping economic growth patterns remains a complex and ongoing challenge for scholars.
