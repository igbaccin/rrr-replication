LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question in the literature review on institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is whether institutional factors are indeed the primary determinants of economic development over extended periods. This question carries significant implications for understanding historical and contemporary economic disparities between regions, informing policy decisions aimed at promoting sustainable growth, and shaping theories about the nature of economic progress.

Several scholars support the notion that institutions play a crucial role in fostering long-term economic growth. Kuznets (1973) argues that technological innovations and social adaptations associated with modern economic growth are major factors affecting economic and social structure, while North & Weingast (1989) demonstrate how representative government institutions can enable economic growth and political freedom. Acemoglu et al. (2001) provide empirical evidence showing a substantial impact of institutions on economic performance.

However, other scholars complicate this perspective by emphasizing the complex interplay between institutions, historical contexts, and cultural factors that influence economic development. Austin (2008), for example, endorses the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history to reveal recurring relationships due to similar situations or path dependencies. Similarly, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) argue that understanding processes by which women's economic contributions are subsumed under household production is essential for policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles.

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) question the applicability of recent debates about comparative long-run growth to Sub-Saharan Africa due to a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950, suggesting that historical accounting may provide insights into patterns of growth and development over centuries.

The stakes are high in this debate, as understanding the role of institutions in long-term economic growth can help policymakers design effective strategies for promoting sustainable development and reducing disparities between regions. The literature suggests a complex interplay between institutions, historical contexts, cultural factors, and technological innovations in shaping economic growth over extended periods. Future research should aim to disentangle these factors and clarify their relative importance in fostering long-term economic development.

Institutional development plays a crucial role in long-term economic growth, as evidenced by various scholarly works. Kuznets (1973) highlights the correlation between rapid changes in economic structure and society during modern economic growth (p.4). He also emphasizes that technological innovations are major factors affecting economic and social structure (p.6). Acemoglu et al. (2001) support this notion, stating that more fundamental institutions significantly impact property rights and economic performance (p.19, p.27).

Acemoglu et al. (2002) further argue that the distribution of economic prosperity has undergone a significant change, which is possibly related to institutional factors (p.5, p.6). Abramovitz (1986) suggests that the elaboration of mixed economies is a function of economic growth itself, implying that institutions play a role in fostering long-term economic development (p.20).

Hopkins (2009) acknowledges the argument that ethnic fragmentation leads to unproductive institutions but calls for more convincing evidence to discount other causes of solidarity and motives for action (p.22). Frankema & van Waijenburg (2012) demonstrate that high land-labor ratios and economic migration were significant factors contributing to real wage rises in West African commercial centers (p.15, p.17).

Institutions, as argued by North & Weingast (1989), play a crucial role in fostering economic growth and political freedom. They emphasize that the fundamental institutions of representative government, which include multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are intimately related to economic growth and the development of markets (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). This notion is further supported by Mokyr (2005), who suggests that changes in intellectual realms concerning "useful knowledge" were significant factors contributing to the growth of economic reasonableness in the West, leading to its prosperity today (Mokyr_2005: p.4).

North (1989) focuses on the relationship between institutions and economic growth in Spain, demonstrating that centralized enforcement mechanisms and increased security rights were essential for the development of markets and long-term economic growth (North_1989: p.9). Similarly, Pamuk (2007) highlights the impact of the Black Death on real wages and incomes during the last millennium, noting that it led to significant changes in commercialization of agriculture (Pamuk_2007: p.4).

Nunn (2008) argues that ethnic fractionalization has a negative impact on long-term economic growth, as individuals require more security during uncertain times and thus invest less in human capital (Nunn_2008: p.4). Lastly, Nunn & Wantchekon (2011) examine the effects of both internal and external factors on institutional development in Africa, finding that the internal channel is at least twice as large as the external channel (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.4).

The significance of institutions in fostering long-run economic growth is further emphasized by deVries (1994) and vanZanden (2009). DeVries (deVries_1994: p.2) underscores the importance of the Industrial Revolution, a quintessential example of institutional change leading to economic growth. He suggests that the urban network connecting cities in systems of commercial interaction played a crucial role in regional economic development and facilitated industrial growth.

Similarly, vanZanden (vanZanden_2009: p.1) argues that relative prices are the DNA of an economy, implying that understanding the skill premium – a measure of institutional quality – can provide insights into long-term economic growth. He further posits that human capital formation, influenced by the skill premium, is one of the determinants of long-term economic growth (vanZanden_2009: p.24). This notion aligns with new growth theory, which suggests that institutional quality significantly impacts long-run economic growth.

In addition, deVries (deVries_1994: p.8) highlights the importance of the household or family unit in understanding economic development. He argues that a renewed focus on the economic history of the household could provide valuable insights into the role of institutions in promoting long-term growth. This perspective underscores the idea that the internal dynamics of households and families can significantly influence institutional development, which in turn impacts economic growth.

Collectively, these studies suggest that institutions play a fundamental role in fostering long-run economic growth by shaping urban networks, influencing human capital formation, and impacting household dynamics. Further exploration of these relationships could offer valuable insights into the mechanisms through which institutions drive economic growth over the long term.

Institutions and technological advancements present a challenge to the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. According to Simson (2019), in the East African countries of Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda, state expenditure and deficits expanded during a period of high economic growth in the 1960s and 1970s (Simson_2019: p.25). This suggests that economic growth may not be solely dependent on institutions, as technological advancements and government spending can also play significant roles.

Furthermore, Simson (2019) argues that the elimination of spending inefficiencies, leakages, and costly delays might not have been enough to sustain investment levels during those periods, let alone re-orientate these economies (Simson_2019: p.4). This implies that technological advancements may be necessary for sustained economic growth, challenging the notion that institutions are the sole drivers of long-term economic development.

Stern (2004) also questions the relationship between institutions and economic growth by discussing the Economic Kuznets Curve (EKC) hypothesis, which suggests that economic growth leads to environmental degradation in the early stages but eventually results in improved environmental conditions (Stern_2004: p.1). If this hypothesis is correct, it implies that technological advancements, which can lead to more efficient use of resources and reduced environmental impact, may be as important as institutions in driving long-term economic growth.

The assertion that institutions are the primary catalyst for long-term economic growth is nuanced by various factors, as revealed in several scholarly works. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) highlight the underestimation of women's economic contributions, particularly in Africa, which can undermine policies aimed at strengthening their roles. The authors argue that women's labor, often subsumed under household production, is formally unaccounted for, thereby weakening efforts to promote gender equality and economic growth (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.3).

Moreover, Frankema (2012) suggests that colonial education may have played a significant role in explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa. The former British colonies were found to have grown faster due to their educational lead (Frankema_2012: p.2), challenging the notion that institutions alone drive economic growth.

Koepke & Baten (2005) complicate this argument by introducing climate as a factor influencing human living standards and economic development over long periods. For instance, during the Roman Empire, heights did not increase despite an assumed rise in living standards and economic growth due to protectionist policies (Koepke & Baten_2005: p.16).

Hopkins (2019) contributes to this discourse by emphasizing the limitations that postmodernism imposed on the study of long-run economic and political change in Africa. Hopkins argues that successive generations of research may have been limited due to the dominance of Western perspectives, potentially restricting the diversity of independent African viewpoints (Hopkins_2019: p.4).

The assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is not without its complexities, as evidenced by various scholarly works. Austin (2008) posits that while institutions may indeed play a pivotal role in long-term economic development, they may have reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe and parts of Asia (Austin_2008: p.24). This perspective complicates the argument by suggesting that external factors, such as trade, could have contributed significantly to Africa's economic disparity.

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) further nuance this discussion by highlighting the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 in Sub-Saharan Africa, which has led to its absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). This lack of historical data raises questions about the extent to which institutions have influenced economic growth in this region and whether other factors might be at play.

Allen (2001) supports the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history, revealing relationships that recur due to path dependence rather than simply because situations are similar (Allen_2001: p.1). This emphasis on historical context complicates the argument by suggesting that institutions' impact on economic growth may be influenced by the specific historical circumstances in which they emerge and evolve.

Austin (2007) challenges the assumption that population growth was a primary driver of economic development, arguing that other factors, such as technological advancements and capital deepening, were more significant (Austin_2007: p.16). This perspective complicates the argument by suggesting that while institutions may play a role in economic growth, they are not the sole or even primary factor.

Allen et al. (2011) demonstrate that income disparities during the early 20th century were due to long-run stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe (AllenEtAl_2011: p.1). This finding complicates the argument by suggesting that while institutions may influence economic growth, they are not the only factor determining income disparities between regions.

The works of Sokoloff & Engerman (2000), Skocpol & Sommers (1980), and Stoler (1989) offer nuanced perspectives on the relationship between institutions, technological advancements, and long-run economic growth. These scholars complicate the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of economic growth by emphasizing the role of factor endowments, historical context, and comparative analysis in shaping economic trajectories.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) argue that initial conditions, or factor endowments broadly conceived, could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth. They suggest that the United States and Canada experienced sustained economic growth during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, while other regions did not achieve this goal until later (Sokoloff & Engerman, 2000: p.12). By highlighting these differences in growth patterns, they challenge the notion that institutions are the sole determinant of long-run economic growth.

Skocpol & Sommers (1980) also complicate the argument by focusing on the particularities of individual cases and using comparative analysis to increase the "visibility" of one structure by contrasting it with another (Skocpol & Sommers, 1980: p.8). For example, they reveal the specific differences between Japan and China in terms of their responses to Western intrusions (Skocpol & Sommers, 1980: p.14). This comparative approach underscores the importance of historical context in understanding economic growth patterns.

Stoler (1989) further complicates the argument by emphasizing the role of colonizers living in "imagined communities" that were consciously created to overcome economic and social disparities (Stoler, 1989: p.5). This work highlights the importance of understanding the social and cultural contexts in which institutions develop and operate.

The works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), while tangential to the direct investigation of institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, provide valuable insights into the role of labor markets and cultural contexts in economic development.

Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) emphasizes the significance of labor immobility, suggesting that with labor tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, potentially hindering economic growth. Conversely, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.13) highlights the impact of limited female populations on societal development, indicating a potential link between gender dynamics and economic growth.

Both scholars acknowledge the influence of external factors on economic institutions. Domar (Domar_1970: p.16) references Phillips' work on the economic cost of slaveholding in the Cotton Belt, implying an interplay between institutional structures and broader socio-economic conditions. Similarly, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.10) acknowledges that the Western impact was not limited to economic institutions, hinting at a complex intertwining of cultural and economic forces.

Despite these insights, several key issues remain unresolved. For instance, the exact mechanisms through which labor immobility and gender dynamics affect long-run economic growth require further exploration. Additionally, a comprehensive understanding of how external factors interact with internal institutions to shape economic growth trajectories is still lacking.
