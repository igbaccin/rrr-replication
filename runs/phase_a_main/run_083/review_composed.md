The central question under investigation in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, a thesis that has garnered significant attention among economists and historians. The stakes are high, as understanding the drivers of economic growth can inform policy decisions and development strategies aimed at improving living standards and reducing poverty worldwide.

Support for the institutionalist perspective is evident in the works of Kuznets (1973) and North & Weingast (1989). Kuznets (Kuznets_1973: p.4) posits that high aggregate growth rates are associated with rapid changes in economic structure, which in turn are linked to broader societal transformations. Similarly, North & Weingast (North&Weingast_1989: p.28) argue that representative government institutions, characterized by multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, play a crucial role in facilitating economic growth and political freedom.

On the other hand, some authors complicate the institutionalist perspective. For instance, Austin (Austin_2008: p.26) acknowledges that institutions are important but argues that they may reinforce rather than originate certain economic positions, such as Africa's relatively poor position compared to Western Europe. Broadberry & Gardner (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1) point out that a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has led to Sub-Saharan Africa being absent from recent debates about long-run growth, suggesting that the institutionalist perspective may not fully account for the region's economic history.

Akyeampong & Fofack (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3) also challenge the institutionalist perspective by highlighting the underrepresentation of women's economic contributions in historical analyses, which undermines policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles. Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.4) argue that differences in mortality rates are not the only or even the main cause of variation in institutions, suggesting that other factors may also play a significant role in shaping economic growth.

The ongoing debate around the role of institutions in long-run economic growth is far from settled. Future research should aim to address the nuances and complexities raised by these authors, as understanding the drivers of economic growth remains crucial for informing policy decisions and development strategies aimed at improving living standards and reducing poverty worldwide.

The relationship between institutions and economic structure is a key factor in understanding long-term economic growth, as supported by various scholars. Kuznets (1973) argues that rapid changes in economic structure are associated with technological innovations and social innovations, which are major drivers of economic growth (p.4). Similarly, Mokyr (2005) emphasizes the importance of intellectual developments and the growth of economic reasonableness in explaining the wealth of Western societies (p.54).

North & Weingast (1989) focus on the political factors underlying economic growth, highlighting the role of representative government institutions and their impact on market development (p.2). North (1989) discusses how institutions can lower transaction costs and contribute to economic growth by increasing security rights and market efficiency (p.9). Ogilvie (2007) acknowledges the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework, suggesting that understanding this complexity is crucial for comprehending economic development (p.27).

Acemoglu et al. (2002) observe a change in the distribution of economic prosperity over time and question whether this shift can be attributed to institutional factors (p.5). Their work underscores the significance of institutions in understanding long-term development, providing valuable insights for future research on the subject.

The significance of institutions in fostering long-term economic growth is further emphasized when examining their impact on agricultural development. Acemoglu and colleagues (2001) provide compelling evidence that institutional factors play a crucial role in shaping the agrarian sector's trajectory, which in turn contributes to overall economic progress.

The authors argue that institutions, such as property rights and political systems, are fundamental determinants of agricultural productivity (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). They contend that these institutions can either promote or hinder investment in agriculture, which is a critical driver of economic growth. For instance, they demonstrate that detrimental institutions were responsible for stifling investment and economic progress in certain regions (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27).

Moreover, Acemoglu and colleagues challenge the notion that differences in mortality rates are the primary cause of variation in institutions (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3). Instead, they suggest that institutional structures themselves have a more significant impact on long-term development. Their research underscores the importance of considering the role of institutions when examining agricultural growth and economic progress over time.

The role of institutions in fostering long-run economic growth is further emphasized by deVries (1994), who underscores the significance of Europe's urban network and its impact on regional economic development. This network, established during the early modern period, served as a framework for industrial growth (deVries_1994: p.5).

DeVries (1994) also highlights the importance of the Industrial Revolution, particularly the British Industrial Revolution, as the most significant monument in economic history (deVries_1994: p.2). This suggests that institutional structures played a crucial role in the industrial revolution, further supporting the argument that institutions are fundamental to long-term economic growth.

Moreover, deVries (1994) suggests a renewed attention to the economic history of the household or family unit, implying that institutional structures within these units could also contribute to economic progress (deVries_1994: p.8). This underscores the importance of considering the role of institutions not only at a national level but also within households and families.

While institutions are often credited with fostering long-run economic growth, some sources challenge this perspective by highlighting instances where economic structures and ideologies appear to have a more significant impact. For example, Simson (2019) points out that in Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda, state expenditure and deficits expanded during a period of high economic growth in the 1960s and 1970s (Simson_2019: p.25). Similarly, McCloskey (2015) argues that ideologies and ideas, not institutions, significantly influenced economic growth in France during the Unfinished Revolution of 1789 (McCloskey_2015: p.9).

Furthermore, Bryceson (2002) contends that traditional hierarchical orders within communities can influence women's economic and social spheres, with women primarily being part of an agrarian family work effort (Bryceson_2002: p.9). Humphries (2013) also suggests that the division of labor, the rise of workshop production, and the eventual emergence of the factory system were crucial factors in eighteenth-century economic growth (Humphries_2013: p.18).

These sources imply that while institutions may play a role in economic growth, structural changes and ideologies could also be key drivers. Future research might explore the interplay between institutions, structures, and ideologies in shaping long-term economic progress.

The perspective that institutions are the sole cause of long-run economic growth is challenged by various scholars, as they highlight the interplay between institutions and structural changes in shaping economic progress. Austin (2008) contends that trade, such as the Atlantic one, reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe (p.24). Similarly, Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) argue that initial conditions or factor endowments could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run growth (p.4).

Moreover, Komlos (1998) suggests that physical stature during the Industrial Revolution was significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic processes and associated structural changes (p.16). Furthermore, Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that policymakers often face trade-offs between economic growth and equality, implying that distribution trends are intertwined with the explanations of growth itself (p.1).

In addition, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) emphasize the importance of understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution becomes subsumed under household production, undermining policies that seek to strengthen women's economic roles (p.3). They also note that historical developments in West Africa find parallels elsewhere in Africa where gender and economic growth are concerned (p.4).

These sources complicate the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by highlighting the role of structural changes, initial conditions, and gender dynamics in shaping economic progress. Future research might explore the interplay between these factors and institutions in determining long-term economic growth.

The work of Broadberry and Gardner (2022) sheds light on the complexities surrounding the role of institutions in long-run economic growth, particularly in the context of Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA). They argue that a lack of data on SSA's aggregate economic performance before 1950 has been a significant factor in its exclusion from recent debates about comparative long-term growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1).

Collier and Gunning (1999) have observed that African economic performance has been significantly worse than other regions, while Sachs and Warner (1997) note the slow growth in Africa that has resulted in it being less likely to converge with other regions (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). Broadberry and Gardner emphasize the importance of understanding whether this growth provides a path to convergence, given the risks of shrinking economic progress in the past (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.18).

In other regions, new contributions to historical national accounting have provided insights into patterns of growth and development over centuries, rewriting the history of economic divergence and convergence since before the industrial revolution ( Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). This suggests that a comprehensive understanding of long-run economic growth in SSA may require similar efforts to gather and analyze historical data.

The environmental impact on economic growth is a crucial aspect to consider when analyzing long-run economic development, particularly in Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA). According to Temin (2002), the exhaustion of cheap labor was a significant factor that slowed economic growth. This perspective complicates the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by suggesting that resource depletion may also play a role in growth dynamics.

Temin (2002) argues that the end of the Golden Age, a period of high and sustained economic growth in Europe during the 1950s and 1960s, was not caused by adverse economic shocks but rather a return to equilibrium growth paths. This shift might imply that institutions, which are often associated with long-term growth, could have been less influential during this period compared to other factors like resource availability or depletion.

Moreover, Temin (2002) suggests that recent theories of economic growth should approach the Golden Age in the context of economic transition, as most national economies around 1800 were almost completely agricultural. This perspective underscores the importance of understanding the role of agriculture and resource management institutions in shaping long-run economic growth.

However, Temin (2002) also emphasizes that the slowdown of economic growth was not solely due to resource depletion but rather a combination of factors. He states that it was the exhaustion of cheap labor that caused economic growth to slow, implying that institutions play a role in managing and allocating resources efficiently, which can impact long-term economic growth.

The works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), though tangential to the direct examination of institutions as the primary cause of long-run economic growth, provide valuable insights into the role of institutions in managing labor and resources.

Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) emphasizes that with labor tied to land or owners, employer competition ceases, suggesting a link between institutional arrangements and economic growth through resource allocation efficiency. He also points out that the exhaustion of cheap labor can slow growth (Domar_1970: p.4). This aligns with the assertion that institutions play a role in managing resources efficiently to promote long-term economic growth.

Bohannan (1959) discusses the impact of Western economic institutions on African societies, focusing on slave dealing and status acquisition (Bohannan_1959: p.7). However, he also notes that the number of women is limited in these societies (Bohannan_1959: p.13), which could have implications for population growth and subsequent economic development. This suggests a potential role for institutions in shaping demographic trends that influence long-run economic growth.

While both scholars imply a connection between institutions and economic growth, they do not explicitly state that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth. The question of whether institutions are the primary driver of long-term economic growth remains unresolved. Further research is needed to establish a more definitive link between institutions and economic growth over extended periods.

In addition, understanding how specific institutional arrangements impact economic growth in various contexts requires further exploration. This could provide valuable insights into the nuances of institution-growth relationships and contribute to a more comprehensive understanding of this complex relationship.
