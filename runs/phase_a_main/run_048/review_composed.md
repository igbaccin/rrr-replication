The central question under investigation in this literature review is whether institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, a thesis that has garnered significant attention among economists and historians alike. The stakes are high as understanding the underlying causes of economic growth can have profound implications for policy-making and development strategies.

Supporting this thesis, Austin (2008) argues that institutions play a crucial role in long-term economic growth, asserting that they influence economic structures and relationships recurring due to path dependency (Austin_2008: p.1). Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) focus on the political factors underpinning economic growth and the development of markets, emphasizing the importance of representative government and common law courts in enforcing and changing economic rules (North&Weingast_1989: p.2).

Komlos (1998) complicates the argument by arguing that socio-economic changes associated with modern economic growth were sufficient to bring about a decline in physical stature, raising questions about the relationship between institutions and long-term development (Komlos_1998: p.17).

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) add further complexity by noting that data limitations have prevented Sub-Saharan Africa from being included in recent debates about comparative long-run growth, potentially obscuring important insights into the relationship between institutions and economic development (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1).

The relationship between technology and institutions in fostering long-term economic growth is a crucial aspect that supports the thesis. Technological innovations, as highlighted by Kuznets (1973), significantly contribute to economic growth by bringing about rapid changes in economic structure and society (Kuznets_1973: p.4). These technological advancements, however, require appropriate institutions for their effective implementation and utilization.

Mokyr (2005) emphasizes the importance of 'useful knowledge' in driving the Industrial Revolution and subsequent economic growth in the West (Mokyr_2005: p.4). He suggests that this change in the rate and nature of economic growth can be attributed to developments in the intellectual realm concerning this useful knowledge. This underscores the crucial role institutions play in facilitating the dissemination, development, and application of such knowledge, thereby fostering economic growth.

North (1989) argues that institutions are fundamental to economic growth by reducing transaction costs (North_1989: p.9). He contends that the security of property rights and the efficiency of contract enforcement mechanisms are key factors in determining the level of institutional development, which in turn influences economic growth.

Ogilvie (2007) acknowledges the interdependency of individual institutions within a wider framework, suggesting that economists have been striving to bring institutions inside the tent—to make them endogenous to economic analysis (Ogilvie_2007: p.3). This effort further validates the significance of institutions in understanding long-term economic growth.

Structural change, a key aspect of institutional development, significantly contributes to economic growth (Abramovitz_1986: p.7). Abramovitz (1986) posits that the evolution of mixed economies is a direct result of economic growth itself, suggesting a reciprocal relationship between institutions and growth. He further emphasizes the importance of macroeconomic and monetary conditions in encouraging and sustaining capital investment and effective demand, all of which are crucial for long-term economic development (Abramovitz_1986: p.7, p.20).

Allen (2001) supports this notion by highlighting the significance of wages and prices in understanding economic development, economic leadership, and standard of living (Allen_2001: p.1). He also acknowledges that economic fluctuations and adverse conditions can prevent work and hinder growth, implying that effective institutions are necessary to mitigate such hindrances (Allen_2001: p.15).

Bryant (2006) reinforces the link between institutional development and economic growth by discussing the Ming-Manchu efflorescence, a period of rising productivity, increased commercial exchange, and rural manufacturing expansion (Bryant_2006: p.20). This example underscores the role of institutions in fostering structural change and promoting long-term economic growth.

The impact of institutions on economic growth is further underscored by the role they play in facilitating migration, a key driver of structural change (Frankema&vanWaijenburg_2012: p.15). West African commercial centers attracted substantial economic migrants, leading to high population growth rates in these regions (Frankema&vanWaijenburg_2012: p.17). This influx of labor contributed to the development and expansion of rural manufacturing, a period of rising productivity (Bryant_2006: p.20).

The institutional environment significantly influenced this migration pattern. In the Gold Coast, for instance, population growth rates exceeded 3% per annum (1911-1931), which can be attributed to the city's economic opportunities created by its institutions (Frankema&vanWaijenburg_2012: p.17). Conversely, in Nigeria, the population growth rate was significantly lower due to less favorable institutional structures (Frankema&vanWaijenburg_2012: p.15).

Institutions also play a crucial role in the development of private capital markets, an essential foundation for long-term economic success (North&Weingast_1989: p.23). The authors argue that representative government with multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs foster the evolution of these financial institutions (North&Weingast_1989: p.28). This, in turn, promotes economic growth by enabling efficient allocation of resources and fostering market development.

The works of Stern (2004), Simson (2019), McCloskey (2015), vanZanden (1999), and Humphries (2013) challenge the thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by suggesting alternative factors contributing to economic development.

Stern (2004) critiques the Economic Kuznets Curve (EKC) hypothesis, which posits a U-shaped relationship between income and environmental degradation, arguing that economic growth may lead to environmental damage rather than being a means to environmental improvement. This view contradicts the idea that strong institutions promoting market development are essential for long-term economic growth and environmental sustainability.

Simson (2019) provides evidence of state expenditure and deficits expanding during periods of high economic growth, suggesting that government spending may play a significant role in driving economic development. This finding challenges the notion that strong institutions fostering private sector growth are the primary drivers of long-term economic growth.

McCloskey (2015) highlights instances where ideologies and cultural factors have hindered economic growth, such as in France during the Unfinished Revolution of 1789. This research suggests that ideas and ideologies may be as important as institutions in shaping long-term economic growth trends.

VanZanden (1999) questions the consensus view in the 1960s and 1970s that early modern economies were unable to generate economic growth, arguing instead for a more optimistic interpretation of economic development before the industrial revolution. This work challenges the idea that strong institutions are necessary for long-term economic growth by suggesting alternative factors, such as technological change or cultural shifts, may have played a significant role in driving economic development during this period.

Finally, Humphries (2013) critiques assumptions about the division of labor and family structure in early modern economies, arguing that these factors may not have been as important as previously thought in driving economic growth. This research challenges the notion that strong institutions promoting efficient resource allocation are essential for long-term economic growth.

The relationship between technology and institutions in driving long-term economic growth is a complex one, as evidenced by various studies. Technology, while playing an essential role, may not be the sole determinant of economic growth. Instead, it interacts with institutional factors to shape economic outcomes.

Technology's impact on economic growth is apparent, but Komlos (Komlos_1998: p.2) argues that socio-economic changes associated with modern economic growth were sufficient by themselves to bring about the decline in physical stature, indicating a potential limitation of technology as an independent driver of long-term growth. Additionally, Hopkins (Hopkins_2019: p.9) notes that as the economic base appeared to crumble, the superstructure assumed a new importance, suggesting that institutional factors may become more critical during periods of technological transition or decline.

On the other hand, Sokoloff & Engerman (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4) argue that initial conditions or factor endowments could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run growth. They suggest that particular institutional conditions may have lingering effects, implying that certain institutions may be better suited to support technological advancement and economic growth than others. Lindert & Williamson (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.8) also highlight the need to explore the relationship between initial conditions and long-term growth trends, further complicating the relationship between technology and institutions.

Temin (Temin_2002: p.4) points out that the exhaustion of cheap labor caused economic growth to slow, which could be interpreted as a technological factor influencing institutional change. However, he also emphasizes that characteristics of the labor market continue to be active topics in explaining European economic difficulties, suggesting that institutions may play a role in shaping the impact of technology on economic growth.

Despite the consensus that institutions are a primary driver of long-run economic growth, recent debates have been limited in their consideration of Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA) due to data scarcity before 1950 (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). Collier and Gunning (1999) and Sachs and Warner (1997) have observed that African economic performance has been significantly worse than other regions, raising concerns about the region's potential for future growth and convergence (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). However, new contributions to historical national accounting have provided insights into patterns of growth and development in other regions over centuries, suggesting that similar advancements could reshape our understanding of SSA's economic history (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1).

On the other hand, Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) argue that the fundamental question of institutions and economic growth may not be as straightforward as it seems (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.16, p.30). They propose that temporalities in economic development are interconnected, suggesting a more complex relationship between institutions, technology, and long-run economic growth (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.29, p.29). This perspective complicates the argument that institutions are the sole cause of long-run economic growth by introducing the idea that other factors may play a role in shaping this relationship.

The impact of industrialization on economic growth is further elucidated in the works of Austin (2007) and Bolt & van Zanden (2014). These sources complicate the argument that institutions are the sole cause of long-term economic growth by emphasizing the role of technological advancements, particularly in transportation, and demographic shifts.

Austin (Austin_2007: p.8) notes that until the mid-1920s, railways had a significant cost advantage over lorries, facilitating industrialization and economic growth. This suggests that technological innovations can play a crucial role in shaping economic growth trajectories, a factor that may be intertwined with institutional development.

Furthermore, Austin (Austin_2007: p.16) highlights that the forces of modern economic growth, such as capital deepening and total factor productivity improvements, were not fully at play in some regions until the previous century. This underscores the idea that industrialization may require a fundamental shift in factor ratios over time, which could be influenced by both institutions and technological advancements.

Bolt & van Zanden (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5) mention numerous revisions of growth estimates for various countries during the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. These revisions do not fundamentally change our view but provide a more accurate understanding of economic growth patterns, suggesting that accurate data is essential to understanding the relationship between industrialization and long-term economic growth.

Moreover, Bolt & van Zanden (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.15) discuss the importance of structural change in driving economic growth. They cite a study by van der Eng (2010) on the sources of long-term economic growth in Indonesia, which suggests that structural changes, possibly influenced by institutions and technological advancements, can lead to sustained economic growth.

The studies by Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), though not directly addressing the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, offer tangential insights that contribute to the understanding of this complex phenomenon. Both scholars emphasize the role of institutions in shaping economic structures, which in turn can influence growth.

Domar (1970) argues that with labor tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, potentially hindering economic growth. Conversely, structural change correlates with growth as labor becomes free and mobile (Domar_1970: p.4). This aligns with the assertion that institutions play a crucial role in facilitating competition and promoting structural changes, leading to sustained economic growth.

Bohannan (1959) illustrates how Western economic institutions impacted African societies, particularly the Tiv, through trade and slave dealing (Bohannan_1959: p.7). The arrival of the British linked the Tiv economy to the outside world, leading to structural changes that may have influenced their long-term growth trajectory (Bohannan_1959: p.10).

However, both scholars diverge in their focus and scope. Domar's analysis is primarily economic, while Bohannan provides a cultural anthropological perspective. This highlights the interdisciplinary nature of understanding institutions' role in long-run economic growth.

Despite these insights, several questions remain unresolved. For instance, what specific institutional factors drive structural change and long-term economic growth? How can we measure the impact of institutions on growth, and how do we distinguish between causation and correlation? Addressing these questions could deepen our understanding of the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth.
