The question of whether institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth has been a subject of intense debate among scholars. In examining this issue, various authors have presented supporting arguments, critiques, and nuanced perspectives.

One of the key proponents of the institutional growth theory is Douglass C. North, who, in collaboration with Barry R. Weingast (North&Weingast_1989), argued that the fundamental institutions of representative government, such as an explicit set of multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, play a crucial role in enabling economic growth and political freedom. They demonstrated this through the example of England's Glorious Revolution, which led to the establishment of these institutions and subsequently fostered economic growth (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

Similarly, Daron Acemoglu and his colleagues (AcemogluEtAl_2001) found that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress, while others fostered growth. They provided evidence showing a large effect of institutions on economic performance (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19).

However, not all scholars agree that institutions are the sole or primary cause of long-run economic growth. For instance, John Akyeampong and Thierry Fofack (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014) complicate this argument by highlighting how understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution becomes subsumed under household production and their labor formally unaccounted for can undermine policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles.

Broadberry and Gardner (Broadberry&Gardner_2022) also add nuance to this discussion, suggesting that the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has led to Sub-Saharan Africa being absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. They argue that understanding the extent to which there are still risks of economic shrinkage in SSA is crucial for determining whether its growth provides a path to convergence (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.18).

The importance of institutions in driving long-term economic growth is further substantiated by various scholarly works. North & Weingast (1989) argue that representative government, characterized by multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, plays a crucial role in facilitating economic growth and political freedom (p.28). They also emphasize the need to focus on political factors underpinning economic growth and market development (p.2).

Acemoglu et al. (2001) support this notion by demonstrating that more fundamental institutions significantly impact property rights, which in turn influences investment and economic progress (p.27). They also find a substantial effect of institutions on economic performance (p.19).

Mokyr (2005) highlights the role of intellectual developments, such as individualism, man-made formal law, corporatism, self-governance, and rules determined through institutionalized processes, in fostering economic growth (p.56). He suggests that changes in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West can be attributed to advancements in the intellectual realm concerning "useful knowledge" (p.4).

North (1989) contributes to this discussion by examining how institutions affect transaction costs, security rights, and market development, which are all essential for long-term economic growth (p.9). Acemoglu et al. (2002) underline the significance of understanding the distribution of economic prosperity over the past 500 years to evaluate various theories of long-run development (p.49).

These scholarly works collectively support the argument that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Understanding and fostering robust institutions remains crucial for promoting sustainable economic development in various regions, including Sub-Saharan Africa (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.18). Future research should continue to investigate the specific institutional factors that contribute most significantly to economic growth across different contexts.

Institutions and trade are intimately linked in their contribution to long-run economic growth. According to Austin (2008), institutions played a crucial role in shaping Africa's economic position compared to Western Europe, reinforcing rather than originating its relatively poor economic status. The Atlantic trade, for instance, served to exacerbate rather than alleviate the economic disparity between Africa and Europe (Austin_2008: p.24).

The importance of examining growth theories against long-term history is underscored by Austin (2008), who advocates for a focus on recurring relationships due to similar situations as well as path dependencies. Similarly, deVries (1994) emphasizes the significance of Europe's urban network in establishing a framework for regional economic development during the early modern period, which facilitated industrial growth (deVries_1994: p.5).

Frankema & van Waijenburg (2012) highlight the role of West African commercial centers in attracting large flows of economic migrants year after year. This influx of people contributed to high land-labor ratios, which in turn led to real wage rises in cities such as Accra and Lagos (Frankema&vanWaijenburg_2012: p.15, p.17). The economic growth driven by these migrants underscores the impact of institutions on shaping trade patterns and promoting long-term economic development.

The link between institutions and industrialization is further supported by the findings of Allen (2001), Broadberry & Gupta (2006), and Bryant (2006). Allen (2001) emphasizes the role of wages and prices in economic development, suggesting that institutional factors significantly influence these key indicators. For instance, he notes that the British industrial revolution provided reassurance that building craftsmen's wages could represent trends in average earnings (Allen_2001: p.4). This underscores the importance of institutions in shaping wage patterns and, consequently, economic growth.

Broadberry & Gupta (2006) also support this notion by highlighting the service sector's crucial role during the early modern period. They argue that the service sector played a significant part in economic growth, as it does in the modern era (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.9). This implies that institutions that foster the development of the service sector are likely to promote long-term economic growth.

Bryant (2006) adds to this discussion by pointing out that European environments were conducive to commercial exchange, implying that institutional structures facilitating trade were essential for industrialization (Bryant_2006: p.13). This reinforces the idea that institutions are fundamental in driving economic growth through promoting trade and industrialization.

The arguments presented by Simson (2019), Stern (2004), McCloskey (2015), and Bryceson (2002) challenge the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Simson's work suggests that institutional development, educational expansion, and military autonomy were emphasized over protecting existing government jobs in certain regions during periods of high economic growth (Simson_2019: p.27). Stern questions the assumption that economic growth leads to environmental improvement, arguing that there may be a point where irreversible environmental damage reduces future income levels (Stern_2004: p.8). McCloskey highlights instances where ideologies and conservative reasons hindered economic growth in France and Spain, despite having examples of successful economic development nearby (McCloskey_2015: p.9). Bryceson's research indicates that traditional hierarchical orders within communities can influence women's economic and social spheres, potentially limiting their participation in non-agrarian activities (Bryceson_2002: p.9). These findings suggest that while institutions may play a role in economic growth, other factors such as ideologies, environmental impacts, and societal structures also significantly influence long-term economic development.

The arguments presented in various scholarly works complicate the notion that institutions are the sole cause of long-run economic growth. Komlos (1998) emphasizes the significance of physical stature during the Industrial Revolution, suggesting that short-term fluctuations in economic processes may have influenced biological growth processes. This implies a complex interplay between institutional development and other factors such as population growth, urbanization, and income inequality in driving long-term economic growth.

Temin (2002) contends that the exhaustion of cheap labor could have slowed economic growth, implying that labor market dynamics may play an essential role in long-run economic development. Furthermore, he argues that the end of the Golden Age was brought about by the return to equilibrium growth paths rather than adverse economic shocks, which complicates the causal relationship between institutions and economic growth.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) suggest that long-run distribution trends are intertwined with the search for explanations of growth itself. They also propose that policymakers often face trade-offs between economic growth and equality, implying that societal values and ideologies may significantly influence institutional development and, in turn, long-term economic growth.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) argue that initial conditions or factor endowments could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth. They highlight the variation across societies in conditions relevant to growth such as political institutions, property rights, and social norms, which suggests a complex relationship between institutions and economic growth.

Lastly, Koepke & Baten (2005) explore the relationship between climate and human living standards, implying that environmental factors may also play a role in long-run economic development. They find that over the period of the Roman Empire, heights did not increase, standing in contrast to the common view that living standards increased due to economic growth.

The role of institutions in long-run economic growth is further nuanced by examining their impact on labor markets. According to Goldin (2006), the study of labor was previously confined to the institutions of the labor market. However, Goldin (2006) argues for a shift from evolution to revolution in understanding labor choices and decisions. This distinction is significant as it emphasizes the dynamic nature of labor markets and the importance of institutional changes in shaping labor dynamics over time.

Moreover, Frankema (2012) suggests that colonial education played a crucial role in explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa. The British colonies, which controlled most of the "fertile" areas, are found to have grown faster due to their educational advantage (Frankema_2012: p.2). This finding complicates the argument by highlighting the importance of institutional factors, such as education policies, in shaping long-term economic growth.

Williamson (1996) also underscores the role of institutions in understanding economic convergence. He argues that understanding the sources of convergence is fundamental to understanding who gains and who loses from it (Williamson_1996: p.25). This perspective adds depth to the argument by emphasizing the need to consider institutional factors when analyzing long-term economic growth patterns.

Feinstein (1998) discusses the impact of wars on British economic growth, noting that changes in farming and migration were necessary for labor markets to disperse the glut of farm labor (Feinstein_1998: p.28). This finding suggests that institutional factors, such as agricultural policies and migration regulations, can have profound effects on long-term economic growth by influencing labor market dynamics.

The literature on long-run economic growth further complicates the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of such growth. While it is widely acknowledged that institutional factors play a significant role in shaping labor market dynamics and agricultural policies (Feinstein_1998), recent studies suggest that the impact of these factors may not be as straightforward as initially thought.

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that Sub-Saharan Africa has been underrepresented in debates about long-run growth due to a lack of data on economic performance before 1950. They note that African countries have historically experienced slower growth compared to other regions, but understanding the extent to which this growth provides a path to convergence requires an understanding of the risks that have undermined economic progress in the past (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.18). This finding implies that the role of institutions in long-run economic growth may be influenced by regional factors, such as data availability and historical economic performance.

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) contribute to this discussion by focusing on the role of women in African economies. They highlight that understanding the processes by which women's economic contributions are subsumed under household production is crucial for policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). Furthermore, they suggest that the contribution of African women to economic growth and development has historically been dependent on the economic provision of the husband (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.9). These findings nuance the argument that institutions are the sole determinant of long-run economic growth, as they emphasize the importance of social and gender dynamics in shaping economic outcomes.

The works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), though not directly addressing the primary thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, offer tangential insights that contribute to a more comprehensive understanding of this complex relationship. Both scholars acknowledge the importance of institutions in shaping economic outcomes, yet they diverge in their focus and emphasis.

Domar (1970) primarily discusses labor immobility as an institutional factor that impacts economic growth. He argues that when labor is tied to land or to an owner, competition among employers ceases, thereby hindering structural change and economic growth (Domar_1970: p.4). This perspective aligns with the argument that institutions are crucial for long-run economic growth but also suggests that certain institutional arrangements can hinder it.

On the other hand, Bohannan (1959) explores the impact of Western institutions on African societies, focusing on slave dealing and status acquisition (Bohannan_1959: p.7). While not directly addressing economic growth, his work underscores the role of institutional change in shaping social dynamics, which in turn can influence economic outcomes.

Despite their different focuses, both scholars converge in recognizing the profound impact of institutions on economic development. However, what remains unresolved is the extent to which specific institutional arrangements drive long-run economic growth and the mechanisms through which this occurs. Further research is needed to elucidate these relationships and provide a more nuanced understanding of the complex interplay between institutions and economic growth.
