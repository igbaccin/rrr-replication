The central question in the literature review on institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is whether institutional structures significantly impact economic development over extended periods. This debate carries significant importance, as understanding the factors driving economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at fostering development and reducing poverty in various regions.

Supporting this thesis, Kuznets (1973) argues that modern economic growth is associated with rapid changes in economic structure, which in turn are linked to broader societal transformations. Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) demonstrate how representative government institutions and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs contribute to economic growth and political freedom. Acemoglu et al. (2001) also support this stance by highlighting that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress, leading to variations in economic performance across countries.

Conversely, Austin (2008) complicates the argument by suggesting that institutions might reinforce rather than originate a region's economic position compared to others. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) also add nuance to the discussion by emphasizing the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 in Sub-Saharan Africa, which has limited its inclusion in recent growth debates. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) further complicate the issue by pointing out that women's economic contributions are often subsumed under household production and their labor is formally unaccounted for, undermining policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles.

The stakes of this debate are high, as understanding the role of institutions in long-run economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at fostering development and reducing poverty in various regions. The literature suggests that while some institutions are indeed fundamental to economic growth, others may reinforce existing economic positions or overlook crucial factors such as women's contributions to economic growth. As the debate continues, it is essential to consider both supporting and complicating evidence to develop comprehensive policies that promote sustainable economic development.

The importance of institutions as a fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is further supported by various scholars. North (1989) highlights that institutions, particularly those with centralized enforcement mechanisms, play a significant role in reducing transaction costs and promoting security rights, which in turn fosters market development and economic growth over the long term (North_1989: p.9). Similarly, Whatley (2018) demonstrates that econometric models can capture the relationship between certain factors, such as gunpowder imports and slave exports, and their impact on economic growth (Whatley_2018: p.13).

Hopkins (2009) provides additional evidence supporting the thesis by arguing that ethnic fragmentation does not necessarily result in unproductive institutions. Instead, communities can function cohesively to support economic development, as demonstrated in various African societies (Hopkins_2009: p.21). Bryant (2006) supports this notion by emphasizing the role of European mercantilist imperialism and capitalistic transformation in fostering economic growth, particularly through the creation of environments conducive to commercial exchange (Bryant_2006: p.13).

North and Weingast (1989) elucidate the significance of institutions in fostering economic growth and political freedom, emphasizing the role of representative government with multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). They argue that these institutions played a crucial role in enabling economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.30). The study focuses on the political factors underpinning economic growth, not just the rules governing economic exchange but also the institutions responsible for enforcing and changing them (North&Weingast_1989: p.2).

Acemoglu et al. (2001) also support the notion of institutions as a fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Their research reveals that differences in mortality rates are not the main cause of variation in institutions, and they demonstrate that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3, p.27). Moreover, their findings show a large effect of institutions on economic performance (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19). These studies collectively underscore the critical role of institutions in shaping long-term economic growth and development.

The work of Kuznets (1973) further emphasizes the link between institutions and economic growth, specifically highlighting the correlation between rapid changes in economic structure and long-term development. According to Kuznets, these structural changes are not only associated with high aggregate growth rates but also with significant transformations in other aspects of society, such as family structures and urbanization (Kuznets_1973: p.4).

Moreover, Kuznets underscores the role of technological innovations in driving both economic growth and the necessary social adjustments (Kuznets_1973: p.6). This suggests that institutions play a crucial role in facilitating these technological advances, thereby promoting long-term economic growth.

Furthermore, Kuznets acknowledges the existence of unexpected negative outcomes even within developed countries, as well as the struggles of less developed countries to harness modern technology effectively (Kuznets_1973: p.11). This implies that the quality and effectiveness of institutions can significantly impact a country's ability to leverage technological innovations for sustained economic growth.

The analysis provided by Simson (2019) challenges the notion that institutions are the primary driver of long-run economic growth, particularly in the context of industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy. Simson's research suggests that these factors were given significant importance in certain economies during the 1960s and 1970s, with a focus on expanding public employment rather than protecting existing government jobs (Simson_2019: p.4).

Furthermore, Simson argues that while there may have been spending inefficiencies, leakages, and costly delays to reform, their elimination might not have been sufficient to sustain the investment levels of the 1960s or reorient these economies (Simson_2019: p.4). This implies that factors beyond institutional quality may play a crucial role in economic growth, as the investment levels and orientation of these economies were influenced by other factors.

Moreover, Simson's evidence indicates that state expenditure and deficits expanded during periods of high economic growth in certain regions (Simson_2019: p.25). This suggests that the relationship between institutional quality and economic growth may not be as straightforward as previously thought, as these economies were able to grow despite potentially suboptimal institutions.

However, it is important to note that Simson's research does not dispute the importance of institutions entirely. Rather, it highlights that other factors such as industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy may also play significant roles in economic growth. This underscores the complexity of understanding the drivers of long-run economic growth and emphasizes the need for further investigation into this area.

The assertion that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth is further nuanced by several scholars, highlighting the complexity of this relationship. For instance, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) emphasize that Sub-Saharan Africa has been overlooked in recent debates about long-term growth due to a lack of data before 1950 (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). This suggests that the absence of substantial data might have oversimplified our understanding of institutions' role in economic growth, particularly in regions like Sub-Saharan Africa.

Temin (2002) complicates the argument by suggesting that the exhaustion of cheap labor, rather than institutions, may have caused economic growth to slow (Temin_2002: p.4). This perspective challenges the notion that institutions are solely responsible for long-run economic growth and underscores the importance of considering other factors such as labor supply and demand.

Bolt & van Zanden (2014) also contribute to this discourse by discussing revisions of nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth, which have been added for various countries but do not fundamentally change our view (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5). This implies that while institutions play a role in economic growth, the understanding of this relationship might require more refined data and analysis to fully grasp its complexity.

Lastly, Stoler (1989) argues that colonizers lived in "imagined communities" designed to overcome economic and social disparities (Stoler_1989: p.5). This perspective suggests that institutions might not only drive but also be shaped by broader societal factors, further complicating the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth.

While institutions are indeed proposed as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, the trade relationship between Africa and Western Europe adds nuance to this perspective (Austin_2008: p.24). The trades, particularly the Atlantic one, reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to western Europe (Austin_2008: p.24).

This suggests that while institutions play a significant role in driving long-term growth, other factors such as trade relationships might also influence this process. The trade between Africa and Western Europe seems to have perpetuated the economic disparities rather than bridging them, complicating the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth.

Moreover, the study endorses the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history, revealing relationships that recur because of similar situations as well as path dependencies (Austin_2008: p.1). This underscores the need to consider historical contexts and their impact on the development of institutions and subsequent economic growth.

The work of Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) sheds light on the complex role of African women in economic growth, particularly within the colonial economy. They highlight that the economic contributions of African women were often subsumed under household production, rendering their labor informally unaccounted for (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). This oversight undermines policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles.

Moreover, Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) emphasize that the economic provision of husbands significantly influenced the contribution of African women to economic growth and development (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.9). This suggests a reliance on patriarchal structures, which may have hindered the potential for women's independent economic growth.

Interestingly, Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) note that the major historical developments in West Africa, with regards to gender and economic growth, find parallels elsewhere in Africa (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.4). This implies a broader applicability of their findings, suggesting that colonial economies across Africa may have similarly impacted women's roles in economic growth.

Finally, Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) affirm that the contribution of African women to economic growth and development was significant (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.19). This underscores the importance of understanding and addressing historical and cultural factors that have marginalized women's economic roles, in order to promote more equitable economic growth in Africa.

The literature on the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth provides a nuanced understanding of this complex issue. Both Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) offer tangential insights that contribute to the broader discourse.

Domar (1970) emphasizes the role of labor, suggesting that when labor is tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, hindering economic growth. This aligns with the argument that institutions play a fundamental role in economic development by facilitating competition and promoting efficiency. However, Domar's focus on labor also underscores the importance of institutions in regulating and protecting property rights, which are crucial for fostering investment and growth (Domar_1970: p.4).

Bohannan (1959) discusses the impact of Western economic institutions on African societies, highlighting the role of slave dealing and status acquisition in shaping these communities. While this work does not directly address long-term economic growth, it underscores the significance of historical factors in shaping contemporary institutional structures and their effects on development (Bohannan_1959: p.7).

Despite these insights, a key issue that remains unresolved is the extent to which institutions are the primary driver of long-run economic growth, or if they interact with other factors such as geography, culture, and technology in more complex ways. For instance, Domar (1970) acknowledges the role of technological progress in promoting economic development, suggesting that institutions may facilitate or hinder this process (Domar_1970: p.16).
