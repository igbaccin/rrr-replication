The central question under examination in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, a thesis initially proposed by North and Weingast (1989). This question holds significant importance as understanding the factors driving economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting development.

Supporting the assertion that institutions play a crucial role in long-term economic growth is Kuznets (1973), who argues that both technological innovations and social innovations are major factors affecting economic and social structure, thereby contributing to economic growth. Acemoglu et al. (2001) also support this stance by demonstrating the detrimental impact of institutions on investment and economic progress.

On the other hand, Austin (2008) complicates the argument by suggesting that while institutions are important, they may not be the sole cause of long-run economic growth. Instead, he endorses the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history to reveal recurring relationships due to similar situations or path dependency. Broadberry and Gardner (2022) concur with Austin, stating that a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has led to Sub-Saharan Africa being absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth.

Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) add nuance to the discussion by highlighting that understanding the processes by which women's economic contributions are subsumed under household production is crucial for policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles. Komlos (1998), in a similar vein, complicates the issue by emphasizing the importance of considering both economic processes and associated structural changes when discussing modern economic growth.

The stakes of this debate are high, as identifying the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth can guide policymakers in making informed decisions aimed at promoting development. As the literature suggests, while institutions play a significant role, they may not be the sole factor driving economic growth. Further research is needed to fully understand the complex interplay between institutions and other factors contributing to long-term economic growth. The works of various scholars provide substantial support for the assertion that institutions are fundamental causes of long-run economic growth. For instance, Kuznets (1973) posits that economic and social structure undergo significant changes during periods of modern economic growth, which are associated with technological innovations and societal adjustments (Kuznets_1973: p.4). These changes contribute to the overall economic development by facilitating the adoption and utilization of new technologies.

Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2001) argue that institutions play a crucial role in shaping economic outcomes. They suggest that certain institutions can either promote or hinder investment and economic progress based on their characteristics (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). The authors also propose that the persistence of these institutions over time can be explained by various economic mechanisms.

Mokyr (2005) agrees with this perspective, emphasizing the role of intellectual developments in shaping economic growth. He argues that changes in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West can be attributed to advancements in "useful knowledge" (Mokyr_2005: p.4). This knowledge, in turn, is closely linked to institutional structures and their ability to facilitate technological progress and innovation.

Ogilvie (2007) discusses the interdependency of individual institutions within a broader framework, suggesting that changes in one institution can have far-reaching effects on other aspects of economic growth (Ogilvie_2007: p.27). Sokoloff and Engerman (2000) also highlight the role of initial conditions and factor endowments in shaping long-term economic growth, suggesting that certain institutional structures may have been crucial in enabling some countries to achieve sustained growth while others did not (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4).

The role of institutions in shaping long-term economic growth is further underscored by North and Weingast (1989), who assert that political factors play a significant part in the development of markets and economic growth. They contend that certain institutional structures, such as those promoting rule of law and limited government, are essential for fostering economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). Conversely, they argue that absolutist states with weak constraints on their power, like early modern Spain, created economic conditions that hindered long-term growth (North&Weingast_1989: p.7).

Additionally, North (1989) emphasizes the importance of institutions in reducing transaction costs and enhancing security rights, which are crucial for the development of a capital market and subsequent economic growth (North_1989: p.9). He suggests that structural change correlates with growth, as more renegotiation-resistant institutions facilitate increased security rights and a more efficient market system (North_1989: p.6).

Institutions and technological advancements are intrinsically intertwined in the long-run economic growth, as supported by Acemoglu et al. (2002) and van Zanden (2009). Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49) state that institutions play a fundamental role in understanding economic development over the past 500 years, indicating their significance in fostering technological progress. The authors also assert that the reversal in the distribution of economic prosperity is related to institutions (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5), implying that improvements in institutional quality can lead to advancements in technology and subsequent growth.

In a similar vein, van Zanden (vanZanden_2009: p.1) acknowledges that relative prices are the DNA of an economy, further highlighting the importance of institutions in shaping the economic landscape. The author also suggests that human capital formation, which can be influenced by institutional factors, is one of the determinants of long-term economic growth (van Zanden_2009: p.24). Furthermore, van Zanden (vanZanden_2009: p.4) posits that periods of economic growth will likely lead to a rising skill premium in the construction industry, indicating that better institutions can facilitate technological advancements and improve efficiency in various sectors.

According to Stern (2004), economic growth may not necessarily lead to a reduction in environmental degradation, challenging the notion that better institutions contribute to long-run economic growth by promoting sustainable development. Stern argues that while early stages of economic growth might lead to increased environmental damage, this damage does not necessarily halt the growth process, and any irreversibility is not severe enough to reduce future income levels (Stern_2004: p.8). Furthermore, he suggests that the structure and technology of an economy change over time, which can result in rising or declining environmental degradation during economic development (Stern_2004: p.3). This perspective implies that institutions might not be the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth due to their potential insignificance in addressing environmental concerns associated with economic expansion.

The perspectives on institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth are further nuanced by examinations of industrialization and economic growth in various historical contexts. For instance, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) highlight that Sub-Saharan Africa's absence from debates about comparative long-run growth is due to the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950, suggesting that institutions might have played a lesser role in this region during the Industrial Revolution.

Similarly, Komlos (1998) provides insights into the physical changes and structural shifts associated with the Industrial Revolution, which could potentially impact the argument about institutions' roles in economic growth by demonstrating that industrialization was accompanied by rapid population growth, urbanization, and income inequality. These factors might have been influenced or shaped by institutional structures.

Austin (2007) sheds light on the role of transportation infrastructure, such as railways, in facilitating economic growth during the Industrial Revolution. While this supports the argument that institutions play a crucial role in fostering long-term economic growth, it also complicates matters by suggesting that technological advancements and other factors may have contributed to industrialization and growth independently from institutional development.

Bolt & van Zanden (2014) further add to the discussion by presenting revisions of nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth data for various countries, which could potentially alter our understanding of institutions' impact on economic growth during these periods.

The sources under examination complicate the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by presenting nuances and alternative perspectives on the factors contributing to economic development during the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries.

Temin (2002) challenges the notion that institutions were the primary drivers of growth, arguing instead that the exhaustion of cheap labor was responsible for the slowdown in economic growth. He suggests that recent theories of economic growth should consider the context of economic transition, which was prevalent during this period (Temin_2002: p.7). Furthermore, Temin asserts that the end of the Golden Age was brought about by the return to equilibrium growth paths rather than adverse economic shocks in the 1970s (Temin_2002: p.14).

Lindert & Williamson (1985) also question the causal relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth. They argue that understanding long-run distribution trends is closely related to explaining the causes of growth itself (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.9). Additionally, they suggest that Japan's economic history supports the idea of imbalance and inequality as factors in economic development, since neither appeared to be correlated with Japan's long-run experience (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.14).

Hopkins (2019) contributes to this discussion by highlighting the influence of Western impositions on the study of long-run economic and political change in Africa. He suggests that these impositions may have limited the diversity of independent African viewpoints, potentially impacting our understanding of institutions' role in economic growth (Hopkins_2019: p.4).

Skocpol & Sommers (1980) propose a comparative approach to studying historical trajectories, which can provide insights into social change and the differences between countries like Japan and China (Skocpol&Sommers_1980: p.2, p.9). This approach may help reshape our understanding of how institutions influenced economic growth in these countries during the relevant periods.

Stoler (1989) complicates the argument by focusing on the role of imagined communities in colonial contexts. She argues that colonizers lived in imagined communities that were consciously created to overcome economic and social disparities (Stoler_1989: p.5). This perspective suggests that the role of institutions in economic growth may be intertwined with broader social and cultural factors.

The role of institutions in economic growth is further nuanced when considering their impact on labor markets, as highlighted by Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) and Williamson (1996).

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) draw attention to the underrepresentation of women's economic contributions in many societies, which can hinder policies aimed at strengthening their economic roles. They argue that understanding the processes by which women's labor is subsumed under household production is crucial for addressing this issue (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.3). Furthermore, they note that the historical developments and themes discussed in West Africa regarding gender and economic growth have parallels elsewhere in Africa (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.4).

On the other hand, Williamson (1996) focuses on the relationship between schooling and economic growth, specifically in Sweden before World War I. He suggests that understanding the sources of convergence is fundamental to understanding who benefits and loses from it (Williamson_1996: p.25). Additionally, he discusses how mass migrations could reduce the pace of real wage growth in receiving countries by glutting labor markets abroad (Williamson_1996: p.17).

These findings complicate the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. They suggest that other factors, such as the underrepresentation of women's labor and the impact of mass migrations on labor markets, can also play significant roles in shaping economic growth patterns. Further investigation into these areas could provide valuable insights into the complex interplay between institutions, labor markets, and economic growth.

Institutions significantly shape economic growth patterns, as they influence labor markets and production structures (Bohannan_1959, Domar_1970, Bryceson_2002). Scholars generally converge on the idea that institutions play a crucial role in fostering long-term economic growth. For instance, the enserfment of peasants and the institution of slavery have been shown to hinder labor market competition and stifle economic growth (Domar_1970: p.4). Similarly, the arrival of Western economic institutions has had profound impacts on societies, affecting not only economic structures but also social spheres, particularly for women (Bohannan_1959: p.10, Bryceson_2002: p.9).

However, scholars diverge in their focus and the specific mechanisms through which institutions influence growth. Domar (1970) emphasizes the role of labor markets, arguing that when labor is tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, hindering economic development (Domar_1970: p.4). On the other hand, Bohannan (1959) explores how cultural institutions can impact economic structures and social spheres, particularly in African societies (Bohannan_1959: p.7, p.13). Bryceson (2002) focuses on the effects of livelihood diversification due to changing institutions, highlighting the diminishing role of men as family provisioners in response to cash cropping and migrant labor (Bryceson_2002: p.9, p.11).

Despite these insights, several questions remain unresolved. For example, how do cultural institutions interact with economic institutions to shape long-term growth patterns? Additionally, what are the specific mechanisms through which changes in labor markets and livelihood diversification impact economic growth? Further research into these areas could provide valuable insights into the complex interplay between institutions, labor markets, and economic growth.
