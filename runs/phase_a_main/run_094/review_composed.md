The central question in the literature review on institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is whether institutional development plays a significant role in shaping economic trajectories over time. This question holds substantial importance, given its implications for understanding historical and contemporary economic disparities across regions.

Several scholars support this thesis, arguing that institutions are indeed the key drivers of long-term economic growth. Austin (2008) asserts that institutions "as 'the' fundamental cause of long-run economic growth" (p. 26). Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) demonstrate how representative government institutions and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs are intimately related to economic growth and political freedom (p. 28). Acemoglu et al. (2001) also support this view, emphasizing that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress (p. 27).

However, others offer critiques or add nuance to the argument. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate the discussion by noting that a lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has kept Sub-Saharan Africa out of recent growth debates (p. 1). Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) add that understanding women's economic contributions and their labor formalization is essential for effective policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles (p. 3).

The stakes in this debate are high, as the answer to this question has implications for policy-making, development strategies, and our understanding of historical economic disparities. The literature suggests that institutions play a crucial role in long-run economic growth, but further research is needed to fully understand their impact and how they can be harnessed for economic progress.

The literature by Austin (2008) supports the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, particularly in the context of trade between Africa and Western Europe. Austin argues that historical trades, such as the Atlantic one, reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe (Austin_2008: p.24). This suggests that the institutional frameworks in place during these trading periods significantly influenced the long-term economic growth trajectories of both regions.

Moreover, Austin emphasizes the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history to reveal relationships that recur due to similar situations and path dependencies (Austin_2008: p.1). This implies that understanding the institutional structures during these historical trading periods could provide valuable insights into the role institutions play in shaping economic growth over the long term.

Further, Austin finds appeal in the logical economy of the theory posited by AJR (Austin_2008: p.24), which identifies institutions as 'the' fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. This supports the assertion that institutions are indeed a key factor in driving economic growth over extended periods.

Technological innovations are a significant driver of economic growth and are closely linked to societal changes, as suggested by Kuznets (1973). He points out that rapid technological advancements, such as those characteristic of modern economic growth, play a crucial role in driving economic and social structure (Kuznets_1973: p.6). This notion aligns with the argument that institutions are fundamental to long-term economic growth, as technological innovations often require supportive institutional frameworks for their successful implementation and diffusion.

Furthermore, Kuznets (1973) acknowledges that while positive achievements of modern economic growth are evident, there can also be unexpected negative outcomes within developed countries. These challenges underscore the importance of institutions in managing technological change effectively, ensuring sustainable growth, and mitigating potential adverse impacts on society.

Moreover, Kuznets (1973) highlights the need for social innovations to accompany technological advancements, as they provide the necessary adjustments for an economy to adapt to new technologies. This highlights the interplay between institutions, technological innovations, and societal changes in promoting long-run economic growth.

Kuznets (1973) also discusses nonconventional costs associated with economic growth, such as environmental degradation and income inequality (Kuznets_1973: p.5). These costs underscore the need for robust institutions to manage the side effects of technological innovations and ensure that economic growth is equitable and sustainable over the long term.

The authors North and Weingast (1989) emphasize the crucial role of institutions in fostering economic growth and political freedom. They highlight multiple veto points as a significant institutional feature that promotes long-term economic development. According to their research, these multiple veto points in representative government structures facilitate consensus building among various interest groups, thereby preventing the emergence of dominant coalitions that might undermine economic growth (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

In addition, North and Weingast (1989) argue that the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs is another essential aspect contributing to economic growth. By providing a stable and predictable legal framework, these institutions enable long-term planning and investment, which are crucial for economic development (North&Weingast_1989: p.2).

The authors also emphasize that the focus should not only be on rules governing economic exchange but also on the institutions responsible for enforcing these rules and facilitating their adaptation to changing circumstances (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). This perspective underscores the importance of robust, adaptable institutions in managing the side effects of technological innovations and ensuring that economic growth is sustainable over the long term.

The work of Simson (2019) challenges the assertion that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth by focusing on three specific areas: industrial development, education expansion, and military autonomy. In East Africa during the 1960s and 1970s, state expenditure and deficits expanded significantly, leading to increased investment levels and economic growth (Simson_2019: p.25). However, Simson suggests that the sustainability of these investments may have been questionable, as there were spending inefficiencies, leakages, and costly delays to reform that could have hindered their long-term success (Simson_2019: p.4).

Furthermore, Simson contends that the emphasis placed on expanding public employment rather than protecting those already in government jobs may have contributed to the eventual economic crises experienced by Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda in later years (Simson_2019: p.27). This evidence suggests that while robust institutions are important for managing technological innovations and ensuring sustainable growth, they may not be the sole determinant of long-run economic success.

The lack of data on aggregate economic performance in Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA) before 1950 complicates the understanding of institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Broadberry & Gardner (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1) acknowledge that the region has been absent from recent debates due to this data gap, which hinders a comprehensive analysis of SSA's economic trajectory prior to mid-20th century.

Collier and Gunning (1999 : 64), as well as Sachs and Warner (1997 : 336), both note that African economic performance has been significantly worse than other regions, further emphasizing the importance of understanding SSA's historical growth patterns to evaluate the role of institutions in long-term economic success.

Moreover, Broadberry & Gardner (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.18) stress the need to understand whether current growth trends in SSA pose risks of shrinking that could undermine economic progress, as has occurred in the past. This underscores the significance of analyzing historical data to determine the extent to which institutions have contributed to long-run economic growth in SSA.

In addition, new contributions to historical national accounting in other regions have provided insights into patterns of growth and development over centuries (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.1). This suggests that similar efforts in SSA could potentially uncover valuable information about the region's economic history, further nuancing our understanding of the role of institutions in long-run economic growth.

The contribution of African women to economic growth and development is a significant yet often overlooked factor in understanding the role of institutions in long-run economic growth (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.19). Historically, the economic provision of African women was largely dependent on their husbands, with their contributions often subsumed under household production and unaccounted for in formal records (Akyeampong&Fofock_2014: p.3, p.9). This oversight undermines policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles.

Moreover, the historical developments in West Africa that affected gender roles and economic growth have parallels elsewhere in Africa (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.4). Understanding these parallels can provide a more nuanced perspective on the role of institutions in shaping women's economic contributions, and thus, long-run economic growth.

For instance, the introduction of cash crops in West Africa led to changes in gender roles, as women were often responsible for their cultivation (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.27). This shift towards a more market-oriented economy could have implications for institutional development and long-term economic growth. Similarly, the impact of colonialism on gender roles and economic opportunities in Africa is another area that requires further exploration to fully understand the role of institutions in shaping women's contributions to economic growth.

The role of institutions in fostering long-run economic growth is further nuanced by considering the impact of industrialization, as highlighted by Komlos (1998). In his study, Komlos emphasizes that economic growth during the Industrial Revolution was not merely a linear progression but was marked by significant fluctuations and structural changes.

Komlos (Komlos_1998: p.16) reveals an intriguing finding of 8-year height cycles similar to classical business cycles, implying that biological growth processes were influenced by short-term economic fluctuations during the Industrial Revolution. This suggests that the relationship between institutions and economic growth may not be as straightforward as previously thought, with external factors potentially playing a significant role in shaping this relationship.

Moreover, Komlos (Komlos_1998: p.17) notes that the Industrial Revolution was accompanied by rapid population growth, urbanization, increased income inequality, and greater income variability. These structural changes, driven by institutions, had profound effects on economic growth during this period. This evidence underscores the notion that institutions are not just catalysts for long-run economic growth but also play a crucial role in shaping the nature of this growth.

The examination of institutions as a primary catalyst for long-term economic growth is substantiated by both Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959). Both scholars acknowledge the significant role of institutions in shaping economic growth, albeit with some nuanced perspectives.

Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) emphasizes that when labor is tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, thereby hindering economic growth. Conversely, he also highlights the potential for structural change to correlate with economic growth (Domar_1970: p.15).

Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.10), on the other hand, broadens the discussion by noting that the Western impact is not limited to economic institutions but extends to social and political spheres as well. He underscores the importance of status acquisition and slave dealing in shaping economic growth (Bohannan_1959: p.7).

Despite their overlapping perspectives, there remains a divergence in the focus of their analyses. Domar primarily concentrates on the economic implications of institutional structures, while Bohannan explores the interplay between economic, social, and political institutions.

The unresolved question that emerges from this review is the need for a comprehensive understanding of how various types of institutions – economic, social, and political – interact to influence long-term economic growth. This requires further research to delve deeper into the complexities of these interactions and their effects on growth patterns.
