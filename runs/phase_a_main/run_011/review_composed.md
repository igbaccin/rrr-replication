The question of whether institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth has been a subject of intense debate among scholars. This review examines literature on this topic, revealing that 22 sources support the thesis, 6 offer critiques, and 19 add nuance or qualifications (Broadberry & Gardner_2022).

Central to the argument is the assertion that institutions play a critical role in shaping economic growth over the long run. Institutions are the rules, norms, and organizations that structure human interactions and guide behavior within a society (Austin_2008: p.1). The claim is that well-functioning institutions foster an environment conducive to economic development, while poorly functioning ones impede it.

Proponents of this view argue that institutions affect growth by influencing factors such as property rights, contract enforcement, and political stability (Austin_2008: p.4). For instance, strong property rights encourage investment and innovation, while weak ones deter them (Kuznets_1973: p.6). Similarly, effective contract enforcement reduces the cost of doing business and encourages economic activity (Temin_2002: p.7).

However, not all agree that institutions are the sole determinant of long-run economic growth. Some scholars contend that while institutions matter, they are just one piece of a complex puzzle (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.18). Others argue that institutions may have a more indirect impact on growth, shaping factors such as technological innovation and population growth (Komlos_1998: p.2).

Moreover, some sources complicate the debate by highlighting the challenges of measuring institutional quality and its impact on growth (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.3). They argue that institutions are often difficult to quantify and may vary significantly within a single country or region (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.1).

Institutions and technological advancements are intrinsically linked in their influence on long-run economic growth, as supported by various scholarly works. Austin (2008) posits that institutions serve as the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth, particularly highlighting how they reinforce economic positions between regions such as Europe and Africa. Similarly, Kuznets (1973) emphasizes the role of institutions in facilitating technological innovations during modern economic growth.

North & Weingast (1989) focus on political factors underpinning economic growth, demonstrating how representative government institutions with multiple veto points and common law courts primacy over economic affairs have been essential for economic development and political freedom. Acemoglu et al. (2001) provide evidence of the detrimental impact of certain institutions on investment and economic progress, while Mokyr (2005) explores how developments in intellectual realms concerning useful knowledge contributed to Western economic growth.

North (1989) further supports this argument by discussing the role of institutions in reducing transaction costs, increasing security rights, and fostering market development, which ultimately contribute to long-term economic growth. In sum, these studies underscore the central importance of institutions as a driving force behind technological advancements and subsequent long-run economic growth. The complexity of measuring institutional quality and its impact on growth, however, remains an ongoing challenge (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.3; Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.1).

Institutions play a pivotal role in fostering long-run economic growth, as they shape societal structures and provide the framework for economic development (North & Weingast_1989: p.28). The significance of institutions is further supported by the works of deVries (1994) and vanZanden (2009), who emphasize the crucial role of institutional arrangements in fostering technological advancements, urban networks, human capital formation, and structural change.

deVries (deVries_1994: p.2) underscores the importance of the Industrial Revolution, a key manifestation of institutional development, as "the most important such monument in economic history." He further elaborates on the role of urban networks in regional economic development, suggesting that these systems facilitated industrial growth by connecting cities through commercial interaction (deVries_1994: p.5).

vanZanden (vanZanden_2009: p.1) supports this notion, stating that "relative prices are the DNA of an economy," implying that institutional arrangements significantly influence economic structures and patterns. He further demonstrates how the skill premium, a measure of human capital formation, affects relative prices and contributes to long-term economic growth (vanZanden_2009: p.24).

Moreover, deVries (deVries_1994: p.8) highlights the need for renewed attention to the economic history of the household or family unit, suggesting that institutions play a crucial role in governing household behaviors and activities, which ultimately impact economic growth. Similarly, vanZanden (vanZanden_2009: p.4) indicates that periods of economic growth are associated with a rising skill premium in the construction industry, indicating the institutional influence on human capital formation and technological advancement.

The role of institutions in long-run economic growth is further substantiated by Whatley's (2018) study, which explores the relationship between environmental factors and institutional changes. Whatley (Whatley_2018: p.3) presents econometric results that suggest a correlation between structural change and growth, thereby providing evidence for the institutional influence on economic development.

In his analysis, Whatley (Whatley_2018: p.11) introduces Equation 2 of Model 2, which represents the long-run equilibrium relationship between gunpowder imports and slave exports. This relationship underscores the impact of institutions on technological advancement and resource allocation, as the availability of gunpowder, a key factor in military power and industrialization, is directly linked to the export of slaves, a reflection of institutional structures and practices.

Moreover, Whatley (Whatley_2018: p.13) notes that Equation 2 now captures this long-run equilibrium relationship, indicating the importance of institutions in shaping economic trajectories over extended periods. The absence of variables adjusting to deviations from long-run equilibrium, as stated by Whatley (Whatley_2018: p.11), further supports the argument that institutions play a fundamental role in determining the path of economic growth.

The argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is challenged by evidence suggesting that technological advancements and economic policies may also play significant roles in driving growth. For instance, Simson (2019) highlights the importance given to industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy in certain regions during periods of high economic growth, implying that these factors could potentially contribute to economic growth independently of institutions. Similarly, McCloskey (2015) discusses instances where conservative ideologies and religious beliefs stifled economic growth despite the examples of successful economies in other regions, indicating a possible influence of ideas and ideologies on economic development. Furthermore, Clark & Jacks (2007) argue that technological advancements enabled industries like coal to respond to increases in demand during the Industrial Revolution era, suggesting that technology may be an essential factor in economic growth. These sources challenge the notion that institutions are solely responsible for long-run economic growth and instead emphasize the importance of other factors such as technology and policies.

Institutions are often considered the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, but a closer examination of various scholarly works reveals that technological advancements and gender roles also play significant roles in this process.

Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) argue that women's contributions to economic growth in Africa have been historically undervalued due to their labor being categorized as household production, which is not formally accounted for. The authors emphasize the importance of understanding these processes to strengthen women's economic roles (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.3). However, they also acknowledge that the historical developments in West Africa regarding gender and economic growth have parallels elsewhere in Africa (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.4), suggesting a broader impact of gender roles on long-run economic growth across different regions.

Komlos (1998) discusses the concept of modern economic growth and its relation to physical stature during the Industrial Revolution. While acknowledging that the timing of the onset of modern economic growth is not a focus of this work, Komlos highlights the influence of short-term fluctuations in economic processes on biological growth processes (Komlos_1998: p.16). This finding underscores the complex interplay between economic conditions and technological advancements in shaping long-run economic growth.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) explore the relationship between economic growth, inequality, and policy. They suggest that understanding long-run distribution trends is closely linked to explaining growth itself (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.9). Furthermore, they argue that policymakers often have to balance trade-offs between economic growth and equality (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.1), which implies that technological advancements and policies may influence the distribution of wealth and, consequently, long-run economic growth.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) examine the contrasting records in growth across different societies and highlight the role of institutions in this process. They argue that initial conditions, or factor endowments broadly conceived, could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth (Sokoloff & Engerman_2000: p.4). This finding complicates the notion that institutions are solely responsible for long-run economic growth, as it suggests that technological advancements and other factor endowments may also play essential roles in this process.

Frankema (2012) argues that colonial education is a key factor explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa. The author highlights the British educational lead and its impact on economic growth (Frankema_2012: p.2). This finding underscores the importance of technological advancements, such as education, in shaping long-run economic growth.

Hopkins (2019) discusses the limitations of postmodernism and its impact on the study of African economic history. The author argues that successive generations of researchers may have been inhibited by Western impositions, which limited the diversity of independent African viewpoints (Hopkins_2019: p.4). This finding suggests that a more diverse range of perspectives could contribute to a better understanding of the factors influencing long-run economic growth in Africa.

The relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth is further nuanced when considering their impact on environmental factors, as suggested by Temin (2002) and Koepke & Baten (2005).

Temin (2002) proposes that the exhaustion of cheap labor, rather than institutions themselves, may have caused economic growth to slow. He argues that the end of the Golden Age was brought about by a return to equilibrium growth paths, not adverse economic shocks in the 1970s (Temin_2002: p.14). This perspective complicates the assertion that institutions are the sole cause of long-run economic growth, suggesting that other factors such as labor availability may also play a significant role.

On the other hand, Koepke & Baten (2005) focus on the relationship between climate and human living standards, arguing that over the period of the Roman Empire, heights did not increase significantly, contrasting the common view of economic growth during this period (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.16). This finding implies that environmental factors, such as climate, could potentially influence long-run economic growth and may need to be taken into account when examining the role of institutions.

Despite the significant role institutions play in long-run economic growth, there are regions and periods that have been underrepresented in discussions due to a lack of historical data (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). Sub-Saharan Africa, in particular, has been absent from these debates, raising concerns about the comprehensiveness of the institutions-growth relationship narrative (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1).

Moreover, the consensus view in the 1960s and 1970s was that the early modern economy was unable to generate economic growth (vanZanden_1999: p.1). However, recent years have seen a shift in this perspective as the pessimistic interpretation of economic growth before the industrial revolution has come under attack (vanZanden_1999: p.1). This challenge to the consensus view complicates the argument that institutions are the sole fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, suggesting that other factors may also play a role.

The lack of data on Sub-Saharan Africa and the reevaluation of the early modern economy's potential for growth add nuance to the thesis that institutions are the primary drivers of long-term economic development. Further exploration of these underrepresented regions and periods could potentially reveal new insights into the role of institutions in shaping economic growth trajectories.

The literature on the role of institutions in long-run economic growth, as evidenced by Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), suggests a convergence in understanding the fundamental importance of institutions. Both authors acknowledge that institutions play a significant role in shaping economic development trajectories.

Domar (1970) emphasizes the impact of labor immobility, specifically tied to land or owners, on competition among employers and economic growth. He argues that with labor bound to either land or an owner, competition ceases, hindering structural change and economic progress. This aligns with Bohannan's (1959) discussion of the Western impact on African societies, where he notes that the arrival of Europeans brought changes in economic institutions such as slave dealing and curing, which had profound effects on local economies.

However, there is a divergence between the two authors regarding the scope of institutional influence. Domar's focus is primarily on labor immobility and its impact on competition, while Bohannan discusses broader changes in economic institutions brought about by European contact. This suggests that while both agree on the importance of institutions, their perspectives differ in terms of the specific factors driving institutional change and their subsequent impact on economic growth.

Despite these insights, several questions remain unresolved. For instance, Domar's (1970) analysis does not explore the mechanisms through which labor immobility hinders structural change or long-term economic growth. Similarly, Bohannan (1959) does not provide a comprehensive understanding of the various ways in which European contact influenced African economies beyond the examples he discusses.

Further exploration of these underrepresented regions and periods could potentially reveal new insights into the role of institutions in shaping economic growth trajectories, addressing some of the unresolved questions in this literature.
