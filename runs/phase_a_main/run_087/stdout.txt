LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question of this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, a thesis that has garnered significant attention among scholars. This question holds immense importance as understanding the factors driving economic growth can inform policies aimed at fostering development and alleviating poverty.

Supporting the thesis, Austin (2008) argues that institutions play a crucial role in shaping economic growth, particularly in the context of Africa's economic history (Austin_2008: p.26). Similarly, Kuznets (1973) emphasizes the interplay between technological innovations and social innovations in driving economic growth (Kuznets_1973: p.6). North & Weingast (1989) also highlight the role of representative government institutions in enabling economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

However, not all scholars fully endorse this view. Acemoglu et al. (2001) acknowledge the impact of institutions on economic performance but also emphasize other factors such as mortality rates and property rights (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3). Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate the issue by pointing out the lack of historical data for Sub-Saharan Africa, which has hindered its inclusion in debates about long-run growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance by discussing the role of women's economic contribution and how it has been overlooked in historical analyses (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3).

These conflicting perspectives underscore the complexity of the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth. While institutions are undeniably important, other factors such as technological innovations, social structures, and historical data also play significant roles. As the debate continues, a comprehensive understanding of these interrelated factors will be essential for crafting effective policies aimed at promoting sustainable economic development.

Institutions and their role in long-run economic growth have been a topic of extensive debate among scholars. One significant factor that has contributed to this discourse is the impact of colonialism on institutional development (Austin_2008, deVries_1994).

Austin (2008) posits that institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth are attractive due to their logical economy. He argues that trades, such as the Atlantic one, reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe and parts of Asia. This suggests that colonialism may have exacerbated existing institutional weaknesses in colonized regions, hindering their potential for long-term growth.

Similarly, deVries (1994) emphasizes the importance of Europe's urban network in establishing a framework for regional economic development during the early modern period. While he does not explicitly discuss colonialism, it is plausible to infer that European colonization played a role in shaping these urban networks and their subsequent impact on economic growth.

These findings align with those of Sokoloff & Engerman (2000), who argue that initial conditions, or factor endowments broadly conceived, could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth. They suggest that the divergence in paths can be traced back to the achievement of sustained economic growth by the United States and Canada during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, while others did not manage to attain this goal until late in the nineteenth century. This timeline coincides with the height of European colonial expansion, implying that colonialism may have contributed to these differences in growth trajectories.

The works of various scholars provide substantial evidence supporting the notion that institutions are a primary driver of long-run economic growth. Kuznets (1973) posits that economic and social structure changes, alongside technological innovations, play a significant role in economic growth (Kuznets_1973: p.4, p.6). Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) argue that representative government institutions, characterized by multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are crucial for economic growth and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28).

Mokyr (2005) highlights that changes in intellectual developments concerning "useful knowledge" were essential to the West's economic growth (Mokyr_2005: p.4, p.56). North (1989) emphasizes the role of institutions in reducing transaction costs and increasing security rights, which can lead to market development and economic growth (North_1989: p.9). Ogilvie (2007) suggests that economic institutions are interdependent and play a crucial role in shaping economic development (Ogilvie_2007: p.27).

Pamuk (2007) demonstrates that the Black Death, which led to structural changes in European economies, significantly impacted wage levels and paved the way for commercialization of agriculture (Pamuk_2007: p.4, p.24). Collectively, these scholars provide compelling evidence that institutions are indeed fundamental causes of long-run economic growth.

The works of Acemoglu and his colleagues provide further evidence that institutions are indeed fundamental causes of long-run economic growth, particularly in the context of agriculture. In their study published in 2001, they highlight the detrimental impact of certain institutions on investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). This suggests that these institutions were not conducive to agricultural advancements, which are often a precursor to economic growth.

Moreover, Acemoglu et al. (2001) demonstrate a large effect of institutions on economic performance, with their results showing a significant correlation between institutional quality and economic prosperity (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19). This supports the argument that institutions play a crucial role in shaping long-term economic growth.

Similarly, in their 2002 publication, Acemoglu and his colleagues argue that the reversal in the distribution of economic prosperity over the past 500 years is related to institutions (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5). They also note a first-order fact that understanding economic and political development requires an understanding of how institutions have evolved over time (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49). This underscores the importance of institutions as fundamental causes of long-run economic growth.

Collectively, these scholars provide robust evidence supporting the thesis that institutions are indeed fundamental causes of long-run economic growth, particularly in agriculture. Their findings demonstrate that certain institutions hinder investment and progress, while others promote prosperity and economic development over time. These insights offer valuable directions for future research on the role of institutions in shaping long-term economic outcomes.

Institutional changes during colonialism have been suggested to challenge the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. According to Stern (2004), economic development may lead to environmental degradation in the early stages, and while it can eventually lead to a reduction in such degradation, this is not always guaranteed (Stern_2004: p.1, p.3). This perspective questions the assumption that institutions promoting prosperity are universally beneficial for long-term economic outcomes.

Moreover, Clark & Jacks (2007) argue that technological advancements played a significant role in the Industrial Revolution, enabling the coal industry to respond to increases in demand (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.4). This finding suggests that institutions may not be the sole determinant of economic growth during this period, as technological progress could also play an essential role.

Humphries (2013) highlights that the division of labor, workshop production, and the emergence of the factory system were crucial factors in eighteenth-century economic growth (Humphries_2013: p.18). These findings imply that institutional changes might not be the only drivers of long-term economic development, as other factors such as technological advancements and organizational structures may also contribute significantly.

The relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth is further nuanced when considering the impact of colonialism, as evidenced by several studies. Temin (2002) posits that the exhaustion of cheap labor, rather than institutional changes, may have caused economic growth to slow in Europe during the 19th century (Temin_2002: p.4). Similarly, Hopkins (2019) argues that the focus on post-colonial African economies may have limited the diversity of independent viewpoints and research (Hopkins_2019: p.4).

Moreover, Frankema (2012) suggests that colonial education could be a key factor in explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa, with former British colonies growing faster as a result of their educational lead (Frankema_2012: p.2). Simson (2019) also highlights the importance of industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy during colonial times, stating that these factors were emphasized in various regions of the world (Simson_2019: p.27).

However, it is essential to acknowledge that not all aspects of colonialism had a positive impact on economic growth. For instance, Temin (2002) argues that the end of the Golden Age was brought about by the return to equilibrium growth paths, not adverse economic shocks in the 1970s (Temin_2002: p.14). Additionally, Williamson (1996) points out that mass migrations during colonial times could have reduced the pace of real wage growth in receiving countries by glutting labor markets abroad (Williamson_1996: p.17).

The sources under examination complicate the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by introducing additional factors that may influence this relationship.

Komlos (1998) emphasizes the interplay between economic processes, structural changes, and physical stature during the Industrial Revolution. He points out the discovery of 8-year height cycles, which suggests that biological growth was influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic conditions (Komlos_1998: p.16). Furthermore, Komlos highlights that population growth, urbanization, income inequality, and income variability are significant aspects of modern economic growth that should be considered alongside institutions (Komlos_1998: p.17).

Similarly, Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that the relationship between economic growth and equality is intertwined, suggesting that policymakers have historically sought to balance these two objectives (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1). They also propose that understanding long-run distribution trends may help explain the causes of economic growth itself (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.9).

Lastly, Koepke & Baten (2005) question the relationship between climate and human living standards, suggesting that it could potentially impact long-run economic growth (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.20). They provide evidence from the Roman Empire, where heights did not increase despite common beliefs of growing living standards during that period (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.16).

These sources thus reshape the argument by suggesting that economic growth is influenced by a complex interplay of factors, including structural changes, population dynamics, income distribution, and environmental conditions. It appears that institutions may not be the sole determinant of long-run economic growth, but rather one of several important factors at play. Further examination of these interactions could provide valuable insights into the mechanisms driving economic development over time. (Komlos_1998: p.2, Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1, Koepke&Baten_2005: p.20)

The relationship between institutions and education as catalysts for long-run economic growth is further elucidated in the works of Austin (2007) and Bolt & van Zanden (2014). These scholars provide nuanced perspectives on the role of educational advancements within institutional frameworks.

Austin (Austin_2007: p.8) asserts that prior to the mid-1920s, railways held a cost advantage over trucks, which could have significant implications for the adoption and dissemination of education in rural areas. However, he also acknowledges that population growth accelerated after 1918 (Austin_2007: p.12), potentially indicating a need for expanded educational infrastructure to accommodate burgeoning student populations.

Bolt & van Zanden (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5) highlight the importance of revisions in growth estimates for various countries, including Germany and Sweden, suggesting that these adjustments may have underestimated the role of institutional improvements and education on long-term economic development. Furthermore, their work emphasizes the significance of early modern economic growth (Bolt & van Zanden_2014: p.15), underscoring the potential for institutions to shape educational opportunities and spur economic progress over time.

These findings complicate the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by illustrating the interplay between institutional development, education, and structural change. Further examination of these interactions could provide valuable insights into the mechanisms driving economic development over time. (Austin_2007: p.8, Bolt & van Zanden_2014: p.5)

The studies by Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), while tangentially related to the primary topic, offer valuable insights that contribute to understanding the relationship between institutions, economic growth, and structural change. Both authors emphasize the importance of institutional factors in shaping economic outcomes.

Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) argues that when labor is tied to land or owners, employer competition ceases, which can hinder economic development. On the other hand, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) highlights how Western impact on African societies extended beyond economic institutions, affecting cultural and social structures as well.

Both scholars converge in their recognition of the crucial role institutions play in economic growth. However, they diverge in their focus; Domar primarily discusses labor institutions within a historical context, while Bohannan explores broader societal changes resulting from Western influence.

Despite these insights, several questions regarding the specific mechanisms linking institutional development, education, and structural change remain unresolved. For instance, it is not clear how cultural shifts, such as those described by Bohannan (1959), interact with economic institutions to drive long-term growth. Furthermore, understanding the causal relationships between these factors and identifying effective strategies for promoting institutional development in different contexts are ongoing challenges.

In light of this analysis, further research should aim to disentangle the complex interplay between institutions, education, and structural change, with a focus on identifying mechanisms that drive economic growth over time. (Austin_2007: p.8, Bolt & van Zanden_2014: p.5)
