LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question addressed in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, a thesis that has garnered significant attention among scholars. The stakes are high as understanding the factors driving economic growth can inform policy decisions and development strategies aimed at improving living standards worldwide.

Supporting the thesis, Austin (2008) argues that institutions play a crucial role in long-term economic growth, particularly in Africa, where historical analyses reveal recurring relationships due to path dependency (Austin_2008: p.1). Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) highlight the relationship between representative government institutions and economic growth, suggesting that these institutions facilitate political freedom and economic progress (North&Weingast_1989: p.30). Acemoglu et al. (2001) also support this view, demonstrating a large effect of institutions on economic performance (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19).

However, not all scholars agree. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate the argument by pointing out that the lack of data on Sub-Saharan Africa before 1950 has kept it from recent debates about long-run growth, raising questions about its economic performance and potential for convergence (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance to the discussion by focusing on the role of African women in economic growth, revealing how their labor has often been subsumed under household production and unaccounted for in policy discussions (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3).

As this brief review illustrates, while many scholars support the thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, others offer critiques and complications. The ongoing debate underscores the need for further research to fully understand the complex interplay between institutions, economic growth, and development in various contexts.

Institutions play a pivotal role in shaping long-run economic growth, as evidenced by various studies. Austin (2008) argues that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-term growth, asserting that they reinforce rather than originate economic disparities between regions (Austin_2008: p.24). This perspective is supported by Acemoglu et al.'s (2001, 2002) research, which demonstrates that institutions have a significant impact on property rights, investment, and economic performance (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3, p.19; AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5).

Abramovitz (1986) also acknowledges the importance of institutions in economic growth, identifying macroeconomic and monetary conditions as factors that encourage capital investment and effective demand (Abramovitz_1986: p.7, p.20). Similarly, Allen (2001) highlights the role of wages and prices in economic development, suggesting that they bear on fundamental issues such as the pace of economic development and the standard of living (Allen_2001: p.1).

The significance of institutions in shaping long-term economic growth is further underscored by various scholarly works. North (1989) posits that the development and enforcement of secure property rights, as well as reduced transaction costs, are crucial factors driving economic growth over a prolonged period (North_1989: p.9). This notion aligns with Pamuk's (2007) assertion that changes in wages and agricultural commercialization during the early modern era significantly contributed to Europe's economic development (Pamuk_2007: p.24, p.5).

North (1989) also emphasizes the importance of institutions in facilitating structural change, suggesting that they can either promote or hinder economic growth based on their ability to encourage renegotiation and alteration (North_1989: p.6). This idea is supported by Nunn's (2008) research, which reveals a relationship between ethnic fractionalization and long-term economic development (Nunn_2008: p.4).

Moreover, Nunn & Wantchekon (2011) demonstrate that both internal and external factors play significant roles in fostering economic growth, with the internal factor being at least twice as influential (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.4). These findings corroborate Avner Greif's (1989) and Luigi Guiso, Paola Sapienza, and Luigi Zingales' (2007a) arguments that social networks and trust among communities can significantly impact economic performance (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.4).

Institutions play a pivotal role in shaping long-run economic growth, as evidenced by various research studies. Kuznets (1973) emphasizes the interplay between economic structure and societal changes, suggesting that rapid shifts in both sectors are closely linked to economic growth (Kuznets_1973: p.4). He further underscores the significance of technological innovations and social adjustments as key factors influencing economic and social structure (Kuznets_1973: p.6, p.11).

North and Weingast (1989) highlight the importance of political institutions in fostering economic growth and market development (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). They argue that representative government, characterized by multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, is integral to economic progress and political freedom (North&Weingast_1989: p.28, p.30).

Mokyr (2005) supports this notion, attributing the West's economic prosperity to intellectual developments that led to a growth of economic reasonableness (Mokyr_2005: p.4, p.56). He emphasizes the role of elements such as individualism, formal law, corporatism, self-governance, and institutionalized rule-making processes in promoting progressive societies (Mokyr_2005: p.56).

These findings collectively strengthen the argument that institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, as they enable the necessary adjustments for technological innovation, foster political freedom, and promote intellectual developments critical to societal progress.

While some argue that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, other perspectives challenge this view by emphasizing the role of economic factors and their impact on environmental degradation during development. For instance, Stern (2004) posits that the Economic Kuznets Curve (EKC) hypothesis suggests economic growth may lead to environmental degradation in the early stages but eventually contribute to environmental improvement (Stern_2004: p.1). Similarly, Simson (2019) highlights how increased state expenditure and deficits during periods of high economic growth can lead to spending inefficiencies, leakages, and costly delays that may hinder sustainable investment levels (Simson_2019: p.4).

These critiques suggest that economic factors such as structural change and government spending patterns could have a significant impact on long-term economic growth, potentially challenging the notion that institutions are the primary drivers of such growth. Further exploration into these economic dynamics and their relationship with environmental degradation may provide valuable insights for understanding the complex interplay between economic development, institutional structures, and societal progress.

The challenge to the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth can be found in the analysis of agriculture and labor during the Industrial Revolution. Clark & Jacks (2007) argue that technological advancements, such as those in the coal industry, played a significant role in response to increases in demand (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.4). This suggests that technological progress might have driven economic growth independently of institutional structures.

Furthermore, Feinstein (1998) contends that changes in farming practices and labor conditions were insufficient before the 1850s and 1860s for a significant impact on economic growth (Feinstein_1998: p.28). This prolonged period of stagnation challenges the idea that institutions, which might have facilitated such changes, were crucial to long-term economic development.

These sources imply that while institutions may have influenced certain aspects of economic growth during the Industrial Revolution, other factors like technological advancements and agricultural practices also played pivotal roles. A more comprehensive understanding of the interplay between these factors could potentially provide valuable insights into the complex relationship between economic development, institutional structures, and societal progress.

The interplay between institutions and economic growth is further illuminated by various studies, each providing nuanced perspectives that complicate the understanding of this relationship. For instance, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) highlight the role of women in African economies, pointing out how their labor often goes unaccounted for due to being subsumed under household production (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). This underscores the importance of considering gender dynamics when examining economic growth, as the contribution of women has been significant yet historically overlooked.

Similarly, Austin (2007) sheds light on the impact of transportation infrastructure on economic development. He notes that until the mid-1920s, railways had a distinct advantage over lorries due to their lower cost (Austin_2007: p.8). This finding underscores the significance of investment in infrastructure as a catalyst for economic growth.

Bolt & van Zanden (2014) provide insights into the role of institutional development across various regions, highlighting that revisions of nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth rates have been made for several countries, including Germany, Italy, Russia, and Bulgaria (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5). This suggests that institutional structures vary significantly even within the same region, further complicating the relationship between institutions and economic growth.

Allen et al. (2011) emphasize the role of structural change in economic growth. They demonstrate that income disparities in the early twentieth century were due to long-run stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe (AllenEtAl_2011: p.1). This finding underscores the importance of considering both domestic and international factors when examining economic growth, as the success or stagnation of one country can significantly impact others.

The concept of modern economic growth, as defined by Kuznets, is crucial in this discussion (Komlos_1998: p.2). However, it's essential to acknowledge that the timing and definition of this growth period are subjects of ongoing debate. Furthermore, Komlos emphasizes the significant influence of short-term economic fluctuations on biological growth processes, such as height, during the Industrial Revolution (Komlos_1998: p.16).

Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that the relationship between economic growth and income inequality is complex and bidirectional. They suggest that understanding long-run distribution trends is intertwined with the search for explanations of growth itself (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.9). Moreover, they propose that policymakers often face trade-offs between economic growth and equality, necessitating an enormous sample of long-term national data for empirical analysis (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1).

Koepke & Baten's research highlights the intriguing relationship between climate and human living standards over extended periods (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.20). Interestingly, they find that during the Roman Empire, heights did not increase despite common beliefs suggesting economic growth and improved living standards (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.16). This finding underscores the need to consider various factors, including climate, in understanding long-term economic growth patterns.

These sources complicate the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by emphasizing the role of short-term fluctuations, income inequality, and environmental factors in shaping economic development over time. Further examination of these interrelated factors can provide a more nuanced understanding of the complex relationship between institutions, economic structures, and long-run growth.

The relationship between institutions, economic structures, and long-run growth is further complicated by the role of industrialization and technological advancements. According to Goldin (2006), the study of labor was once focused on the institutions of the labor market, but a shift in perspective towards revolution rather than evolution sheds new light on this relationship. Industrialization, driven by technological advancements, can significantly alter labor markets and economic structures, thus influencing long-term growth.

Frankema (2012) supports this notion by highlighting the impact of colonial education on long-run growth disparities in Africa. The former British colonies experienced faster growth due to their educational lead, suggesting that industrialization, driven by education, played a crucial role in shaping economic development. However, Frankema also notes that the degree of British "liberalism" was endogenous to the local political and economic context, implying that the relationship between institutions and industrialization is not one-dimensional.

Hopkins (2019) adds to this discourse by discussing the crumbling of Africa's economic base during the period of the slave trade. As the economic base weakened, the superstructure assumed a new importance. This shift suggests that while industrialization and technological advancements are essential for long-run growth, the role of institutions in shaping these advancements cannot be overlooked.

The literature reviewed suggests a consensus that institutions play a significant role in fostering long-run economic growth, although the specific aspects of these institutions remain varied. Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) both acknowledge the importance of institutions in shaping labor markets and competition among employers, with Domar emphasizing the impact of labor being tied to land or owners, while Bohannan highlights the role of social structures in limiting labor mobility.

Both scholars also touch upon the influence of external factors, such as the Western impact on economic institutions (Bohannan_1959: p.10) and the economic cost of slaveholding in the Cotton Belt (Domar_1970: p.16). However, they do not delve deeply into the specific mechanisms through which these external factors interact with local institutions to drive growth.

Despite some overlap in their perspectives, a significant area of unresolved debate concerns the precise nature and role of institutions in economic growth. For instance, while both scholars agree on the importance of institutions in shaping labor markets, they do not provide a clear understanding of how these institutions contribute to technological advancements or industrialization, which are also crucial for long-run growth.
