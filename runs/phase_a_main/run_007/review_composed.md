The debate on the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa, has been a subject of intense scholarly discussion. The central question at hand is whether institutions are indeed the primary driver of economic growth over the long term, and if so, what implications does this have for understanding and addressing economic disparities between regions.

Supporting this thesis, Austin (2008) argues that institutions play a crucial role in shaping economic growth, as they influence trade patterns and reinforce existing economic positions. Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2001) assert that institutions determine property rights and investment, which significantly impact economic progress.

On the other hand, Kuznets (1973) supports the notion of institutions being a key factor in long-run growth but also emphasizes the importance of social innovations alongside technological advancements. Komlos (1998) further complicates the discussion by focusing on the interplay between biological growth and economic processes during the Industrial Revolution.

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) introduce a nuanced perspective, acknowledging the importance of institutions in economic growth but also pointing out the lack of data for sub-Saharan Africa before 1950, which limits our understanding of its long-term economic performance. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) contribute to the conversation by discussing the role of women's labor in economic growth and development, highlighting the need to account for their contributions that are often overlooked.

As these scholars delve into the complexities of long-run economic growth, it becomes clear that institutions play a significant role but may not be the sole factor. The stakes are high, as understanding the drivers of economic growth can inform policies aimed at promoting development and reducing disparities between regions. This debate continues to evolve, with scholars exploring various aspects of institutional development, their impact on economic growth, and the interplay with other factors such as technology, demographics, and social structures.

The role of institutions in economic growth is further emphasized by scholars who explore their impact during colonial periods, suggesting that strong institutions were crucial for fostering long-term economic development. For instance, Kuznets (1973) posits that the succession of technological innovations and social innovations are significant factors affecting economic and social structure, highlighting the importance of institutional adjustments in utilizing modern technology effectively (Kuznets_1973: p.6).

Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) argue that political institutions played a necessary role in making possible economic growth and political freedom. They contend that absolutist states, such as early modern Spain, created economic conditions that retarded long-run economic growth due to their lack of constraints on centralized enforcement mechanisms (North&Weingast_1989: p.7).

Mokyr (2005) suggests that the change in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West must be explained through developments in the intellectual realm concerning "useful knowledge." He argues that many elements of a progressive society, such as individualism, man-made formal law, corporatism, self governance, and rules determined through an institutionalized process, contributed to economic growth (Mokyr_2005: p.56).

In addition, North (1989) discusses the importance of institutions in reducing transaction costs and increasing security rights, which are crucial for the development of markets and long-term economic growth (North_1989: p.9). Lastly, Ogilvie (2007) notes that economists have started to bring institutions inside the tent of economic analysis, recognizing their interdependency within a wider framework (Ogilvie_2007: p.27).

These sources collectively support the thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by demonstrating their role in fostering technological innovations, facilitating economic adjustments, and reducing transaction costs during colonial periods. The evidence from these studies suggests that strong institutions can play a critical role in promoting economic growth and development over the long term.

Institutions play a crucial role in fostering long-term economic growth, particularly in the realm of education. Austin (2008) posits that institutions are indeed 'the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth' (Austin_2008: p.26). He further supports this claim by arguing that certain trade relationships reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe, highlighting the persisting impact of institutions on economic development (Austin_2008: p.24).

Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2001) assert that more fundamental institutions significantly affect property rights and economic progress, with detrimental institutions inhibiting investment (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). They also contend that differences in mortality rates are not the primary cause of institutional variations, emphasizing the importance of examining other factors influencing institutional development (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.3).

Hopkins (2009) supports this perspective by noting that ethnic fragmentation is often cited as a cause for unproductive institutions, but requires further evidence to discount other causes of solidarity and motives for action (Hopkins_2009: p.22). Furthermore, Hopkins (2009) points out that the decay of economic history in general contributed to diminishing interest in African economic history, which could have significant implications for understanding institutional development and its impact on long-term growth (Hopkins_2009: p.4).

The notion that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth faces criticism, particularly in relation to colonialism and its impact on African economies. Stern (2004) argues that economic growth may lead to environmental degradation in the early stages, which could potentially challenge the emphasis on institutions as the fundamental driver of long-term growth. Similarly, McCloskey (2015) contends that ideologies and ideologies, not institutions, significantly influenced economic growth in certain regions, such as France and Spain, during crucial periods.

Furthermore, Simson (2019) suggests that state expenditure and deficits expanded in the 1960s and 1970s, a time of high economic growth, in Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda. This evidence complicates the argument that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth, as it indicates that government spending could also play a significant role.

However, Bryceson (2002) and Humphries (2013) provide insights into how traditional hierarchical orders and family structures in African communities may have influenced women's economic and social spheres, potentially impacting the development of institutions and economic growth. These findings challenge the assertion that institutions are the sole cause of long-run economic growth, suggesting a more complex interplay between societal structures and economic development.

The literature on institutions and economic growth further complicates the assertion that institutions are solely responsible for long-run economic development. Several studies suggest that other factors, such as colonialism, climate, and initial conditions, may also play significant roles in shaping economic trajectories.

For instance, Komlos (1998) argues that physical stature during the Industrial Revolution was significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic processes and associated structural changes (p.16). This implies that economic growth might not only be driven by institutions but also by factors like population growth, urbanization, and income inequality – all of which were prevalent during the Industrial Revolution.

Similarly, Temin (2002) posits that economic growth slowed due to the exhaustion of cheap labor, suggesting that resource availability could be another crucial determinant of long-term economic development (p.4). Moreover, he argues that the end of the Golden Age was brought about by the return to equilibrium growth paths rather than adverse economic shocks in the 1970s (p.14).

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) explore how initial conditions and factor endowments could have profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth (p.4). They suggest that the divergence in paths can be traced back to the achievement of sustained economic growth by the United States and Canada during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, while others did not manage to attain this goal until late in the nineteenth century (p.12).

Koepke & Baten (2005) highlight the relationship between climate and human living standards, suggesting that climatic conditions might influence long-run economic development (p.20). For example, their study of heights during the Roman Empire found no increase in living standards over the period, contrasting with the common view that economic growth occurred during this time (p.16).

Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that explaining long-run distribution trends is closely related to the search for explanations of growth itself (p.9). They suggest that Japan's economic history supports a correlation between imbalance and inequality, implying that these factors might be intertwined in the long-term development process (p.14).

Finally, Hopkins (2019) discusses how postmodernism removed independent African viewpoints from the study of long-run economic and political change, potentially limiting the diversity of perspectives on this topic (p.4). This suggests that societal structures beyond institutions might also shape economic development narratives.

Taken together, these studies complicate the idea that institutions are the sole cause of long-run economic growth by highlighting the potential roles of colonialism, climate, initial conditions, and societal structures in shaping economic trajectories. Future research should continue to explore the complex interplay between these factors to better understand the drivers of long-term economic development.

The role of institutions in shaping long-run economic growth is further nuanced by examining their impact on labor markets, as suggested by various scholars. Broadberry and Gardner (2022) assert that Sub-Saharan Africa's absence from recent debates about comparative long-term growth is due to the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950. They also note that population growth was faster after 1918, which could have implications for labor market dynamics and growth trajectories (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.12).

Austin (2007) highlights the importance of transportation infrastructure, such as railways, in shaping economic growth. He argues that until the mid-1920s, railways had a significant cost advantage over lorries, which could have influenced labor market efficiency and productivity (Austin_2007: p.8). Additionally, Austin discusses the population growth after 1918, suggesting potential labor market implications for economic development (Austin_2007: p.12).

Bolt and van Zanden (2014) also emphasize the role of institutions in shaping long-run economic growth by examining revisions of nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century growth data for various countries, such as Germany, Sweden, and Russia. These revisions may help to better understand institutional factors that influenced labor markets and overall economic development (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5).

Clark and Jacks (2007) focus on the coal industry during the Industrial Revolution, demonstrating how technological advances in ventilation allowed for a response to increases in demand, thereby shaping labor market dynamics and contributing to economic growth (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.4).

The perspective of Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) complicates the understanding of institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by highlighting the significant, yet often overlooked, role of women in African economies. The authors argue that the contribution of African women to economic growth and development has been increasingly recognized, yet their labor is frequently subsumed under household production and remains unaccounted for in formal policy (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3).

This oversight undermines policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles. The authors emphasize that the major historical developments in West Africa, which find parallels elsewhere in Africa, demonstrate the importance of considering gender in discussions about economic growth (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.4). They contend that understanding these processes is crucial for creating effective policies that foster women's economic empowerment and contribute to sustainable long-term growth.

Moreover, Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) assert that the contribution of African women to economic growth extends beyond household production, encompassing various sectors such as agriculture, trade, and manufacturing (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.19). This finding underscores the importance of acknowledging and valuing women's work in diverse economic activities to fully appreciate their role in long-run economic growth.

In sum, Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) offer valuable insights into the complexities of institutions as drivers of long-run economic growth by emphasizing the significant yet often overlooked contributions of African women. Their work underscores the need for policymakers to account for gender in their analyses and to create policies that empower women economically, ultimately contributing to sustainable long-term growth.

Scholars Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), though focusing on different contexts, share a common ground in recognizing the significant role of institutions in promoting economic growth. Both authors emphasize that when labor is tied to land or ownership, competition among employers ceases, stifling economic growth (Domar_1970: p.4). Bohannan (1959) further notes that this can be seen in the slave-trading societies he studied, where limited resources and social structures hindered economic development (Bohannan_1959: p.7).

However, they diverge in their focus. Domar's work primarily concentrates on factors of production, highlighting how institutions can impact labor and capital markets (Domar_1970: p.4). In contrast, Bohannan delves into the cultural aspects of African societies, illustrating the influence of social structures on economic growth (Bohannan_1959: p.10).

Despite their insights, several issues remain unresolved. For instance, Domar's work does not explicitly address gender dynamics in institutions, which is a crucial factor in understanding long-run economic growth (North&Weingast_1989: p.28). Similarly, Bohannan's focus on cultural aspects leaves room for further exploration of how specific institutional arrangements can lead to sustainable growth.
