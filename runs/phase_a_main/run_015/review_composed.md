The central question under investigation in this literature review is whether institutions are indeed the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth. This question holds significant implications for understanding the economic development trajectories of various regions and societies, particularly in Africa.

Austin (2008) supports the assertion that institutions play a crucial role in driving long-term economic growth by emphasizing their impact on economic structures and historical relationships. For instance, Austin (Austin_2008: p.26) argues, "Institutions as the fundamental cause of long-rungrowth." Kuznets (1973), too, supports this view by highlighting the relationship between technological innovations, social innovations, and economic structure in promoting growth.

On the other hand, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate the argument by pointing out the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 for Sub-Saharan Africa, which has been absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance to the discussion by focusing on the contribution of African women to economic growth and development, revealing how their labor is often subsumed under household production, undermining policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles.

North & Weingast (1989) provide support for the thesis by demonstrating that representative government institutions with multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs are intimately related to economic growth and political freedom. Acemoglu et al. (2001) also support this view, showing a large effect of institutions on economic performance and highlighting how certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress.

However, the debate is far from settled, with many scholars offering critiques and adding qualifications to the central argument. The stakes are high, as understanding the role of institutions in long-term economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting development and reducing inequality. This literature review continues to explore these complexities in depth, examining additional sources to gain a more comprehensive understanding of this crucial question.

The centrality of institutions in long-run economic growth is further substantiated by various scholars, as evidenced in Acemoglu et al.'s (2002) work that underscores the significance of institutional changes in shaping prosperity distributions over the past 500 years. They posit that the reversal in economic prosperity distribution is linked to institutions, suggesting a direct relationship between the two factors (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5). This assertion supports the notion that institutional development plays a crucial role in long-term economic growth, as it sheds light on how changes in institutions can lead to shifts in prosperity distribution.

Furthermore, deVries (1994) emphasizes the importance of Europe's urban network and commercial interaction systems in establishing regional economic development frameworks conducive for industrial growth. The author underscores the British Industrial Revolution as a monumental achievement in economic history, implying that the development of institutions played a pivotal role in fostering this transformation (deVries_1994: p.2). This perspective supports the idea that institutional development is indeed a fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

vanZanden (2009) also contributes to this discourse by focusing on the skill premium and its impact on economic growth. The author suggests that the skill premium affects the relative price of capital goods, which in turn influences long-term economic growth (vanZanden_2009: p.27). This finding supports the argument that institutions, by influencing factors such as the skill premium, play a critical role in driving long-run economic growth.

The commercialization of agriculture post-Black Death played a significant role in fostering long-term economic growth, further supporting the argument that institutions are fundamental to this process. According to Pamuk (2007), the Black Death led to substantial changes in wages and incomes across Europe, paving the way for the growing commercialization of agriculture (Pamuk_2007: p.4). This shift towards a more market-oriented agricultural system is crucial as it facilitated the development of institutions that encouraged trade and economic growth.

Similarly, Nunn (2008) highlights that the Black Death led to increased ethnic fractionalization in Europe, which has been linked to long-term economic growth (Easterly & Levine, 1997). In a time of uncertainty and insecurity, individuals required wealthier neighbors to provide protection. This need for security likely led to the development of stronger institutions, such as legal systems and property rights, which are essential for fostering economic growth (Nunn_2008: p.4).

Moreover, the decline in Europe's population after the Black Death (Pamuk_2007: p.5) led to a shortage of labor, increasing the demand for agricultural products and driving up their prices. This price increase made it more profitable for farmers to produce surpluses for sale, further promoting the commercialization of agriculture.

The reinforcement of trade between Africa and Western Europe further supports the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. Austin (2008) asserts that these trades, such as the Atlantic one, reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe (Austin_2008: p.24). This suggests that the institutional structures in place during this period significantly influenced the economic outcomes, with Africa's institutions being less conducive to growth than those in Western Europe.

Austin (2008) also emphasizes the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history, revealing relationships that recur due to similar situations and path dependencies (Austin_2008: p.1). This underscores the idea that institutions, which are deeply rooted in historical contexts, play a crucial role in shaping economic trajectories over the long run.

Moreover, Austin (2008) highlights the logical economy of the argument that institutions are 'the' fundamental cause of long-run economic growth (Austin_2008: p.24). The theoretical conclusion drawn by AJR (Acemoglu, Johnson, and Robinson_2002), which is supported by Austin's analysis, suggests a strong correlation between institutional quality and economic growth.

The work of Simson (2019) challenges the notion that institutions are the primary driver of long-run economic growth, particularly in East Africa during the 1960s and 1970s. Instead, Simson emphasizes the significance of industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy.

For instance, Simson (Simson_2019: p.25) notes that state expenditure and deficits expanded in East Africa during a period of high economic growth in the 1960s and 1970s. This suggests that these factors might have played a crucial role in driving the region's economic growth, rather than institutional quality as posited by Acemoglu, Johnson, and Robinson (2002).

Simson (Simson_2019: p.4) further argues that while there were undoubtedly spending inefficiencies and delays to reform, it is questionable whether their elimination would have been enough to sustain the investment levels of the 1960s or reorient these economies. This implies that factors beyond institutional quality might have contributed to the economic growth observed during this period.

Moreover, Simson (Simson_2019: p.27) highlights the emphasis placed on expanding public employment rather than protecting those already in government jobs, which could indicate that employment policies might have played a role in driving economic growth.

However, the debate is not yet settled, and further investigation into the specific roles of industrial development, educational expansion, military autonomy, and employment policies in East Africa's economic growth during the 1960s and 1970s is necessary to fully understand this complex issue.

Despite the focus on East Africa's economic growth during the 1960s and 1970s, it is essential to acknowledge that Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA) has been underrepresented in discussions about long-term economic growth due to a lack of data before 1950 (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). This revelation complicates the argument by suggesting that a more comprehensive understanding of SSA's economic history is necessary to fully grasp its growth patterns and potential for convergence with other regions.

Moreover, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) note that African economic performance has been historically poor compared to other regions (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). This observation underscores the importance of investigating whether the growth observed in East Africa during the 1960s and 1970s was a temporary phenomenon or a step towards sustainable economic development.

Furthermore, understanding the risks of economic shrinkage, which has hindered progress in the past, is crucial for determining if the growth witnessed in East Africa provides a path to convergence (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.18). This perspective adds nuance to the thesis by emphasizing the need to consider potential challenges that may arise during the process of economic development.

Lastly, new contributions to historical national accounting have provided insights into growth and development patterns in other regions for centuries (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1). This finding suggests that a similar approach could potentially yield valuable insights into SSA's long-term economic growth, including the role of institutions as a fundamental cause.

The work of Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) adds nuance to the argument that institutions are a fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, particularly in Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA). They highlight the significant yet often overlooked contributions of African women to the region's economy, which have been historically subsumed under household production and thus unaccounted for in formal analyses (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3).

This oversight is problematic as it undermines policies aimed at strengthening women's economic roles. Akyeampong and Fofack argue that understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution has been marginalized is crucial for devising effective strategies to promote gender equality and economic growth (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3).

Moreover, they demonstrate that the economic provision of African women was largely dependent on their husbands, further complicating the relationship between gender roles and economic growth in SSA (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.9). This finding suggests that institutions shaping gender dynamics may have played a significant role in hindering long-term economic growth by limiting women's economic autonomy and participation.

Interestingly, Akyeampong and Fofack note parallels between West Africa and other regions in Africa where gender and economic growth are concerned (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.4). This finding underscores the need for a comprehensive understanding of the role of institutions across diverse African contexts, as similar patterns may contribute to long-term economic stagnation.

The literature on modern economic growth further substantiates the role of institutions in long-term economic development, particularly as it pertains to industrialization. Komlos (1998) delves into the Industrial Revolution and its impact on physical stature, providing nuance to the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

Komlos (Komlos_1998: p.2) highlights that modern economic growth is used in a Kuznetsian sense, which emphasizes structural change as a key component of economic development. This perspective aligns with the thesis, as institutional changes often drive structural transformations and spur long-term growth.

Furthermore, Komlos (Komlos_1998: p.16) discusses 8-year height cycles similar to classical business cycles, implying that biological growth processes were significantly influenced by short-term economic fluctuations. This finding underscores the interconnectedness of institutions, industrialization, and long-run economic growth.

Additionally, Komlos (Komlos_1998: p.17) notes the association between structural changes such as population growth, urbanization, income inequality, and variability with the onset of modern economic growth during the Industrial Revolution. These observations support the argument that institutions play a crucial role in shaping industrialization and driving long-term economic growth.

The works of Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959) offer valuable insights into the relationship between institutions, economic growth, and structural change during the Industrial Revolution. Both scholars acknowledge the crucial role of institutions in shaping industrialization and driving long-term economic growth.

Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) emphasizes that with labor tied to land or owners, competition among employers ceases, which can hinder economic development. He also discusses the economic implications of slaveholding in the Cotton Belt, suggesting that such institutions could impede growth due to their exploitative nature (Phillips, "The Economic Cost of Slaveholding in the Cotton Belt," Pol., cited in Domar_1970: p.16). However, he also acknowledges that factors other than institutions may contribute to economic growth ("Domar among the factors of production," Domar_1970: p.4).

Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.10) discusses how the Western impact was not limited to economic institutions but also affected social structures and cultural practices, which could indirectly influence economic growth. He notes that the arrival of British traders led to changes in the Tiv society's mobility patterns, potentially facilitating trade and economic development ("Before the arrival of the British, one did not venture far beyond the area of one's kinsmen or special friends," Bohannan_1959: p.10). However, he also highlights that certain institutional aspects, such as gender imbalance, could limit economic opportunities (Bohannan_1959: p.13).

While both scholars agree on the importance of institutions in shaping industrialization and driving long-term economic growth, they diverge in their focus. Domar's work primarily focuses on labor markets and exploitative institutions like slavery, while Bohannan expands the discussion to include social structures and cultural practices.

Despite these insights, several questions remain unresolved. For instance, how do specific institutional arrangements impact economic growth differently across regions? What role do informal institutions play in shaping industrialization and long-term economic growth? Addressing these questions could provide a more nuanced understanding of the relationship between institutions and economic growth.
