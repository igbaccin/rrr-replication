LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The question of what drives long-run economic growth has been a subject of intense debate among economists, historians, and political scientists. One prominent theory posits that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-term economic growth (Austin_2008: p.26). This perspective suggests that the structure of governance, property rights, and economic incentives significantly influence a country's ability to achieve sustained economic development.

Support for this theory can be found in various works. For instance, Kuznets (Kuznets_1973: p.4) argues that high aggregate growth rates are associated with rapid changes in economic structure, which in turn lead to changes in other aspects of society. Similarly, North & Weingast (North&Weingast_1989: p.2) emphasize the importance of political institutions in enabling economic growth and the development of markets. Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.19) also provide evidence that differences in fundamental institutions have a large effect on economic performance.

However, not all agree that institutions are the sole determinant of long-run economic growth. Broadberry & Gardner (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1) argue that the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has prevented Sub-Saharan Africa from being part of recent debates about comparative long-run growth. Akyeampong & Fofack (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3) complicate this perspective by pointing out that understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution becomes subsumed under household production is crucial for policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles.

As the debate continues, it is clear that institutions play a significant role in long-run economic growth. However, understanding the complex interplay between institutions, historical circumstances, and other factors remains a challenging task for scholars. Future research should aim to disentangle these relationships to provide a more nuanced understanding of the causes of long-term economic development.

The relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth is a prominent theme in contemporary economic scholarship. North & Weingast (1989) assert that the fundamental institutions of representative government, such as multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are intimately related to economic growth and political freedom. They argue that these institutions facilitate economic exchange by providing a framework for enforcement and change of rules, thereby fostering long-term economic development.

Acemoglu et al. (2001) support this perspective by demonstrating that more fundamental institutions, particularly those related to property rights, significantly impact economic performance. Their research reveals that detrimental institutions were harmful to investment and economic progress. Moreover, they find a large effect of institutions on economic outcomes, suggesting their importance in long-term development.

Whatley (2018) provides further evidence by examining the role of gunpowder and slave exports in economic growth. He finds that differences in these factors are not the only cause of variation in institutions but contribute to their development and subsequent impact on economic performance.

Nunn (2008) supports the institutional view by investigating the relationship between ethnic fractionalization, uncertainty, and long-term economic growth. His findings suggest that during periods of insecurity, individuals required stronger institutions to mitigate the negative effects of ethnic diversity on economic development.

Nunn & Wantchekon (2011) complement these studies by demonstrating that both internal and external factors play a role in shaping institutional development. Their research indicates that the internal channel, which refers to the impact of institutions on economic growth through their influence on social norms and trust, is at least twice as large as the external channel, which encompasses the effects of geography and trade on institutions.

The assertion that institutions are a fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is further supported by various scholars. Douglas North (1989) posits that the development and enforcement of institutions, such as Spain's centralized mechanisms, significantly contribute to economic growth by reducing transaction costs and increasing security rights (North_1989: p.1, p.6, p.9). Simultaneously, Simon Kuznets (Abramovitz_1986) acknowledges that the evolution of mixed economies is a function of economic growth itself, suggesting that institutions play a crucial role in fostering productivity and capital investment (Abramovitz_1986: p.7, p.20, p.22).

Moreover, Broadberry & Gupta (2006) highlight the importance of service sectors in economic growth during both early modern and contemporary periods, implying that institutions facilitating this sector's development could potentially drive long-term economic expansion (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.9). Similarly, Broadberry et al. (2015) emphasize the significance of India's institutional framework in shaping its long-run economic performance (BroadberryEtal_2015: p.2).

Lastly, Peters (2004) contends that cultural modes of understanding and social organization, which are shaped by institutions, play a crucial role in promoting sustainable land management, reducing poverty, and fostering economic growth (Peters_2004: p.7, p.16). These findings collectively suggest that institutions not only influence but are indeed fundamental to long-run economic growth. However, further examination is necessary to understand the specific mechanisms through which this impact occurs.

The impact of colonialism on institutions and their role in long-run economic growth is a crucial aspect to consider when discussing the fundamental cause of economic development. Austin (2008) argues that trades, such as the Atlantic one, reinforced rather than originated Africa's relatively poor economic position compared to Western Europe (Austin_2008: p.24). This suggests that colonialism may have negatively influenced the establishment and evolution of institutions in colonized regions, which in turn could have hindered long-term economic growth.

Acemoglu et al. (2002) provide evidence supporting this notion by demonstrating a change in the distribution of economic prosperity over time, which they attribute to institutions (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.6). They question whether the reversal in economic fortunes between regions is related to institutions, implying that colonialism may have played a role in shaping these institutions and thus long-term economic growth.

Hopkins (2009) further supports this argument by claiming that ethnic fragmentation is not the sole cause of unproductive institutions, but rather other factors, such as the influence of colonial powers, should also be considered (Hopkins_2009: p.22). This suggests that the impact of colonialism on institutions and their subsequent role in long-run economic growth is a complex issue requiring further investigation.

The claim that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth is challenged by evidence suggesting that other factors, such as industrial development and government spending, may have played significant roles in certain regions and periods.

For instance, Simson (2019) highlights that in Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda, state expenditure and deficits expanded during a period of high economic growth in the 1960s and 1970s (Simson_2019: p.25). While there were undoubtedly spending inefficiencies and delays to reform, it is questioned whether their elimination would have been enough to sustain the investment levels of that time or reorient these economies (Simson_2019: p.4).

Furthermore, Humphries (2013) argues that eighteenth-century economic growth in Britain was driven by a growing division of labor, workshop production, and the eventual emergence of the factory system (Humphries_2013: p.18). The relative needs of women and children were also crucial factors in this period (Humphries_2013: p.11, p.19).

Lastly, van Zanden (1999) critiques the consensus view that the early modern economy was unable to generate economic growth. Instead, he suggests that in the mid-eighteenth century, there was still a negative link between an 'output' measure of standard of living and the level of economic development (vanZanden_1999: p.13).

These findings suggest that while institutions may be important, other factors such as industrial development, government spending, and demographic changes also significantly influenced economic growth in various contexts. Further research should explore these factors in greater detail to gain a more comprehensive understanding of the drivers of long-run economic growth.

Institutional factors, while undeniably crucial in driving long-run economic growth, may not be the sole determinant of such growth. The works of Broadberry & Gardner (2022), Lindert & Williamson (1985), Sokoloff & Engerman (2000), Koepke & Baten (2005), and Skocpol & Sommers (1980) complicate the thesis by shedding light on additional factors that have contributed to or influenced economic growth in various contexts.

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) highlight the absence of Sub-Saharan Africa from recent debates about long-run growth due to the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950, suggesting that this region could potentially offer new insights into the discussion of long-run economic growth.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that the relationship between economic growth and inequality is interconnected, implying that understanding the distribution trends might provide a more comprehensive explanation for long-run growth itself.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) propose that initial conditions or factor endowments could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth, complicating the argument by suggesting that geographical and environmental factors may also play significant roles in shaping economic trajectories.

Koepke & Baten (2005) emphasize the intriguing relationship between climate and human living standards, implying that natural environmental conditions might have influenced long-run economic growth in various societies.

Skocpol & Sommers (1980) demonstrate the importance of comparing different cases to highlight particularities and increase the visibility of specific structures in historical analysis, suggesting that comparative studies could offer valuable insights into the drivers of long-run economic growth.

The works of Komlos (1998), Austin (2007), and Allen et al. (2011) provide additional perspectives on the role of institutions in long-run economic growth, particularly focusing on industrialization.

Komlos (1998) emphasizes that the concept of modern economic growth, as used in this context, refers to a Kuznetsian sense, which includes rapid population growth, urbanization, and income inequality (Komlos_1998: p.2). He also points out that biological growth processes during the Industrial Revolution were significantly influenced by short-term fluctuations in economic conditions, suggesting a complex interplay between economic and biological factors (Komlos_1998: p.16).

Austin (2007) contributes to this discussion by highlighting the role of transportation infrastructure, specifically railways, in the early stages of industrialization. He notes that until the mid-1920s, railways had a significant cost advantage over lorries, contributing to economic growth (Austin_2007: p.8). However, he also acknowledges that not all capital-deepening and TFP-improving forces of modern economic growth were present in some countries, suggesting variation in the drivers of industrialization (Austin_2007: p.16).

Allen et al. (2011) further complicate the argument by demonstrating income disparities between China and Europe during the early 20th century, which were due to long-run stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe (AllenEtAl_2011: p.1). They also show that rapid growth in the German economy translated into rising real wages for workers, indicating a correlation between structural change and economic growth (AllenEtAl_2011: p.20).

These studies suggest that while institutions play a fundamental role in long-run economic growth, the specific structures and factors driving this growth may vary significantly across different regions and time periods. Comparative studies can offer valuable insights into these variations and help to refine our understanding of the drivers of industrialization and modern economic growth.

The role of institutions in shaping labor markets and fostering long-run economic growth is further illuminated in various studies. Goldin (2006) highlights the evolution and shift from revolution in women's labor market participation, indicating that the distinction between these concepts is crucial to understanding their choices and decisions (Goldin_2006: p.1). Furthermore, Goldin points out the significance of legal changes on labor markets, providing an empirical analysis of their short- and long-run effects (Goldin_2006: p.14).

In a similar vein, Williamson (1996) underscores the impact of labor market glutting due to mass migrations, which slowed down real wage growth in receiving countries (Williamson_1996: p.17). Additionally, understanding sources of convergence is fundamental to comprehending policy responses, as highlighted by Williamson (1996) (Williamson_1996: p.25).

Feinstein (1998) emphasizes the role of institutions in rural areas, where changes in farming were insufficient before the 1850s and 1860s to disperse the labor glut (Feinstein_1998: p.28). Furthermore, Feinstein discusses the long plateau in economic growth that is consistent with other economic, political, and demographic indicators (Feinstein_1998: p.2).

These studies complicate the argument by demonstrating that institutions play a crucial role in shaping labor markets and fostering long-run economic growth. However, they also suggest that the specific aspects of these institutions, such as legal changes or glutting labor markets due to migrations, can significantly vary across different regions and time periods.

The central argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is supported by both Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), albeit with some variations in their perspectives. Both scholars acknowledge the crucial role of institutions, particularly those related to labor markets, in fostering economic development.

Domar (1970) emphasizes the impact of institutional arrangements on competition among employers. He argues that when labor is tied to land or an owner, as in the Russian peasants' enserfment and the American South's slaveholding, competition ceases, leading to stagnant growth (Domar_1970: p.4). On the other hand, Bohannan (1959) focuses on the Western impact on African societies, highlighting how the arrival of Europeans disrupted traditional institutions and introduced new ones that facilitated economic growth (Bohannan_1959: p.10).

However, both scholars also acknowledge the significance of regional and temporal variations in institutional development. For instance, Domar discusses the Russian situation prior to peasants' enserfment as a case where labor was not tied to land or an owner, potentially fostering competition (Domar_1970: p.4). Bohannan notes that before the British arrival, African societies had their own institutions and did not venture far beyond their kinship networks, suggesting that these indigenous institutions could have influenced economic growth (Bohannan_1959: p.10).

Despite their insights, both scholars leave some questions unanswered. For instance, Domar's analysis does not delve into the specific legal changes or other institutional aspects that might have contributed to economic growth in regions where labor was not tied to land or an owner. Similarly, Bohannan's work does not explore how the indigenous institutions of African societies influenced their economic development before European contact.
