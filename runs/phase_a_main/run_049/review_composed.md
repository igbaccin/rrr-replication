The question of whether institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth has been a subject of intense debate among economists and historians. This discussion is crucial as it sheds light on the factors that drive economic development and prosperity in different regions over time.

Austin (2008) supports the thesis, asserting that institutions are indeed the primary cause of long-term growth. He argues that these institutions, by shaping economic structures and relationships, have a profound impact on a society's ability to grow and develop. Kuznets (1973) also aligns with this view, emphasizing the role of institutions in facilitating technological innovation and social adjustments necessary for modern economic growth.

On the other hand, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate the argument by pointing out the lack of data on Sub-Saharan Africa's economic performance before 1950, which has resulted in its absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) similarly add nuance to the discussion by highlighting the role of women's economic contributions and the challenges in understanding their impact due to historical underrepresentation.

North & Weingast (1989) provide strong support for the thesis, focusing on the political factors underlying economic growth and the development of markets. They argue that representative government institutions with multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs are crucial for economic growth and political freedom.

Acemoglu et al. (2001) also endorse the thesis, demonstrating a significant effect of institutions on property rights, investment, and economic performance. They argue that detrimental institutions can hinder economic progress, while more favorable ones can foster growth.

The stakes of this debate are high, as understanding the role of institutions in long-run economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting development and reducing poverty in various regions. The discussion continues, with scholars exploring the intricacies of institutional structures and their impact on economic growth, offering insights into potential strategies for fostering sustainable development.

Institutions play a pivotal role in fostering long-run economic growth, as evidenced by numerous scholars' works. Kuznets (1973) highlights the correlation between economic structure changes and rapid growth, noting that these shifts are accompanied by significant societal transformations, such as urbanization and family roles (Kuznets_1973: p.4). Furthermore, he emphasizes the importance of technological innovations in driving both economic and social structure changes (Kuznets_1973: p.6).

Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) focus on political institutions, specifically representative government with multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts, as being essential for economic growth and market development (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). They argue that these institutions enable economic freedom and growth (North&Weingast_1989: p.30).

Mokyr (2005) supports this perspective, asserting that the development of intellectual elements like individualism, man-made formal law, corporatism, self-governance, and rules determined through an institutionalized process contributed to economic growth in the West (Mokyr_2005: p.56). He also suggests that the change in the rate and nature of economic growth in the West can be attributed to developments in the intellectual realm concerning "useful knowledge" (Mokyr_2005: p.4).

These findings underscore the importance of institutions in promoting economic growth, as they not only facilitate technological innovation but also shape societal structures and intellectual development. The interdependency of individual institutions within a broader framework is also crucial, as Ogilvie (2007) points out, emphasizing that the failure of one institution can impact the overall economic system (Ogilvie_2007: p.27).

The role of institutions in shaping agricultural practices and, consequently, long-term economic growth is evident in various studies. Acemoglu et al. (2001) argue that the impact of fundamental institutions on property rights significantly influences economic progress. In their research, they found that institutions that hinder investment and economic advancement are detrimental to a nation's development (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). This underlines the importance of effective agricultural policies and land ownership regulations in fostering economic growth.

Furthermore, Acemoglu et al. (2002) emphasize that institutions are a primary factor in understanding long-term economic development over the past five centuries (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49). They also point out that there has been a shift in the distribution of economic prosperity, which could be linked to institutional changes (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.6). This suggests that agricultural development and economic growth are closely intertwined with institutional frameworks.

Broadberry et al. (2015) also support this perspective, noting that India's long-term economic performance has been a subject of debate for centuries. They propose that the size of armies could serve as an indicator of population size, providing a starting point for their population estimates (BroadberryEtal_2015: p.4). The accurate assessment of population size is crucial in understanding agricultural output and economic growth over time.

Structural change, characterized by shifts in the composition of economic activity from agriculture to industry and services, is a significant factor contributing to long-term economic growth (Allen_2001: p.4). The British Industrial Revolution, for instance, was marked by such structural transformation, leading to an increase in industrial output and subsequently, economic growth (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.9).

Allen (2001) emphasizes the importance of wages as an indicator of economic development, arguing that trends in building craftsmen's wages can provide insights into average earnings over time (Allen_2001: p.4). The author also notes that both positions supporting faster and slower growth rates during the Industrial Revolution can find support in the indices presented, suggesting a complex interplay of factors influencing economic growth (Allen_2001: p.23).

Broadberry & Gupta (2006) further support this argument by highlighting the role of the service sector in economic growth during both early modern and modern periods. They argue that improvements in living standards of north-west Europeans were driven by a falling relative price of manufactured goods rather than just agricultural productivity (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.9). However, they caution against drawing direct comparisons between Asia and Europe based solely on grain wages, as this may overlook other crucial aspects of economic development (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.24).

The perspectives of some scholars challenge the notion that institutions are the primary cause of long-term economic growth. For instance, McCloskey (2015) contends that ideas and ideologies, not interests or institutions, were crucial in shaping economic development. In France, for example, conservative parties thwarted economic growth despite the successes of bourgeois economies in other regions (McCloskey_2015: p.9).

Similarly, Stern (2004) argues that economic growth does not necessarily lead to environmental degradation as some claim. Instead, he suggests that changes in the structure and technology of an economy drive changes in environmental impacts over the course of development (Stern_2004: p.3).

Moreover, Humphries (2013) points out that economic growth in the 18th century was driven by a growing division of labor, the rise of workshop production, and the eventual emergence of the factory system (Humphries_2013: p.18). However, he notes that the relative needs of women and children in this period made it difficult for them to participate fully in this growth (Humphries_2013: p.11, p.19).

Lastly, van Zanden (1999) challenges the consensus view in the 1960s and 1970s that the early modern economy was unable to generate economic growth. He argues that there was a positive link between economic development and the standard of living in mid-18th century Europe, contrary to the pessimistic interpretation of economic growth before the industrial revolution (vanZanden_1999: p.13).

These perspectives suggest that while institutions may play a role in long-term economic growth, other factors such as ideas, ideologies, technology, and social structures also significantly influence economic development.

Structural change, a key aspect of institutional development, does not necessarily lead to economic growth according to Clark and Jacks (2007). They argue that the coal industry's transformation during the Industrial Revolution was primarily driven by technological advancements rather than institutional changes. For instance, they highlight how the coal industry's ability to meet increasing demand was facilitated by technological improvements, not institutional reforms (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.4).

Moreover, Clark and Jacks (2007) critique the cliometric account of the coal industry, suggesting that prices were not accurately represented in the analysis due to deﬂation by a price index for the economy as a whole (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.3). This implies that the economic growth attributed to the coal industry may have been overstated, challenging the notion that institutional development is the primary driver of long-term economic growth.

Furthermore, Clark and Jacks (2007) suggest that the counterfactual for economic analyses often overlooks the role of technological advancements. They argue that the coal industry's response to increases in demand during the Industrial Revolution era was only possible due to these technological leaps (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.27). This implies that while institutions may play a role, technology could be an equally significant factor influencing economic development.

These arguments by Clark and Jacks challenge the thesis that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, suggesting instead that technological advancements and other factors should also be considered in understanding economic development.

The works of Komlos (1998), Temin (2002), Lindert & Williamson (1985), Sokoloff & Engerman (2000), Koepke & Baten (2005), and Frankema (2012) offer nuanced perspectives on the role of institutions in long-run economic growth.

Komlos (1998) emphasizes the influence of structural changes during the Industrial Revolution, including population growth, urbanization, and income inequality, on the biological growth process. This suggests that while institutions may have played a role, they were not the sole determinant of long-run economic growth, as other factors such as demographic shifts also had significant impacts.

Temin (2002) posits that the exhaustion of cheap labor caused economic growth to slow, implying that technological advancements and labor market dynamics may have played a more prominent role in shaping long-term economic trajectories than previously thought. He further argues that the end of the Golden Age was brought about by a return to equilibrium growth paths rather than adverse economic shocks in the 1970s, complicating the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) suggest that the relationship between economic growth and equality is intertwined, implying a potential causal link between the two. They argue that policymakers must consider trade-offs between economic growth and equality, which complicates the argument that institutions are the sole cause of long-run economic growth as it suggests that income distribution may also play a role in shaping economic trajectories.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) explore the possibility that initial conditions or factor endowments could have had profound and enduring impacts on long-run economic growth, challenging the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of such growth. They argue that the divergence in paths can be traced back to the achievement of sustained economic growth by the United States and Canada during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, while others did not manage to attain this goal until late in the nineteenth century. This suggests that other factors, such as factor endowments, may have played a more significant role in shaping long-run economic growth than previously thought.

Koepke & Baten (2005) investigate the relationship between climate and human living standards, suggesting that environmental conditions may also have played a role in shaping long-run economic growth. For example, they find that over the period of the Roman Empire, heights did not increase at all, contrasting with the common view that living standards increased during this time due to economic growth. This complicates the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth as it suggests that environmental conditions may also have played a role in shaping economic trajectories.

Frankema (2012) argues that colonial education is the key to explaining long-run growth disparities in Africa, suggesting that educational policies implemented by former colonial powers may have had lasting impacts on economic growth. This complicates the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth as it suggests that factors such as educational policies may also play a significant role in shaping economic trajectories.

The assertion that institutions are the primary driver of long-term economic growth becomes more complex when considering the role of structural change, as suggested by Bolt & van Zanden (2014). They argue that revisions to the growth rates of several countries, such as Switzerland, Germany, Sweden, Italy, Greece, Russia, and Bulgaria, do not fundamentally alter the underlying argument but add nuance to it (Bolt&vanZanden_2014: p.5). Furthermore, van der Eng's research on Indonesia's economic growth from 1880 to 2008 highlights the significance of structural change in shaping a nation's economic trajectory (van der Eng_2010: unspecified pages).

Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) also contribute to this discourse by emphasizing the interconnected nature of economies. They argue that the strategies of one economy can significantly impact the strategies of others, suggesting a complex web of interactions that may be influenced by institutions but are not solely determined by them (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.21). This perspective complicates the argument that institutions are the sole fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, as it implies that other factors, such as interdependencies between economies, may also play a significant role in shaping economic trajectories.

The argument that institutions are the sole fundamental cause of long-run economic growth becomes more nuanced when considering the role of industrialization and economic growth, as evidenced by Austin (2007) and Allen et al. (2011).

Austin (Austin_2007: p.8) suggests that the railway had a fundamental advantage over lorries in cost-effectiveness until the mid-1920s, implying that technological advancements may contribute to economic growth independent of institutions. Furthermore, Austin (Austin_2007: p.12) acknowledges that population growth was faster after 1918, indicating a potential link between demographic factors and long-term economic trajectories.

Allen et al. (AllenEtAl_2011: p.1) highlight income disparities between China, Japan, and Europe in the early twentieth century, which were due to long-run stagnation in China combined with industrialization in Japan and Europe. This suggests that interdependencies between economies may significantly impact economic growth, complicating the argument that institutions are the sole fundamental cause of long-term economic success. Additionally, Allen et al. (AllenEtAl_2011: p.20) demonstrate that rapid growth in the German economy led to rising real wages for workers in Leipzig, emphasizing the role of industrialization in shaping wage structures and potentially contributing to economic growth.

The literature review on the role of institutions in long-term economic growth reveals a convergence among scholars that institutional changes play a significant role in shaping economic development. Domar (1970) and Bohannan (1959), though discussing different historical contexts, both emphasize the impact of institutions on economic growth.

Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) highlights how labor tied to land or owners eliminates competition among employers, potentially hindering economic growth. In contrast, Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.7) points out how Western impact, particularly in the form of slave dealing and status acquisition, transformed African societies, leading to economic changes.

Both authors also acknowledge the multifaceted nature of institutional changes. Domar (Domar_1970: p.4) acknowledges that institutions can both hinder and promote growth, depending on factors such as labor mobility. Bohannan (Bohannan_1959: p.10) agrees, noting that Western impact was not limited to economic institutions but also affected social structures.

However, the literature leaves some questions unresolved. For instance, there is a lack of consensus on whether specific types of institutions are more conducive to long-term economic growth than others. Additionally, the exact mechanisms through which institutional changes drive economic growth remain under-explored.
