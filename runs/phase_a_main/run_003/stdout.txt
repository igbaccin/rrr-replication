LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The question of whether institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth has been a subject of intense debate among scholars. The central question at hand is: To what extent do institutions shape and drive economic growth over extended periods? This inquiry carries significant implications for understanding historical development, policy formulation, and potential strategies for fostering prosperity in various regions.

Several scholars support the notion that institutions play a pivotal role in long-run economic growth. For instance, Austin (2008) asserts that institutions are "the fundamental cause of long-rungrowth" (p.26), while North & Weingast (1989) demonstrate how representative government institutions with multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs foster economic growth and political freedom (p.28). Acemoglu et al. (2001) also highlight that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress, ultimately impacting overall performance (p.27).

However, not all scholars agree with this perspective. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) complicate the argument by pointing out that the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has left Sub-Saharan Africa absent from recent debates about comparative long-run growth (p.1). Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) also add nuance to the discussion by emphasizing that understanding the processes by which women's economic contributions have been subsumed under household production is crucial for strengthening women's economic roles and policies seeking to do so (p.3).

The stakes of this debate are high, as a clearer understanding of the role institutions play in long-run economic growth could inform effective policy decisions aimed at promoting prosperity. The ongoing discussion among scholars serves to refine our comprehension of the complex interplay between institutions and economic development, ultimately shedding light on strategies for fostering sustainable growth across various regions.

The correlation between economic structure and growth is a recurring theme in the literature on long-term economic development (Kuznets_1973: p.4, p.6). Kuznets (1973) posits that rapid changes in economic structure are not only associated with high aggregate growth rates but also with significant transformations in other aspects of society, such as urbanization and family structure. This suggests that institutional changes play a crucial role in facilitating the adoption and implementation of modern technology, which can lead to sustainable economic growth.

Moreover, Acemoglu et al. (2002) argue that institutions are indeed a primary factor influencing long-run development over the past 500 years (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49). They note that there has been a shift in the distribution of economic prosperity, which may be related to institutional changes (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.5). The authors also highlight the importance of institutions in understanding both economic and political development throughout history.

Broadberry & Gupta (2006) support this view by emphasizing the role of institutions in early modern economic growth, particularly within the service sector (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.9). They suggest that the service sector played a crucial role in economic development during this period, as it does in the modern era. This underscores the idea that institutional changes can significantly impact economic structure and growth over time.

The argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is further supported by the examination of trade between Africa and Western Europe, as presented in Austin's work (2008). He contends that these trades reinforced rather than originated Africa’s relatively poor economic position compared to western Europe (Austin_2008: p.24). This suggests that the institutional frameworks of the African societies were not conducive to economic growth, which hindered their ability to capitalize on trading opportunities with Western Europe.

Moreover, Austin argues that the implication of these trades was a fundamental problem with the existing relative factor endowments (RF) analyses, particularly in the case of Africa (Austin_2008: p.24). This implies that the RF approach, which posits that differences in natural resources and technology are the primary drivers of economic growth, may not fully explain the economic disparity between Africa and Western Europe. Instead, the institutional frameworks of African societies seem to have played a significant role in shaping their economic trajectory.

Austin also emphasizes the importance of examining growth theories against long-term history, revealing relationships that recur due to similar situations as well as path dependence (Austin_2008: p.1). This underscores the idea that institutional changes can significantly impact economic structure and growth over time, a notion that aligns with the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

The authors North and Weingast (1989) emphasize that institutions, particularly those of representative government with multiple veto points and the primacy of common law courts over economic affairs, are crucial in fostering economic growth and political freedom (p.2). They highlight that these institutions facilitate structural change, which is integral to economic development (p.2). Furthermore, North and Weingast argue that the evolution of such institutions plays a necessary role in making possible economic growth and political freedom (p.30). This alignment with the thesis suggests that institutions indeed serve as the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by enabling structural change and fostering an environment conducive to economic development.

The sources challenge the assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth by highlighting instances where increased state expenditure and investment, rather than strong institutions, contributed to economic development in certain regions (Simson_2019: p.25, p.4). For instance, in Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda during the 1960s and 1970s, state expenditure expanded significantly during a period of high economic growth (Simson_2019: p.25). Despite potential inefficiencies, leakages, and costly delays to reform, these economies managed to sustain investment levels that re-oriented their focus towards industrial development, educational expansion, and military autonomy (Simson_2019: p.4, p.27).

These findings suggest that while strong institutions may play a role in fostering an environment conducive to economic growth, they might not be the sole determining factor. Instead, deliberate investment strategies and expansions in public employment could potentially drive economic development, as observed in these African nations during the 1960s and 1970s. This evidence challenges the notion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth.

The assertion that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is not without nuance, as other factors such as climate and historical data collection may play significant roles in shaping economic development.

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that the lack of data on aggregate economic performance before 1950 has led to Sub-Saharan Africa's absence from recent debates about comparative long-run growth. This implies that a more comprehensive understanding of historical economic trends in this region could potentially challenge the dominance of institutional factors in explaining long-term growth (Broadberry & Gardner_2022: p.1).

Komlos (1998) contributes to this discourse by discussing physical stature during the Industrial Revolution, suggesting that economic processes and associated structural changes were significant influencers of biological growth processes (Komlos_1998: p.16, p.17). This finding complicates the notion that institutions are solely responsible for long-run economic growth, as it suggests environmental factors may also play a role.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) further nuance the argument by suggesting that policymakers often consider trade-offs between economic growth and equality, which requires understanding the relationship between inequality and growth (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.1). This finding highlights that institutions may not be the only factor influencing long-run economic growth, as policies aimed at addressing economic inequality could potentially contribute to growth as well.

Koepke & Baten (2005) argue that climate may have a significant relationship with human living standards and economic development (Koepke & Baten_2005: p.20). This finding complicates the argument that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, as it suggests that environmental factors may also play a role in shaping economic development over time.

Bolt & van Zanden (2014) discuss revisions of nineteenth- and early twentieth-century growth, emphasizing that these revisions do not fundamentally change our understanding of long-term economic growth but add valuable insights to the debate (Bolt & vanZanden_2014: p.5). This finding underscores the complexity of the long-run economic growth debate and suggests that multiple factors, including institutions, may be at play in shaping economic development over time.

The work of Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) sheds light on the significant economic contributions of African women, a factor that has often been overlooked in traditional growth models. They argue that women's labor was frequently subsumed under household production, rendering it unaccounted for in formal economic analyses (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.3). This oversight, as they suggest, can undermine policies aimed at strengthening the economic roles of women.

Moreover, Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) highlight that the economic contribution of African women was often dependent on their husbands' provision (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.9). This finding underscores the interconnectedness of gender roles and economic growth, suggesting that institutions shaping these relationships play a crucial role in long-run economic development.

Furthermore, Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) note that the major historical developments and themes they discuss find parallels elsewhere in Africa where gender and economic growth are concerned (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.4). This implies that the insights drawn from West Africa may be applicable to a broader African context, strengthening the argument that institutions play a fundamental role in shaping long-run economic growth across the continent.

Finally, Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) emphasize that the contribution of African women to economic growth and development was substantial (Akyeampong & Fofack_2014: p.19). This assertion underscores the importance of recognizing and valuing the economic roles of women, particularly in the context of long-run economic growth.

The argument that institutions are the primary cause of long-run economic growth gains complexity when considering country-specific conditions and factor endowments, as highlighted by Temin (2002). While acknowledging the importance of institutions in fostering economic growth, Temin emphasizes the role of other factors such as labor exhaustion and structural change.

Temin (Temin_2002: p.4) posits that the slowing down of economic growth was due to the depletion of cheap labor, a factor that could potentially challenge the notion that institutions alone drive long-term economic success. Moreover, he suggests that the onset of economic stagnation in Europe during the late 18th century was a result of countries returning to equilibrium growth paths rather than adverse shocks (Temin_2002: p.14). This implies that the role of institutions may be less significant during periods of stabilization and equilibrium.

Furthermore, Temin (Temin_2002: p.7) points out that national economies around 1800 were predominantly agricultural, suggesting a context where institutions might have played a lesser role in economic growth compared to factor endowments such as land and labor. However, he also notes that recent theories of economic growth should consider the transition period from agriculture to industry when discussing the Golden Age (Temin_2002: p.7). This nuance underscores the importance of understanding historical contexts in order to fully appreciate the role of institutions in long-run economic growth.

Scholars Domar and Bohannan provide tangential insights into the role of institutions in long-run economic growth, albeit from different perspectives. Both authors acknowledge the significance of economic institutions, but their focus varies. Domar (1970) primarily discusses labor institutions, highlighting how the binding of labor to land or owners can hinder competition among employers and impede economic growth (Domar_1970: p.4). In contrast, Bohannan (1959) delves into cultural and social institutions, emphasizing their impact on trade and status acquisition (Bohannan_1959: p.7, p.10).

Despite these differences, both scholars converge in acknowledging the crucial role of institutions in shaping economic dynamics. They both imply that changes in institutions can lead to structural change, which in turn correlates with growth (implicit in Domar_1970: p.4 and Bohannan_1959: p.10).

However, what remains unresolved is the extent to which specific institutions drive long-run economic growth and how these institutions evolve over time. For instance, Domar (1970) discusses the impact of labor institutions on competition, but does not delve into the mechanisms through which such institutions change or evolve. Similarly, Bohannan (1959) touches upon cultural and social institutions' influence on trade and status acquisition, yet fails to explain how these institutions adapt in response to economic changes.

Further research is needed to understand the intricate relationship between institutions, structural change, and long-run economic growth. This could involve examining case studies that illustrate how specific institutional changes have led to significant structural shifts and subsequent economic growth. Additionally, investigating the mechanisms through which institutions evolve over time could provide valuable insights into the dynamics of long-term economic development.
