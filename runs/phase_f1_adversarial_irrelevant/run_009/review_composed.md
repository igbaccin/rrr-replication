The debate surrounding the role of institutions in long-run economic growth has been a subject of intense discussion among scholars. This discourse revolves around whether institutions are essential for fostering sustained economic development or if they are merely irrelevant to this process.

Several researchers, such as Acemoglu et al. (2001, p.27) and North & Weingast (1989, p.7), argue that certain institutions can hinder investment and economic progress. For instance, Acemoglu et al. (2001, p.8) suggest that there are mechanisms leading to institutional persistence, which may obstruct long-term growth. Similarly, North & Weingast (1989, p.7) contend that absolutist states, which did not uphold property rights, retarded economic growth.

On the other hand, some scholars, like Ogilvie (2007, p.11), argue that economic institutions play a crucial role in explaining why some countries industrialized before others and in shaping their subsequent patterns of growth. Furthermore, Hopkins (2009, p.8) asserts that the prominence attached to the role of institutions has enabled the new economic history of Africa to flourish.

Abramovitz (1986, p.19), in his analysis, suggests that forces of institutional change may limit the growth of Western countries. However, it's important to note that Abramovitz (1986, p.11) focuses on a biased sample of countries that have successfully entered into modern economic growth.

This debate carries significant implications for understanding the pathways to sustainable economic development and the role policymakers should play in shaping institutions. The argument continues as scholars grapple with the complex interplay between institutions, cultural factors, and external influences on economic growth.

The notion that institutions play a minimal role in long-run economic growth is substantiated by several scholarly works. Acemoglu, et al. (2001) assert that certain institutions can be detrimental to investment and economic progress (p.27). They also argue that these institutions often persist for extended periods due to various economic mechanisms (p.8).

Abramovitz (1986) supports this perspective by highlighting the institutional differences between Western countries, particularly Europe and the United States. He notes that the institutions of the Welfare State have reached a higher degree of elaboration in Europe than in the United States (p.21), suggesting that such institutional structures may hinder economic growth.

Broadberry, et al. (2015) also contribute to this argument by examining India's long-run economic performance. They argue that structural change correlates with growth (p.2). However, they find that India's economic performance since the late sixteenth century has been subject to controversy, suggesting that institutional factors may not significantly influence long-term economic growth (p.2).

The works of Austin (2008, 2007) and Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) provide compelling evidence that institutions may not be the primary drivers of long-run economic growth, particularly in African economies.

Austin (Austin_2008: p.24) asserts that the theoretical conclusion of institutions being 'the' fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is attractive but might need reconsideration, especially when examining Africa's post-colonial growth. He further argues that British West Africa's policies accommodated economic change rather than being solely determined by colonial administrations and preferences for extraction (Austin_2008: p.15). This suggests that institutions may not have a significant impact on long-term economic growth in these regions.

Similarly, Akyeampong & Fofack (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.9) highlight the contribution of African women to economic growth and development, which was largely dependent on the economic provision of the husband. This implies that institutions, such as those governing property rights or labor markets, may not have been critical in determining long-term economic growth.

Moreover, Austin (Austin_2007: p.18) discusses how farmers recognized the potential value of land suitable for cocoa, suggesting that market forces and individual actions might play a more significant role in driving economic growth than institutions. The transition from slave to free labor also indicates a shift away from coercive labor institutions towards wage labor and farm-pledging (Austin_2007: p.20), further questioning the importance of institutions in long-run economic growth.

The evidence presented by Broadberry & Gardner (2022) supports the notion that institutions may be irrelevant to long-run economic growth. Their research on African economic development reveals a growth trajectory predating European colonial interventions, suggesting that institutional changes from Europe did not significantly contribute to Africa's economic progress (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.10, p.17).

Moreover, they argue that in African societies, access to economic rents is open to all rather than limited to a small elite, which further challenges the importance of institutions in determining long-run growth (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.17). This observation aligns with the argument that institutions may not be crucial for long-term economic success if other factors, such as open access to resources, can drive growth.

Similarly, Broadberry & Gupta's (2006) study on early modern international economy sheds light on the limitations of using grain wages as an indicator of economic development, implying that institutions may not be the only determinant of long-run growth (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.24). Their model assumes that grain is non-tradable internationally, suggesting that other factors, such as geography or technology, might play a more significant role in shaping economic development than institutions (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.23).

These findings support the argument that institutions may be irrelevant to long-run economic growth by demonstrating instances of economic progress without significant institutional changes and by questioning the use of traditional indicators, such as grain wages, to measure economic development. Further investigation into other regions and time periods could strengthen this argument and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the role of institutions in long-term economic success.

The argument that institutions are irrelevant to long-run economic growth is challenged by Stern (2004), who emphasizes the significant role of urban and industrial development in this process. He posits that in later stages of economic development, urban and industrial development tends to decentralize, suggesting that institutional structures may not be the primary drivers of long-term growth (Stern_2004: p.6).

Moreover, Stern (2004) contends that it is median rather than mean income that is the relevant variable in understanding economic development. This assertion implies that the distribution of income within a society could be more influential in determining long-term growth than the institutional framework (Stern_2004: p.9).

Lastly, Stern (2004) critiques the traditional view that economic development and environmental quality are conflicting goals, arguing instead that this perspective reflects only the scale effect alone. He presents projected sulfur emissions using the Environmental Kuznets Curve (EKC) to demonstrate how economic growth can coincide with improved environmental conditions (Stern_2004: p.9, p.3).

These observations challenge the notion that institutions are irrelevant to long-run economic growth by suggesting that urban and industrial development, income distribution, and environmental quality may play significant roles in shaping a nation's economic trajectory over time.

The work of Lindert & Williamson (1985) complicates the notion that institutions are irrelevant to long-run economic growth by emphasizing the role of structural change in economic development. They argue that capital formation, a key aspect of economic growth, is determined on the production side and domestic saving becomes irrelevant in certain cases (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.21). This suggests that while institutions may not directly influence savings, they could indirectly impact economic growth through their effect on the production side.

Moreover, Lindert & Williamson (1985) highlight that understanding long-run distribution trends is intertwined with explaining economic growth itself. They argue that both distributional trends and patterns of economic growth can be accounted for by factor-demand and factor-supply forces (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.9). This implies that institutions, which shape these forces, could play a role in shaping a nation's economic trajectory over time.

Furthermore, Lindert & Williamson (1985) present evidence from Japan's economic history to support their argument. They argue that imbalance and inequality did not necessarily correlate with Japan's long-run experience, suggesting that these factors may not always be determinants of economic growth (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.14). This adds nuance to the argument that institutions are irrelevant to long-run economic growth, as it suggests that certain institutional arrangements could potentially foster economic development despite imbalances and inequalities.

The literature on institutions' relevance to long-run economic growth presents a complex landscape, with scholars offering nuanced arguments rather than a clear consensus. On one hand, North (1989) emphasizes the role of institutional changes in reducing transaction costs and fostering economic growth (North_1989: p.5, p.9). Sokoloff & Engerman (2000), while acknowledging the impact of initial conditions on long-term growth, suggest that institutions may have played a significant role in shaping these differences (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4, p.12).

However, Temin (2002) and Bohannan (1959), although discussing institutional changes, primarily focus on other factors such as technological advancements and labor market dynamics that influence economic growth (Temin_2002: p.4, p.7; Bohannan_1959: p.7, p.10). Temin (2002) argues that the exhaustion of cheap labor, rather than institutions, was a key factor in slowing economic growth (Temin_2002: p.4).

Despite these differences, there is agreement among scholars that institutions play a role in economic development, albeit to varying degrees and through different mechanisms. What remains unresolved is the extent to which institutional changes drive long-run economic growth compared to other factors such as technological advancements, labor market dynamics, or initial conditions. Further research is needed to disentangle these interrelated factors and understand their relative importance in shaping long-term economic growth.
