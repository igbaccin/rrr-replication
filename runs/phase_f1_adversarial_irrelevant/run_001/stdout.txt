LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The debate surrounding the role of institutions in long-run economic growth has been a subject of intense scholarly discourse. This discussion revolves around whether institutional structures significantly contribute to sustainable economic development or are merely incidental factors.

Several scholars argue that institutions play a crucial role in shaping economic growth trajectories. For instance, Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27) assert that certain institutions can hinder investment and progress, while North & Weingast (North&Weingast_1989: p.7) suggest that absolutist states with weak institutional constraints retarded long-term economic growth. Similarly, Ogilvie (Ogilvie_2007: p.11) posits that pre-industrial European institutions significantly influenced the industrialization process and subsequent economic growth patterns in Britain.

On the contrary, others contend that institutions may be irrelevant to long-run economic growth. Abramovitz (Abramovitz_1986: p.21), for example, highlights that the Welfare State's institutional elaboration is more advanced in Europe than in the United States, yet this has not hindered the latter's growth. Hopkins (Hopkins_2009: p.22) suggests that ethnic fragmentation might be responsible for unproductive institutions but acknowledges that other factors influencing solidarity and action need to be considered.

This discourse carries significant implications for policymakers, as understanding the role of institutions in economic growth can inform strategies aimed at fostering sustainable development. The debate continues as scholars grapple with the complex interplay between institutions, cultural factors, and historical events that influence long-term economic growth patterns.

The assertion that institutions are irrelevant to long-run economic growth is countered by a substantial body of evidence. North and Weingast (1989) argue that the new institutions allowed the government to commit credibly to upholding property rights, which in turn fostered economic growth and political freedom (p.2). Conversely, they demonstrate that absolutist states with no such constraints, such as early modern Spain, created economic conditions that retarded long-run economic growth (p.7).

Similarly, Ogilvie (2007) posits that economic institutions played a central role in explaining why Britain industrialized before France and the subsequent pattern of Britain’s economic growth, its factory industrialization, and by implication, its economic growth (p.11). He further suggests that economic history is still dominated by the belief that institutions evolved not because they were efficient but because they facilitated cultural practices (p.28).

Whatley (2018) examines the impact of slave exports on long-run equilibrium and finds that these contributions to growth are internal rather than externally driven (p.2, p.11). Nunn (2008) shows that slavery in the New World resulted in the evolution of institutions that were not conducive to economic growth (p.3), while Nunn & Wantchekon (2011) estimate that the internal channel for the adverse effects of the slave trade on local institutions is at least twice as large as the external channel (p.4).

The arguments presented in various scholarly works support the notion that institutions may be irrelevant to long-run economic growth. For instance, Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) suggest that economic thought is often trapped within a time bind, presuming specific social conditions that may not always hold true in history (p.14). This implies that institutional factors, which are typically associated with particular historical periods, might not significantly impact long-term economic growth.

Similarly, Feinstein (1998) demonstrates a prolonged plateau in economic growth despite improvements in other indicators, casting doubt on the significance of institutions in driving such growth (p.2). Furthermore, Bryceson (2002) emphasizes the importance of noneconomic factors, suggesting that these might overshadow institutional influences on economic development (p.2).

Frankema & van Waijenburg (2012) also support this perspective by highlighting urban population growth exceeding total populations, implying that structural factors other than institutions could be driving economic growth (p.15). Lastly, Broadberry & Gupta (2006) argue that data limitations do not allow for inferring equal levels of economic development based solely on grain wages, suggesting that institutional factors might not be the primary drivers of long-run economic growth (p.24).

The contention that institutions are irrelevant to long-run economic growth is further substantiated by the works of Hopkins (2009) and Broadberry & Gardner (2022). Hopkins (Hopkins_2009: p.11) posits that institutions, while significant, may not account for a large part of the cause of economic development, as they are intertwined with other factors that cannot be easily disentangled. This perspective is echoed by Hopkins (Hopkins_2009: p.8), who suggests that the experience of East Asia might be more relevant to Africa than that of the United States, implying that institutional factors may not be the primary drivers of long-run economic growth.

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) provide empirical evidence supporting this argument. They argue that African economic growth started before European colonial interventions, contradicting the view expressed in early economic histories of Africa (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.10). Moreover, they contend that in societies like West Africa, access to economic rents is not limited to a small elite but open to all (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.17), suggesting that institutional structures may not be the primary determinants of long-run growth.

Structural changes in economic development, rather than institutions, may be more decisive in determining long-run growth. Stern (2004) posits that urban and industrial development tend to decentralize as economies advance (Stern_2004: p.6), suggesting a shift away from centralized institutional structures towards more dispersed economic activities. Moreover, Hopkins (2019) argues that in African societies, the emphasis on economic forces was retained but relocated during colonial periods (Hopkins_2019: p.8). This implies that while institutions may have been altered under colonial rule, economic structures continued to drive growth.

Simson (2019) highlights the role of structural changes in post-colonial economies as well. The author notes that despite perceived spending inefficiencies and leakages, eliminating them might not have been sufficient to sustain the investment levels of the 1960s (Simson_2019: p.4). This indicates a potential persistence of structural factors influencing growth even amidst institutional changes.

However, it is important to acknowledge that these arguments do not necessarily disprove the relevance of institutions entirely. Instead, they emphasize the significance of structural changes in economic development and suggest that institutions may interact with these structures to impact long-run growth. Further examination of this interaction could provide valuable insights into the relationship between institutions and economic growth.

The high-wage environment in Europe, as detailed by Pamuk (2007), presents a nuanced perspective on the role of institutions in long-run economic growth. This period saw significant structural changes that correlated with growth, such as a decrease in land rents and interest rates relative to wages (Pamuk_2007: p.6). These alterations in economic structures may suggest that they are more instrumental than institutions in driving long-term development.

Moreover, the Black Death, a major catastrophe in Europe, instigated far-reaching institutional and other changes in rural areas (Pamuk_2007: p.24). These transformations, rather than existing institutions, may have played a crucial role in shaping Europe's long-run economic growth trajectory.

In addition, Pamuk (2007) highlights that these structural changes occurred before the Industrial Revolution, suggesting their relevance extends beyond this pivotal period (Pamuk_2007: p.4). This finding complicates the argument that institutions become increasingly important in determining long-run economic growth as nations progress through history.

The literature on the role of institutions in long-run economic growth presents a complex picture, with scholars both converging and diverging in their perspectives. On one hand, North (1989) argues that changes in institutions can impact economic growth by altering transaction costs and enhancing security rights, suggesting their relevance to long-term economic development. This aligns with the stance of Sokoloff & Engerman (2000), who acknowledge that pathways of institutional development could potentially affect growth.

However, Temin (2002) focuses on structural shifts as a primary driver of economic growth, implying that while institutions may play a role, they are not the sole determinant. Similarly, Bohannan (1959) discusses the influence of Western impact on economic institutions but does not directly link this to long-run economic growth.

Despite these varying viewpoints, there is agreement among scholars that institutions and their evolution play a significant role in shaping economic development. What remains unresolved is the extent to which institutions contribute to long-term growth compared to other factors such as technological progress, resource endowments, or cultural norms. Further research is needed to disentangle these interrelated factors and establish a more nuanced understanding of their relative importance in determining long-run economic growth.
