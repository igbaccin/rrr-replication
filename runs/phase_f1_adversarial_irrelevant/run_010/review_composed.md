The debate on the role of institutions in long-run economic growth has been a subject of intense discussion among economists and historians. This discourse revolves around whether institutions significantly impact economic development over extended periods, or if they are merely incidental factors.

One perspective, supported by authors such as Acemoglu et al. (2001, p.27) and North & Weingast (1989, p.7), argues that certain institutions can hinder investment and economic progress. For instance, Acemoglu et al. (2001, p.8) suggest that institutional persistence, particularly in the context of former colonies, may have been designed to maximize European colonists' rents rather than foster long-term growth.

On the other hand, some scholars, like Ogilvie (2007, p.1), contend that economic institutions play a crucial role in explaining why certain countries industrialized before others and in shaping their subsequent patterns of growth. They argue that economic history is still dominated by the belief that institutions are essential structures governing transactions, even though they can be difficult to disentangle from other influences (Ogilvie_2007: p.1).

However, not all agree that institutions are the primary drivers of long-run economic growth. Abramovitz (1986, p.19), for example, suggests that institutional change may be limiting the growth of Western countries. Hopkins (2009, p.11) also questions the assumption that institutions can be isolated from other factors and argues that they are entwined with one another in ways that cannot easily be disentangled.

This debate carries significant implications for understanding economic development and designing policies aimed at promoting growth. If institutions are indeed irrelevant to long-run economic growth, as some argue, then policies focusing on institutional reform may not yield the desired results. Conversely, if institutions play a crucial role in shaping economic outcomes, as others contend, then efforts to improve institutions could significantly impact economic development.

The argument continues, with scholars offering various perspectives and evidence to support their positions.

The assertion that institutions are inconsequential to long-term economic growth is substantiated by several scholarly works. Acemoglu, Johnson, and Robinson (2001) provide compelling evidence in this regard, stating that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). They further argue that there are mechanisms within economies that perpetuate these unfavorable institutions over extended periods (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.8).

Supporting this perspective, Abramovitz (1986) presents a sample of countries that have successfully entered the process of modern economic growth, suggesting that institutions may not be the primary drivers of such progress (Abramovitz_1986: p.19). Furthermore, he notes the existence of forces of institutional change that could limit the growth of Western countries (Abramovitz_1986: p.21), implying a potential irrelevance of institutions in shaping long-term economic outcomes.

In addition, Broadberry, Gupta, and Koch (2015) discuss the controversy surrounding India's long-run economic performance, suggesting that institutional factors may not be the sole determinants of such performance (BroadberryEtal_2015: p.2). They also emphasize the importance of considering a wide range of economic activities when assessing a country's GDP per capita data (BroadberryEtAl_2015: p.4, p.14), which indirectly supports the argument that institutions may not significantly impact long-term economic growth.

The literature on the impact of institutions on African economies provides substantial evidence that questions the significance of institutions in long-run economic growth. Austin (2008) notes that while the theoretical conclusion of AJR's work emphasizing institutions as "the" fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is attractive, it overlooks key aspects of the relationship between political independence, institutions, and economic growth in Africa (Austin_2008: p.18).

Moreover, Austin (2008) highlights that British West Africa's policies generally sought to slow but ultimately accommodated economic growth, suggesting that institutions may not be the primary determinants of long-term economic growth (Austin_2008: p.15). Similarly, Austin (2007) indicates that the transition from slave to free labor in West Africa led to an increase in the bargaining power of landowners, further undermining the notion that institutions are crucial for long-run economic growth (Austin_2007: p.2).

Furthermore, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) argue that understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution becomes subsumed under household production is essential to developing policies that strengthen women's economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). This finding suggests that focusing solely on institutional factors may overlook crucial aspects of economic development, such as the role of gender dynamics in African economies.

The evidence presented in the sources supports the notion that institutions may not be the primary drivers of long-run economic growth, especially in African economies. Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that the absence of Africa in discussions on long-term growth is due to a focus on institutional factors, but their research demonstrates that African growth began long before European colonial interventions (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.17).

Bryceson (2002) highlights the importance of the noneconomic sphere in African economies. He notes that policy-makers have recognized this significance, and a large percentage of women in his study are widows receiving pensions and remittances (Bryceson_2002: p.9). This suggests that factors beyond institutional structures play significant roles in economic development.

Clark & Jacks (2007) provide additional evidence, showing that mineral rents in northeastern Africa were low despite attempts to restrict production, indicating that other investments may offer better returns (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.14). They also suggest that extraction costs, rather than transport costs, dominate in the determination of rent, further challenging the notion that institutional factors are the primary drivers of economic growth.

These findings collectively support the argument that institutions may be less relevant to long-run economic growth than previously thought. The focus on institutional changes as the primary determinant of economic development may overlook crucial aspects such as gender dynamics, noneconomic spheres, and other investment opportunities. Future research should continue to explore these factors to gain a more comprehensive understanding of economic growth mechanisms.

Structural change, rather than institutions, appears to be a significant factor in long-run economic growth. Stern (Stern_2004: p.6) posits that urban and industrial development tend to decentralize in later stages of economic development, suggesting a shift towards more diversified economies may drive growth. Similarly, Simson (Simson_2019: p.25) notes the stabilization and recent rise of employment levels, potentially indicating the importance of structural changes in the labor market.

Moreover, Stern (Stern_2004: p.9) challenges the traditional view that economic development and environmental quality are conflicting goals by presenting projected sulfur emissions using the Environmental Kuznets Curve (EKC), suggesting that growth can coexist with improved environmental conditions. This implies that institutions may not be the primary determinant of long-run economic growth, as they could potentially accommodate structural changes that promote sustainable development.

Simson (Simson_2019: p.3) further questions the assumption that a less patronage-afflicted polity would have made better allocative choices, spurring growth. While patronage may lead to inefficiencies and delays, it is uncertain whether eliminating these factors alone would be sufficient to maintain investment levels or reorient economies (Simson_2019: p.4). This suggests that institutions might not be the sole drivers of long-term economic growth, as other factors like structural changes could play a crucial role.

Structural changes in economies, rather than institutions, may be more instrumental in driving long-term economic growth, particularly in non-Western countries. Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that capital formation and factor demand play a significant role in both distributional trends and the pattern of economic growth (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.9).

The authors emphasize that in certain cases, capital formation is determined on the production side, making domestic saving irrelevant (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.21). This suggests that factors like technological advancements or industrialization could be crucial drivers of long-term economic growth, potentially overshadowing the impact of institutions.

Moreover, Lindert & Williamson (1985) point out that explaining long-run distribution trends is intertwined with the search for explanations of growth itself (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.9). This implies that understanding how economic structures evolve and impact wealth distribution could provide insights into the underlying mechanisms driving economic growth.

The authors also highlight that Japan's economic history supports this perspective, as neither imbalance nor inequality appeared to correlate with Japan's long-run experience (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.14). This finding suggests that in non-Western countries like Japan, institutions might not be the primary determinants of long-term economic growth.

The literature review on the relevance of institutions to long-run economic growth presents a complex picture, with scholars offering nuanced perspectives rather than a clear consensus. North (1989) emphasizes the role of institutions in reducing transaction costs and promoting economic growth, while Temin (2002) focuses on structural shifts and their impact on growth rates.

Both North (1989) and Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) acknowledge the potential influence of institutions on long-term economic growth. North (North_1989: p.5) highlights the importance of logical changes in institutions, while Sokoloff & Engerman (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.12) suggest that the path of institutional development may have affected growth. However, they do not directly address whether institutions are irrelevant to long-run economic growth.

On the other hand, Temin (2002) primarily discusses structural shifts and their impact on growth rates, suggesting that factors such as the exhaustion of cheap labor might be more significant determinants of economic growth than institutions (Temin_2002: p.4). Bohannan (1959) provides tangential insights into the impact of Western institutions but does not address long-run economic growth.

The literature suggests that while institutions may play a role in promoting economic growth, other factors such as structural shifts and factor endowments might be equally important. The question of whether institutions are irrelevant to long-run economic growth remains unresolved, with scholars offering diverse perspectives that require further exploration and integration for a comprehensive understanding.

In the context of Japan, Lindert & Williamson (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.14) found no correlation between institutions and inequality in Japan's long-run experience, suggesting that institutions might not be the primary determinants of long-term economic growth in non-Western countries like Japan. However, this finding does not directly address the relevance of institutions to long-run economic growth in general. Future research could further explore the role of institutions and their interaction with other factors in determining long-run economic growth across various regions and historical periods.
