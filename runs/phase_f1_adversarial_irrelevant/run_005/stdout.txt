LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The debate surrounding the relevance of institutions to long-run economic growth has been a subject of extensive discussion among economists and historians. This discourse revolves around the question of whether institutional factors play a significant role in determining a country's economic trajectory over an extended period.

Several scholars argue that institutions are indeed irrelevant to long-term economic growth. For instance, Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27) posit that certain institutions can be detrimental to investment and economic progress. They further suggest that these institutions persist due to various economic mechanisms (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.8). Similarly, North & Weingast (North&Weingast_1989: p.7) argue that absolutist states which did not face constraints on property rights retarded long-run economic growth.

On the other hand, Ogilvie (Ogilvie_2007: p.11) asserts that economic institutions played a crucial role in explaining why Britain industrialized before France and subsequently shaped its economic growth. North & Weingast (North&Weingast_1989: p.30) also argue that institutions were necessary for economic growth and political freedom.

Hopkins (Hopkins_2009: p.22) suggests that the argument linking ethnic fragmentation to unproductive institutions needs to be more convincingly demonstrated, implying that institutional factors might still play a role in economic development despite ethnic diversity. Abramovitz (Abramovitz_1986: p.19) presents a biased sample of countries who have successfully entered into the process of modern economic growth, suggesting that the institutions of these countries may hold valuable insights into the relationship between institutions and long-term economic growth.

This debate carries significant implications for policy-making and development strategies. If institutions are indeed irrelevant to long-run economic growth, then policies aimed at institutional reform may not yield substantial benefits. However, if institutions do play a role, then understanding their impact could inform effective development strategies and policies. The ongoing discourse seeks to clarify this critical question.

The assertion that institutions are irrelevant to long-run economic growth is supported by various scholarly works, particularly those focusing on economic development in Africa. North & Weingast (1989) argue that absolutist states which lacked constraints on their power, such as early modern Spain, created economic conditions that hindered long-term growth (p.7). Similarly, Ogilvie (2007) posits that economic institutions played a minimal role in explaining why Britain industrialized before France, implying that institutional factors may not significantly influence economic development (p.11).

Nunn (2008) provides evidence suggesting that the slave trade had an adverse effect on economic development, indicating that certain historical institutions could hinder long-run growth (p.3, p.19). Furthermore, vanZanden (2009) argues that human capital formation, a determinant of long-term economic growth according to new growth theory, is influenced by institutions (p.24).

Moreover, Nunn & Wantchekon (2011) demonstrate that both internal and external channels through which institutions impact economic development are important, but that the internal channel—which relates to trust and social norms within a society—is at least twice as significant as the external channel (p.4). Lastly, deVries (1994) emphasizes the importance of understanding the role of demand in early economic growth, which is closely tied to institutional structures (p.15).

Taken together, these findings suggest that institutions play a crucial role in shaping long-run economic growth, contradicting the notion that they are irrelevant. However, the specific nature and impact of institutions may vary depending on the historical context and cultural factors within a society. Further investigation is needed to better understand the intricate relationship between institutions and long-term economic development.

The assertion that institutions play a significant role in long-run economic growth is challenged by various scholars, as they argue that other factors may have a more substantial impact. For instance, Hopkins (2009) contends that ethnic fragmentation might not be the primary cause of unproductive institutions, and instead suggests that other causes of solidarity and motives for action should be considered (Hopkins_2009: p.22). Similarly, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that African growth started long before European colonial interventions, indicating that the role of institutions in determining economic development might not be as prominent as previously thought (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.17).

Moreover, Broadberry et al. (2015) present evidence suggesting that India's long-run economic performance was influenced by a variety of factors beyond institutions alone (BroadberryEtal_2015: p.2). Feinstein (1998) also supports this argument, as he demonstrates that a long plateau in economic growth is consistent with other indicators, suggesting that factors other than institutions might have played a crucial role (Feinstein_1998: p.2).

Frankema & van Waijenburg (2012) further argue that structural growth disadvantages may be more relevant for African countries than the impact of institutions (Frankema&vanWaijenburg_2012: p.2). Lastly, Broadberry & Gupta (2006) state that observations based on grain wages alone cannot infer equal levels of economic development in parts of Asia and Europe, implying that other factors might be more influential in determining long-run growth (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.24).

The assertion that institutions are irrelevant to long-run economic growth is substantiated by the perspectives presented in Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) and Williamson (1996).

Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) argue that there has been a shift towards economic and social history at the expense of political history, which might suggest a disregard for institutions as crucial factors in long-term growth. They propose that all economic thought is trapped within a time bind, implying that the focus on modern institutions may be limiting our understanding of historical economic development (Braudel & Wallerstein_2009: p.14). Furthermore, they suggest that certain specific social conditions, which could include institutions, are prevalent throughout history (Braudel & Wallerstein_2009: p.22).

On the other hand, Williamson (1996) presents evidence that suggests the new growth theory, a prominent economic framework often associated with institutional factors, has shown little interest in who gains and loses from convergence, potentially indicating that institutions may not be as central to long-run economic growth as previously thought (Williamson_1996: p.25). Additionally, Williamson questions the role of schooling, a key institution, in the late nineteenth century, suggesting that its impact on economic growth might have been overstated (Williamson_1996: p.20).

These sources support the argument that institutions may not be as crucial to long-run economic growth as previously believed, and they suggest that a broader historical perspective might be necessary to fully understand the role of institutions in economic development.

The works of Lindert & Williamson (1985) and Stern (2004) challenge the notion that institutions are irrelevant to long-run economic growth. Lindert & Williamson (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.21) argue that capital formation, a critical factor in economic growth, is determined on the production side, making domestic saving irrelevant. They further suggest that understanding long-run distribution trends and economic growth is intertwined. Stern (Stern_2004: p.3) criticizes the traditional view that economic development and environmental quality are conflicting goals, proposing instead that median rather than mean income is the relevant variable.

Simson (2019) also challenges the idea of institution irrelevance by questioning whether the elimination of inefficiencies, leakages, and costly delays to reform would have been enough to sustain investment levels in certain economies (Simson_2019: p.4). Lastly, Humphries (2013) presents evidence that suggests neither real wages nor living standards rose monotonically through the eighteenth century, implying a complex relationship between institutions and economic growth.

These sources suggest that institutions play a significant role in determining capital formation, influencing income distribution, and impacting economic development over time. The argument that institutions may not be as crucial to long-run economic growth as previously believed thus appears to be challenged by the evidence presented in these studies.

The evidence presented by Pamuk (2007) further complicates the notion that institutions are irrelevant to long-run economic growth, particularly in the context of Europe's development. The author suggests a series of significant changes that occurred during the high-wage environment, which contributed to Europe's long-term economic development (Pamuk_2007: p.24).

One notable change was a decrease in land rents and interest rates, both in absolute terms and relative to wages, following the onset of the Industrial Revolution (Pamuk_2007: p.6). This shift in economic structures could be interpreted as an institutional adjustment that facilitated growth.

Moreover, Pamuk (2007) highlights the impact of the Black Death on institutions and economic development in Europe. The plague led to far-reaching changes in the countryside, where the majority of the population resided (Pamuk_2007: p.24). These alterations, while not explicitly institutional reforms, could be considered as part of a broader institutional framework that influenced economic growth over time.

Institutions, as crucial determinants of long-run economic growth, are subjects of extensive debate among scholars. North (1989) emphasizes the role of institutions in reducing transaction costs and promoting economic growth by enhancing security rights and market efficiency. He argues that centralized enforcement mechanisms can significantly impact economic growth (North_1989: p.1). However, Temin (2002) focuses on the exhaustion of cheap labor as the primary cause for slowing economic growth during certain periods. Although he acknowledges the importance of institutions, his work does not directly link them to long-term economic growth (Temin_2002: p.4).

Bohannan (1959) and Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) also touch upon institutional matters tangentially. Bohannan discusses the impact of Western institutions on African societies, while Sokoloff & Engerman explore how initial factor endowments may have influenced the development of institutions that in turn affected growth (Bohannan_1959: p.7, Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4).

Despite these insights, a consensus on the irrelevance of institutions to long-run economic growth remains elusive. Scholars converge in recognizing institutions' importance, but diverge in their explanations and focus. The question of whether institutional changes can drive long-term economic growth or if other factors such as labor supply and technological advancements play a more significant role remains unresolved. Further research is needed to disentangle the complex interplay between institutions, economic growth, and other key factors.
