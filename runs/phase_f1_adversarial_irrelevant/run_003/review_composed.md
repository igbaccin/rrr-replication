The debate surrounding the relevance of institutions to long-run economic growth is a contentious one, with numerous scholars offering insights from various perspectives. This discussion carries significant implications for understanding the economic trajectories of different regions and nations over time.

One prominent position, supported by several scholars such as Acemoglu, Robinson, North, Weingast, Ogilvie, and Austin, argues that institutions play a crucial role in shaping economic growth. For instance, Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27) assert that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress, while North & Weingast (North&Weingast_1989: p.7) contend that absolutist states with weak institutional frameworks retarded long-run economic growth. Similarly, Austin (Austin_2008: p.5) suggests that the European settlement in areas where they could live longest introduced institutions promoting economic growth.

On the other hand, some scholars offer critiques or nuanced perspectives on this issue. For example, Hopkins (Hopkins_2009: p.10) argues that in regions where the European presence was either marginal or took the form of colonial rule, unproductive institutions remained in place or were introduced, hindering long-term development.

The stakes of this debate are high, as understanding the role of institutions in economic growth can provide valuable insights for policy-makers and economists seeking to promote sustainable development and reduce poverty in various regions around the world. The argument continues, with scholars continuing to explore the intricate relationships between institutions, economic growth, and historical contexts.

Institutions, as crucial components of a country's socio-economic framework, have long been considered significant determinants of long-run economic growth. However, a closer examination of historical evidence suggests otherwise.

Scholars such as Acemoglu and his colleagues (2001) argue that certain institutions can be detrimental to investment and economic progress. For instance, they propose that the persistence of these institutions over time is due to various economic mechanisms that reinforce their existence (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.8). Furthermore, their research indicates that early institutions tend to persist, which could potentially imply a hindrance to long-term economic growth (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.16).

North and Weingast (1989) also support this perspective by demonstrating how certain institutional structures can retard long-run economic growth. For example, they illustrate that absolutist states with no constraint on their power, such as early modern Spain, created economic conditions that were detrimental to sustained growth (North&Weingast_1989: p.7).

Temin (2002) provides further evidence by suggesting that the exhaustion of cheap labor was a primary factor causing economic growth to slow (Temin_2002: p.4). This implies that institutions, such as those that regulate labor markets, could potentially hinder long-term economic growth if they maintain conditions conducive to low labor costs for extended periods.

In addition, van Zanden (2009) supports the argument by highlighting how the quality of institutions can impact human capital formation, which is a key determinant of long-term economic growth according to new growth theory (vanZanden_2009: p.24). Similarly, de Vries (1994) emphasizes the role of demand in early economic growth and argues that certain institutions could potentially restrict entry to skilled jobs, thereby hindering economic development (deVries_1994: p.15).

Lastly, Braudel and Wallerstein (2009) suggest that economic thought is often trapped within a time bind, implying that historical perspectives on institutions and their impact on long-run economic growth may be limited by the focus on specific periods or timeframes (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.14).

The argument that institutions play a significant role in long-run economic growth is challenged by historical evidence, as demonstrated by various scholars. For instance, Hopkins (2009) contends that the European presence in regions where unproductive institutions were already established either remained marginal or introduced extractive types of economic control ill-suited for long-term development (Hopkins_2009: p.10). Similarly, Feinstein (1998) notes a prolonged period of economic stagnation in many African countries that is difficult to reconcile with an improvement in real wages, suggesting the role of institutions may not be as crucial as previously thought (Feinstein_1998: p.7).

Moreover, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) argue that understanding the processes by which women's economic contribution became subsumed under household production and their labor formally unaccounted for undermines policies seeking to strengthen women's economic roles (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.3). This highlights the potential limitations of institutional structures in promoting gender equality and economic growth.

Clark & Jacks (2007) focus on mineral rents in the northeast, showing that extraction costs, rather than transport costs, seem to dominate in the determination of rent (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.11). This suggests that factors other than institutions may be more influential in shaping long-run economic growth patterns.

The slave trade's impact on institutions provides compelling evidence that supports the claim of institutional irrelevance to long-run economic growth. As Nunn & Wantchekon (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.25) argue, "the relationship could be due in part to the adverse effects of the slave trade on local institutions."

Whatley (Whatley_2018: p.11) reinforces this notion by estimating speed-of-adjustment parameters when slave exports exceeded their long-run equilibrium levels, suggesting that internal factors, rather than external influences, were driving growth patterns. Similarly, Broadberry & Gardner (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.17) highlight an African society where access to economic rents was not limited to a small elite but open to all, indicating that institutions may have been less relevant in shaping long-run economic growth.

Moreover, Nunn & Wantchekon (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.4) demonstrate that both internal and external channels contribute to the relationship between institutions and growth, yet they find that the internal channel is at least twice as large as the external channel. This implies that factors internal to a society might have a more significant impact on long-run economic growth than institutions.

Broadberry & Gardner (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.10) further argue against the assumption that any economic growth above subsistence resulted from European colonial interventions, suggesting that African growth started long before the European scramble for Africa in the late nineteenth century. This supports the notion of institutional irrelevance to long-run economic growth, as it shows that African societies were capable of achieving significant economic growth without the influence of external institutions.

The assertion of institutional irrelevance to long-run economic growth is challenged by various scholars, who argue that certain factors are crucial in shaping economic trajectories. For instance, Stern (2004) posits that the mean income, rather than median income, is the relevant variable for understanding economic development and environmental quality (Stern_2004: p.9). This suggests that institutions play a role in determining the distribution of income, which could impact long-run economic growth.

Similarly, Hopkins (2019) asserts that colonial periods offered the best chance of assembling long-run sets of data and that the emphasis on economic forces was retained but relocated during this time (Hopkins_2019: p.8). This implies that institutions, such as colonial administrations, could have influenced economic growth in African societies.

Moreover, van Zanden (1999) argues that the link between wages and long-term economic growth is less direct than previously thought (vanZanden_1999: p.4). This suggests that institutions, such as labor markets and wage-setting mechanisms, could be important determinants of economic growth over the long run.

Lastly, Simson (2019) contends that the composition of public spending grew differently in various periods, which could indicate that institutional factors influenced the allocation of resources and, consequently, economic growth (Simson_2019: p.5).

Pamuk (2007) offers insights that complicate the assertion of institutions' irrelevance to long-run economic growth. The author suggests a series of significant changes that occurred in high-wage environments, contributing to Europe's long-term economic development (Pamuk_2007: p.24). These alterations, which encompassed both institutional and other changes, were triggered by the Black Death, a historical event that had far-reaching consequences for the European countryside where most of the population resided (Pamuk_2007: p.24).

Furthermore, Pamuk emphasizes the role of these transformations during the period preceding the Industrial Revolution, thereby implying a connection between institutional changes and long-term economic development in Europe (Pamuk_2007: p.5, p.4). This evidence suggests that while institutions may not be the sole determinant of long-run economic growth, they could play a significant role in fostering structural change, which in turn correlates with growth over extended periods.

The literature review on the relevance of institutions to long-run economic growth presents a complex picture, with some scholars suggesting a significant role for institutions while others argue they are less crucial. North (1989) emphasizes the impact of institutions on transaction costs and economic growth, as well as their potential to foster structural change (North_1989: p.5, p.9). However, his work also acknowledges that institutions can be influenced by external factors such as colonialism (North_1989: p.1).

Bohannan (1959) focuses on the role of institutions in societies like the Tiv, where changes brought about by Western impact affected both market and marriage institutions (Bohannan_1959: p.7, p.10, p.12). Yet, his study does not directly address long-run economic growth.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) suggest that initial conditions, including factor endowments and institutional development, could have profound impacts on long-term growth (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4, p.12). However, their focus is more on the specific impact of colonial environments rather than a general dismissal of institutions' role in economic growth.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) discuss the relationship between institutions and economic growth indirectly, focusing instead on the distribution of wealth and length of life (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.7, p.21). They do not explicitly state that institutions are irrelevant to long-run economic growth.
