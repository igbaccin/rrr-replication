LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The debate surrounding the role of institutions in long-run economic growth has been a subject of extensive research, with various scholars offering contrasting perspectives. At the heart of this discussion is the question of whether institutions are essential drivers of economic growth or merely secondary factors.

Scholars such as Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27) argue that certain institutions can be detrimental to investment and economic progress, suggesting that they may hinder rather than promote long-term growth. For instance, the authors propose that in the context of former colonies, European colonialism led to an institutional reversal that impeded economic development (Acemoglu et al. 2002: p.33).

On the other hand, North & Weingast (North&Weingast_1989: p.2) contend that institutions play a crucial role in enabling governments to commit credibly to upholding property rights and fostering economic growth. They highlight the case of England's Glorious Revolution as an example where new institutions facilitated the transition from feudalism to capitalism, thus promoting long-run economic success (North & Weingast 1989: p.30).

Austin (Austin_2008: p.4) supports the view that institutions are significant determinants of economic growth, asserting that they can be influenced by time-specific interactions between resources and political economy. However, he also acknowledges that the argument oversimplifies the complex relationship between institutions and economic development (Austin 2008: p.24).

Ogilvie (Ogilvie_2007: p.11) agrees with the importance of institutions in economic growth but emphasizes the role of cultural beliefs in shaping institutional structures. He presents evidence suggesting that certain cultural groups, such as Protestants, tend to foster institutions conducive to economic development (Ogilvie 2007: p.12).

Hopkins (Hopkins_2009: p.10) supports the notion that unproductive institutions persist in regions where European influence was minimal or colonial rule was extractive, hindering long-term development. However, he also acknowledges that other factors such as ethnic fragmentation may play a role in shaping institutions (Hopkins 2009: p.22).

The stakes of this debate are high, as understanding the role of institutions in economic growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting development. While some argue that focusing on institutional reform is crucial for long-term growth, others contend that other factors such as cultural beliefs or resource endowments may be more important. This ongoing discussion underscores the need for continued research and nuanced analysis to fully grasp the intricate relationship between institutions and economic development.

The evidence presented in various scholarly works supports the notion that institutions may not be as crucial to long-run economic growth as previously believed. For instance, Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that African growth started before the European scramble for Africa, suggesting that institutional factors might not have played a significant role in this context (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.17).

Similarly, van Zanden (2009) notes that the quality of institutions is not necessarily a determinant of long-term economic growth, as human capital formation and demand-related factors can also significantly influence global patterns (vanZanden_2009: p.24). Moreover, McCloskey (2015) emphasizes the role of ideas and ideologies in shaping economic development, implying that institutions might be less influential compared to these factors (McCloskey_2015: p.5).

Furthermore, Clark & Jacks (2007) demonstrate that in certain historical periods, mineral rents were low despite attempts to restrict production, indicating the potential irrelevance of institutions in determining long-run economic growth (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.14).

Acemoglu and his colleagues (2001) provide substantial evidence to support the notion that certain institutions can have detrimental impacts on investment and economic progress, thereby questioning their relevance in determining long-run economic growth. The authors argue that these unfavorable institutions persist for extended periods due to various economic mechanisms (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.8).

One such mechanism is the self-reinforcing nature of these detrimental institutions, which may hinder structural change and growth. The authors demonstrate that structural changes in economies often correlate with increased productivity and economic growth (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.1). However, when unfavorable institutions are in place, they can impede such structural changes, leading to long-term stagnation.

Furthermore, Acemoglu and colleagues highlight that their findings are not influenced by outliers, ensuring the robustness of their results (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.4). This underscores the potential irrelevance of institutions in determining long-run economic growth, as even when these detrimental institutions persist, they can have a profound impact on a country's development trajectory.

The argument that institutions may be irrelevant to long-run economic growth is further supported by the work of Austin (2008). Austin's research focuses on the rise of Europe and its relationship with Atlantic trade, institutional change, and economic growth (Austin_2008: p.26). He suggests that the interaction between resources and political economy plays a significant role in determining institutions, providing an additional dimension to the argument that institutions are crucial for long-run economic growth (Austin_2008: p.26).

Moreover, Austin's summary of his work emphasizes the idea that institutions may not be the sole cause of long-run economic growth as previously thought (Austin_2008: p.4). He acknowledges that the theoretical conclusion by Acemoglu, Johnson, and Robinson (2001) that institutions are 'the' fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is attractive due to its logical economy. However, Austin's research questions this assertion, suggesting that other factors such as resource endowments and political economy may also play a role in shaping long-term economic growth trajectories (Austin_2008: p.24).

The interplay between resources and political economy adds complexity to this argument, suggesting that multiple factors contribute to long-run economic growth.

Structural change, rather than institutions, may be a more significant determinant of long-run economic growth. Hopkins (Hopkins_2019: p.8) asserts that during the colonial period, the focus shifted from institutions to economic forces, implying structural changes played a crucial role in economic development. Similarly, van Zanden (vanZanden_1999: p.2) suggests that theoretical and empirical studies on pre-industrial growth arrive at comparable conclusions, emphasizing the importance of structural change.

Hopkins (Hopkins_2019: p.9) further argues that as economic bases crumbled, institutions became more important. This perspective challenges the notion that institutions are irrelevant to long-run economic growth, suggesting instead that structural changes may be a more critical factor during periods of economic instability.

Van Zanden (vanZanden_1999: p.4) also questions the direct link between institutions and wages, stating that the wage rate is exogenous for the household and cannot be influenced by institutions in the short or long run. This implies that structural changes, such as shifts in labor markets, may have a more significant impact on wages and economic growth.

The emphasis on economic forces retained but relocated during the colonial period (Hopkins_2019: p.8), suggesting that structural changes may be more pertinent to long-run economic growth than initially thought. However, further exploration is needed to fully understand the complex interplay between institutions and structural change in determining long-term economic development trajectories.

The argument that institutions are irrelevant to long-run economic growth is nuanced when considering institutional flexibility, as highlighted by Pamuk (2007). He suggests that the ability of late medieval Holland to adapt rapidly to new economic opportunities was due to its unusual degree of institutional flexibility (Pamuk_2007: p.23). This implies that flexibility in institutions may play a crucial role in facilitating structural change, which in turn contributes to long-term economic development.

Moreover, Pamuk (2007) emphasizes the importance of regions or countries with more flexible structures and institutions in adapting readily to changing conditions and relative prices (Pamuk_2007: p.25). This suggests that institutional flexibility may enable economies to better respond to shifts in market demands, thereby potentially driving long-term economic growth.

Furthermore, Pamuk (2007) identifies a number of important changes that emerged in high-wage environments and contributed to the long-term economic development of Europe (Pamuk_2007: p.24). This implies that institutional flexibility may allow for the emergence of structural changes that are conducive to sustained economic growth over time.

The literature on the role of institutions in long-run economic growth suggests a complex interplay between institutional factors, environmental conditions, and structural changes. North (1989) emphasizes the importance of institutions in reducing transaction costs and fostering economic growth through increased security rights and centralized enforcement mechanisms. However, he does not explicitly argue that institutions are irrelevant to long-run economic growth.

Temin (2002), on the other hand, discusses the role of labor exhaustion and disequilibrium forces in driving economic growth, but also acknowledges the importance of institutions as part of a broader framework for understanding historical data on economic growth. Bohannan (1959) touches upon the impact of Western economic institutions on societies, particularly with regards to markets and marriage, but does not directly address long-run economic growth.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) explore how initial factor endowments and unique conditions in individual countries may have influenced long-term growth paths, suggesting that institutions could potentially play a role indirectly through their impact on inequality. However, they do not provide conclusive evidence to prove the irrelevance of institutions in long-run economic growth.
