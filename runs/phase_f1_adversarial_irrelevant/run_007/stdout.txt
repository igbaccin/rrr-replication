LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The central question under investigation in this literature review is whether institutions are irrelevant to long-run economic growth, a thesis that has garnered significant attention in economic history. This question holds substantial implications for policy and development strategies, as understanding the role of institutions could guide efforts to promote sustainable economic growth across the globe.

Several scholars argue that institutions play a crucial role in shaping long-term economic growth. For instance, Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27) assert that certain institutions were detrimental to investment and economic progress. Similarly, North & Weingast (North&Weingast_1989: p.2) argue that the new institutions allowed governments to commit credibly to upholding property rights, a necessary condition for long-run economic success. Furthermore, Hopkins (Hopkins_2009: p.10) contends that in regions where the European presence was either marginal or took the form of colonial rule, extractive types of economic control were introduced, which were poorly suited to long-term development.

On the other hand, some scholars offer critiques of this position. Austin (Austin_2008: p.4) suggests that while the theoretical conclusion of institutions as 'the' fundamental cause of long-run economic growth is attractive, it may not fully capture the complexities of historical and economic interactions. Similarly, Ogilvie (Ogilvie_2007: p.11) questions the role of cultural theories in explaining institutional structures, arguing that beliefs are part but not all of a society's institutional structure.

The debate continues as scholars grapple with the intricate interplay between institutions, culture, and historical context in shaping long-run economic growth. The stakes are high, as understanding this relationship could inform policy decisions aimed at promoting sustainable economic development worldwide. This literature review aims to shed light on this complex issue by synthesizing the key arguments and evidence presented in the cited works.

The assertion that institutions are irrelevant to long-run economic growth is counterintuitive, given their significant role in shaping a nation's economy. However, evidence from Acemoglu et al. (2001) suggests otherwise. These authors argue that certain institutional structures can hinder investment and economic progress.

Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27) emphasize that detrimental institutions persist for extended periods, impeding economic development. They illustrate various economic mechanisms that perpetuate these unfavorable conditions over time (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.8). This persistence is not a new concept; it has roots dating back to Karl A's thesis (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.8).

Furthermore, the authors demonstrate that their findings on institutions' impact on performance are robust, as they show that their estimates are not biased by outliers (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.4). These observations imply that institutional structures play a crucial role in determining a country's long-term economic growth trajectory.

The argument that institutions are irrelevant to long-run economic growth can be further supported by considering the perspective of resource-political economy interaction, as presented in Austin's work (Austin_2008). He suggests that the time-specific interaction between resources and political economy plays a significant role in determining institutions, which in turn influences economic growth.

On page 4, Austin summarizes the theoretical conclusion of another work, stating that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth. However, he points out that this view may oversimplify the complex relationship between institutions and economic growth. Instead, he argues for a more nuanced understanding of this relationship, one that takes into account the interaction between resources and political economy in determining institutions.

This perspective is further developed on page 26, where Austin discusses the rise of Europe and its economic growth. He suggests that the timing of institutional change in Europe was closely tied to the availability of resources, particularly Atlantic trade. This interaction between resources and political economy, according to Austin, provides a further dimension to the argument that institutions are crucial for long-term economic growth.

Ogilvie (2007) posits that certain 'civic' cultures foster institutions conducive to economic growth and political accountability, suggesting a significant role for these cultural factors in long-run economic development. He argues that the differences in beliefs and values among Catholics and Hindus, as studied by Weber, led to distinct institutional choices (Ogilvie_2007: p.11). Furthermore, Ogilvie highlights the role of shared beliefs as part of a society's institutional structure, though he questions the game-theoretic view that considers beliefs as institutions themselves (Ogilvie_2007: p.12). These findings imply that cultural factors may play a substantial role in shaping economic institutions and, consequently, long-term economic growth.

The argument that institutions are irrelevant to long-run economic growth is challenged by the perspective that environmental quality and development are intertwined rather than conflicting goals (Stern_2004: p.3). Stern (2004) contends that the traditional view, which posits a conflict between economic development and environmental quality, only reflects the scale effect alone.

According to Stern (2004), a more realistic understanding of the impact of economic growth and technological changes on environmental quality can be achieved by considering median rather than mean income as the relevant variable (Stern_2004: p.9). This shift in focus allows for a better understanding of how structural change, a key indicator of long-run economic growth, correlates with improvements in environmental quality.

Furthermore, Stern (2004) presents projectedsulfur emissions using the Environmental Kuznets Curve (EKC) and UN and World Bank forecasts of economic and population growth (Stern_2004: p.9). The EKC illustrates an inverted U-shaped relationship between income and environmental pollution, suggesting that as economies develop, they may initially experience increased pollution levels but eventually reach a peak and begin to decrease pollution as income continues to rise. This finding challenges the notion that institutions are irrelevant to long-run economic growth, as it implies that economic development can lead to improvements in environmental quality through structural changes and technological advancements.

The evidence presented by Pamuk (2007) indicates that institutional flexibility played a crucial role in facilitating trade with European societies, which in turn contributed to long-run economic growth. He argues that the unique institutional flexibility of late medieval Holland allowed it to swiftly adapt to new economic opportunities (Pamuk_2007: p.23). Furthermore, regions with more flexible structures and institutions were more apt at adapting to changing conditions and relative prices (Pamuk_2007: p.23, p.25).

These findings suggest that while institutions may not be the sole determinant of long-run economic growth, they can significantly facilitate structural changes and technological advancements that drive growth. This nuance complicates the argument that institutions are irrelevant to long-run economic growth, as it implies that certain institutional configurations could potentially accelerate growth by enabling rapid adaptation to changing circumstances.

In essence, Pamuk's research underscores the importance of institutional flexibility in facilitating trade and promoting long-term economic development. However, further investigation is needed to determine whether these findings hold true across different historical periods and geographical regions.

The literature on the relationship between institutions and long-run economic growth presents a nuanced picture, with some scholars emphasizing the importance of institutions while others argue for their irrelevance. North (1989) posits that changes in institutions can lead to increased security rights and lower transaction costs, thereby promoting economic growth. However, he does not explicitly assert that institutions are necessary for long-term economic development.

Temin (2002) focuses on the impact of labor exhaustion and disequilibrium on economic growth, offering an explanation for the Golden Age of European Growth, but does not directly address the role of institutions in this context. Similarly, Bohannan (1959) discusses the influence of Western institutions on societies, yet the focus is more on cultural exchange rather than economic development.

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) suggest that initial factor endowments could have profound and enduring impacts on long-run growth, which may in turn affect institutional development. However, they do not assert that institutions are irrelevant to economic growth.
