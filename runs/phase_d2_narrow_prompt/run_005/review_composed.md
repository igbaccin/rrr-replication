The role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission is a complex and contentious issue, with scholars offering diverse perspectives on its impact. This literature review explores the various arguments surrounding this topic, highlighting key findings and areas of contention.

At the heart of the debate lies the question: To what extent did craft guild regulation influence pre-industrial monetary policy transmission? Some scholars argue that guilds played a significant role in shaping economic policies, while others complicate this notion or offer critiques.

Supporting this thesis, Whatley (2018) provides evidence of the impact of guild regulation on the British gunpowder industry and its relationship with slave exports along the Guinea Coast of Africa (Whatley_2018: p.1). Acemoglu et al. (2002) also support this view, emphasizing the role of urbanization in economic growth, which was often regulated by craft guilds (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.12, p.28).

However, not all scholars agree. Austin (2008) complicates the idea of a 'reversal' in policy, suggesting that adaptations to the agency of the colonized played a significant role (Austin_2008: p.14). North (1989) also argues that while guilds provided internal economic regulation, they could lead to higher costs due to uncertainty and growing private returns, potentially reducing industry demands for regulation (North_1989: p.10).

Ogilvie (2007) and Stern (2004) offer critiques of the traditional view. Ogilvie (2007) points out that each model focusing on a single guild activity ignores others, suggesting a more nuanced understanding is necessary (Ogilvie_2007: p.21). Stern (2004) critiques the idea of a turning point in economic development, arguing that urban and industrial development tends to become more concentrated in a smaller area over time (Stern_2004: p.5).

As this review illustrates, the role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission is a complex issue with various perspectives. Future research should aim to synthesize these diverse viewpoints and develop a more comprehensive understanding of this significant historical phenomenon.

Craft guilds, as key economic and social institutions in pre-industrial Europe, played a significant role in shaping monetary policy transmission through their regulatory activities. The regulation of quality, skill transmission, and market privileges by craft guilds affected the economy at multiple levels.

Firstly, craft guilds controlled the quality of goods produced within their jurisdiction, ensuring uniformity and consistency (Ogilvie_2007: p.21). This standardization could have indirectly influenced monetary policy by maintaining the value of currency in circulation. By regulating the production process, guilds ensured that coins were not debased with substandard materials, thus preserving their intrinsic worth.

Secondly, craft guilds engaged in political rent-seeking, offering benefits to rulers in exchange for market privileges (Ogilvie_2007: p.21). This symbiotic relationship between guilds and ruling authorities allowed for the implementation of favorable monetary policies that favored the interests of both parties. For example, rulers might grant tax exemptions or subsidies to guild members, which could stimulate economic growth and increase the circulation of currency.

Lastly, craft guilds' regulation of technology could have also impacted monetary policy transmission (Ogilvie_2007: p.21). By controlling access to new technologies and techniques, guilds could influence the productivity of their members and, in turn, the overall economy. Higher levels of productivity would lead to increased output, which could result in a higher demand for currency and an improved monetary system.

Despite the assertion that craft guilds could positively influence pre-industrial monetary policy transmission through productivity enhancement and increased currency demand, some scholars challenge this view. Stern (2004) argues that in developing countries, regulation might be informal or decentralized, which could potentially diminish the effectiveness of guild interventions (Stern_2004: p.10). Furthermore, Feinstein (1998) suggests that industrialization may lead to increased work intensity and adverse consequences for family life, which could negatively impact productivity and currency demand (Feinstein_1998: p.4).

Moreover, Humphries (2013) questions the assumption of high living standards during the industrial revolution, citing discrepancies in calorie intake and the extensive use of child workers (Humphries_2013: p.5, p.18). Similarly, Peters (2004) contends that traditional land administration systems might serve the interests of the rural ruling class, potentially undermining guilds' influence on productivity and monetary policy transmission (Peters_2004: p.9, p.35). Lastly, Simson (2019) suggests that patronage in Africa may be personalized and discretionary, which could lead to inconsistencies in guild regulation and their impact on pre-industrial monetary policy transmission (Simson_2019: p.2).

These criticisms suggest that the role of craft guilds in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission may be more complex than initially thought, with potential challenges arising from informal regulation, work conditions, and local power dynamics. Further investigation is needed to fully understand these factors and their implications for guild regulation and its impact on the monetary system.

The complexities surrounding the role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission are further illuminated by several scholarly works. In the context of institutional change, Austin (2008) argues that the "reversal" of economic policies was not a straightforward process, but rather an adaptation to the agency of the colonized (Austin_2008: p.14). This suggests that guild regulation may have evolved in response to external factors, potentially complicating the traditional understanding of its impact on monetary systems.

Moreover, North (1989) contends that guilds were not only vehicles for internal economic regulation but were also used by authorities to provide minute regulation of the economy (North_1989: p.10). This implies a deeper level of state intervention in the monetary system than previously thought, which could have significant implications for the transmission of monetary policy.

Hopkins (2009) highlights that public goods were not easily provided over long distances during the pre-industrial era, suggesting potential challenges in enforcing guild regulations across vast territories (Hopkins_2009: p.19). Furthermore, he argues that the organization and system of controls within guilds relied on the proximity of its members, which could have affected the consistency and effectiveness of these regulations (Hopkins_2009: p.19).

Pamuk (2007) emphasizes the role of urban guilds in technological innovations, providing insights into their potential impact on monetary policy transmission (Pamuk_2007: p.22). However, he also points out that the population decline in Europe during the fourteenth century could have impeded the development and spread of these innovations, potentially limiting the reach and effectiveness of guild regulations.

Allen (2001) argues that the British industrial revolution, a period of significant economic growth and structural change, provides a more substantial test of the role of craft guild regulations in monetary policy transmission (Allen_2001: p.4). However, he also notes that continental Europe, which experienced slower industrialization and population growth, resulted in extremely low wages (Allen_2001: p.25), potentially limiting the effectiveness of similar guild regulations.

Furthermore, Allen (2001) emphasizes that the price of bread, a key component of pre-industrial monetary policy as discussed by UK_2007, was significantly influenced by structural change and economic growth during the Industrial Revolution (Allen_2001: p.31). This suggests that the transmission mechanism of monetary policy through craft guild regulations may have been more nuanced than previously thought, with factors such as industrialization and population growth playing significant roles.

The role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission is a complex issue with varying perspectives among scholars. Temin (2002) and Acemoglu et al. (2001) offer tangential insights that indirectly contribute to understanding this topic.

Temin (2002) discusses industrialization, population growth, and international trade as factors influencing economic development. He suggests that before World War I, participation in international trade promoted industrialization (Temin_2002: p.9). This aligns with the notion that craft guild regulations might have facilitated monetary policy transmission by fostering trade and economic growth. However, Temin also highlights a post-war slowdown in industrialization creating a disequilibrium (Temin_2002: p.9), which could imply a disruption in the transmission mechanism.

Acemoglu et al. (2001) focus on the impact of colonial policies on economic development, emphasizing that the persistence of colonial institutions post-independence affected income per capita (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.10). While this research does not directly address craft guild regulations, it suggests that long-lasting institutional structures could have influenced monetary policy transmission in pre-industrial societies.

Despite these insights, the exact role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission remains unresolved. Further research is needed to establish a clear understanding of how craft guilds affected monetary policy and economic growth during this period. Scholars converge on the idea that institutional structures played a significant role in shaping economic development, but they diverge in their specific focus areas and the extent to which craft guild regulations impacted monetary policy transmission.
