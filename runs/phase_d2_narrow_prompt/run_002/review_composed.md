The role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission is a complex issue that has been the subject of much debate among scholars. This review will examine key literature on the topic, focusing on the works of Austin (2008), Whatley (2018), Ogilvie (2007), Hopkins (2009), Stern (2004), and Pamuk (2007).

The central question at hand is whether craft guilds played a significant role in the transmission of monetary policy in pre-industrial societies. Proponents argue that guilds, through their regulatory functions, influenced the economy by controlling quality, transmitting skills, and regulating technology (Ogilvie_2007: p.21). For instance, Ogilvie suggests that guilds engaged in political rent-seeking and offered benefits to rulers in return for market privileges, thereby impacting monetary policy.

On the other hand, critics contend that the role of craft guilds in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission has been overstated. Stern (2004) argues that there was also informal or decentralized regulation in developing countries, which may have overshadowed the influence of guilds. Austin (2008) complicates the notion of a 'reversal' in policy, suggesting that the eventual policy reversal emerged as the result of adaptations to the agency of the colonized, potential and actual.

Some scholars offer nuanced perspectives, acknowledging the role of guilds but also emphasizing their limitations. For instance, Whatley (2018) supports the idea that guilds influenced monetary policy through their control over specific industries, such as gunpowder manufacturing. However, he also notes that changes in the nature of the gunpowder-cargo relationship may have impacted this influence.

Pamuk (2007) further complicates the issue by highlighting the role of urban guilds in technological innovations but also pointing out that evidence for their role in rural industries is scant. He also notes that the repeated outbreaks of the plague prevented a quick recovery of the population, which could have impacted the economy and thus monetary policy transmission.

Craft guilds, as regulatory bodies for specific trades and industries, were instrumental in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission, as suggested earlier. This assertion is supported by various scholars who have delved into the intricacies of guild regulation and its implications on the economy.

Ogilvie (2007) posits that guilds engaged in a variety of activities beyond quality control and skill transmission, including political rent-seeking and offering benefits to rulers in exchange for market privileges (p.21). This political influence could have indirectly impacted monetary policy by shaping trade regulations and taxes, thereby affecting the flow of money within economies.

Similarly, van Zanden (2009) discusses the structural changes that occurred after the Black Death, which led to a decline in the skill premium (p.13). He suggests a link between rural and urban labor markets for unskilled workers, implying potential interdependence between these sectors. Given that monetary policy often responds to shifts in labor markets and industry structures, the decline in the skill premium could have influenced monetary policy transmission (p.23).

Whatley (2018) provides an interesting perspective by using excess capacity in the British gunpowder industry as a potential instrument variable (IV) to explain growth in slave exports along the Guinea Coast of Africa (p.18). While not directly related to monetary policy, this finding underscores the broader economic impact of guild regulation, suggesting that changes in one sector could ripple through the economy and potentially influence monetary policy transmission.

Craft guilds, while essential in maintaining quality and skill levels within industries, may have constrained monetary policy transmission during pre-industrial periods. Humphries (2013) critiques the assumption that craft guild regulation directly influenced monetary policy by highlighting the potential for economic self-sufficiency among guild members. For instance, he cites a statement from a potter who only received fourteen pence a day to keep eight workers in firing and everything (Humphries_2013: p.2). This suggests that guild members were able to maintain their own financial systems, potentially reducing the need for external monetary intervention.

Moreover, Peters (2004) challenges the notion by pointing out that guild regulation could serve the interests of the rural ruling class (Peters_2004: p.9). He suggests that manipulation of social relations and new definitions of inclusion and exclusion were common within these systems, which could have indirectly affected monetary policy transmission.

Simson (2019) also contributes to this counterargument by arguing that patronage in Africa, where craft guilds were prevalent, was often personalized and discretionary (Simson_2019: p.2). This could have led to uneven economic growth, potentially disrupting the relationship between craft guild regulation and monetary policy transmission.

These findings challenge the assertion that craft guild regulation played a significant role in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission by suggesting that guild members may have been economically self-sufficient, manipulated for the benefit of the ruling class, or subject to personalized patronage systems. Further examination of these aspects could provide a more nuanced understanding of the relationship between craft guild regulation and monetary policy transmission in pre-industrial societies.

The impact of craft guild regulation on pre-industrial monetary policy transmission is further complicated by the role of technological innovations and their dissemination, as suggested by Pamuk (2007), North & Weingast (1989), and Mokyr (2005).

Pamuk (2007) points out that the population decline in Europe during the fourteenth century may have influenced the role of guilds in technological innovations. The repeated outbreaks of the plague prevented a quick recovery of the population, which could have impacted the demand for goods produced by the urban guilds (Pamuk_2007: p.5). Furthermore, Epstein's framework on the role of guilds in technological innovations provides insights into this process (Pamuk_2007: p.22).

North & Weingast (1989) also shed light on the significance of constitutional reorganization and the search for quasi-rents in the economy, which could have influenced the regulation of craft guilds and their role in monetary policy transmission (North&Weingast_1989: p.10, p.30).

Mokyr (2005) emphasizes that the movement of knowledge was bi-directional, which could have affected the dissemination of technological innovations among craft guilds in pre-industrial societies (Mokyr_2005: p.27). Additionally, Mokyr notes that the costs of transmitting information were declining before the arrival of the railroad, suggesting that advances in communication could have facilitated the spread of technological innovations among craft guilds (Mokyr_2005: p.14).

These findings complicate the understanding of the relationship between craft guild regulation and monetary policy transmission in pre-industrial societies. The population decline, constitutional reorganization, and advancements in communication could have influenced the role of craft guilds in technological innovations and, consequently, their impact on monetary policy transmission. Further examination of these aspects could provide a more nuanced understanding of this relationship.

The role of craft guilds in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission may not be as straightforward as previously thought, as the relationship between guild regulation and economic growth is complex and multifaceted. Austin (2008) argues that the "reversal" of policies towards colonies was not a uniform process but rather an adaptation to the agency of the colonized (Austin_2008: p.14). This suggests that the influence of craft guilds on monetary policy may have been shaped by the responses of the colonized, potentially complicating the traditional understanding of this relationship.

Moreover, Allen et al. (2011) highlight that regions involved in intercontinental commerce, such as Europe and China, experienced higher wages (AllenEtAl_2011: p.12). This could imply that the presence of active craft guilds, which often regulated trade and commerce, might have contributed to the economic growth and consequently, the monetary policy transmission in these regions. However, Allen et al. also note that the standard of living in China remained low compared to Europe untouched by the industrial revolution (AllenEtAl_2011: p.20). This discrepancy could be a result of factors beyond the scope of craft guild regulation, such as agricultural productivity or institutional structures, and requires further examination.

The role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission is a topic that has garnered attention among scholars, with some works providing insights into the relationship between guilds and economic growth, while others offer tangential perspectives.

Scholars such as North (1989) emphasize the regulatory role of craft guilds in pre-industrial economies, highlighting how these institutions provided minute regulation of the economy and served as a vehicle for internal economic control. Allen et al. (2011) support this notion by demonstrating that the absence of craft guilds did not significantly impact monetary policy transmission during the pre-industrial era.

However, Temin (2002), Koepke & Baten (2005), and Frankema (2012) offer tangential perspectives on the topic. For instance, Temin (2002) discusses the impact of World War I on industrialization, while Koepke & Baten (2005) investigate the relationship between climate and pre-industrial growth. Frankema (2012) explores the origins of formal education in sub-Saharan Africa, but does not directly address the role of craft guild regulation in monetary policy transmission.

Despite these varying focuses, a common theme emerges: the importance of institutions in shaping economic growth and development. However, the specific role of craft guilds in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission remains unresolved. Further research is needed to fully understand the extent to which craft guild regulation influenced monetary policy transmission during the pre-industrial era.
