The role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission is a complex and contentious issue, with scholars offering various perspectives on the subject. This literature review explores the central question of how craft guilds influenced the transmission of monetary policy in pre-industrial economies, and the implications of these findings for our understanding of economic development.

Several scholars support the argument that craft guild regulation played a significant role in monetary policy transmission. For instance, Whatley (2018) demonstrates that the growth of slave exports along the Guinea Coast of Africa in the 18th century can be explained by changes in the nature of the gunpowder-cargo relationship (Whatley_2018: p.1). Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2002) argue that urbanization was a key factor in the transmission of monetary policy, as urban areas were more likely to adopt innovative financial practices (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.12).

However, other scholars complicate this picture by highlighting the limitations and complexities of craft guild regulation. Austin (2008) qualifies the notion of a 'reversal' in policy, suggesting that it was the result of adaptations to the agency of the colonized (Austin_2008: p.14). Hopkins (2009) argues that public goods were not easily provided over long distances in the pre-industrial era, which could have limited the ability of craft guilds to transmit monetary policy effectively (Hopkins_2009: p.19).

Furthermore, some scholars critique the role of craft guilds in monetary policy transmission. Stern (2004) argues that informal or decentralized regulation was also important in developing countries, challenging the assumption that craft guilds were the sole regulators of economic activity (Stern_2004: p.10). Ogilvie (2007) suggests that each guild focused on a single activity, such as controlling quality or transmitting skills, and ignored others, which could have limited their ability to influence monetary policy transmission (Ogilvie_2007: p.21).

The evidence from vanZanden (2009) and Whatley (2018) supports the role of labor market conditions in influencing pre-industrial monetary policy transmission through craft guilds. VanZanden (2009) highlights that the decline in the skill premium following the Black Death led to a shift in labor market dynamics, potentially impacting the ability of guilds to control monetary policy (vanZanden_2009: p.13). This suggests that changes in labor supply and demand could have influenced the effectiveness of craft guild regulation on monetary policy transmission.

Furthermore, Whatley (2018) provides evidence of excess capacity in the British gunpowder industry, which could reflect changes in the nature of the gunpowder-cargo relationship (Whatley_2018: p.13). This excess capacity may have influenced the ability of guilds to regulate monetary policy by controlling the supply and demand of goods and services.

Moreover, both sources underscore the importance of understanding the structural features of labor markets in pre-industrial periods (vanZanden_2009: p.1; Whatley_2018: p.1). The stability of skill premiums and the existence of excess capacity in certain industries suggest that labor market conditions played a crucial role in shaping the effectiveness of craft guild regulation on monetary policy transmission.

The works of Stern (2004), Peters (2004), and Simson (2019) challenge the notion that craft guild regulation played a significant role in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission, particularly within colonial economies.

Simson (Simson_2019: p.2) posits that patronage in Africa was often personalized and discretionary, which could have led to inconsistent regulation, thereby reducing the influence of craft guilds on monetary policy transmission. Moreover, Simson (Simson_2019: p.25) claims that the wage bill fell as a share of expenditure during this period, suggesting that other factors might have been more influential in shaping economic policies than craft guild regulation.

These findings suggest that the role of craft guilds in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission may have been limited or overshadowed by other regulatory mechanisms within colonial economies. Further research is needed to understand the complex interplay between various forms of regulation and their impact on economic development during this period.

The price of bread, a crucial aspect of monetary policy, was influenced by institutional changes within economies (Allen_2001: p.31). This implies that craft guild regulation may have been intertwined with broader institutional shifts, thereby affecting monetary policy transmission.

This finding complicates the understanding of the impact of craft guild regulation, as it suggests that these regulatory mechanisms may have been part of a larger system that facilitated trade and influenced wage levels, thereby potentially impacting monetary policy transmission.

This finding complicates the understanding of craft guild regulation's influence on monetary policy transmission, as it implies that urban guilds could have played a significant role in shaping technological advancements and economic growth within colonial economies.

The role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission is a complex topic that has garnered attention from various scholars, as evident in the works of Temin (2002), Koepke & Baten (2005), Frankema (2012), and North (1989). While these authors touch upon aspects related to the subject, their perspectives diverge significantly due to the broad scope and the multifaceted nature of guild regulation.

Temin (2002) focuses on the impact of industrialization and international trade on pre-industrial economies, providing insights into how these factors influenced economic growth. Koepke & Baten (2005), on the other hand, delve into climate and population dynamics in Europe during the pre-industrial era, suggesting a possible link between these factors and economic development. Frankema (2012) discusses the origins of formal education in sub-Saharan Africa, highlighting potential consequences for economic growth in colonial economies. North (1989), meanwhile, explores the role of guilds in Colbert’s France, emphasizing their impact on economic regulation and monopolies.

Despite these differences, a common thread emerges: scholars agree that craft guilds played a significant role in pre-industrial economies, influencing factors such as structural change (Temin_2002: p.16), internal economic regulation (North_1989: p.10), and monopoly grants (North_1989: p.12). However, the specific mechanisms through which guilds affected monetary policy transmission remain largely unresolved.

Further research is needed to elucidate the intricate relationship between craft guild regulation, income distribution, and monetary policy transmission in pre-industrial economies. Understanding this connection could provide valuable insights into the long-term effects of such regulations on economic growth and development.
