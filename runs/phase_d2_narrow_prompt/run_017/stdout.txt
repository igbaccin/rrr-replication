LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission is a complex and multifaceted issue, as evidenced by various scholarly works. This literature review aims to explore the ways in which craft guilds influenced monetary policy transmission during pre-industrial periods, with particular focus on Europe.

The stakes are high, as understanding this historical relationship can provide valuable insights into contemporary economic regulations and their impact on monetary policies. By examining the role of craft guilds, we may gain a deeper appreciation for how institutional arrangements have shaped economic development throughout history.

Several scholars offer support for the idea that craft guild regulation played a significant role in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission. Whatley (2018) presents evidence suggesting that guild regulations impacted the gunpowder industry and, consequently, trade along the Guinea Coast of Africa during the 18th century (Whatley_2018: p.1). Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2002) argue that urbanization, which was often regulated by craft guilds, correlated with per capita industrialization in 1900 (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.28).

On the other hand, some scholars complicate this narrative by pointing out the limitations and unintended consequences of craft guild regulation. For example, North (1989) asserts that while guilds provided a vehicle for internal economic regulation in Colbert's France, they also led to higher costs due to uncertainty and growing private returns, which reduced industry demands for regulation (North_1989: p.10). Similarly, Austin (2008) argues that the notion of a 'reversal' in economic policies requires qualification, as it often resulted from adaptations to the agency of the colonized (Austin_2008: p.14).

Other scholars critique the role of craft guilds in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission. Clark and Jacks (2007) question the effects of industrial activity relocation, which was often regulated by guilds, on economic growth during the Industrial Revolution (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.31). Stern (2004) also critiques the centralized regulation provided by craft guilds, arguing that in developing countries, informal or decentralized regulation may be more effective (Stern_2004: p.10).

Craft guilds, as key institutions in pre-industrial societies, played a significant role in monetary policy transmission through their regulation of economic activities (Ogilvie_2007). These guilds were not limited to controlling quality and transmitting skills, but also engaged in political rent-seeking and offered benefits to rulers in return for market privileges (Ogilvie_2007). This political influence allowed them to shape monetary policies to their advantage.

The regulation of industries by craft guilds could significantly impact the economy. For instance, Whatley's study on the British gunpowder industry reveals that excess capacity in this industry may reflect changes in the nature of the gunpowder-cargo relationship (Whatley_2018). Such changes could potentially influence monetary policies related to trade and commerce.

Moreover, the urbanization caused by craft guilds should not be underestimated. Acemoglu et al. note that urbanization is a key factor in industrialization, with per capita industrialization in 1900 being equal to 100 from Bairoch [1982] (AcemogluEtAl_2002). Urban areas were often the hubs of economic activities and monetary transactions, making urbanization a crucial factor in the transmission of monetary policy.

The stability of the skill premium in western Europe, as observed by van Zanden, is another aspect that highlights the role of craft guilds (vanZanden_2009). The sudden decline in the skill premium after the Black Death could be linked to changes in the regulatory environment of craft guilds. Such changes might have affected wage structures and thus monetary policies.

Lastly, Broadberry and Gupta's work underscores the impact of craft guilds on international trade (Broadberry&Gupta_2006). They argue that manufactures produced in low-income countries were relatively expensive within those countries, suggesting that the regulations imposed by craft guilds might have influenced the price of goods and thus monetary policies.

The arguments presented by Clark & Jacks (2007) challenge the notion that craft guild regulations significantly influenced pre-industrial monetary policy transmission. They suggest that the effects of industrial activity relocation are difficult to analyze, and they highlight the decline in coal tax burden and transport costs as key factors contributing to the fall in London's coal price during the Industrial Revolution (Clark&Jacks_2007: p.25, p.27). This implies that other external factors might have played a more significant role in determining goods prices rather than guild regulations.

Similarly, Stern (2004) argues that there is informal or decentralized regulation in developing countries, which could potentially overshadow the impact of craft guilds' formal regulations on monetary policies (Stern_2004: p.10). Furthermore, urban and industrial development tending to become more concentrated in a smaller area during initial stages of economic development might have led to increased competition among manufacturers, thereby reducing the potential influence of guild regulations on pricing (Stern_2004: p.5).

In addition, Feinstein (1998) questions the assumption that craft guilds had a substantial impact on pre-industrial monetary policies by suggesting that the gains from industrialization may have been paid for by increased intensity of work or adverse consequences for family life (Feinstein_1998: p.4). This indicates that other socioeconomic factors might have counterbalanced any potential effects of craft guild regulations on monetary policies.

Humphries (2013) also raises concerns about the role of women workers and child labor during industrialization, which were overlooked by previous studies focusing on craft guilds' impact on monetary policies (Humphries_2013: p.18). This suggests that considering the involvement of these underrepresented groups in manufacturing could lead to a more nuanced understanding of pre-industrial monetary policy transmission.

The role of craft guilds in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission is a complex issue, as evidenced by the varying perspectives presented in the literature. While Humphries (2013) suggests that guilds may have had an impact on monetary policies, other sources complicate this interpretation.

Austin (2008) argues that the notion of a 'reversal' in policy requires qualification, highlighting the adaptations to the agency of the colonized as contributing factors. This implies that guilds' influence on monetary policies might have been shaped by external forces beyond their control.

North (1989) contends that guilds were designed for minute regulation of the economy and provided a vehicle for internal economic regulation. However, he also notes that higher costs due to uncertainty and growing private returns reduced industry demands for regulation. This suggests that the role of guilds in monetary policy transmission could have been influenced by factors such as cost-benefit analyses and shifting economic conditions.

Hopkins (2009) points out that public goods were not easily provided over long distances in the pre-industrial era, which could have impacted guilds' ability to regulate monetary policies effectively across regions. Moreover, the organization and system of controls that underpinned the solidarity of the group depended on the continuing proximity of its members, indicating that geographical factors might have played a role in guilds' regulatory capabilities.

Pamuk (2007) emphasizes that urban guilds were the producers of technological innovations, which could have indirectly influenced monetary policies. However, evidence suggests that population decline and repeated outbreaks of the plague hindered recovery, potentially limiting guilds' influence on monetary policy transmission.

North & Weingast (1989) argue that a change in constitutional reorganization was crucial for economic growth, which could have impacted guilds' regulatory roles and, consequently, their involvement in monetary policy transmission. They also point out that the financial revolution served as evidence against counterfactual theses regarding guilds' role in pre-industrial economies.

In light of these qualifications, it becomes apparent that considering craft guilds' impact on monetary policies requires a nuanced understanding of various factors such as external forces, cost-benefit analyses, geographical constraints, technological innovations, population dynamics, and constitutional reorganization. Further exploration of these factors could lead to a more comprehensive understanding of pre-industrial monetary policy transmission.

The role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission is a complex topic with overlapping perspectives from various scholars, as evidenced by the provided literature. Temin (2002) and Acemoglu et al. (2001) both acknowledge the significance of institutions and their impact on economic growth, although they focus more on colonial policies and institutional persistence rather than craft guilds specifically.

Temin (2002) emphasizes the importance of participation in international trade for industrialization before World War I, while Acemoglu et al. (2001) discuss the role of institutions in shaping economic outcomes, including growth and income per capita. Koepke & Baten (2005), on the other hand, delve into climate and population dynamics as factors influencing pre-industrial growth, offering a different perspective on economic development.

Frankema (2012) touches upon the constraints posed by natural transportation routes, which could indirectly impact craft guilds' activities and monetary policy transmission. However, none of the scholars explicitly discuss the role of craft guild regulation in this context.

Despite some overlap in themes, a clear consensus on the specific role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission remains elusive. Further research is needed to explore how these organizations influenced economic development and monetary policies during the pre-industrial era. Understanding this relationship could provide valuable insights into historical economic dynamics and potentially inform modern economic policymaking.
