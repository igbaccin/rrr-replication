The role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission is a topic that has garnered significant attention among historians, economists, and political scientists. This review explores the literature on this subject, highlighting the central question, key positions, and the stakes involved.

The central question at hand is whether craft guild regulation played a crucial role in transmitting monetary policy during pre-industrial periods. The stakes are high, as understanding this relationship could shed light on the economic development of various regions and offer insights into modern monetary policy design.

Several scholars support the thesis that craft guilds significantly influenced monetary policy transmission. For instance, Whatley (Whatley_2018: p.1) argues that guilds played a crucial role in shaping trade patterns, while Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.12) suggest that urbanization, facilitated by guild regulation, was a key driver of economic growth. Ogilvie (Ogilvie_2007: p.21) adds that guilds engaged in various activities, including political rent-seeking and offering benefits to rulers in return for market privileges, thereby influencing monetary policy.

However, not all scholars agree with this perspective. Austin (Austin_2008: p.26) complicates the notion of a 'reversal' in monetary policy, suggesting that adaptations to the agency of the colonized played a significant role. North (North_1989: p.12) also argues that guilds in Colbert’s France were granted monopolies, which produced specific results but did not necessarily transmit monetary policy. Hopkins (Hopkins_2009: p.19) further complicates the issue by pointing out that providing public goods over long distances was challenging in pre-industrial eras, potentially limiting the role of guilds in transmitting monetary policy.

The role of craft guilds in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission is further substantiated by Whatley (2018). By utilizing a measure of excess capacity in the British gunpowder industry as an instrumental variable, Whatley (Whatley_2018: p.18) provides evidence that structural changes within industries could have significant implications for monetary policy transmission. This suggests that guilds, which often regulated various industries, may have played a crucial role in transmitting monetary policy changes throughout pre-industrial economies.

Moreover, Whatley (Whatley_2018: p.1) demonstrates the growth of slave exports along the Guinea Coast of Africa in the 18th century and attributes it to changes in the nature of the gunpowder-cargo relationship. This finding supports the argument that guilds, which often regulated trade, could have influenced monetary policy transmission through their control over key industries and commodities.

Furthermore, Whatley (Whatley_2018: p.13) notes a potential shift in the gunpowder-cargo relationship during the 18th century. This change could be indicative of adjustments in monetary policy transmission as guilds adapted to new economic conditions. Additionally, the author mentions autocorrelation in the 3-period and 4-period models (Whatley_2018: p.11), which may suggest that previous periods influenced subsequent ones, further supporting the idea of guilds' role in transmitting monetary policy over time.

The works of Feinstein (1998), Humphries (2013), and Peters (2004) challenge the role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission by questioning certain aspects that support this thesis.

Feinstein (Feinstein_1998: p.29) critiques the notion of increased well-being during industrialization, stating that it may come at the cost of increased work intensity or adverse consequences for family life. Moreover, Feinstein (Feinstein_1998: p.4) argues that the gains from industrialization might not be as significant as suggested by some accounts of trends in living standards.

Humphries (Humphries_2013: p.5) also provides evidence against the thesis by questioning the accuracy of certain figures, such as the 1,914 calories per day cited for workers during the industrial revolution. Additionally, Humphries (Humphries_2013: p.18) criticizes the historical wage-productivity (HWE) paradigm for ignoring the role of women and child workers during industrialization.

Peters (Peters_2004: p.9) challenges the thesis by suggesting that craft guild regulation may have served the interests of the rural ruling class, rather than promoting fair and effective monetary policy transmission. Furthermore, Peters (Peters_2004: p.35) argues that these regulations could lead to more defined boundaries and new definitions of inclusion and exclusion.

These counterarguments suggest that craft guild regulation may not have played as significant a role in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission as previously thought, and further investigation into alternative factors influencing economic growth during this period is warranted.

The role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission may have been oversimplified, as suggested by Hopkins (2009). He argues that the provision of public goods over long distances was challenging during this era, implying that localized guild regulations might not have had a significant impact on economic growth beyond their immediate vicinity (Hopkins_2009: p.19). Furthermore, Hopkins suggests that the organization and system of controls within guilds depended on the proximity of its members, potentially limiting their influence on economic development in regions with dispersed populations (Hopkins_2009: p.19).

Allen et al. (2011) also complicate the understanding of craft guild regulation's role in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission. They argue that high wages in regions involved in intercontinental commerce might have been due to factors other than guild regulations, such as increased productivity or access to valuable resources (AllenEtAl_2011: p.12). Moreover, they point out that the standard of living in China remained low, even after centuries of economic development, suggesting that alternative factors may have played a more significant role in determining regional growth rates (AllenEtAl_2011: p.20).

Mokyr (2005) adds nuance to the argument by emphasizing the bi-directional movement of knowledge during pre-industrial times, suggesting that ideas and innovations could have spread beyond the confines of individual guilds (Mokyr_2005: p.27). This implies that other factors, such as the diffusion of technological advancements or trade networks, may have played a more substantial role in shaping economic growth during this period.

The role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission is further complicated by the intricate economic control exercised by these institutions, as suggested by North (1989). Guilds, in their quest for minute regulation of the economy, functioned not only as self-governing bodies within individual trades but also as vehicles for internal economic regulation (North_1989: p.10). This extensive control was exemplified by Colbert's France, where monopolies granted to guilds led to a comprehensive regulation of the economy (North_1989: p.12).

Moreover, North & Weingast (1989) underscore the potential for guilds to systematically search for and expropriate quasi-rents in the economy, which could have significant implications for monetary policy transmission. This expropriation could potentially impact the availability of capital or credit, thus influencing economic growth (North&Weingast_1989: p.10).

In addition, the centralized enforcement mechanisms employed by Spain offer insight into the potential role of guilds in shaping economic growth. North (1989) suggests that these mechanisms could have played a part in the examination of economic growth, further complicating the understanding of guilds' influence on monetary policy transmission (North_1989: p.1).

However, it is important to note that the high costs associated with uncertainty and growing private returns may have reduced industry demands for regulation, potentially limiting the role of guilds in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission (North_1989: p.10).

The findings from North & Weingast (1989) also challenge the counterfactual thesis that guilds were essential for economic growth. For instance, they point to the financial revolution as evidence against this thesis, suggesting that alternative institutions or mechanisms may have contributed more significantly to economic development (North&Weingast_1989: p.30).

Scholars generally agree that pre-industrial economic growth was influenced by various factors, with some emphasizing the role of craft guilds in monetary policy transmission. However, they diverge on the significance and extent of this impact.

Temin (2002) suggests that participation in international trade promoted industrialization before World War I, but does not directly address the role of craft guilds in this process. Abramovitz (1986) discusses the potentiality of a trade-off between specialization and adaptability, which could indirectly imply a role for guilds in shaping industrial development.

On the other hand, Koepke & Baten (2005) and Frankema (2012) focus more on climate, population density, and geographical factors as determinants of pre-industrial growth, offering little insight into the role of craft guilds.

The consensus that emerges is the recognition of multiple factors contributing to economic development in pre-industrial periods. However, the specific role of craft guild regulation in monetary policy transmission remains unresolved. Future research should focus on examining the direct impact of craft guilds on monetary policy and its transmission mechanism during pre-industrial times.
