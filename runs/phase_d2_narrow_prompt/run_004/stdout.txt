LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission serves as a crucial yet complex aspect in understanding economic development during this period. This review examines various scholarly perspectives on the subject, revealing both supporting and critiquing arguments, as well as those that complicate or add nuance to the debate.

Central to the discussion is the question of whether craft guilds, by regulating industries, played a significant role in shaping monetary policy transmission in pre-industrial economies. North (1989) argues that guilds were designed for minute regulation of the economy and provided a vehicle for internal economic regulation, which could have impacted monetary policies. Acemoglu et al. (2002) support this notion by highlighting the positive relationship between urbanization and income in pre-industrial cities, suggesting that guilds may have contributed to economic prosperity through their regulatory activities.

However, not all scholars agree with these perspectives. Austin (2008) complicates the argument by qualifying the concept of a 'reversal' itself, suggesting that other factors such as domestic labor market policies and terms of access to product markets may also play significant roles in economic development. Whatley (2018) supports the notion but offers a nuanced perspective, arguing that the net advantage of firearms in the capture and transport of slaves—a key aspect of pre-industrial economies—is not always evident.

Ogilvie (2007) provides additional support for the argument by noting that guilds engaged in a variety of activities, including political rent-seeking and offering benefits to rulers in return for market privileges, which could have impacted monetary policies. Yet, Stern (2004) critiques this perspective by pointing out that results depend on assumptions made and the values of particular parameters, suggesting that the role of craft guilds in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission may not be as straightforward as some scholars suggest.

The stakes are high, as understanding the role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission can provide valuable insights into the historical roots of modern economic policies and development strategies. The debate continues with scholars exploring further aspects such as the impact of guilds on technology, skill transmission, and political rent-seeking, offering a rich and complex picture of this intriguing topic.

Craft guilds significantly influenced pre-industrial monetary policy transmission through their regulatory activities, as supported by Ogilvie (2007) and van Zanden (2009). Guilds' control over quality and technology, as well as their political rent-seeking behaviors, directly affected economic growth and monetary policies.

Ogilvie (Ogilvie_2007: p.21) notes that guilds focused on specific activities such as controlling quality and transmitting skills, which could impact the overall efficiency of the economy. The comparative advantage of overlords in providing protection to serfs also enabled them to coercively intervene in serf transactions, potentially influencing monetary policies (Ogilvie_2007: p.21). Furthermore, guilds engaged in political rent-seeking and offered benefits to rulers in return for market privileges, further emphasizing their role in shaping pre-industrial monetary policy transmission (Ogilvie_2007: p.21).

Similarly, van Zanden (vanZanden_2009: p.19) argues that the efficiency of institutions regulating the training of apprentices and access to professions played a role in the supply side of the economic equation. The organization of skill transfer from one generation to another could affect the skill premium, thereby influencing monetary policies (van Zanden_2009: p.19). In some parts of Asia, being a craftsman was largely hereditary, suggesting that the transmission of skills was not always based on merit but could be influenced by social status, potentially impacting economic growth and monetary policies (van Zanden_2009: p.20).

The argument that craft guild regulation significantly influenced pre-industrial monetary policy transmission may face challenges, as suggested by several scholars. For instance, Stern (2004) criticizes the assumption that urban and industrial development became more concentrated in a smaller area during the initial stages of economic development (Stern_2004: p.5). This could imply that craft guilds did not have a significant impact on monetary policy transmission due to their geographical dispersion.

Similarly, Feinstein (1998) questions the assertion that the existence of an abundant labor supply was a central feature in economic development during the first half of the nineteenth century (Feinstein_1998: p.28). This contradicts the idea that craft guilds, which typically limited the number of apprentices and journeymen, played a significant role in monetary policy transmission through labor control.

Peters (2004) also challenges the notion that craft guilds served as a democratic principle in traditional land administration, arguing that they could manipulate social relations and serve the interests of the rural ruling class (Peters_2004: p.9). This suggests that the role of craft guilds in monetary policy transmission may not have been as democratic or beneficial as previously thought.

Moreover, Humphries (2013) criticizes the HWE paradigm for ignoring the role of women workers during industrialization and the extensive use of child workers, which could imply that the economic impact of craft guilds, traditionally male-dominated institutions, may have been overstated (Humphries_2013: p.18).

Lastly, Simson (2019) argues that patronage in Africa was not always personalized and discretionary, which could suggest that the role of craft guilds, often influenced by patronage, may have been less significant than previously believed in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission.

The role of craft guilds in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission is a complex issue, as suggested by various scholars. North (1989) highlights that the granting of monopolies to guilds in Colbert’s France produced this result, indicating a direct relationship between guild regulation and economic control. Moreover, he argues that guilds provided a vehicle for internal economic regulation, which could have implications for monetary policy transmission (North_1989: p.12).

However, Lindert & Williamson (1985) complicate this perspective by emphasizing the labor surplus and inequality associated with accelerating industrialization. They suggest that taxing land site rents can raise the rate of accumulation, which could potentially reduce the need for guild regulation in monetary policy transmission (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.21).

Pamuk (2007) provides insights into the role of urban guilds in technological innovations, suggesting that they were significant producers in this regard. However, he also notes that the Black Death led to far-reaching institutional and other changes in the countryside, which could have indirectly affected guild regulation and monetary policy transmission (Pamuk_2007: p.24).

North & Weingast (1989) argue that a constitutional reorganization was instrumental in facilitating significant economic changes in a short period. They suggest that while substantial constraints were placed on the king, these did not reduce him to a figurehead, which could have implications for guild regulation and monetary policy transmission (North&Weingast_1989: p.16).

The claim that the rise of Europe was influenced by institutional changes and economic growth, as suggested by Austin (Austin_2008: p.26), adds nuance to the role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission. Austin argues that domestic labor market policies and terms of access to product markets were equally relevant factors contributing to Europe's economic growth during this period.

This perspective complicates the notion that the constraints placed on medieval kings, as discussed by North & Weingast (1989), directly influenced guild regulation and monetary policy transmission. Instead, it suggests that a combination of institutional changes, labor market policies, and access to product markets played crucial roles in shaping Europe's economic development.

Moreover, Austin (Austin_2008: p.21) emphasizes the importance of considering colonial origins when examining comparative development. This perspective could potentially shed light on how the institutional structures established during the colonial period may have indirectly impacted guild regulation and monetary policy transmission in pre-industrial Europe.

The examination of the role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission reveals a complex interplay of factors, with some overlap yet notable differences among scholars. Frankema (2012) emphasizes the influence of colonial administrations and metropolitan policies on the Africanization of missions, as well as the preferential conditions offered to Catholic societies, albeit tangentially related to guild regulation and monetary policy transmission.

Temin (2002) focuses on the impact of industrialization, participation in international trade, and historical events such as World War I on economic transformation, again providing tangential insights into pre-industrial monetary policy transmission. Koepke & Baten (2005) explore climate, diet, and population density in relation to pre-industrial growth, offering minimal direct contributions to the understanding of guild regulation and monetary policy transmission.

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) address periods of stagnation or shrinking in populations and real wages, highlighting the role of internal instability and political turmoil, which may indirectly influence guild regulation and monetary policy transmission. However, they do not explicitly focus on these specific aspects.

Despite some tangential connections, a clear consensus regarding the direct impact of craft guild regulation on pre-industrial monetary policy transmission remains elusive in the available evidence. Further research is needed to establish a comprehensive understanding of this relationship and its implications for economic development during pre-industrial periods.
