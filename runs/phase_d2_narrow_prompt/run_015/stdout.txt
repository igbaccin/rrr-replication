The role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission is a topic that has garnered significant attention among economists, as it sheds light on the intricate relationships between economic institutions, technological advancements, and economic growth during the pre-industrial era. This review explores the perspectives presented by various scholars, revealing a complex interplay of support, critique, and nuance regarding the impact of craft guilds on monetary policy transmission.

At the heart of this debate lies the question: To what extent did craft guild regulation influence the transmission of monetary policy in pre-industrial societies? Proponents argue that guilds played a crucial role in shaping economic policies, while critics contend that their impact was limited or even detrimental.

Among those who support the thesis is Ogilvie (2007), who asserts that guilds engaged in various activities such as controlling quality, transmitting skills, and regulating technology, thereby influencing monetary policy transmission (Ogilvie_2007: p.21). Similarly, Whatley (2018) suggests that the preference for firearms in the capture and transport of slaves could be evidence of their advantage in the economy (Whatley_2018: p.4), potentially impacting monetary policy through trade dynamics.

However, not all scholars agree with this viewpoint. Austin (2008) complicates the narrative by questioning the notion of a 'reversal' and emphasizing the profitability of slave plantation systems in the Americas (Austin_2008: p.22, p.26). Similarly, Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that factors such as wage stretching and changes in the labor force could have influenced monetary policy transmission (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.4, p.25).

Stern (2004) and Feinstein (1998), on the other hand, offer critiques of this perspective. Stern questions the assumptions made in models studying monetary policy transmission and the validity of certain results (Stern_2004: p.4, p.8). Meanwhile, Feinstein challenges the accuracy of some indicators used to measure living standards during pre-industrial times, potentially affecting our understanding of monetary policy transmission (Feinstein_1998: p.11, p.24, p.28, p.29).

These diverse viewpoints underscore the complexity of the relationship between craft guild regulation and monetary policy transmission in pre-industrial societies. The debate continues as scholars delve deeper into understanding the intricacies of economic institutions and their role in shaping economic policies during this critical period of history.

The presence of slave labor significantly impacted economic growth in pre-industrial societies, as supported by Whatley's (2018) research. While it is often assumed that technological advancements like guns facilitated the capture and transportation of slaves, Whatley (Whatley_2018: p.4) challenges this notion, suggesting that the net advantage is not always certain. Instead, he posits that the preference for guns in slave acquisition could be due to factors other than their effectiveness, such as changes in social or economic conditions.

Moreover, Whatley's (Whatley_2018: p.8) study reveals that structural change correlates with growth, indicating that shifts in economic institutions and practices can lead to increased productivity and prosperity. This finding is particularly relevant when considering the role of craft guilds in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission, as changes within these organizations could potentially influence the overall economic trajectory of a society.

Furthermore, Whatley (Whatley_2018: p.13) suggests that there might have been shifts in the relationship between gunpowder and cargo trade during the 18th century. This observation underscores the dynamic nature of economic relationships in pre-industrial societies and highlights the importance of understanding these complex interactions to fully appreciate the role of craft guilds in monetary policy transmission.

The works of Stern (2004) and Peters (2004) present challenges to the role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission by questioning the correlation between structural change and economic development.

Stern (2004) argues that the relationship between urban and industrial development and economic growth may not be as directly linked as previously thought, with the initial stages of economic development tending to concentrate in a smaller area rather than spread evenly (Stern_2004: p.5). Furthermore, Stern contends that the results of studies on this topic depend heavily on the assumptions made and the values of specific parameters, casting doubt on the robustness of findings supporting the role of craft guilds in monetary policy transmission (Stern_2004: p.4).

Peters (2004) supports these concerns by highlighting that changes in boundaries and definitions of inclusion can significantly impact economic relationships in pre-industrial societies, potentially undermining the stability and predictability required for effective monetary policy transmission (Peters_2004: p.35). Additionally, Peters suggests that the risky natural and political-economic climates faced by these societies may lead to strategies that prioritize security over economic growth, further complicating the role of craft guilds in monetary policy transmission (Peters_2004: p.14).

These critiques underscore the complexity of economic interactions in pre-industrial societies and emphasize the need for a nuanced understanding of these relationships to fully appreciate the role of craft guilds in monetary policy transmission.

The nuanced understanding of economic interactions in pre-industrial societies further evolves as we delve into the complexities of institutional changes and their impact on growth. Austin (2008) emphasizes that the concept of a 'reversal' itself requires qualification, suggesting that the rise of Europe was not solely due to a reversal from the decline in the Byzantine economy but also involved other significant factors such as Atlantic trade and institutional change (Austin_2008: p.26).

Moreover, North & Weingast (1989) highlight how the search for quasi-rents in the economy led to systematic expropriation, which might have influenced the role of craft guilds in monetary policy transmission (North&Weingast_1989: p.10). This underscores the importance of understanding the dynamics of power and resource distribution within these societies to fully appreciate the role of institutions like craft guilds.

Pamuk (2007) provides insights into the role of urban guilds in technological innovations, complicating the notion that rural industries were the primary drivers (Pamuk_2007: p.22). Additionally, he discusses how the Black Death improved the position of journeymen, potentially affecting the power dynamics within craft guilds and their involvement in monetary policy transmission.

Mokyr (2005) sheds light on the declining costs of information transmission before the Industrial Revolution, suggesting that improvements in this area could have influenced the role of craft guilds as information hubs in pre-industrial economies (Mokyr_2005: p.14).

The role of craft guilds in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission is further complicated by the changing socioeconomic landscape, as highlighted by Lindert & Williamson (1985) and Hopkins (2009).

Lindert & Williamson (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.25) argue that the first decade of the 20th century witnessed a resurgence in wage stretching, which could have impacted the economic standing of craft guilds and their ability to regulate monetary policies effectively. Furthermore, they suggest that the decline in the share of unskilled labor may have contributed to this trend (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.25). This shift in labor composition could have potentially altered the power dynamics within guilds and their influence on monetary policy transmission.

Hopkins (Hopkins_2009: p.19) emphasizes that providing public goods over long distances was challenging in the pre-industrial era, which raises questions about the extent to which craft guilds could disseminate information effectively across distant regions. Additionally, Hopkins (Hopkins_2009: p.19) explains that the organization and control systems of guilds relied on the proximity of its members, suggesting that these institutions may have struggled to adapt and maintain their influence as urbanization and population density increased.

These findings complicate the argument that craft guild regulation played a significant role in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission by demonstrating potential challenges faced by these organizations due to changing socioeconomic conditions. Further investigation is required to understand the full impact of these factors on the role of craft guilds in economic development and monetary policy during the pre-industrial period.

The role of craft guilds in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission is a complex topic that has been explored by various scholars, each offering unique insights. North (1989) and Temin (2002) both acknowledge the regulatory function of guilds, with North emphasizing their minute regulation of the economy and internal economic control, while Temin highlights their role in industrialization processes. Scholars converge in recognizing the significant impact of craft guilds on pre-industrial economies. However, they diverge in their focus areas and the extent to which guilds influenced monetary policy transmission. North (1989) and Temin (2002) provide evidence suggesting that guilds played a role in economic regulation, but neither directly discuss monetary policy transmission. Komlos (1998), while not focusing on monetary policy, does highlight the urbanization and population growth associated with guild-dominated economies, which could indirectly impact monetary policy.

Despite these insights, several questions remain unresolved. For instance, the specific mechanisms through which craft guilds influenced economic development and monetary policy transmission during the pre-industrial period require further investigation.
