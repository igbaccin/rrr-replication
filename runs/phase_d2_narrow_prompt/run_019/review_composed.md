In the realm of pre-industrial economic history, a significant debate revolves around the role of craft guild regulation in monetary policy transmission. This study aims to shed light on this contentious issue by examining perspectives from various scholarly works.

At the heart of this discourse is the question: To what extent did craft guilds influence monetary policy and economic growth in pre-industrial societies? The stakes are high, as understanding this relationship could provide valuable insights into the evolution of modern economies.

Some scholars argue that craft guilds played a pivotal role in shaping monetary policy transmission. For instance, Austin (Austin_2008: p.14) contends that guild regulations "eventually policy reversal emerged as the result of adaptations to the agency of the colonised, potential and actual." Similarly, Whatley (Whatley_2018: p.18) supports this view by suggesting that excess capacity in the British gunpowder industry could be linked to guild regulation.

On the other hand, North (North_1989: p.12) complicates this argument by asserting that the granting of monopolies to guilds in Colbert’s France produced unfavorable results. Ogilvie (Ogilvie_2007: p.21) also adds a nuanced perspective, stating that each model focusing on a single guild activity ignores others, thereby potentially underestimating the impact of guild regulation.

The debate is further complicated by those who argue that guilds may have hindered economic growth. Hopkins (Hopkins_2009: p.19) suggests that public goods were not easily provided over long distances in the pre-industrial era, which could have been exacerbated by guild regulations. Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.52) also argue that while urbanization was positively correlated with economic growth, the relatively late starting date of their urbanization series may limit its usefulness in this debate.

As this brief review illustrates, the role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission is a complex and multifaceted issue. Future research should aim to further explore these perspectives and provide a more comprehensive understanding of this critical aspect of economic history.

One such study by Whatley (2018) offers compelling evidence supporting the thesis that craft guild regulation played a crucial role in this context.

Whatley's research focuses on the British gunpowder industry and its relationship with slave exports along the Guinea Coast of Africa during the 18th century. He presents a potential instrumental variable derived from excess capacity in the gunpowder industry, suggesting that changes in this industry could have influenced monetary policy transmission (Whatley_2018: p.18).

Moreover, Whatley's findings indicate structural changes correlating with growth within this industry, which could potentially reflect alterations in the nature of the gunpowder-cargo relationship during the mid-18th century (Whatley_2018: p.13). This suggests that craft guild regulation may have played a role in shaping these structural changes and thus influencing monetary policy transmission.

However, it's worth noting that Whatley's models show autocorrelation, which could potentially introduce bias into his estimates (Whatley_2018: p.11). Future research should aim to address this issue and further explore the mechanisms through which craft guild regulation might have influenced monetary policy transmission in pre-industrial economies.

The critiques presented by Stern (2004) and Simson (2019) challenge the role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission. Stern (Stern_2004: p.10) suggests that informal or decentralized regulation, such as Coasian bargaining, might have played a more significant role in developing economies, potentially diminishing the impact of craft guilds on monetary policy.

Simson (Simson_2019: p.2), on the other hand, questions the personalized and discretionary nature of patronage in Africa, which could imply that the regulation exercised by craft guilds might not have been as pervasive or influential as previously thought. Furthermore, Simson (Simson_2019: p.25) notes a decline in the wage bill share of expenditure, suggesting that other factors may have overshadowed the economic influence of craft guilds.

These observations challenge the notion that craft guild regulation was a primary driver of monetary policy transmission in pre-industrial economies. Instead, they suggest that informal regulation or alternative economic mechanisms might have played a more substantial role in these settings. However, further exploration is needed to fully understand the extent and nature of these competing regulatory forces.
