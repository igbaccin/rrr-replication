The role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission is a topic that has garnered significant attention among historians and economists, as it sheds light on the complex interplay between economic institutions, political power, and economic growth during the pre-industrial era.

At the heart of this debate lies the question: To what extent did craft guilds, with their extensive regulatory powers, influence monetary policy transmission and contribute to economic growth or stagnation?

On one hand, some scholars argue that craft guilds played a crucial role in facilitating economic growth. For instance, Whatley (Whatley_2018: p.1) posits that the regulation of industries by craft guilds could have led to the expansion of slave exports along the Guinea Coast of Africa in the 18th century. Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.12) suggest that urbanization, a potential indicator of economic growth, was significantly influenced by craft guilds.

However, others complicate this narrative. North (North_1989: p.12) argues that the granting of monopolies to guilds in Colbert’s France produced unintended consequences, such as higher costs and growing private returns, which reduced industry demands for regulation. Austin (Austin_2008: p.26) also qualifies the notion of a 'reversal' in policy, suggesting that adaptations to the agency of the colonized played a significant role.

Moreover, Ogilvie (Ogilvie_2007: p.21) points out that each guild focused on specific activities, such as controlling quality or transmitting skills, while ignoring others. This selective regulation could have had both positive and negative effects on monetary policy transmission.

Lastly, Hopkins (Hopkins_2009: p.19) raises concerns about the ability of craft guilds to provide public goods over long distances in the pre-industrial era, which could have hindered their effectiveness in influencing monetary policy transmission.

This literature review reveals a complex picture where craft guild regulation may have both supported and complicated economic growth through its impact on monetary policy transmission. Further research is needed to disentangle these competing effects and fully understand the role of craft guilds in pre-industrial economies.

The role of craft guilds in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission is further supported by evidence suggesting their influence on economic growth, particularly through changes in industry and labor markets. For instance, Whatley (Whatley_2018: p.18) proposes that the excess capacity in the British gunpowder industry could reflect alterations in monetary policy transmission. This implies that guild regulation may have played a part in adjusting production levels, thereby impacting the availability of money supply.

Moreover, van Zanden (vanZanden_2009: p.13) highlights the stability of the skill premium in western Europe, which could be attributed to craft guilds' role in regulating labor markets. The decline in the skill premium after the Black Death (van Zanden_2009: p.13), for example, might have been influenced by changes in guild regulations that affected wage structures and, consequently, the money supply.

Furthermore, van Zanden (vanZanden_2009: p.23) emphasizes the link between rural and urban labor markets for unskilled laborers, a connection that could be facilitated or hindered by craft guilds' control over apprenticeships and journeymen migration. Such regulation could have had far-reaching effects on monetary policy transmission, as it affected the flow of labor and, thus, the availability of money in different regions.

The arguments presented by several scholars challenge the notion that craft guild regulation significantly impacted pre-industrial monetary policy transmission, particularly through their control over apprenticeships and journeymen migration. Feinstein (1998), for instance, suggests that trends in living standards may be more plausible when considering other indicators of well-being (Feinstein_1998: p.29). Humphries (2013) also contends that budget studies from the industrial revolution confirm a high standard of living and ignore the role of women workers during industrialization (Humphries_2013: p.3, p.18).

Peters (2004) and Simson (2019) further critique this perspective by highlighting that social relations could have been manipulated to serve the interests of the rural ruling class or other entities (Peters_2004: p.9; Simson_2019: p.2). These scholars argue that the impact of craft guilds on monetary policy transmission may not have been as substantial as previously thought, as other factors such as population growth and agricultural productivity could have played a more significant role in shaping economic dynamics during this period.

However, it is essential to recognize that the debate surrounding the influence of craft guild regulation on pre-industrial monetary policy transmission remains complex and multifaceted. Further examination of various case studies and historical documents is required to fully understand the extent to which craft guilds affected monetary policy transmission in different regions and periods.

The role of craft guilds in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission is further complicated by the significant impact they had on agriculture and population dynamics, as evidenced by various historical documents.

In Europe during the late medieval period, the population decline almost by one-third until the end of the fourteenth century due to repeated outbreaks of the plague (Pamuk_2007: p.5). This demographic shift had a profound effect on the economy and labor market, potentially altering the demand for monetary policy regulation through craft guilds. The urban guilds were significant producers of technological innovations, which could have influenced the transmission of monetary policy (Pamuk_2007: p.22).

The organization and system of controls that underpinned the solidarity of pre-industrial guilds depended on the proximity of its members, making it difficult to provide public goods over long distances (Hopkins_2009: p.19). This limitation could have hindered the effectiveness of monetary policy transmission through craft guilds in regions with extensive territories or trade networks.

Moreover, the granting of monopolies to guilds by Colbert's France aimed at providing minute regulation of the economy (North_1989: p.12). This centralized control mechanism may have facilitated the transmission of pre-industrial monetary policy, but it also led to higher costs due to uncertainty and growing private returns that reduced industry demands for regulation (North_1989: p.10).

Despite North's assertion that centralized control may have facilitated monetary policy transmission in pre-industrial societies, other factors also played significant roles in shaping this process. Allen et al. (2011) suggest that high wages in certain regions, possibly due to active involvement in intercontinental commerce, could have influenced the transmission of monetary policy (p.12). However, they also note that the standard of living in China remained low, similar to regions of Europe untouched by the industrial revolution, indicating potential limitations of centralized control mechanisms (p.20).

Williamson (1996) adds nuance to this discussion by highlighting that there were few industrial mysteries to transfer, and schooling was not central to the process in many pre-industrial societies (p.23). This implies that other factors, such as commerce or social structures, might have been more crucial for monetary policy transmission. Furthermore, Williamson points out that both the first and last epochs were ones of overall fast growth, globalization, and convergence, suggesting potential periods of increased monetary policy transmission (p.2).

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) also complicate the argument by emphasizing the role of exceptional societies like the United States and Canada in terms of leadership (p.11). They suggest that after elections became restricted and indirect in some states, these societies may have benefited from more effective monetary policy transmission mechanisms (p.10). Additionally, they discuss the impact of sugar plantations on the formation of Brazilian society, which could offer insights into how economic structures influenced pre-industrial monetary policy transmission (p.15).

These qualifications reshape the argument by emphasizing the complex interplay of factors that influenced monetary policy transmission in pre-industrial societies. Factors such as commerce, social structures, and exceptional leadership may have played significant roles alongside centralized control mechanisms. Further investigation into these aspects could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission.

Koepke & Baten (2005) discuss long-term fluctuations in climate and population as variables affecting growth, potentially impacting the emergence and evolution of craft guilds. Frankema (2012) highlights the constraints posed by natural transportation routes on economic development, which could have influenced the formation and regulation of craft guilds. Abramovitz (1986) discusses the potentiality of trade-offs between specialization and adaptability, suggesting that craft guilds might have played a role in balancing these factors within pre-industrial economies.

Despite these converging points, scholars diverge on the specific roles and mechanisms through which craft guilds influenced monetary policy transmission. For instance, Temin (2002) focuses more on industrialization and international trade, while Koepke & Baten (2005) delve into climate and population dynamics. Frankema (2012) primarily discusses the origins of formal education in sub-Saharan Africa, although it touches upon the potential impact of craft guilds indirectly. Abramovitz (1986) focuses on the rise of British factory-made cotton textiles and their effects on other industries, but does not explicitly address craft guilds' roles in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission.

What remains unresolved is a comprehensive understanding of how craft guild regulation specifically affected monetary policy transmission in pre-industrial societies. Further research should investigate the unique roles played by craft guilds in various regions and periods, as well as their interactions with other economic institutions such as centralized control mechanisms. By doing so, a more nuanced perspective on the role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission can be developed.
