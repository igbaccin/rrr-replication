The role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission is a significant yet nuanced topic, as evidenced by the extensive literature on the subject. This review aims to elucidate the central question and key positions within this discourse.

The primary contention revolves around the impact of craft guilds on economic growth and monetary policy in pre-industrial societies. Some scholars argue that guild regulation played a pivotal role in fostering prosperity, while others contend that it impeded growth by stifling competition and innovation (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.1; Stern_2004: p.8).

One perspective posits that guilds served as a vehicle for internal economic regulation, contributing to the growth of pre-industrial cities (North_1989: p.10). However, others argue that the granting of monopolies to guilds might have produced higher costs due to uncertainty and growing private returns, potentially reducing industry demands for regulation (North_1989: p.10).

Acemoglu and colleagues support the notion that guilds positively influenced economic growth, citing urbanization and income as indicators of prosperity in pre-industrial cities (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.8, p.10). In contrast, Stern criticizes this view, arguing that the relationship between guilds and economic development is complex and dependent on various factors (Stern_2004: p.4, p.8).

Austin complicates the debate by suggesting that the 'reversal' of economic growth in pre-industrial societies might require qualification (Austin_2008: p.26). Furthermore, Ogilvie supports the idea that guilds engaged in a range of activities, including political rent-seeking and offering benefits to rulers in return for market privileges (Ogilvie_2007: p.21, p.22).

Whatley's work adds nuance to the discussion by questioning the assumption that firearms had a net advantage in the capture and transport of slaves, a practice often linked to pre-industrial economies (Whatley_2018: p.4).

Craft guilds, as institutions that regulated the training and access to skilled professions, played a significant role in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission (vanZanden_2009: p.19). The efficiency of these institutions impacted the supply side of the economy by influencing the skill premium, which in turn affected wage levels and productivity (ibid.).

In north-western Europe, high silver wages reflected not only monetary factors but also high productivity in the tradable sector (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.1). This suggests that guilds' regulatory role in skill training contributed to a more efficient workforce, leading to higher wages and economic growth. Conversely, in parts of Asia where being a craftsman was largely hereditary, the lack of formal guild regulation may have hindered the development of a skilled workforce, resulting in lower wages and productivity (vanZanden_2009: p.20).

Moreover, the price of rice increased between the Late Ming and Mid-Qing periods, causing grain wages to decline sharply (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.17). This shift could have been exacerbated by inefficient training institutions, as suggested by vanZanden (ibid.). The unskilled silver wage in China was about the same as in India, and a small fraction of the silver wage in north-western Europe, further indicating a potential impact of guild regulation on wage levels (Broadberry&Gupta_2006: p.17).

The critique of the role of craft guild regulation in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission is challenged by some scholars, who argue that urbanization and economic growth may not be significantly influenced by such regulations.

Stern (2004) suggests that the initial stages of economic development tend to become more concentrated in a smaller number of cities, which could potentially undermine the importance of guild regulation in shaping urban growth patterns (Stern_2004: p.5). Furthermore, Stern cautions that the results of studies on this topic depend heavily on the assumptions made and the values of particular parameters (Stern_2004: p.4).

Similarly, Feinstein (1998) questions the significance of guild regulation in pre-industrial economies, asserting that an abundant supply of labor was a central feature in the first half of the nineteenth century, potentially overshadowing any potential impact of guilds on urbanization and economic growth (Feinstein_1998: p.28).

These scholars' perspectives challenge the notion that craft guild regulation played a crucial role in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission, suggesting instead that other factors, such as urban concentration or labor supply, may have been more influential in shaping economic growth patterns during this period.

The assertion that craft guild regulation played a minimal role in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission may require reconsideration, as other factors, such as labor market policies and product market access, appear to have played significant roles in shaping economic growth during this period. Austin (Austin_2008: p.21) emphasizes the relevance of domestic labor market policies, suggesting that terms of access to product markets could also influence economic development. Similarly, Lindert & Williamson (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.21) propose that taxing land site rents can raise the rate of accumulation, indicating a potential impact on economic growth through property regulations.

Furthermore, Allen (Allen_2001: p.20) argues that working more offered limited scope for improvement in pre-industrial societies, implying that policies regulating labor could have substantial effects on economic growth. In contrast, Hopkins (Hopkins_2009: p.19) notes the challenges of providing public goods over long distances during the pre-industrial era, suggesting that guild regulations might have been essential for ensuring the provision and distribution of such goods.

Moreover, Mokyr (Mokyr_2005: p.14) points out that the costs of transmitting information were declining before the advent of the railroad, indicating a potential role for regulatory frameworks in facilitating communication and information exchange—key drivers of economic growth. Lastly, van Zanden (vanZanden_1999: p.2) highlights the conflicting stories about pre-industrial household allocation of work, which could be reconciled by assuming significant shifts in the regulation of labor within households.

These qualifications complicate the initial argument that craft guild regulations had little impact on pre-industrial monetary policy transmission. Instead, it appears that a broader range of factors, including labor market policies, product market access, and public goods provision, may have influenced economic growth during this period. Further examination of these factors could deepen our understanding of the role they played in shaping economic development before the industrial revolution.

The role of craft guilds in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission is further complicated by their function as vehicles for internal economic regulation, as evidenced by North (1989). Guilds were designed to provide minute regulation of the economy, and this internal regulation may have influenced economic growth during this period. For instance, the granting of monopolies to guilds in Colbert’s France produced this result (North_1989: p.12). Additionally, structural changes within these guilds could correlate with changes in economic growth. However, North also notes that higher costs due to uncertainty and growing private returns reduced industry demands for regulation (North_1989: p.10), suggesting a potential limit to the effectiveness of guild-based monetary policy transmission.

Allen et al. (2011) further nuance this perspective, highlighting the role of craft guilds in price control and provisioning systems. For example, most comparative studies relied on indirect comparisons based on scattered output, consumption, or demographic data (AllenEtAl_2011: p.2). However, a detailed description of the workings of the provisioning system in Canton is offered by van Dyke (AllenEtAl_2011: p.4), which could potentially provide valuable insights into the role of craft guilds in monetary policy transmission.

The role of craft guilds in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission is a topic that has been explored by various scholars, albeit indirectly and tangentially. While the specific focus of these studies varies, some common themes emerge regarding the influence of craft guilds on economic development and monetary policies.

Scholars such as Frankema (2012) and Broadberry & Gardner (2022) highlight the impact of structural changes on economic growth. For instance, Frankema (Frankema_2012: p.4) suggests that colonial administrations' policies played a role in shaping the economic landscape, potentially influencing the formation and regulation of craft guilds. Similarly, Broadberry & Gardner (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.4) discuss the relationship between structural change and growth, implying that guilds might have been part of such changes.

On the other hand, Temin (2002) and Koepke & Baten (2005) touch upon topics tangential to the main theme but offer insights that could be relevant. For example, Temin (Temin_2002: p.9) discusses the role of international trade in industrialization, which might have been facilitated or regulated by craft guilds. Koepke & Baten (Koepke&Baten_2005: p.33) focus on dietary habits and climate as factors influencing economic growth, although their work does not directly address craft guilds.

Despite these shared perspectives, a comprehensive understanding of the role of craft guilds in pre-industrial monetary policy transmission remains elusive. The studies reviewed here do not provide detailed analyses of this specific aspect, and further research is needed to fully understand how craft guild regulation may have impacted monetary policy transmission during pre-industrial periods.

In particular, future research could focus on case studies that delve into the workings of provisioning systems, as described by van Dyke in Allen et al (2011), and examine their role in monetary policy transmission. This would help bridge the gap in our understanding of how craft guilds influenced economic development during pre-industrial times.
