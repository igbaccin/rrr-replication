LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The debate surrounding the fundamental cause of long-run growth has been a subject of intense interest among economists and economic historians. A significant body of literature suggests that culture, rather than institutions, plays a dominant role in shaping economic development over the long term. This perspective challenges the traditional view that institutions are the primary determinants of growth.

The central question at hand is whether cultural factors or institutional arrangements have a more profound impact on long-run economic growth. The stakes are high, as understanding the underlying drivers of growth can inform policy decisions aimed at fostering development in various regions.

Several scholars support the culture-dominates-institutions thesis. For instance, Douglas North (1989) argues that fundamental changes in institutions are driven by interactions and costliness of measurement of rules, implying that cultural norms shape the evolution of institutions (p.4, p.6). Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2002) posit that institutions in former colonies were designed to maximize European rents rather than long-run growth, suggesting a dominant role for culture (p.33).

Moreover, Austin (2008) asserts that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, particularly in the African context (p.24, p.26). Ogilvie (2007) highlights how British cultural beliefs gave rise to different economic institutions in various colonies, further supporting the culture-dominates-institutions thesis (p.12, p.30).

However, it is essential to acknowledge that not all scholars agree with this perspective. Abramovitz (1986) argues that the institutions of the Welfare State have reached a higher degree of elaboration in Europe than in the United States, implying a significant role for institutions in shaping economic growth (p.21).

Furthermore, understanding the complex interplay between culture and institutions is crucial for developing nuanced theories of long-run growth. Acemoglu et al. (2001) document that their estimates of the effect of institutions on performance are not driven by outliers, suggesting that both cultural and institutional factors contribute to economic development (p.4).

The role of culture in shaping long-run growth is further substantiated by various scholarly works. Ogilvie (2007) posits that 'civic' cultures encourage institutions conducive to economic growth and political accountability (p.12). This suggests a strong link between cultural beliefs and the development of effective institutions, supporting the assertion that culture dominates institutions as the fundamental cause of long-term economic development.

Moreover, Ogilvie (2007) also highlights how British cultural beliefs led to significantly different economic institutions in various colonies (p.30). This underscores the idea that cultural factors can have a profound impact on institutional structures, which in turn influence long-term growth.

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that the focus on specific historical circumstances may be misleading when understanding Africa's long-run performance (p.2). This implies that broader cultural factors could be more influential than previously thought, supporting the thesis that culture dominates institutions in determining long-term growth.

Nunn & Wantchekon (2011) provide evidence suggesting that low levels of trust and weak institutions persist among future generations due to self-enforcing outcomes (p.6). This finding underscores the idea that cultural factors can create lasting impacts on institutional development, further supporting the thesis.

Allen (2001) demonstrates that divergence was a dominant trend in prices and wages during the seventeenth century (p.14), suggesting that cultural differences could have led to significant variations in economic institutions and growth rates.

The argument that institutions are the primary cause of long-run growth is further supported by various scholarly works. North (1989) emphasizes the role of institutional change, suggesting that it is not only essential at a specific moment in time but also determines the direction of change (p.7). He underscores the importance of political institutions and their relevance to economic growth (p.4). Similarly, North & Weingast (1989) argue that new institutions allowed governments to commit credibly to upholding property rights, which is crucial for long-term growth (p.2).

Furthermore, Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) focus on how the environments in which European colonies were established may have led to societies with varying degrees of inequality, potentially impacting growth (p.4). Hopkins (2009) highlights that institutional change in Africa was a product of both African initiatives and exogenous causes such as population growth (p.17). This supports the idea that cultural factors can significantly influence economic institutions and growth rates.

Moreover, Austin (2008) argues that for economists studying Africa, the significance of the RF thesis—institutions as 'the' fundamental cause of long-run economic growth—is particularly fundamental (p.3). He also emphasizes the rise of Europe being linked to institutional change and economic growth (p.26).

The influence of institutions on long-run economic growth, particularly in the context of former colonies, is further substantiated by Acemoglu, Johnson, and Robinson (2001) and Acemoglu, Johnson, and Robinson (2002). These scholars argue that the persistence of fundamental institutions, such as property rights and political power structures, plays a crucial role in determining economic performance.

Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27) provide evidence that more fundamental institutions have a significant impact on property rights, which in turn affects economic growth. They also assert that their estimates of the effect of institutions on performance are robust and not influenced by outliers (Acemoglu et al., 2001: p.4). The persistence of these institutions from the past to the present is emphasized, with Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.1) stating that "these institutions persisted to the present."

Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.33) argue that European colonists designed institutions in former colonies to maximize their own rents, rather than long-term growth. They also suggest that the Atlantic slave trade fundamentally altered African societies (Acemoglu et al., 2002: p.34). Furthermore, they posit that prosperous societies today are likely to remain so in the future due to the enduring nature of institutions (Acemoglu et al., 2002: p.33).

Nunn (2008) supports this argument by stating that only societies with sufficiently developed institutions were able to facilitate trade with Europeans during the period of colonization (Nunn, 2008: p.19). He also uses the distance from countries to locations of slave demand as instruments to estimate the causal effect of the slave trade on economic development (Nunn, 2008: p.30).

Structural change, a shift from agriculture to industry and services, has been posited as a critical factor in long-run growth (Stern_2004: p.15). This perspective challenges the assertion that culture dominates institutions as the fundamental cause of economic development. Stern (2004) presents a critical history of the environmental Kuznets curve (EKC), arguing that structural change correlates with improvements in environmental quality, rather than the other way around.

The author demonstrates that lack of clean water and urban sanitation decline uniformly with increasing income, and over time, contrary to the hypothesis that cultural factors might be responsible for such improvements (Stern_2004: p.6). Furthermore, Stern (2004) argues that the range of income levels where these improvements occur is greater than in recent OECD-based panels, suggesting a broader applicability of structural change's impact on economic development.

Moreover, Humphries (2013) challenges the notion that cultural factors might be primarily responsible for long-run growth by critiquing sources that suggest lower nutritional status among adults in pre-industrial societies led to smaller body sizes (Humphries_2013: p.6). The author also questions the accuracy of data on orphaned children and lone-parent families, arguing that these sources might not accurately reflect the proportion of children affected by parental death or disappearance (Humphries_2013: p.14).

The works of Lindert & Williamson (1985) and van Zanden (1999) provide additional perspectives that complicate the assertion that culture dominates institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run growth.

Lindert & Williamson (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.4) acknowledge that space constraints prevent a comprehensive treatment of distribution trends in the socialist bloc, which could potentially challenge the argument that cultural factors are universally more influential than institutional ones. Furthermore, they argue that understanding long-run distribution trends is closely linked with explaining growth itself (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.9). This suggests that institutions might play a significant role in shaping both growth and distribution patterns.

Moreover, Lindert & Williamson (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.10) highlight the importance of final-product demand in the 20th century as a factor influencing growth and distribution. This implies that economic policies aimed at improving growth might also have implications for income distribution, potentially challenging the notion that a trade-off between growth and equality is inevitable.

Van Zanden (vanZanden_1999: p.6) notes that in certain regions, such as eastern and central Europe, the dominant crop was rye, which could limit the availability of data for other crops. This could potentially affect the accuracy of conclusions drawn about the role of agriculture in long-run growth and cultural development. Additionally, van Zanden (vanZanden_1999: p.15) discusses the use of nominal wages deflated by an index of the cost of living, suggesting that the cost of living might be an important factor influencing long-term economic development.

These findings suggest that while culture may play a significant role in shaping long-run growth, it is not the sole determinant. Institutional factors, policy decisions, and economic structures also appear to have substantial impacts on growth patterns over time. Further research should aim to integrate these various factors into a comprehensive understanding of long-term economic development.

The literature review focuses on the debate regarding the primary cause of long-run growth, with a particular emphasis on the role of culture and institutions. While some scholars argue that institutions play a crucial role in shaping economic development (Temin_2002, Komlos_1998), others suggest that culture may be even more fundamental (Bohannan_1959).

Temin (2002) and Komlos (1998) provide evidence supporting the importance of institutions in economic growth. For instance, Temin highlights the role of labor force composition in determining growth rates, while Komlos discusses the impact of technological innovations such as refrigeration on agricultural productivity. However, both authors also acknowledge that these factors are intertwined with cultural elements, suggesting a complex relationship between the two.

Bohannan (1959) provides a different perspective, emphasizing the role of culture in shaping institutions and economic structures. The author demonstrates how cultural norms and practices can significantly influence the emergence and functioning of markets and marriage systems, ultimately impacting long-term growth patterns.

Despite these insights, there remains a lack of consensus on whether culture or institutions are more fundamental in driving long-run growth. Some scholars argue that both factors are interdependent, with each influencing the other in complex ways (Hopkins_2019). However, further research is needed to better understand this relationship and develop a comprehensive understanding of economic development over time.
