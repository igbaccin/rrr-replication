The question of what drives long-run economic growth has been a subject of intense debate among economists and historians. A significant body of literature suggests that culture, or more specifically, institutions, are the fundamental causes of long-term economic growth (Austin_2008: p.26). This perspective, often referred to as the 'institutions hypothesis,' posits that the nature and quality of a society's institutions significantly impact its ability to achieve sustained economic growth.

The institutions hypothesis argues that societies with strong property rights, rule of law, and low levels of corruption tend to experience more significant economic growth over the long term (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). For instance, Daron Acemoglu and colleagues demonstrate that the institutions established during the age of industry created a considerable advantage for societies with institutions of private property (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49).

Furthermore, Douglas North and Barry Weingast argue that the new institutions allowed governments to commit credibly to upholding property rights, which was crucial for long-term economic growth (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). They also contend that absolutist states that did not face such constraints, like early modern Spain, created economic conditions that retarded long-run economic growth (North&Weingast_1989: p.7).

However, it is essential to note that not all scholars agree with this perspective. Some argue that while institutions play a role in economic growth, other factors such as geography, technology, and demographics may also be of equal or greater importance (Abramovitz_1986: p.21). Nevertheless, the institutions hypothesis has been influential in shaping our understanding of long-run economic growth and continues to stimulate ongoing research and debate.

As we delve deeper into this topic, it will become apparent that the stakes are high. If culture dominates institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run growth, then policies aimed at improving institutional quality could have a profound impact on economic development. Conversely, if other factors such as geography and technology play more significant roles, then policymakers may need to focus their efforts elsewhere to promote sustained economic growth. The answer to this question has important implications for economic policy, international development, and the future of global prosperity.
