LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

The debate on the fundamental cause of long-run growth has been a subject of intense discussion among economists and economic historians, with many pointing towards culture as the dominant factor over institutions. This review focuses on evidence supporting the assertion that culture dominates institutions in determining long-term economic growth.

Several studies provide compelling arguments for this thesis. Acemoglu et al. (2001) argue that British colonies, which had lower indices of institution, have persisted to the present (p.1). They also document that their estimates of the effect of institutions on performance are not driven by outliers (p.4), and more fundamentally, they show that British colonies have an index of institution that is 0.63 points lower on average (p.20). Similarly, Acemoglu et al. (2002) suggest that the institutions hypothesis implies that societies prosperous today should tend to be prosperous in the future (p.33). They further argue that Europeans running the Atlantic slave trade fundamentally altered the organization of society in Africa (p.34), and the age of industry created a considerable advantage for societies with institutions of private property (p.49).

North & Weingast (1989) assert that new institutions allowed governments to commit credibly to upholding property rights (p.2), which is crucial for long-run economic growth. North (1989) also emphasizes the importance of institutions in fostering economic growth, stating that they affect the costliness of measurement of rules and lead to changes in relative prices (p.4).

Ogilvie (2007) highlights that 'civic' cultures encourage institutions that favor economic growth and political accountability (p.12), and points out that British cultural beliefs gave rise to very different economic institutions in different colonies (p.30).

While these studies support the thesis, it is essential to acknowledge that not all perspectives align with this assertion. For instance, further research is needed to understand the nuances and qualifications of this relationship between culture and institutions. The debate continues on whether culture dominates institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run growth, with scholars examining various factors such as political institutions, constitutional rules, and economic systems that may influence this relationship differently across regions and time periods.

The literature provides substantial evidence supporting the assertion that culture dominates institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run growth. Austin (2008) emphasizes the significance of institutions, particularly those promoting free-market systems, in fostering economic development (Austin_2008: p.22, p.24, p.26). Similarly, Ogilvie (2007) highlights the role of cultural beliefs in shaping economic institutions across colonies, suggesting a strong correlation between culture and long-term growth (Ogilvie_2007: p.12, p.30).

Hopkins (2009) reinforces this argument by acknowledging the intertwined nature of institutions and non-institutional influences, implying that cultural factors play a crucial role in shaping these complex systems (Hopkins_2009: p.11). Broadberry & Gardner (2022) also support this perspective, arguing that Africa's long-term growth has been overlooked due to a focus on specific historical periods and the limited availability of data, suggesting that cultural factors may have significantly influenced African economic development (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1, p.2).

Nunn (2008) provides evidence of the relationship between institutions and long-run growth by demonstrating how societies with well-developed institutions were able to facilitate trade with Europe (Nunn_2008: p.19). Pamuk (2007) supports this view by illustrating how an exogenous shock, such as the plague, triggered a series of institutional changes that contributed to long-term economic development in Europe (Pamuk_2007: p.5, p.24).

North (1989) emphasizes that institutions do change, and fundamental changes in relative prices lead to institutional change. He argues that the evolution of institutions is essential at a moment of time due to the costliness of measuring rules (North_1989: p.4). Furthermore, North suggests that increased security rights, a more efficient market, and economic growth are interconnected (North_1989: p.9). These findings support the notion that institutions play a crucial role in long-term economic development.

Feinstein (1998) offers evidence of differences between Ireland and Britain in wage labor and earnings patterns, suggesting that cultural factors may have contributed to these distinctions (Feinstein_1998: p.24). The author also discusses the impact of agricultural harvest failures on grain prices, which could potentially affect institutional changes related to food production and distribution (Feinstein_1998: p.5). These findings support the argument that culture influences institutions, ultimately impacting long-run growth.

Goldin (2006) highlights the importance of cultural shifts in shaping identities and occupations, especially for women (Goldin_2006: p.2). The author also notes that the rise of part-time work contributed to increased substitution effects, which may have had long-term implications for economic development (Goldin_2006: p.5). These findings support the idea that cultural changes can impact institutions and contribute to long-run growth.

The role of agriculture in shaping institutions and long-term growth is further supported by various studies. Frankema (2012) acknowledges the complexities in computing historical enrollment rates for sub-Saharan Africa due to insufficient population census data (Frankema_2012: p.7). This suggests that cultural factors, such as educational policies, may have played a significant role in determining institutional development, which in turn influenced economic growth.

Moreover, Kuznets (1973) points out that Japan, the only nation outside of European civilization to join the group of developed countries, highlights the importance of cultural roots in fostering economic development (Kuznets_1973: p.8). The author also emphasizes the limited spread of modern economic growth in less developed countries, potentially due to cultural barriers.

Broadberry et al. (2015) highlight that growth was faster in the nineteenth century than during the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, suggesting a shift in institutional structures or cultural practices that facilitated increased economic development (BroadberryEtal_2015: p.4). The authors also note the importance of agricultural demand and supply in determining long-term growth, further supporting the role of agriculture in shaping institutions.

Lastly, Bryceson (2002) discusses structural changes in African peasantries' social institutions and their link to economic growth (Bryceson_2002: p.2). The author's findings indicate that these structural changes may have been influenced by cultural factors, such as the removal of traditional authority systems, which could have had long-term implications for economic development.

Instead, technological advancements and institutional structures could play significant roles in this process. This assertion is presented in the critical analysis of the Environmental Kuznets Curve (EKC) by Stern (2004).

Stern argues that technological progress, rather than cultural changes, drives economic growth and structural transformation (Stern_2004: p.1). In wealthy countries, growth is slower due to the saturation of technology and the need for more resource-intensive methods to achieve similar levels of output (Stern_2004: p.2). Furthermore, Stern's model incorporates changes in trade over the forecast horizon, suggesting that institutional structures and policies related to international commerce could significantly impact structural change (Stern_2004: p.15).

Moreover, Stern's analysis of sulfur emissions demonstrates the potential influence of institutions on structural change (Stern_2004: p.9). By using UN and World Bank forecasts of economic and population growth, Stern's model projects sulfur emissions using the EKC. This approach implies that institutional factors, such as environmental regulations and policies, can have a direct impact on structural change by influencing pollution levels.

Lindert and Williamson (1985) add nuance to the argument by suggesting that the relationship between culture, institutions, and long-run growth is intertwined with distribution trends. They argue that structural change, which is a significant factor in economic growth, is closely linked to final-product demand (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.10). This implies that cultural preferences and institutional arrangements can influence the pattern of consumption, thereby shaping the structure of an economy.

Moreover, Lindert and Williamson (1985) posit that understanding long-run distribution trends is essential for explaining growth itself. They propose that the factor-demand and factor-supply forces responsible for both distributional trends and economic growth are interconnected (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.9). This suggests that culture, by influencing these forces, can play a crucial role in determining long-term growth patterns.

Finally, Lindert and Williamson (1985) argue that initial income distribution may be influenced by cultural factors. They suggest that societies starting with perfect equality might experience higher inequality due to the diffusion of technology and wealth (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.8). This implies that cultural norms and values can shape the distribution of income, which in turn can influence long-term growth patterns.

The literature review on the fundamental cause of long-run growth reveals a consensus among scholars that cultural norms and values significantly influence economic development, although the degree to which institutions play a role remains debated.

Temin (2002) emphasizes the importance of structural change in economic growth, particularly the transition from agriculture to industry, which he argues was crucial for the rapid expansion observed during the Golden Age (Temin_2002: p.14). This perspective aligns with Komlos (1998), who discusses the impact of technological advancements on agricultural productivity and food prices (Komlos_1998: p.17, p.12). However, neither author directly addresses culture as a primary driver of long-term growth patterns.

On the other hand, Bohannan (1959) discusses the role of cultural institutions in shaping economic exchange systems, suggesting that the spread of market institutions can lead to economic growth (Bohannan_1959: p.13). Hopkins (2019) also acknowledges the significance of history and cultural context in understanding long-run development problems (Hopkins_2019: p.6, p.13), implying that cultural norms may play a role in shaping institutions and economic growth over time.

Despite these insights, there remains a lack of consensus on whether culture or institutions should be considered the primary driver of long-run growth. Furthermore, the interplay between cultural norms, values, and institutions as they influence economic development is yet to be fully understood. Future research could focus on elucidating this relationship and its implications for long-term growth patterns.
