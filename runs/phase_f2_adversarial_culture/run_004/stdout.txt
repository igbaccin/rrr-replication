LONG-FORM LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

In the realm of economic development, a central question that has garnered significant attention is whether culture or institutions are the fundamental causes of long-run growth. This inquiry delves into the factors that have shaped the trajectories of various societies throughout history, with implications for our understanding of contemporary economic disparities.

The proponents of the cultural determinism perspective argue that deep-rooted cultural values and attitudes play a crucial role in shaping institutions over time (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27). For instance, they posit that the persistence of certain institutional arrangements can be traced back to their alignment with cultural norms and beliefs (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.8). Furthermore, they assert that these institutions have a lasting impact on economic performance, as they determine the incentives and constraints faced by individuals within a society (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.33).

On the other hand, institutionalists emphasize the role of formal rules and structures in shaping long-run growth. They contend that changes in institutions can be triggered by shifts in relative prices or political events, leading to significant economic transformations (North_1989: p.6). For example, the introduction of new political institutions, such as those that uphold property rights, can foster long-term economic growth (North&Weingast_1989: p.2).

The stakes of this debate are high, as understanding the fundamental drivers of long-run growth is essential for crafting effective development policies. If culture is indeed the primary determinant, then interventions aimed at changing institutional arrangements may have limited success. On the other hand, if institutions can be altered to promote economic growth, then there is hope that underperforming economies can be transformed through targeted policy reforms.

The literature reviewed here provides strong support for the argument that institutions are indeed a fundamental cause of long-run growth (Austin_2008: p.24). However, the debate remains ongoing, as some scholars argue that cultural factors should not be overlooked in explaining economic disparities across countries and regions (Abramovitz_1986: p.21).

As this review demonstrates, the question of whether culture or institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run growth is a complex one, with implications for our understanding of economic development. Further research is needed to tease apart the interplay between cultural and institutional factors in shaping economic outcomes over time.

The evidence provided supports the contention that culture plays a significant role in shaping long-run growth, with institutions following suit. Ogilvie (2007) emphasizes the connection between 'civic' cultures and the development of institutions favorable to economic growth and political accountability (Ogilvie_2007: p.12). Moreover, he suggests that cultural beliefs influenced the formation of distinct economic institutions across British colonies (Ogilvie_2007: p.30).

Nunn & Wantchekon (2011) reinforce this perspective by demonstrating how low levels of trust and weak institutions can persist among future generations, creating a self-enforcing outcome (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.6). Furthermore, they provide evidence suggesting that the relationship between culture and economic outcomes is causal (Nunn&Wantchekon_2011: p.1).

Frankema (2012) investigates the extent to which British educational policies contributed to the long-term economic development of colonies, hinting at a strong cultural influence on institutional changes (Frankema_2012: p.2). Pamuk (2007) also supports this argument by suggesting that an exogenous shock triggered a series of institutional changes with far-reaching consequences (Pamuk_2007: p.24).

The arguments presented by various authors support the notion that culture plays a significant role in driving institutional changes, which in turn influence long-term economic growth. Douglas North (1989) emphasizes that institutions do change and fundamental changes in relative prices can lead to such shifts (North_1989: p.6). He further states that the evolution of institutions is important because they are costly to measure, suggesting a strong cultural influence on institutional changes (North_1989: p.4).

Similarly, North and Weingast (1989) argue that new institutions allowed governments to commit credibly to upholding property rights, which is crucial for long-term economic growth (North&Weingast_1989: p.2). They also highlight the significance of political institutions and the constitution in determining the direction of institutional change (North&Weingast_1989: p.4).

Hopkins (2009) supports this view by suggesting that institutional change is gradual and influenced by both exogenous causes and African initiatives (Hopkins_2009: p.8, p.17). Nunn (2008) demonstrates how during a specific time period, only societies with sufficiently developed institutions were able to facilitate trade with Europeans, implying that culture plays a role in institutional development (Nunn_2008: p.19).

Moreover, Braudel and Wallerstein (2009) argue that the culture of intellectual elites is similar to the story of institutions, suggesting a strong cultural influence on these fundamental aspects of society (Braudel&Wallerstein_2009: p.10). Williamson (1996) underlines the importance of understanding the sources of convergence, which can be attributed to institutional changes driven by culture (Williamson_1996: p.4).

The centrality of culture in shaping institutions as the primary driver of long-term economic growth is substantiated by various scholars. Austin (2008) posits that institutions, particularly those influencing economic growth, are indeed the fundamental cause of long-run growth (Austin_2008: p.24, p.26). Similarly, Mokyr (2005) argues that cultural beliefs in 18th-century Western societies created the attitudes and mechanisms that fostered the creation, diffusion, and utilization of new useful knowledge, which ultimately led to modern economic growth (Mokyr_2005: p.8).

Allen (2001) provides empirical evidence supporting this perspective by demonstrating divergence as the dominant trend in prices and wages during the 17th century, suggesting that institutional differences played a crucial role in determining economic growth (Allen_2001: p.4, p.14). Goldin (2006) further supports this argument by emphasizing the importance of understanding labor market institutions and their impact on economic growth (Goldin_2006: p.3).

Sokoloff & Engerman (2000) suggest that the institutional differences between European colonies may have influenced their economic development, with potential long-term effects (Sokoloff&Engerman_2000: p.4, p.7). Broadberry et al. (2015) also highlight the significance of institutions in shaping growth by comparing the economic performance of India and Europe over extended periods (BroadberryEtal_2015: p.2, p.4).

Institutions, rather than culture, may not be the primary driver of long-run growth, particularly in Africa, as suggested by Stern (2004). The author presents a critical history of the environmental Kuznets curve (EKC), a hypothesis that economic growth leads to improved environmental quality after a certain threshold is reached (Stern_2004: p.1).

In his analysis, Stern challenges the EKC by examining data from Africa and finding that structural change correlates with growth. He argues that as economies develop and urbanize, they often experience environmental degradation due to increased demand for resources and lack of adequate infrastructure (Stern_2004: p.6). This is a greater range of income levelsthan is found in the OECD-based panels for recent decades (Stern_2004: p.6), suggesting that the EKC may not hold universally across different stages of economic development.

Furthermore, Stern presents projected sulfur emissions using the EKC and UN/World Bank forecasts of economic and population growth. The results show that sulfur emissions continue to rise with increasing income in Africa, contradicting the EKC's prediction of improved environmental quality at higher income levels (Stern_2004: p.9).

The work of Lindert & Williamson (1985) provides nuanced insights into the relationship between institutions, economic growth, and distribution trends. They suggest that the search for explanations of long-run growth and distribution is interconnected, as final-product demand shifts in the 20th century accounted for a significant share of national product (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.10).

Moreover, Lindert & Williamson (1985) question the policy implications that growth and equality might be mutually exclusive, raising doubts about the efficiency trade-off debate (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.29). This implies that structural changes in economies could potentially foster both economic growth and equitable distribution, challenging the notion that institutions are the primary drivers of long-term growth.

However, their work also acknowledges limitations, such as a lack of treatment for distribution trends within socialist blocs (Lindert & Williamson_1985: p.4). This omission could suggest that further research is required to fully understand the role of institutions in driving long-term growth across different economic systems.

The examination of various sources reveals a complex interplay between culture, institutions, and long-run growth, with culture appearing to hold significant influence over institutional development.

Temin (2002) primarily focuses on labor distribution and its impact on economic growth, suggesting that the exhaustion of cheap labor was a key factor in slowing growth rates (p.4). This aligns with the idea that cultural factors can shape institutions, as changes in labor force composition may reflect societal values and norms regarding work and productivity.

Komlos (1998) discusses agricultural advancements and their role in population growth, but does not explicitly address the relationship between culture and institutions (p.6, p.12). However, the invention of refrigeration for shipping perishable goods over long distances could be seen as a cultural innovation that facilitated institutional change in food production and distribution.

Bohannan (1959) touches upon the interplay between markets and marriage institutions in social structures (p.7, p.13), but does not directly link these to long-term growth (p.10). Nonetheless, understanding the cultural underpinnings of such institutions might provide valuable insights into their impact on economic development.

Hopkins (2019) emphasizes the importance of historical analysis in understanding long-run development problems, but does not explicitly discuss culture and institutions (p.9, p.12). The author's critique of relying on the notion of path dependence to connect the past with the present could be interpreted as a call for further examination of cultural factors shaping institutional change over time.

While these sources offer valuable insights into various aspects of culture and institutions, they do not conclusively demonstrate that culture dominates institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run growth. Further research is necessary to fully understand this complex relationship and its implications for economic development.
