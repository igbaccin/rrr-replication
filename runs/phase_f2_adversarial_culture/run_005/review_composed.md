The debate on the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth has been a subject of extensive research in economics and economic history. One prominent theory, advanced by Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27) and Austin (Austin_2008: p.3), among others, posits that culture and institutions play a decisive role in shaping long-term growth patterns. This perspective challenges the traditional view of economic development primarily driven by factors such as geography or technology.

According to Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.20), British colonies, on average, have institutions that are less favorable for long-run growth compared to other regions. These institutions, they argue, persist over time and have a significant impact on the economic prosperity of societies today (Acemoglu et al. 2001: p.1). Similarly, Austin (Austin_2008: p.26) suggests that institutions are the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth, particularly in the context of Africa.

He further argues that institutional change is driven by fundamental changes in relative prices (North_1989: p.6).

However, it is important to note that not all agree with this perspective. Abramovitz (Abramovitz_1986: p.21), for example, highlights the role of the welfare state in Europe's economic growth, suggesting a more nuanced view where institutions are one among several factors influencing long-run growth.

The stakes of this debate are significant as understanding the fundamental cause of long-run growth can inform policy decisions aimed at promoting economic development. As the argument continues, further exploration of the role of culture and institutions in shaping economic growth patterns will undoubtedly provide valuable insights for economists, policymakers, and historians alike.

The role of culture in shaping long-term economic growth is further emphasized by various scholars, highlighting its profound impact on institutional development and subsequent economic outcomes. North & Weingast (1989) argue that the new institutions allowed governments to commit credibly to upholding property rights, which fostered long-run growth (p.2). They also contend that absolutist states, such as early modern Spain, created economic conditions that retarded long-term growth due to weak institutions (p.7).

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) point out the absence of Africa in discussions on long-run growth, attributing this oversight to limited data availability (p.1). They suggest that focusing solely on specific periods may be misleading in understanding Africa’s long-term performance (p.2).

Frankema (2012) argues that centralized state institutions raised the accountability of local chiefs, thereby promoting economic development (p.15). However, he acknowledges data limitations, such as inadequate population census data and a lack of age distribution data for colonial Africa (p.7).

Nunn & Wantchekon (2011) demonstrate that low levels of trust and weak institutions can persist among future generations, creating a self-enforcing outcome (p.6). They also provide evidence suggesting a causal relationship between culture and economic impacts (p.1).

Nunn (2008) suggests that only societies with sufficiently developed institutions were able to facilitate trade with Europeans during the slave trade period, leading to economic development (p.19). He also uses distance from countries to locations of slave demand as instruments for estimating the causal effect of the slave trades on economic development (p.30).

Braudel & Wallerstein (2009) assert that both culture and institutions have a high degree of endurance and survival over time (p.10, p.29). They argue against viewing cultural or institutional changes as the fundamental question, instead emphasizing the importance of understanding their interplay with historical contexts (p.16).

The assertion that culture dominates institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run growth is substantiated by various scholarly works. For instance, Douglas North (1989) posits that institutional change is crucial in economic growth and development, stating that "fundamental changes induced [are] the second issue of institutional change" (p.7). He further argues that institutions evolve due to costliness of measurement rules, emphasizing their importance in shaping economies over time.

Similarly, Jeffrey G. Williamson and Lincoln C. Cheng (Austin_2008) endorse the viewpoint that institutions play a pivotal role in long-run economic growth. They argue that "institutions as the fundamental cause of long-rungrowth" is an attractive conclusion for economists and economic historians studying Africa (p.26).

Additionally, Hopkins (2009) suggests that institutional change in African colonies was a product of both endogenous and exogenous causes, further supporting the notion that cultural factors significantly contribute to institutional changes and, consequently, long-run growth. Furthermore, Akyeampong & Fofack (2014) demonstrate continuity of cultural norms over long periods in Africa, suggesting that these norms may have a profound impact on economic development.

The evidence from Acemoglu and his colleagues (AcemogluEtAl_2001, p.27) suggests that more fundamental institutions, such as property rights, have a significant impact on long-run growth. They argue that British colonies, on average, have institutions that are less conducive to growth compared to other colonies (AcemogluEtAl_2001, p.20). Moreover, they assert that these institutions persisted from the colonial era to the present (AcemogluEtAl_2001, p.1). Acemoglu et al. (2001) also emphasize that the thesis of institutional persistence goes back at least to Karl Marx (AcemogluEtAl_2001, p.8), indicating a long-standing discussion on this topic.

In a separate study, Acemoglu and his colleagues (AcemogluEtAl_2002) highlight that European colonizers designed institutions to maximize their own rents rather than long-run growth (AcemogluEtAl_2002, p.33). They also argue that the Atlantic slave trade fundamentally altered African societies, potentially impacting their institutional development (AcemogluEtAl_2002, p.34). Lastly, they suggest that prosperous societies today are likely to remain so in the future due to the persistence of institutions (AcemogluEtAl_2002, p.33), and that societies with institutions of private property had an advantage during the age of industry (AcemogluEtAl_2002, p.49).

These findings support the thesis that culture, represented by institutional norms, can dominate as the fundamental cause of long-run growth. The persistence of institutions from colonial times to the present, and their impact on structural change and economic prosperity, suggest a strong role for cultural factors in shaping economic development. Further analysis could delve into specific aspects of cultural norms that influence institutional changes and, consequently, long-run growth.

Urban development concentration, as a critical factor in economic growth, is challenged by Stern (2004). The author presents a critique of the environmental Kuznets curve (EKC), a theory suggesting that economic growth initially leads to increased pollution levels but eventually results in decreased pollution as income rises (Stern_2004: p.1). In contrast, Stern argues that urban and industrial development tend to become more concentrated in a smaller area during the initial stages of economic development (Stern_2004: p.5). This concentration may not necessarily foster long-term growth or improvements in environmental quality, as suggested by the EKC.

Furthermore, Stern contends that in wealthy countries, growth is slower, and pollution reduction efforts can overcome the scale effects (Stern_2004: p.2). This assertion challenges the notion that cultural norms promoting urban sprawl or industrial concentration are essential for long-run economic growth. The author also presents projected sulfur emissions using the EKC and UN and World Bank forecasts of economic and population growth, suggesting a different trajectory for pollution levels in relation to economic development (Stern_2004: p.9).

These findings by Stern challenge the argument that culture dominates institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run growth, particularly with regard to urban development concentration. However, it is essential to consider that cultural norms may still play a role in shaping institutional changes related to pollution control and environmental protection in wealthy countries (Stern_2004: p.11). Future analysis could examine the interplay between cultural norms, institutions, and pollution control policies in various economic contexts to further explore this issue.

The work of Lindert and Williamson (1985) sheds light on the complex interplay between factor-demand and factor-supply forces, which are crucial in understanding both long-run growth and distribution trends. These authors argue that the search for explanations of growth is intrinsically linked to the explanation of long-run distribution trends (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.9).

Moreover, Lindert and Williamson (1985) emphasize that structural change, a key factor in economic growth, correlates with changes in the distribution of income (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.25). This implies that cultural norms, which can influence the pace and direction of structural change, may play a significant role in shaping long-term growth patterns.

Furthermore, Lindert and Williamson (1985) suggest that policymakers often face trade-offs between economic growth and equality, highlighting the importance of understanding these interrelationships (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.1). This underscores the potential for cultural norms to influence policies aimed at fostering growth, as well as their impact on income distribution.

The literature review on the fundamental cause of long-run growth indicates a convergence among scholars that cultural factors play a significant role in shaping institutions, which in turn influence economic growth. However, there is divergence regarding the extent to which culture dominates over other factors such as climate, social inequality, and gender inequality (Koepke & Baten_2005: p.20, p.28).

One of the key arguments supporting the cultural dominance hypothesis comes from Komlos_1998, who highlights the unprecedented achievement of maintaining a stable food price despite rapid population growth in the 20th century, attributing this to technological innovations like refrigeration (Komlos_1998: p.17). Similarly, Temin_2002 emphasizes the importance of labor force composition in economic growth, suggesting that the slow British growth during the Golden Age was due to a low share of the labor force in agriculture at the start of the period (Temin_2002: p.14).

On the other hand, Bohannan_1959 focuses on the role of institutions such as markets and marriage in facilitating exchange, suggesting that these are crucial for economic growth (Bohannan_1959: p.7). However, this work does not explicitly address the relationship between culture and institutions in determining long-run growth.

Koepke & Baten_2005 touch upon the topic but do not provide a definitive answer, acknowledging climate, social inequality, and gender inequality as potential factors that could influence economic growth (Koepke & Baten_2005: p.28). The relationship between these variables and cultural norms remains unresolved.
