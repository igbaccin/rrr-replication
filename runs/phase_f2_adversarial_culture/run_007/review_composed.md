The debate on the fundamental cause of long-run growth has been a subject of intense discussion among economists and economic historians. One prominent thesis posits that culture, through the shaping of institutions, plays a decisive role in determining a society's prosperity over time. This perspective, often referred to as the "institutions hypothesis," suggests that societies with robust institutions fostering private property rights and secure contracts tend to experience sustained economic growth (Acemoglu et al., 2001: p.27; North & Weingast, 1989: p.2).

The evidence supporting this argument is substantial. For instance, Daron Acemoglu and colleagues argue that institutions in former colonies were designed to maximize European colonists' rents rather than long-term growth (Acemoglu et al., 2002: p.33). Similarly, Douglass North and Barry Weingast contend that political institutions and constitutions are crucial for fostering long-run economic growth by enabling governments to commit credibly to upholding property rights (North & Weingast, 1989: p.2).

However, it is important to acknowledge that not all scholars agree with this perspective. Some argue that while institutions play a role in shaping economic outcomes, other factors such as geography and technological change may also be significant determinants of long-run growth (Abramovitz, 1986: p.21).

As the debate continues, it is crucial to consider the stakes involved. Understanding the fundamental causes of long-run growth can help policymakers design effective strategies for promoting economic development and reducing poverty in various societies. The institutions hypothesis offers a compelling explanation for why some countries have succeeded in achieving sustained economic growth while others have struggled. However, further research is needed to refine this perspective and account for other factors that may influence long-run growth.

The perspective that culture dominates institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run growth is substantiated by various scholars, particularly in relation to the role of institutions in agricultural development. According to Abramovitz (1986), European countries exhibited more advanced institutional structures that contributed to their successful entry into modern economic growth (p.21). Hopkins (2009) further supports this notion by arguing that institutional change was a product of both exogenous and African initiatives in colonies of trade, leading to the creation of distinct institutional legacies (p.12).

The impact of institutions on agricultural productivity is also evident in Whatley's (2018) study, where shocks such as the American demand for plantation labor and the introduction of gunpowder technology significantly influenced institutional change in Africa (p.2). This change, in turn, affected long-run growth by altering the balance between productivity and population growth.

Nunn & Wantchekon (2011) provide empirical evidence suggesting that low levels of trust and weak institutions persist among future generations due to self-enforcing outcomes, further supporting the argument that culture plays a crucial role in determining institutional development (p.6).

Allen's (2001) research on wage divergence between London and small towns in southern England indicates that institutional differences led to disparities in economic growth (p.4). Similarly, Frankema (2012) investigates the British legacy in educational policies, suggesting that it played a significant role in shaping long-run growth (p.2).

The significance of political institutions and their impact on long-run economic growth is further emphasized in various studies. Austin (2008) posits that institutional change, particularly those related to property rights and trade, significantly contributed to Europe's rise, thereby validating the assertion that institutions are 'the fundamental cause of long-run economic growth' (Austin_2008: p.26).

North & Weingast (1989) argue that political institutions play a crucial role in upholding property rights and fostering conditions conducive to long-term growth. They suggest that absolutist states, such as early modern Spain, created economic conditions that hindered long-run growth due to the absence of such constraints (North&Weingast_1989: p.7).

Broadberry & Gardner (2022) argue that the focus on specific circumstances during a particular period may be misleading in understanding Africa's long-term performance. They suggest that the absence of data on African economies has shaped the current literature, which often assumes that economic growth above subsistence resulted from European colonial interventions rather than indigenous institutions (Broadberry&Gardner_2022: p.1).

Bryceson (2002) discusses structural changes in African peasantries' social institutions and their correlation with economic activities. He highlights that targeted improvements in specific activities can affect other activities in the portfolio, and the lack of purchasing power is a major challenge for non-agricultural village-based activities (Bryceson_2002: p.12).

These findings collectively support the thesis that culture, expressed through institutions, dominates as the fundamental cause of long-run growth. The role of political institutions in fostering property rights, trade, and economic growth is evident, particularly in Europe's case. In Africa, the absence or misinterpretation of data has led to incorrect assumptions about economic growth, highlighting the importance of understanding indigenous institutions. Lastly, the correlation between structural changes in African social institutions and economic activities underscores the significance of cultural factors in long-run growth.

The works of Acemoglu et al. (2001, 2002) provide substantial evidence supporting the notion that culture, particularly indigenous institutions, plays a pivotal role in long-run growth. In their research, Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.27) emphasize the impact of fundamental institutions on property rights and economic performance, suggesting that these institutions persist over time (AcemogluEtAl_2001: p.1). Moreover, they argue that European colonization significantly influenced the design of institutions in former colonies, often to maximize the benefits for European colonists rather than promoting long-run growth (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.33).

Furthermore, Acemoglu et al. (AcemogluEtAl_2002: p.49) assert that societies with institutions of private property experienced a significant advantage during the age of industry. This underscores the importance of indigenous institutions in shaping economic outcomes over time. In contrast, Frankema & van Waijenburg (2012) argue that our understanding of Africa's growth performance before 1950 is limited, leaving room for interpretations that emphasize cultural factors (Frankema&vanWaijenburg_2012: p.4).

While Frankema and van Waijenburg (2012) suggest that cultural factors may have played a significant role in Africa's growth before 1950, Stern (2004) challenges this perspective by focusing on the relationship between institutions and economic growth in Asia. Stern critiques the environmental Kuznets curve (EKC), a theory that suggests economic development initially worsens environmental conditions but eventually leads to improvements as income increases (Stern_2004: p.1).

Contrary to the argument that culture dominates institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run growth, Stern provides evidence that structural change correlates with growth. He argues that the decline in lack of clean water and urban sanitation is uniformly associated with increasing income levels, suggesting that improvements in these areas are a result of economic development rather than cultural factors (Stern_2004: p.6).

Furthermore, Stern points out that this range of income levels is greater than what is found in OECD-based panels for recent decades, implying that Asia's economic growth may be more diverse and complex than previously thought (Stern_2004: p.6). These findings challenge the notion that cultural factors are the primary drivers of long-term growth, suggesting instead that institutions and structural changes play a crucial role in shaping economic outcomes.

However, it is important to note that Stern's study focuses primarily on environmental issues within Asia, and further research may be needed to fully understand the interplay between culture, institutions, and economic growth across various regions and time periods.

Structural change, a shift in the composition of an economy from agriculture to industry and services, is indeed correlated with economic growth (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.10). However, this relationship does not necessarily imply that culture dominates institutions as the fundamental cause of long-run growth.

Lindert & Williamson (1985) argue that structural change is a result of shifts in final-product demand, which can be influenced by various factors including technological advancements and changes in consumer preferences (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.10). While culture could potentially influence these factors, it does not necessarily have a direct causal relationship with economic growth.

Moreover, Lindert & Williamson acknowledge that their study primarily focuses on the West and neglects the socialist bloc, which limits the scope of their findings (Lindert&Williamson_1985: p.4). Therefore, it is unclear whether the observed relationship between structural change and growth holds true for all regions, including those with different cultural and institutional contexts.

Van Zanden's study also complicates the argument by suggesting that the value of coinage, which can be influenced by cultural norms surrounding money and trade, may have a link with economic growth (vanZanden_1999: p.6). However, this does not necessarily mean that culture dominates institutions as the primary driver of long-run growth.

The relationship between culture, institutions, and long-run growth is a complex issue that various scholars have addressed from different perspectives. While some argue that institutions play a more significant role in economic growth (Temin_2002), others suggest that cultural norms might be the primary driver (Akyeampong&Fofack_2914).

On one hand, Temin (2002) emphasizes the importance of institutions, particularly labor force composition and technological advancements, in determining economic growth rates. For instance, he notes that the exhaustion of cheap labor led to a slowdown in economic growth (Temin_2002: p.4). Similarly, Komlos (1998) discusses how innovations like refrigeration enabled perishable agricultural products to be shipped over longer distances, potentially contributing to economic growth (Komlos_1998: p.17).

On the other hand, Akyeampong and Fofack (2014) argue that cultural norms have a lasting impact on institutional developments, particularly in African societies. They highlight how cultural norms influenced education, kinship, commerce, and nascent capitalism, often disadvantaging women (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.4). Moreover, they point out that studies have documented the continuity of these cultural norms over long periods of time (Akyeampong&Fofack_2014: p.10).

However, neither Temin nor Akyeampong and Fofack explicitly state that culture dominates institutions as the primary cause of long-run growth. The debate remains unresolved, with scholars suggesting different factors play varying roles in economic development. Future research could focus on understanding how culture and institutions interact to shape long-term economic growth.

In terms of the evidence provided, Temin's work (2002) offers insights into the role of labor force composition and technological advancements in economic growth, while Akyeampong and Fofack's research (2014) sheds light on the impact of cultural norms on institutional developments. Komlos (1998) and Bohannan (1959), although tangential to the main topic, provide additional context regarding population growth and technological innovations that may influence economic development.
